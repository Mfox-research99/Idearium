# Term: Jihad

## At a glance

Jihad (Arabic, جهاد, from root j-h-d “to strive/exert”) is a central concept in Islamic theology and law, carrying a polysemy that the English “holy war” or “crusade” cannot capture. Within the Islamic tradition it has at least two major senses: the “greater jihad” of inner spiritual struggle against the lower self (nafs), and the “lesser jihad” of external armed struggle in defense of the community. A third, more technical sense appears in classical jurisprudence as a collective duty to expand or defend the territory of Islam. ## Polysemy

1. **Greater Jihad (al-jihād al-akbar) — the inner spiritual struggle** — The primary meaning in Sufi and later moral theology: the believer’s lifelong effort to purify the soul, resist temptation, and submit to God. Overlaps with the Idea of Virtue and the Sufi concept of Nafs.

   > *No corpus passage available.*

2. **Lesser Jihad (al-jihād al-aṣghar) — armed struggle in defense of the community** — The classical legal meaning: a collective duty (farḍ kifāya) to fight when the Muslim community is attacked or to remove oppression. Governed by strict rules of conduct (prohibition of killing non-combatants, etc.). Overlaps with the Idea of War and Justice.

   > *No corpus passage available.*

3. **Jihad as a legal category of expansion** — In classical fiqh, jihad could also refer to military campaigns to expand the territory of Islam (dār al-islām) against non-Muslim polities, though this sense was debated and often conditional. Overlaps with the Idea of Government and Law.

## Common misuses

In modern English-language discourse, “jihad” is frequently reduced to “holy war” or “terrorism,” collapsing the inner spiritual meaning and the legal constraints of the lesser jihad. The term is also used colloquially to mean any obsessive campaign (“my jihad against clutter”). Neither usage reflects the classical Islamic understanding, which distinguishes sharply between the greater and lesser forms and binds the lesser to strict ethical and legal conditions.
