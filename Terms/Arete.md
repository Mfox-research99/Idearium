# Term: Arete

## At a glance

Arete (ἀρετή) is the central Greek term for excellence — the quality that makes a thing good at being what it is. The arete of a knife is its sharpness; the arete of a horse is its speed and endurance; the arete of a human being is the full realization of the distinctively human function (ergon) — living well in accordance with reason. In Homer, arete is primarily martial: the warrior's prowess, courage, and honor. In Aristotle, it becomes the systematic concept of moral and intellectual virtue (ēthikē and dianoētikē aretē), defined as a mean between extremes and cultivated through habituation. The term thus bridges a pre-philosophical heroic ideal and a fully theorized ethical framework. The Western Ideas of Virtue, Excellence, and Happiness (eudaimonia) each depend on it; Aristotle's functional argument in the Nicomachean Ethics makes arete inseparable from the question of what a human being is *for*. The corpus contains Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics (Browne/Ross translations) and Metaphysics, plus Homer and Cicero as secondary sources.

## Polysemy

1. **Arete as functional excellence** — The most philosophically distilled sense: the arete of any thing is what enables it to perform its characteristic function (ergon) well. The arete of the eye is to see well; the arete of man is to live well. This functional account is the foundation of Aristotle's ethics.

   > *Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, trans. W.D. Ross / R.W. Browne, Book II, Ch. 5* — "We must observe then that all excellence makes that whereof it is the excellence both to be itself in a good state and to perform its work well. The excellence of the eye, for instance, makes both the eye good and its work also: for by the excellence of the eye we see well. So too the excellence of the horse makes a horse good, and good in speed, and in carrying his rider, and standing up against the enemy. If then this is universally the case, the excellence of Man, i.e. Virtue, must be a state whereby Man comes to be good and whereby he will perform well his proper work." (equivalent match — "excellence" and "virtue" both render aretē; the functional account is the philosophical core)

2. **Arete as the mean** — Moral arete is a settled disposition (hexis) that chooses the mean relative to us, as determined by practical wisdom (phronēsis). Each virtue lies between two vices — courage between cowardice and rashness, temperance between insensibility and self-indulgence. Arete is thus not a single quality but a pattern of calibrated responses.

   > *Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, Book II* — "In all quantity then, whether continuous or discrete, one may take the greater part, the less, or the exactly equal, and these either with reference to the thing itself, or relatively to us: and the exactly equal is a mean between excess and defect. Now by the mean of the thing, i.e. absolute mean, I denote that which is equidistant from either extreme... and by the mean relatively to ourselves, that which is neither too much nor too little for the particular individual." (equivalent match — the doctrine of the mean as the structure of moral aretē)

3. **Arete as contemplative activity** — The highest arete is the excellence of the best part of the soul — intellect (nous) — exercised in contemplation (theōria). This is the "perfect happiness" of Book X: the life according to the highest excellence. Arete thus has a hierarchy from the practical-moral to the theoretical-contemplative.

   > *Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics / Metaphysics, Book X / Metaphysics I* — "Now if Happiness is a Working in the way of Excellence of course that Excellence must be the highest, that is to say, the Excellence of the best Principle. Whether then this best Principle is Intellect or some other which is thought naturally to rule and to lead and to conceive of noble and divine things... the Working of this in accordance with its own proper Excellence must be the perfect Happiness." (equivalent match — the hierarchy of aretai culminating in contemplative excellence)

4. **Arete as heroic prowess (Homeric)** — In Homer, arete is the warrior's excellence: courage in battle, skill with weapons, and the honor (timē) that accrues to the display of these qualities. This is arete before philosophy — a public, competitive, and gender-specific ideal (the Greek word for man, anēr, is embedded in the etymology). The Iliad's heroes pursue arete as the defining goal of a noble life, even at the cost of death.

## Common misuses

In modern English, "virtue" has been narrowed by its Christian history to mean moral rectitude or chastity — a flattening that obscures the term's Greek breadth. Arete is excellence across the full range of human capacities: a virtuoso violinist exhibits arete no less than a just judge. The reduction of arete to "being a good person" in the moralistic sense loses the functional dimension — the idea that to be good *at* being human is like being good *at* anything else. Another misuse treats Aristotle's doctrine of the mean as a counsel of moderation or compromise, when it is actually a claim about precision: the mean is not "a little of everything" but exactly the right response at the right time in the right way — something only practical wisdom can discern.

## Homer — The Iliad (trans. Samuel Butler, 1898)

> *Book I — Achilles swears his withdrawal* — "'Surely and solemnly do I swear that hereafter they shall look fondly for Achilles and shall not find him. In the day of your distress, when your men fall dying by the murderous hand of Hector, you shall not know how to help them, and shall rend your heart with rage for the hour when you offered insult to the bravest of the Achaeans.' With this the son of Peleus dashed his gold-bestudded sceptre on the ground and took his seat."
>
> *Gloss: Arete and timē (honor) are inseparable in the Homeric world. Achilles withdraws not because his life is threatened but because his arete has been publicly dishonored — Agamemnon took Briseis, the visible token of Achilles's excellence. The oath is consequentialist: the Achaeans will discover the value of what they have dishonored only through its absence. Arete in Homer is not a private quality but a public claim requiring public recognition.*

> *Book I — Nestor's intervention* — "'Of a truth,' he said, 'a great sorrow has befallen the Achaean land. Surely Priam with his sons would rejoice, and the Trojans be glad at heart if they could hear this quarrel between you two, who are so excellent in fight and counsel. ... Never again can I behold such men as Pirithous and Dryas shepherd of his people, or as Caeneus, Exadius, godlike Polyphemus, and Theseus son of Aegeus, peer of the immortals. These were the mightiest men ever born upon this earth: mightiest were they, and when they fought the fiercest tribes of mountain savages they utterly overthrew them.'"
>
> *Gloss: Arete is generational and comparative. Nestor establishes his authority to mediate by invoking the greater heroes of an earlier age — men whose arete exceeded even that of Achilles and Agamemnon. This is the Homeric version of the "golden age" trope: arete is always in decline, always greater in the generation before. The function is not nostalgia but a call to live up to the standard.*

> *Book I — Agamemnon's complaint* — "And Agamemnon answered, 'Sir, all that you have said is true, but this fellow must needs become our lord and master: he must be lord of all, king of all, and captain of all, and this shall hardly be. Granted that the gods have made him a great warrior, have they also given him the right to speak with railing?'"
>
> *Gloss: The tension between two kinds of arete — martial excellence (Achilles) and political authority (Agamemnon). Agamemnon concedes Achilles's martial arete ("the gods have made him a great warrior") but disputes that it grants him authority over speech and command. The Iliad's tragic structure arises from this incompatibility: arete is not unified; the best warrior and the best king are not the same man, and their conflict destroys both.*

> *Book XII — Sarpedon's speech to Glaucus* — "'Glaucus, why in Lycia do we receive especial honour as regards our place at table? Why are the choicest portions served us and our cups kept brimming, and why do men look up to us as though we were gods? Moreover we hold a large estate by the banks of the river Xanthus, fair with orchard lawns and wheat-growing land; it becomes us, therefore, to take our stand at the head of all the Lycians and bear the brunt of the fight, that one may say to another, "Our princes in Lycia eat the fat of the land and drink best of wine, but they are fine fellows; they fight well and are ever at the front in battle." My good friend, if, when we were once out of this fight, we could escape old age and death thenceforward and forever, I should neither press forward myself nor bid you do so, but death in ten thousand shapes hangs ever over our heads, and no man can elude him; therefore let us go forward and either win glory for ourselves, or yield it to another.'"
>
> *Gloss: The most concentrated statement of the Homeric heroic code — arete as the obligation that privilege imposes. The logic is brutally clear: the Lycian princes receive the best food, the best wine, the largest estates, and the honor due to gods — therefore they must be first into battle. The hypothetical ("if we could escape old age and death... I should neither press forward") reveals that the code is not a death-wish but a calculus: since death is inevitable, glory (kleos) is the only immortality available. This is arete before philosophy — excellence not as a state of soul but as a performed claim on eternal memory. The alternative — to "yield glory to another" — is the worst outcome: to die without kleos is to die twice.*
