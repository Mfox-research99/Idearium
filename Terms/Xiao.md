# Term: Xiao

## At a glance

Xiao (孝) is a foundational virtue in Confucian ethics, often translated as “filial piety.” It denotes the duty of children to honor, serve, and care for their parents and ancestors, and it extends to the broader social and political order. The term is polysemous: it can refer to a specific family obligation, a model for all hierarchical relationships, a ritual practice of ancestor veneration, and a principle of moral cultivation. The Western Ideas of Duty, Family, and Filial Piety each capture part of Xiao, but none fully conveys its centrality to Confucian social harmony and self-cultivation.

## Common misuses

In modern Western usage, “filial piety” is often reduced to simple obedience or respect for parents, ignoring its ritual, ancestral, and political dimensions. It is sometimes caricatured as blind submission, whereas classical Confucian texts emphasize remonstration with parents when they err (Analects 4.18). Another misuse is to treat Xiao as a uniquely Chinese concept, when analogous ideas exist in many cultures (e.g., Roman pietas, Jewish kibbud av va’em). The term is also occasionally invoked in popular self-help as a generic “honor your parents” slogan, stripped of its specific Confucian framework.
