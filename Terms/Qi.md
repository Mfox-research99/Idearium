# Term: Qi

## At a glance

Qi (氣, also romanized as *ch'i*) is the foundational concept of classical Chinese cosmology, philosophy, and medicine. It denotes the animating energy or vital force that flows through all things, constituting both matter and spirit in a continuous, dynamic field. The term resists reduction to any single Western Idea: it overlaps with *Nature*, *Life*, *Matter*, *Spirit*, *Energy*, and *Breath*, but none captures its simultaneous materiality and subtlety. Qi warrants its own Term page because its polysemy — ranging from the literal breath of the body to the primordial substance of the cosmos — is internal to the Chinese tradition and would be flattened by any one English equivalent.

**Methodological note:** The pre-1929 English translations in the corpus (e.g., Legge's *Tao Te Ching* and *Analects*, Giles's *Chuang Tzu*) overwhelmingly translate 氣 as "breath," "vapor," "spirit," or "force" rather than transliterating it. As a result, direct keyword searches for "qi" or "ch'i" return no relevant passages from Chinese sources. Equivalent-match searches for "breath" and "vital force" filtered to the Chinese tradition also returned zero usable passages in the current corpus. ## Polysemy

1. **Cosmic / primordial qi** — The undifferentiated, all-pervading substance from which the ten thousand things arise. In early Daoist cosmology, qi is the medium of transformation, the "one breath" that becomes yin and yang. Overlaps with *Nature*, *Matter*, *Being*.

   > *(No primary-source quotation available in the current corpus.)*

2. **Vital breath / life force** — The animating energy within living beings, especially humans. In Chinese medicine, qi flows through meridians; health depends on its balanced circulation. Overlaps with *Life*, *Spirit*, *Breath*.

   > *(No primary-source quotation available in the current corpus.)*

3. **Psycho-physical energy / disposition** — In Confucian texts, qi can refer to one's inner energy, temperament, or moral force. Mencius speaks of the "flood-like qi" (浩然之氣) that fills the body when one cultivates righteousness. Overlaps with *Virtue*, *Will*, *Emotion*.

   > *(No primary-source quotation available in the current corpus.)*

4. **Material force / ether** — In Neo-Confucian metaphysics (Zhu Xi), qi is the material aspect of reality, contrasted with *li* (principle). All things are composed of li and qi; qi gives them form and individuation. Overlaps with *Matter*, *Form*, *Substance*.

   > *(No primary-source quotation available in the current corpus.)*

## Common misuses

In modern Western popular culture, "qi" (often spelled "chi") is frequently reduced to a vague "energy" that can be manipulated through martial arts or New Age practices, divorced from its precise cosmological and medical contexts. This flattening ignores the term's role in Chinese philosophy as a technical concept with specific debates (e.g., the relation of qi to li, the cultivation of qi in self-cultivation, the medical theory of qi circulation). The popular usage also tends to treat qi as a substance that can be "sent" or "blocked" in a simplistic way, whereas classical texts treat it as a continuous field of transformation.

## Adjacent Terms

- [[Tao]] — The Way, the ultimate principle; qi is the dynamic expression of the Tao in the phenomenal world.
- [[De]] — Virtue or power; the manifestation of the Tao in an individual, often linked to the cultivation of qi.
- [[Wu Wei]] — Non-action or effortless action; achieved when one's qi is aligned with the Tao.
- [[Li]] — Principle or pattern; in Neo-Confucianism, li is the normative structure that qi actualizes.
- [[Ren]] — Humaneness or benevolence; the Confucian virtue that, when cultivated, transforms one's qi.
- [[Yi]] — Rightness or righteousness; the moral disposition that Mencius connects to the "flood-like qi."
