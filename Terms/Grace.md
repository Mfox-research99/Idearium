# Term: Grace

## At a glance

Grace (from Latin *gratia*, "favor, thanks, charm") is a term with at least three distinct senses in the Western corpus: (1) theological — the unmerited divine favor that enables salvation and sanctification; (2) aesthetic — natural or acquired elegance of movement, form, or expression; (3) social — an honorific title ("Your Grace") for a duke or archbishop. The theological sense is the most load-bearing, appearing in Eckhart's sermons and Leibniz's metaphysics as the bridge between nature and God. The aesthetic sense, articulated by Wollstonecraft, distinguishes true grace (independence of mind) from artificial grace (affected deportment). The title usage is a conventional honorific in Shakespeare. These meanings are not interchangeable, and the term warrants its own page because the English Idea pages for God, Beauty, and Virtue each catch only one facet.

## Polysemy

1. **Theological Grace (Divine Favor)** — The unmerited gift of God that quickens the soul, makes it Godlike, and unites it with God. This is the central meaning in Christian theology and in Leibniz's philosophical theology, where grace constitutes a realm of final causes distinct from but perfecting nature. Overlaps with the Ideas of God, Religion, Sin, and Soul.

   > *Meister Eckhart, Sermons, trans. C. de B. Evans (1924)* — "The function of grace is to quicken the soul and fit her for all divine uses, for grace flows out in a divine stream which savours of God and is a likeness of God and makes the soul Godlike. When grace with its savour is warped into the will it is called love and when this grace with its savour is warped into the intellect it is called the light of faith, and this same grace and savour warped into the irascible power goes by the name of hope." (Passage D4)

2. **Aesthetic Grace (Elegance of Form or Movement)** — Natural or acquired beauty of deportment that expresses the mind rather than mere bodily habit. Wollstonecraft distinguishes "true grace" (arising from independence of mind) from "artificial grace" (affected, learned behavior). Overlaps with the Ideas of Beauty and Art.

   > *Mary Wollstonecraft, A Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792)* — "I say artificial, for true grace arises from some kind of independence of mind. Children, careless of pleasing, and only anxious to amuse themselves, are often very graceful; and the nobility who have mostly lived with inferiors, and always had the command of money, acquire a graceful ease of deportment, which should rather be termed habitual grace of body, than that superiour gracefulness which is truly the expression of the mind." (Passage D3)

3. **Honorific Title ("Your Grace")** — A formal address for a duke or archbishop, used in Shakespeare to denote rank and deference. This is a conventional social usage with no theological or aesthetic weight.

   > *William Shakespeare, Richard III, Complete Works (c. 1592)* — "He doth entreat your Grace, my noble lord, / To visit him tomorrow or next day." (Passage D7)

## Common misuses

In modern colloquial English, "grace" is often used as a synonym for poise or elegance, conflating the aesthetic sense with the theological. A person may be said to have "grace under pressure," which borrows the theological connotation of unmerited composure but secularizes it. The phrase "grace period" (an extension of time) derives from the theological sense of leniency but is now entirely commercial. None of these modern drifts preserves the metaphysical weight of Eckhart's or Leibniz's usage.

## Adjacent Terms

- [[Hesed]] — Hebrew term for covenantal loving-kindness, often translated as "grace" in the Old Testament; overlaps with theological grace but emphasizes covenant fidelity rather than unmerited gift.
- [[Fana]] — Sufi term for annihilation of the self in God; parallels Eckhart's description of grace "robbing the soul of self" and uniting her with God.
- [[Logos]] — Greek term for divine reason; in Johannine theology, grace and truth come through Jesus Christ (John 1:17), linking Logos and grace.

## Status notes

The corpus provides strong direct matches for the theological and aesthetic senses of grace, and a clear example of the honorific title. No equivalent matches were needed because the term itself appears in the English texts. The Greek term *charis* (χάρις) and the Hebrew *hen* (חֵן) are not represented in the corpus as separate terms; they are subsumed under the English "grace" in biblical translations. A future expansion might add a Term page for *Charis* if the Greek corpus warrants it. The aesthetic sense is less developed in the corpus than the theological; Wollstonecraft's passage is the only extended treatment. Leibniz's distinction between nature and grace is philosophically rich but appears only in editorial commentary (D1) and the title of his work (D6); the actual text of the *Principles of Nature and of Grace* is not quoted in the supplied passages.
