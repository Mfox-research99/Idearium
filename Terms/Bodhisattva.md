# Term: Bodhisattva

## At a glance

Bodhisattva (Sanskrit: बोधिसत्त्व, from *bodhi* "enlightenment" + *sattva* "being") is the central ideal of Mahayana Buddhism and a significant concept in Theravada and Vajrayana. It refers to a being who has generated *bodhicitta* (the aspiration for awakening) and, in its fullest sense, vows to attain Buddhahood for the sake of all sentient beings rather than seeking personal nirvana alone. The term bears at least four distinct meanings across Buddhist traditions — a historical Buddha-to-be, a compassionate practitioner on the path, a celestial savior-figure, and a universal vocational ideal — that cannot be flattened into any single Western Idea. The English ideas of Duty, Witness, Good and Evil, and Soul each capture part of it; none captures the whole.

The corpus currently contains no direct or equivalent primary-source passages for this term (see Status notes). The descriptions below are based on standard scholarly understanding and will be updated when passages are added.

## Polysemy

**Methodological note:** The pre-1929 English translations in the corpus rarely transliterate "bodhisattva"; they instead use phrases like "Buddha to be" or "enlightened being." Future retrieval of equivalent matches would need to search those phrases. The following definitions are given without cited quotations until such passages are available.

1. **The historical Buddha-to-be (Theravada / early Buddhism)** — In the Pali canon, the term *bodhisatta* (Pali) refers to the previous lives of Gotama Buddha, from his first aspiration under the Buddha Dipankara up to his final birth as Siddhartha. It designates a specific individual destined to become a Fully Enlightened Buddha (*sammāsambuddha*) after accumulating perfections (*pāramī*) over countless lifetimes. This meaning is restricted to a single being per eon and is not offered as a universal path.

2. **The Mahayana bodhisattva ideal — the compassionate practitioner who postpones nirvana** — In Mahayana scriptures (e.g., the Bodhicaryavatara, Lotus Sutra), the bodhisattva is anyone who has aroused *bodhicitta* — the mind of awakening — and vows to work for the liberation of all beings. Unlike the Theravada arhat ideal, the Mahayana bodhisattva deliberately turns away from personal nirvana until every last being is freed. This meaning is universal: all Mahayana practitioners are encouraged to take the bodhisattva vow.

3. **Celestial bodhisattvas — transcendent savior figures** — In Mahayana and Vajrayana, certain bodhisattvas (e.g., Avalokiteśvara, Mañjuśrī, Samantabhadra, Kṣitigarbha) are advanced beings who have attained high levels of realization but remain active in the world out of compassion. They are often depicted as semi-divine figures who can be invoked for aid. These are distinct from human bodhisattvas on the path; they function like bodhisattva "saints" with specific powers and vows.

4. **The bodhisattva as a universal ethical-archetypal ideal** — In modern Buddhist modernism and some Western adaptations, the term is detached from its cosmological framework (rebirth, merit, perfections) and used to describe any person dedicated to compassionate action. While this usage is contested by traditionalists, it has become a recognizable ethical concept — a person who embodies selfless service. Overlap with the Western Idea of Witness (bearing witness through compassionate action) is strong here, but the term loses its distinctively Buddhist anchor.

## Common misuses

- **"Bodhisattva" as any good person** — In popular usage, the term is often applied loosely to anyone who does charitable work or shows compassion. This ignores the formal vow, the generation of *bodhicitta*, and the doctrinal framework of the ten perfections (*pāramitā*). The classical bodhisattva path is not merely altruism; it is a systematic spiritual discipline aimed at Buddhahood.
- **Confusion with "bodhi" (enlightenment)** — The term is sometimes treated as synonymous with "enlightened being" (i.e., a Buddha). In fact, a bodhisattva is one *on the path to* enlightenment, not yet a fully awakened Buddha (except for celestial bodhisattvas understood to be very high, even equivalent to Buddhas in some traditions).
- **Romanticization of self-sacrifice** — Western commentaries sometimes portray the bodhisattva ideal as a purely self-sacrificing altruism in contrast to an allegedly selfish arhat. This ignores the complexity of Mahayana debates and the fact that the bodhisattva also seeks their own ultimate benefit as inseparable from others'. The arhat is not "selfish" in Theravada's own terms.

## Adjacent Terms

- [[Nirvana]] — The bodhisattva postpones nirvana to help others; the term contrasts with the arhat's personal nirvana.
- [[Anatman]] — The bodhisattva's practice of selflessness (*anātman*) is central; the term clarifies that the "being" in "enlightenment being" is not a permanent self.
- [[Dukkha]] — The bodhisattva's compassion arises from seeing suffering; the term anchors the motivation.
- [[Sunyata]] — The bodhisattva's wisdom realizes emptiness, enabling compassion without attachment.
- [[Karma]] — The bodhisattva accumulates merit (*puṇya*) through karma, but is not bound by it; the term shows karma's relation to liberation.
- [[Dharma]] — The bodhisattva embodies the Dharma as teaching and practice.

## Status notes

The corpus currently has zero direct or equivalent matches for "bodhisattva" (or pre-1929 English renderings such as "Buddha-to-be," "enlightened being," etc.). This is a known limitation of the pre-1929 translation conventions: Müller's *Buddhist Suttas* (1895), Warren's *Buddhism in Translations* (1896), and similar works use the term "bodhisattva" only sparingly and often paraphrase instead. More modern translations (e.g., Conze, Edgerton) would contain the term but fall outside the current corpus date range.
