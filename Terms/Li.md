# Term: Li

## At a glance

Li (禮, lǐ) is the Confucian term for ritual propriety — the ensemble of ceremonial forms, social norms, and cultivated dispositions through which human beings become fully human. It is simultaneously outward (ritual performance, ceremonial etiquette) and inward (proper attitude, sincerity, reverence). In the Analects, Confucius teaches that li is the necessary form that gives shape to virtue: benevolence (ren) without li becomes indiscriminate; courage without li becomes violent. The Western Ideas of Law, Virtue, and Custom each catch part of it; none catches its dual character as both external practice and internal cultivation. Because James Legge consistently translated li as "propriety" or "the rules of propriety," all passages below are equivalent matches flagged with his English rendering.

## Polysemy

1. **Li as ritual and ceremonial form** — The most concrete sense: prescribed observances of sacrifice, mourning, court audience, and daily etiquette. These are not arbitrary conventions but the patterned forms through which human relationships are enacted.

   > *Confucius, Analects, trans. James Legge, 1861 (Project Gutenberg #3330)* — "The Master, when he entered the grand temple, asked about everything. Some one said, 'Who will say that the son of the man of Tsau knows the rules of propriety! He has entered the grand temple and asks about everything.' The Master heard the remark, and said, 'This is a rule of propriety.'" (equivalent match — even asking about ritual is itself an act of li)

   > *Confucius, Analects, trans. James Legge, 1861* — "'It is according to the rules of propriety,' they say. 'It is according to the rules of propriety,' they say. Are gems and silk all that is meant by propriety?" (equivalent match — li is not reducible to its material trappings; the spirit animates the form)

2. **Li as the moderating and civilizing principle** — Li restrains excess and corrects one-sidedness. Every virtue, untempered by li, becomes a vice: respectfulness becomes "laborious bustle," boldness becomes insubordination.

   > *Confucius, Analects, trans. James Legge, 1861 (Book VIII, Chap. II)* — "The Master said, 'Respectfulness, without the rules of propriety, becomes laborious bustle; carefulness, without the rules of propriety, becomes timidity; boldness, without the rules of propriety, becomes insubordination; straightforwardness, without the rules of propriety, becomes rudeness.'" (equivalent match — the most concentrated statement of li as the moderating principle that prevents virtue from curdling into vice)

3. **Li as self-cultivation (ke ji fu li)** — The inward dimension: li is the discipline through which the self is subdued and transformed. Confucius's teaching to Yan Yuan makes li the path of self-mastery.

   > *Confucius, Analects, trans. James Legge, 1861 (Book XII, Chap. I)* — "Yen Yuan asked about perfect virtue. The Master said, 'To subdue one's self and return to propriety, is perfect virtue. ... Look not at what is contrary to propriety; listen not to what is contrary to propriety; speak not what is contrary to propriety; make no movement which is contrary to propriety.'" (equivalent match — li as total self-discipline encompassing perception, speech, and action)

4. **Li as social and political order** — Li governs the full spectrum of human relationships. Confucius contrasts li-governance with law-governance (fa), where order depends on external punishment rather than internalized norms.

   > *Confucius, Analects, trans. James Legge, 1861 (Book II, Chap. III)* — "If the people be led by laws, and uniformity sought to be given them by punishments, they will try to avoid the punishment, but have no sense of shame. If they be led by virtue, and uniformity sought to be given them by the rules of propriety, they will have the sense of shame, and moreover will become good." (equivalent match — the classic li/fa contrast)

5. **Natural ease within li** — Li is not rigid legalism; the highest practice wears its form lightly. But ease without li collapses into license; the form disciplines the spontaneity.

   > *Confucius, Analects, trans. James Legge, 1861 (Book I, Chap. XII)* — "In practising the rules of propriety, a natural ease is to be prized. ... If one, knowing how such ease should be prized, manifests it, without regulating it by the rules of propriety, this likewise is not to be done." (equivalent match — the paradox: natural ease is prized, but ease without li collapses into license)

## Common misuses

In modern English, "Confucian ritual" often evokes stiff formalism or empty ceremony. This misses the Analects' insistence that li is worthless without the corresponding inner attitude. The outer form is hollow without inner substance, but the inner substance cannot develop without the outer form — the relationship is dialectical. Another misuse reduces li to "Chinese etiquette," stripping its philosophical weight as a comprehensive theory of how human beings are formed through patterned practice.
