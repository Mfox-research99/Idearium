# Term: Karma

## At a glance

Karma (Sanskrit, कर्म, from √kṛ "to do/make") is the Sanskrit term for action and its consequences — the universal law of moral causation that links deed to result across lifetimes. In the Bhagavad Gītā it anchors three distinct yogas: karma-yoga (the path of selfless action), jñāna-yoga (the path of knowledge), and bhakti-yoga (the path of devotion). In the Vedānta-sūtras it is the opponent that knowledge must overcome: liberating knowledge (brahmavidyā) burns the accumulated karmic residues, freeing the self from saṃsāra. In Buddhist thought it is an impersonal law of cause and effect operating without a transmigrating self (see [[Anatman]]). The Western Ideas of Causality, Justice (as retributive), and Duty each catch part of it; none catches the law's impersonality, its operation across lifetimes, and its centrality as the problem that all Indian philosophy must solve. Because pre-1929 translators consistently rendered karma as "action," "work," "deed," or "fruit of works" rather than transliterating it, all passages below are equivalent matches flagged with the translator's English word.

## Polysemy

1. **Action as moral causation** — The foundational sense: every action (physical, verbal, mental) produces a consequence (phala, "fruit") that accrues to the agent, either in this life or a future one. This is not a divine judgment — no god administers karma — but an impersonal law built into the structure of reality, as automatic as gravity. The law operates through saṃskāras (mental impressions) and vāsanās (latent tendencies) that shape future experience.

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890 (Sacred Books of the East, vol. 34)* — "Good deeds likewise lose their efficiency... but on the fall of the body, i.e. when death takes place, there results mukti of the vidvan, who through his knowledge has freed himself from the bonds of works." (equivalent match — "works" and "deeds" render karma; the passage describes how liberating knowledge annihilates accumulated karma)

2. **Karma-yoga — the path of selfless action** — The central teaching of the Bhagavad Gītā: act without attachment to the fruits of action (niṣkāma karma). Perform your duty (svadharma) as an offering, renouncing not action itself but the desire for its results. This transforms karma from a binding force into a liberating one — action becomes yoga.

   > *Vyāsa (attrib.), Bhagavad Gītā, trans. Edwin Arnold, 1885* — "Do thine allotted task! / Work is more excellent than idleness; / The body's life proceeds not, lacking work. / There is a task of holiness to do, / Unlike world-binding toil, which bindeth not / The faithful soul; such earthly duty do / Free from desire, and thou shalt well perform / Thy heavenly purpose." (equivalent match — Arnold's "work," "toil," and "task" render karma; the passage teaches niṣkāma karma)

   > *Vyāsa (attrib.), Bhagavad Gītā, trans. Edwin Arnold, 1885* — "Therefore, thy task prescribed / With spirit unattached gladly perform, / Since in performance of plain duty man / Mounts to his highest bliss. By works alone / Janak and ancient saints reached blessedness!" (equivalent match — "works" renders karma; the Gītā's core teaching that selfless action is a path to liberation)

3. **The inescapability of action** — The Gītā's argument that complete inaction is impossible: even the maintenance of the body requires action, and the three guṇas (qualities of nature) compel all beings to act. The question is not whether to act but how — with attachment (binding) or without (liberating).

   > *Vyāsa (attrib.), Bhagavad Gītā, trans. Edwin Arnold, 1885* — "All things are everywhere by Nature wrought / In interaction of the qualities. / The fool, cheated by self, thinks, 'This I did' / And 'That I wrought'; but—ah, thou strong-armed Prince!— / A better-lessoned mind, knowing the play / Of visible things within the world of sense, / And how the qualities must qualify, / Standeth aloof even from his acts." (equivalent match — the doctrine that the guṇas are the true agents, and the wise recognize this, freeing themselves from karmic bondage)

4. **Karma vs. knowledge in the Vedānta** — A central tension in the Vedānta-sūtras: the Pūrva Mīmāṃsā holds that ritual action (karma) is the path to the highest good; the Uttara Mīmāṃsā (Vedānta) holds that knowledge (jñāna) of Brahman alone liberates. Karma is not denied but relativized — it purifies the mind and prepares it for knowledge, but cannot itself produce liberation. The non-operation of past karma upon the knower of Brahman is a major technical doctrine.

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890* — "For an action cannot exist without modifying that in which it abides. But if the Self were modified by an action its non-eternality would result therefrom, and texts such as the following, 'unchangeable he is called,' would thus be stultified; an altogether unacceptable result. Hence it is impossible to assume that any action should abide in the Self." (equivalent match — the argument that karma cannot inhere in the unchanging ātman, a cornerstone of the knowledge-over-action position)

5. **Prārabdha, sañcita, āgāmi — the three karmas** — Classical Vedānta distinguishes three categories: prārabdha (karma that has begun to bear fruit — the momentum sustaining the present life, which must exhaust itself even for the liberated), sañcita (accumulated karma from past lives, destroyed by knowledge), and āgāmi (future karma that would be generated from present actions, precluded by the knowledge of non-agency). This taxonomy, while not fully explicit in the corpus passages, is implicit in the distinction the Vedānta-sūtras draw between "works that have begun to produce their effects" and those that "have not yet begun."

## Common misuses

In modern Western popular culture, "karma" is often reduced to a simplistic tit-for-tat mechanism: "what goes around comes around" in a single lifetime. This flattens the doctrine in three ways: (1) it collapses the multi-lifetime framework into a single life, making karma a form of immediate cosmic payback rather than a law operating across rebirths; (2) it moralizes karma as reward/punishment, ignoring the doctrine's impersonality — karma is causation, not retribution; (3) it treats karma as something one "has" or "accumulates" like a score, whereas in the classical framework karma is the very fabric of embodied existence, not an optional add-on. Another misuse, common in Western appropriations of "karma yoga," treats "selfless action" as a therapeutic technique for reducing stress, detached from the metaphysical framework of saṃsāra, rebirth, and liberation that gives the doctrine its urgency.

## In which Ideas does this Term appear

- [[../Causality]] — Karma reframes causality as moral and trans-lifetime, extending the Western concept of cause-and-effect beyond the material and the immediate.
- [[../Justice]] — Karma is cosmic justice without a judge: an impersonal law that ensures every action bears its appropriate fruit, challenging the Western framework of justice as externally administered.
- [[../Duty]] — Karma-yoga reframes duty not as obligation to an external authority but as a path of inner liberation performed without attachment.
- [[../Soul]] — The karma doctrine requires a transmigrating self to carry karmic residues across lives, though Buddhism's anātman doctrine challenges this assumption.

## Adjacent Terms

- [[Dharma]] — The duties whose performance (or neglect) generates karma; dharma prescribes right action, karma tracks its consequences.
- [[Atman]] — The self that transmigrates, carrying the residues of karma; the central question of Vedānta is how the unchanging ātman can be affected by karma at all.
- [[Moksha]] — Liberation from saṃsāra, achieved when all karma (except prārabdha) is destroyed by knowledge.
- [[Anatman]] — The Buddhist doctrine of no-self; if there is no self, what carries karma across lives? This is the central philosophical problem Buddhist karma doctrine must solve.
- [[Samsara]] — The cycle of birth, death, and rebirth driven by karma; the two terms are inseparable.
