# Term: Mokṣa

## At a glance

Mokṣa (मोक्ष, from √muc "to release") is the Hindu term for final liberation — release from the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth (saṃsāra), the exhaustion of karma, and the realization of the true nature of the self (ātman) as identical with or related to Brahman. It is the fourth and ultimate puruṣārtha (aim of human life), beyond dharma (duty), artha (wealth), and kāma (pleasure). The Vedānta-sūtras treat mokṣa as the goal of the entire philosophical enterprise: "Brahman has to be enquired into because it is the cause of absolute beatitude." Knowledge (jñāna) alone liberates — not action, not ritual, not devotion by itself. The Bhagavad Gītā adds the path of selfless action (karma-yoga) and devotion (bhakti-yoga) as means to the same end. The corpus texts (Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, Thibaut 1890; Bhagavad Gītā, Arnold 1885) use "Release," "emancipation," and "final emancipation."

## Polysemy

1. **Mokṣa as knowledge-liberation** — The Vedānta-sūtras' central claim: mokṣa is achieved through knowledge (jñāna) of Brahman, not through action (karma). Release is not something produced or attained — it is the removal of ignorance (avidyā), the clearing away of the false superimposition that makes the self appear separate from Brahman. The "released" simply realize what was always true.

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890 (Sacred Books of the East)* — "Nor, again, can it be said that there is a dependence on action in consequence of (Brahman or Release) being something which is to be obtained; for as Brahman constitutes a person's Self it is not something to be attained by that person. And even if Brahman were altogether different from a person's Self still it would not be something to be obtained; for as it is omnipresent it is part of its nature that it is ever present to every one." (equivalent match — "Release" renders mokṣa; the argument that mokṣa is not produced but realized)

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890* — "Those, on the other hand, who consider Release to be something to be effected properly maintain that it depends on the action of mind, speech, or body. So, likewise, those who consider it to be a mere modification. Non-eternality of Release is the certain consequence of these two opinions." (equivalent match — mokṣa must be eternal, therefore it cannot be an effect of action)

2. **Mokṣa as the removal of ignorance** — The causal chain: wrong knowledge (mithyā-jñāna) → faults (doṣa) → activity (pravṛtti) → birth → pain. Mokṣa is the reversal: knowledge removes wrong knowledge, which removes faults, which removes the motivation for activity, which ends birth, which ends pain. This is the Nyāya schema, endorsed and adapted by the Vedānta.

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890* — "Final release results from the successive removal of wrong knowledge, faults, activity, birth, pain, the removal of each later member of the series depending on the removal of the preceding member; and wrong knowledge itself is removed by the knowledge of one's Self being one with the Self of Brahman." (equivalent match — the causal chain of bondage and its reversal through knowledge)

3. **Mokṣa as the goal of the Gītā** — The Bhagavad Gītā expands the paths to mokṣa beyond knowledge alone: selfless action (karma-yoga), devotion (bhakti), and knowledge (jñāna) are all valid routes. The liberated person acts without attachment, "enlightened and emancipate," free from the binding power of works.

   > *Vyāsa (attrib.), Bhagavad Gītā, trans. Edwin Arnold, 1885* — "He that, being self-contained, hath vanquished doubt, / Disparting self from service, soul from works, / Enlightened and emancipate, my Prince! / Works fetter him no more!" (equivalent match — "emancipate" renders mokṣa; the liberated person as the Gītā's ideal)

## Common misuses

In modern English, "liberation" in spiritual contexts is often used loosely for any feeling of freedom, release from stress, or psychological breakthrough. This conflates mokṣa with therapeutic outcomes. Mokṣa is not a feeling but an ontological change: the irreversible dissolution of the karmic chain and the cessation of rebirth. A person who "feels liberated" after a retreat is not (in the classical sense) a jīvanmukta. Another misuse equates mokṣa with the Buddhist nirvāṇa. The two share structural features (both are releases from saṃsāra), but mokṣa requires a self (ātman) to be liberated and identified with Brahman, while nirvāṇa is the blowing out of the illusion of self altogether. The two doctrines are mutually incompatible at the metaphysical level.
