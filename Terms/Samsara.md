# Term: Samsara

## At a glance

Samsara (saṃsāra, संसार, from √sṛ "to flow") is the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth that constitutes conditioned existence in Indian traditions. It is the central problem that Hindu, Buddhist, and Jain soteriologies address — release (moksha, nirvana) is defined as escape from samsara. The term carries distinct emphases across these traditions: in Vedāntic Hinduism it is the realm of transmigration governed by karma and ultimately ending for each soul; in Buddhism it is the suffering-laden round of rebirth driven by ignorance and craving; in Jainism it is the bondage of the soul by karmic matter. The Western Ideas of Fate, Immortality, Time, and World each touch part of samsara, but none captures its dual character as both a cosmic process and a personal predicament.

## Polysemy

1. **Vedāntic cycle of rebirth that can end for souls** — In the Vedānta-sūtras, samsara is the state of being subject to transmigration, governed by the Lord (Īśvara) and pradhāna (primordial matter). Souls gradually obtain release, so samsara will eventually cease for all. This meaning emphasizes the metaphysical structure of the cycle and its eventual termination.

   > *Vedānta-sūtras, Adhyāya 2, Pāda 2, Sūtra 42 (with Śaṅkara's commentary). Trans. George Thibaut, 1890.* — "From among this limited number of souls some obtain release from the saṃsāra, that means their saṃsāra comes to an end, and their subjection to the samsara comes to an end. Gradually all souls obtain release, and so there will finally be an end of the entire saṃsāra and the saṃsāra state of all souls."

2. **Buddhist round of suffering (dukkha)** — In Buddhism, samsara is not merely rebirth but the unsatisfactory, conditioned nature of all existence, characterized by impermanence (anicca) and no-self (anattā). The term is often paired with dukkha to emphasize that the cycle is inherently painful. *Note: The corpus does not contain a direct transliteration match for this usage; the Pali canon in the corpus uses English translations such as "round of rebirths" or "transmigration." This meaning is attested in the Dhammapada and other suttas but is not directly quotable from the supplied passages.*

3. **Jain bondage of the soul** — In Jainism, samsara is the state of the soul entangled with karmic matter, undergoing rebirth in various realms (hells, heavens, human, animal). Release requires ascetic practice to shed karma. *No direct corpus passage available; the Jain texts are not yet in the corpus.*

## Common misuses

In modern Western spiritual discourse, "samsara" is sometimes used as a neutral or even positive term for the "cycle of life" — a natural rhythm of birth and death without the connotation of suffering or bondage. This flattens the classical Indian understanding, where samsara is always a problem to be escaped, not a cycle to be celebrated. The Buddhist emphasis on dukkha (suffering) as intrinsic to samsara is especially lost in this drift.

## Status notes

The corpus is thin for this term. Only one direct match (Vedānta-sūtras) was retrieved. No equivalent matches were found for the Buddhist or Jain usages, likely because pre-1929 translators (e.g., Max Müller for the Dhammapada, T.W. Rhys Davids for the Pali suttas) rendered saṃsāra as "transmigration" or "round of rebirths" rather than transliterating. A future expansion should add the Dhammapada (e.g., verses 153–154 on the breaking of the house-builder) and the Sutta Nipāta or Majjhima Nikāya passages that explicitly use the term. The Jain usage is entirely absent from the corpus and would require separate sourcing. The polysemy section above lists the Buddhist and Jain meanings for completeness, but they are not supported by direct corpus quotations in this version.
