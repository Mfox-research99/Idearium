# Term: Nafs

## At a glance

Nafs (نفس) is the Arabic term for the self or soul in Islamic thought, particularly central to Sufi psychology where it designates the ego — the lower self that must be disciplined, purified, and ultimately transcended on the path to God. Classical Sufism distinguishes seven stages of the nafs (from the commanding self to the pure self), each representing a level of spiritual development. The term is the direct counterpart to fanā' (annihilation): what is annihilated in fanā' is precisely the nafs — not the soul as such but the ego's claim to independent agency. The Western Ideas of Self and Soul each catch part; neither catches the term's role as the primary obstacle to spiritual realization. The Sufi corpus is severely underpopulated. This page is a scaffold.

## Polysemy

1. **Nafs al-ammāra — the commanding self** — The lowest stage: the self that "commands to evil" (Qur'an 12:53), driven by appetite, passion, and ego. This is the nafs in its untrained, unrefined state — the source of all that separates the soul from God.

2. **Nafs al-lawwāma — the self-accusing self** — The self that has begun to recognize its faults and reproaches itself. This is the awakening of conscience, the internalization of moral standards, and the beginning of the spiritual path.

3. **Nafs al-mulhama — the inspired self** — The self that receives inspiration (ilhām) and begins to discern right from wrong intuitively rather than through external rules alone.

4. **Nafs al-muṭma'inna — the serene self** — The self at peace, addressed in Qur'an 89:27: "O serene soul, return to your Lord, well-pleased and well-pleasing." This is the self that has been purified and rests in God.

5. **Nafs al-rāḍiya / al-marḍiyya — the pleased and pleasing self** — The highest stages: the self that is content with God and with which God is content. This is the nafs transformed — not annihilated but perfected, what remains after fanā' and baqā'.
