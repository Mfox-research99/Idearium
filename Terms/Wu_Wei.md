# Term: Wu-Wei

## At a glance

Wu-wei (無為, wú wéi, lit. "without action") is the central Daoist teaching on the proper mode of human activity — action that does not force, governance that does not coerce, doing that accords with the spontaneous unfolding (ziran) of the Tao. It is emphatically not passivity or inertia; the sage "does nothing, and nothing is left undone." The term appears throughout the Dao De Jing in three registers: the Tao's own mode of operation (producing all things without claiming ownership), the sage's inner practice (stillness, desirelessness, simplicity), and the art of governance (ruling without legislation). Legge translates wu-wei inconsistently — as "non-action," "inaction," "abstinence from action," "freedom from action and purpose," and (for the sage) "doing nothing." All passages are equivalent matches.

## Polysemy

1. **Wu-wei as governance** — The most politically radical register: the sage-ruler governs by not governing. Prohibitions, laws, and coercive measures are self-defeating; they stimulate the disorder they aim to suppress. The ruler who practices wu-wei empties the people's minds of desire and ambition, and social order arises spontaneously.

   > *Laozi, Dao De Jing, trans. James Legge, 1891 (Project Gutenberg #216, Ch. 57)* — "Therefore a sage has said, 'I will do nothing (of purpose), and the people will be transformed of themselves; I will be fond of keeping still, and the people will of themselves become correct. I will take no trouble about it, and the people will of themselves become rich; I will manifest no ambition, and the people will of themselves attain to the primitive simplicity.'" (equivalent match — wu-wei as the governing principle that produces spontaneous social order)

   > *Laozi, Dao De Jing, trans. James Legge, 1891 (Ch. 3)* — "When there is this abstinence from action, good order is universal." (equivalent match — Legge's "abstinence from action" renders wu-wei; the consequence of non-interference is universal order)

2. **Wu-wei as the sage's inner practice** — Wu-wei is not merely a policy but a spiritual discipline: the quieting of desire, the emptying of ambition, the return to simplicity. The sage "desires what other men do not desire" and "learns what other men do not learn" — desirelessness and unlearning as the inner conditions of wu-wei.

   > *Laozi, Dao De Jing, trans. James Legge, 1891 (Ch. 64)* — "The sage does not act (so), and therefore does no harm; he does not lay hold (so), and therefore does not lose his hold. ... Therefore the sage desires what (other men) do not desire, and does not prize things difficult to get; he learns what (other men) do not learn, and turns back to what the multitude of men have passed by. Thus he helps the natural development of all things, and does not dare to act (with an ulterior purpose of his own)." (equivalent match — wu-wei as the convergence of desirelessness and non-interference; the sage helps the natural development of things without imposing his own will)

3. **Wu-wei as the Tao's own mode** — The Tao itself operates by wu-wei: it produces all things without claiming possession, completes them without asserting control. The sage's wu-wei is an imitation of the Tao's own manner of action. This is wu-wei as the cosmic principle, not merely a human technique.

   > *Laozi, Dao De Jing, trans. James Legge, 1891 (Ch. 51)* — "It produces them and makes no claim to the possession of them; it carries them through their processes and does not vaunt its ability in doing so; it brings them to maturity and exercises no control over them;—this is called its mysterious operation." (equivalent match — the Tao's wu-wei as the model for the sage; producing without possessing, acting without asserting)

4. **Wu-wei and the reversal principle** — Wu-wei is not weakness but the strategic deployment of weakness to overcome strength. "The movement of the Tao by contraries proceeds; and weakness marks the course of Tao's mighty deeds." The practitioner of wu-wei achieves by not contending, conquers by yielding, and is preserved by not striving for self-preservation.

   > *Laozi, Dao De Jing, trans. James Legge, 1891 (Ch. 40)* — "The movement of the Tao / By contraries proceeds; / And weakness marks the course / Of Tao's mighty deeds." (equivalent match — wu-wei as the practical expression of the Tao's dynamic of reversal where weakness overcomes strength)

## Common misuses

In modern English, "wu-wei" is frequently reduced to "going with the flow" or "being laid back" — a passive, anything-goes attitude that is the opposite of the Daoist teaching. Wu-wei requires intense discipline: the quieting of desire, the stilling of ambition, the unlearning of socially conditioned drives. It is hard work to do nothing — harder, the Dao De Jing implies, than doing something. Another common misuse conflates wu-wei with the Bhagavad Gita's niṣkāma karma (action without attachment to fruits). The family resemblance is real (both counsel non-attachment to outcomes), but the metaphysical frameworks are incompatible: the Gita's karma-yoga operates within a theistic framework of duty and devotion; the Dao De Jing's wu-wei operates within a non-theistic cosmology where the Tao "might appear to have been before God."

## Zhuangzi (Chuang Tzŭ) — trans. Herbert Giles, 1889

> *Chapter on inaction and action* — "In inaction there is action. Therefore the true Sage looks up to God, but does not offer to aid. He perfects his virtue, but does not involve himself... He undertakes affairs without declining them. He metes out law without confusion. He relies on his fellow-men and does not make light of them. He accommodates himself to matter and does not ignore it. Thus the action of the Sage is after all inaction. While there should be no action, there should be also no inaction."
>
> *Gloss: The mature Zhuangzian formulation of wu-wei — more dialectical than the Dao De Jing. "In inaction there is action" is the core paradox, but the second move is equally important: "while there should be no action, there should be also no inaction." Wu-wei must not itself become a fixed rule. The sage who refuses to act out of attachment to non-action has merely traded one entanglement for another. The activity of the sage — undertaking affairs, meting law, relying on people — is continuous with worldly life; what distinguishes it is the absence of "entanglement" (Giles's term).*

> *Chapter on the TAO of God and the TAO of man* — "What then is TAO? There is the TAO of God, and the TAO of man. Inaction and compliance make the TAO of God: action and entanglement the TAO of man. The TAO of God is fundamental: the TAO of man is accidental. The distance which separates them is great. Let us all take heed thereto!"
>
> *Gloss: Wu-wei elevated to a cosmological principle. The Tao of God operates by "inaction and compliance" (wu-wei); the Tao of man operates by "action and entanglement" (you-wei). The distance between the two is not absolute — humans can return to the fundamental — but the framing makes clear that wu-wei is not a technique among techniques but a return to the Tao's own mode of operation.*

> *Giles's summary of the Zhuangzian sage* — "The divine man ignores action... the sage knows nothing of the distinction between subjective and objective. It exists only ex analogiâ hominis. 'From the standpoint of Tao' all things are one. People 'guided by the criteria of their own mind,' see only the contradiction, the manifoldness, the difference; the sage sees the many disappearing in the One."
>
> *Gloss: Wu-wei is rooted in a transformed epistemology. The reason the sage can act without entanglement is that he no longer perceives the world as divided into self and other, subject and object. Action that proceeds from the perception of separation is necessarily entangled; action that proceeds from the experience of unity is necessarily wu-wei. This is the deeper claim behind the paradox: the problem is not action itself but the dualistic consciousness that makes action into grasping.*

> *Giles, introducing Chuang Tzŭ's rejection of the world* — "For the sage, then, life means death to all that men think life, the life of seeming or reputation, of doing or action, of being or individual selfhood. This leads on to the 'budget of paradoxes'... As in the moral and active region we escape from the world and self, and are able to reverse and look down upon the world's judgments, so in the speculative region we get behind and beyond the contradictions of ordinary thinking."
>
> *Gloss: The three renunciations — reputation, action, selfhood — are the conditions of wu-wei. The sage does not retreat from the world but "escapes from the world and self" — that is, escapes the identification with world and self that makes action into entanglement. The result is the capacity to "look down upon the world's judgments" — detachment as clear sight, not as indifference.*

The Zhuangzi's treatment of wu-wei is more dialectical and paradoxical than the Dao De Jing's, making it an essential complement to the existing Dao De Jing passages. The Zhuangzi also develops wu-wei through concrete craft-stories (Cook Ding butchering an ox without his knife ever dulling, the swimmer at the waterfall, the wheelwright who cannot teach his son) — these are Zhuangzi's most distinctive contribution to the concept and have not been extracted in this pass. The Giles text is a selection with extensive commentary interspersed; some passages attributed to Zhuangzi may be Giles's interpretive paraphrase. The Confucian critique of wu-wei (Xunzi, Mencius) is not represented in the existing corpus.
