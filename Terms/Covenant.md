# Term: Covenant

## At a glance

Covenant (from Latin *convenire*, "to come together") is the central operative concept in Hobbes's political philosophy and a load-bearing term in Western theological traditions (Hebrew *berith*, Latin *foedus*). In the Great Books corpus, it carries at least three distinct senses — the Hobbesian social contract that constitutes sovereignty, the theological covenant between God and a people, and the legal concept of a conditional promise binding parties. The Western Ideas of Justice, Law, Government, and Religion each catch part of it; none catches the whole. This Term page focuses on the Hobbesian political sense as the dominant corpus usage, with theological and legal meanings noted as adjacent.

## Polysemy

1. **The Hobbesian social covenant** — The foundational agreement by which a multitude of individuals, in a state of nature, mutually transfer their natural right to a sovereign in exchange for security and civil order. This covenant is *between subjects, not between subject and sovereign*; the sovereign is not a party to it and therefore cannot breach it. This is the load-bearing sense in the corpus, and it grounds Hobbes's entire theory of justice, obligation, and commonwealth.

   > Thomas Hobbes. *Leviathan*, Chapter 14. Trans. Hobbes, 1651. — "And in this law of Nature, consisteth the Fountain and Originall of JUSTICE. For where no Covenant hath preceded, there hath no Right been transferred, and every man has right to every thing; and consequently, no action can be Unjust. But when a Covenant is made, then to break it is Unjust: And the definition of INJUSTICE, is no other than The Not Performance Of Covenant."

   > Thomas Hobbes. *Leviathan*, Chapter 18. Trans. Hobbes, 1651. — "Because the Right of bearing the Person of them all, is given to him they make Soveraigne, by Covenant onely of one to another, and not of him to any of them; there can happen no breach of Covenant on the part of the Soveraigne; and consequently none of his Subjects, by any pretence of forfeiture, can be freed from his Subjection."

2. **Covenant as conditional promise under fear** — Hobbes's distinctive claim that covenants made under fear (including fear of death or captivity) are valid and binding. This distinguishes his theory from those that require free, uncoerced consent. The only invalidating fear is a *new* fear arising after the covenant is made, not the fear that motivated it.

   > Thomas Hobbes. *Leviathan*, Chapter 14. Trans. Hobbes, 1651. — "And even in Common-wealths, if I be forced to redeem my selfe from a Theefe by promising him mony, I am bound to pay it, till the Civill Law discharge me. For whatsoever I may lawfully do without Obligation, the same I may lawfully Covenant to do through feare: and what I lawfully Covenant, I cannot lawfully break."

3. **Covenant as authorization** — The act by which the multitude authorizes the sovereign's actions as their own. When an actor (representative) makes a covenant by authority, the author (the one who granted the authority) is bound as if they had made the covenant themselves. This is the mechanism by which the sovereign's acts become the acts of every subject.

   > Thomas Hobbes. *Leviathan*, Chapter 16. Trans. Hobbes, 1651. — "From hence it followeth, that when the Actor maketh a Covenant by Authority, he bindeth thereby the Author, no lesse than if he had made it himselfe; and no lesse subjecteth him to all the consequences of the same."

## Common misuses

- **Covenant as a contract between ruler and ruled.** Hobbes explicitly denies this. The sovereign is not a party to the covenant; the covenant is among the subjects. The idea that a ruler holds power "on condition" of good performance is, for Hobbes, a dangerous confusion that leads to civil war. "Covenants being but words, and breath, have no force to oblige, contain, constrain, or protect any man, but what it has from the publique Sword" — the sovereign is the enforcer, not the signatory.

- **Covenant as a merely religious or theological term.** While the Hebrew *berith* and Christian *foedus* traditions are historically prior, the dominant corpus usage (Hobbes) is relentlessly political and juridical. Reducing covenant to "a religious promise" misses its function as the constitutive act of the commonwealth.

- **Covenant as synonymous with "promise" or "contract."** Hobbes distinguishes covenant from simple promise: a covenant involves the transfer of right, not merely a commitment to future action. And a covenant in the state of nature (without a common power to enforce it) is void if there is mutual fear of non-performance. This is not true of ordinary promises in civil society.

## In which Ideas does this Term appear

- [[../Justice]] — Hobbes grounds justice entirely in the performance of covenant; without covenant, no action can be unjust.
- [[../Government]] — The social covenant is the act that institutes sovereignty and constitutes the commonwealth.
- [[../Law]] — Civil law derives its binding force from the covenant that established the sovereign as lawgiver.
- [[../Religion]] — The theological covenant tradition (berith/foedus) is an adjacent meaning; Hobbes explicitly rejects the pretense of a direct covenant with God as a political claim.

## Adjacent Terms

- [[../Terms/Logos]] — In Stoic and Johannine traditions, Logos carries a sense of divine ordering principle that parallels the covenantal structure of law; but Logos is cosmic and immanent, while covenant is historical and voluntary.
- [[../Terms/Hesed]] — The Hebrew term for covenantal loyalty/loving-kindness, which grounds the theological covenant tradition that Hobbes's political covenant displaces.

## Status notes

The corpus passages for Covenant are overwhelmingly from Hobbes's *Leviathan*. This is not a weakness — Hobbes is the canonical Western political theorist of covenant, and the term's load-bearing function in his system justifies a Term page. The theological covenant tradition (Hebrew *berith*, Latin *foedus*) is present in the corpus through biblical and patristic sources but was not returned by the direct-match query; a future expansion could add passages from Augustine, Calvin, or the Hebrew prophets. The legal sense of covenant as a conditional promise in contract law is present in Hobbes but not developed as a separate tradition in the corpus. The page is complete for v1 as a Hobbes-focused entry; the theological and legal meanings are noted in the polysemy section and adjacent terms but not independently sourced.
