# Term: Eudaimonia

## At a glance

Eudaimonia (εὐδαιμονία) is Aristotle's term for the highest human good — not a momentary feeling of happiness but the condition of living well and doing well over a complete life. It is "an activity of the soul in accordance with excellence" (Nicomachean Ethics I.7), realized through the exercise of the distinctively human function (ergon): rational activity. Unlike modern "happiness," which suggests a subjective feeling, eudaimonia is objective — a person can be mistaken about whether they are eudaimon, just as they can be mistaken about whether they are healthy. The Nicomachean Ethics (translated by R.W. Browne and W.D. Ross; the corpus has both) is the primary source. The term is rendered by translators as "happiness" or "the Happy Life," producing systematic ambiguity that this page flags.

## Polysemy

1. **Eudaimonia as activity in accordance with excellence** — The core definition: eudaimonia is not a state or possession but an activity (energeia) — specifically, the activity of the rational soul in accordance with the highest excellence (aretē). This is the ergon argument: the good of a thing is found in its function; the human function is rational activity; therefore the human good is rational activity performed excellently.

   > *Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, trans. R.W. Browne / W.D. Ross, Book I* — "Now with those who assert it to be Virtue (Excellence), or some kind of Virtue, our account agrees: for working in the way of Excellence surely belongs to Excellence. And there is perhaps no unimportant difference between conceiving of the Chief Good as in possession or as in use, in other words, as a mere state or as a working. For the state or habit may possibly exist in a subject without effecting any good, as, for instance, in him who is asleep... but the working cannot so, for it will of necessity act, and act well." (equivalent match — "the Chief Good" renders eudaimonia; the crucial distinction between possessing excellence and exercising it)

2. **Eudaimonia as contemplative** — The highest eudaimonia is the activity of the best part of the soul — intellect (nous) — exercised in contemplation (theōria). This is "perfect happiness" because it is the most continuous, most self-sufficient, and most divine activity available to human beings. Practical virtue (the life of moral excellence) is eudaimon in a secondary sense.

   > *Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics / Metaphysics, Book X / Metaphysics I* — "Now if Happiness is a Working in the way of Excellence of course that Excellence must be the highest, that is to say, the Excellence of the best Principle. Whether then this best Principle is Intellect or some other which is thought naturally to rule and to lead and to conceive of noble and divine things... the Working of this in accordance with its own proper Excellence must be the perfect Happiness." (equivalent match — the hierarchy: contemplative excellence as the highest happiness)

3. **Eudaimonia requires a complete life** — One swallow does not make a spring, and one good day does not make eudaimonia. Aristotle's insistence that eudaimonia requires a "complete life" (bios teleios) distinguishes it from a passing mood. It also introduces the problem of posthumous fortune: can Priam be called eudaimon after Troy falls? The answer is complex — virtue is the anchor, but fortune matters at the margins.

4. **Eudaimonia vs. pleasure** — Aristotle repeatedly distinguishes eudaimonia from pleasure (hēdonē). The life of pleasure is "fit for cattle," not for beings capable of reason. Pleasure accompanies eudaimonia but is not identical with it — the eudaimon life is pleasant because it is the realization of human nature, not because it maximizes pleasant experiences.

## Common misuses

"Eudaimonia" is frequently appropriated in modern self-help and positive psychology as "true happiness" or "human flourishing" — an upbeat alternative to the narrower "happiness" of hedonic psychology. This typically strips out the hierarchical dimension: for Aristotle, eudaimonia is not whatever makes you feel fulfilled but the specific activity of the rational soul, culminating in contemplation. A life of service or craft, however satisfying, is only eudaimon in a secondary sense. Another misuse treats eudaimonia as a psychological state rather than an activity — the "eudaimonic" person as someone who feels a sense of purpose. For Aristotle, you do not feel eudaimonia; you *are* it, through the exercise of excellence. The feeling follows the act.

## Adjacent Terms
