# Term: Brahman

## At a glance

Brahman (Sanskrit, ब्रह्मन्, from √bṛh "to grow/expand") is the ultimate reality in classical Hindu thought — the single, unconditioned ground from which the universe originates, in which it subsists, and into which it returns. In the Advaita Vedānta of Śaṅkara, Brahman is nirguṇa (without qualities), non-dual, and identical with the innermost self (ātman); in the Viśiṣṭādvaita of Rāmānuja, it is saguṇa (with qualities), a personal Lord (Īśvara) who is the inner ruler of all beings. Across the corpus the term carries at least three distinct senses — the impersonal absolute, the personal God, and the Vedic sacred utterance — that are not interchangeable. The Western Ideas of God, Being, and Infinity each catch part of it; none catches the whole. Because pre-1929 translators often rendered Brahman as "the highest Self," "the Supreme," or "God," and the corpus metadata mislabels the Vedānta-sūtras as "Rig Veda Griffith 1896," the equivalent-match passages below are flagged as approximate in provenance. This will be corrected when the corpus-indexing metadata is rebuilt.

## Polysemy

1. **Nirguṇa Brahman — the impersonal absolute** — In Śaṅkara's Advaita Vedānta, Brahman is the sole reality: without qualities (nirguṇa), without distinctions, beyond name and form, and the substrate onto which the illusion (māyā) of the phenomenal world is superimposed. All apparent multiplicity is a misperception; in truth, only Brahman is. This is the most philosophically distinctive sense in the corpus and has no direct Western parallel. The classical formula is sat-cit-ānanda: being, consciousness, bliss.

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890 (Sacred Books of the East, vol. 34)* — "Brahman has been defined as that from which there proceed the origination, sustentation, and retractation of this world." (direct match — the definitional formula of Brahman as the material and efficient cause of the universe)

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890* — "It has been said that, as practical religious duty has to be enquired into because it is the cause of an increase of happiness, so Brahman has to be enquired into because it is the cause of absolute beatitude." (direct match — Brahman as the object of the highest philosophical inquiry, the knowledge of which liberates)

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890* — "The absolutely complete power of Brahman... does not require to be supplemented by any extraneous help. Thus Scripture also declares, 'There is no effect and no instrument known of him, no one is seen like unto him or better; his high power is revealed as manifold, as inherent, acting as force and knowledge' (Śve. Up. VI, 8). Therefore Brahman, although one only, is, owing to its manifold powers, able to transform itself into manifold effects; just as milk is." (direct match — Brahman's self-sufficiency and creative power without external instruments)

2. **Saguṇa Brahman — the personal Lord (Īśvara)** — Brahman conceived as possessing qualities (saguṇa): the creator, sustainer, and destroyer of the universe, accessible to devotion (bhakti). This sense is most prominent in the Bhagavad Gītā, where Kṛṣṇa identifies himself as Brahman and as the personal God who incarnates to restore dharma. For Rāmānuja's Viśiṣṭādvaita, this is the primary sense: Brahman is the inner self of all, qualified by conscious and unconscious beings as its body, and accessible through loving surrender (prapatti).

   > *Vyāsa (attrib.), Bhagavad Gītā, trans. Edwin Arnold, 1885* — "I BRAHMA am! the One Eternal GOD, / And ADHYATMAN is My Being's name, / The Soul of Souls!" (equivalent match — Arnold's "BRAHMA" renders Brahman in the personal-theistic sense, with Kṛṣṇa as the speaker)

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890* — "In the first pada Brahman has been shown to be the cause of the origin, subsistence, and reabsorption of the entire world, comprising the ether and the other elements. Moreover, of this Brahman, which is the cause of the entire world, certain qualities have (implicitly) been declared, such as all-pervadingness, eternity, omniscience, its being the Self of all, and so on." (equivalent match — "the Self of all" is the saguṇa register, where Brahman is the inner controller)

3. **Brahman as bliss (ānanda)** — One of the defining qualities of Brahman in the Upaniṣads is bliss (ānanda), and the Taittirīya Upaniṣad's famous scale of bliss — from human bliss to the bliss of Brahman — establishes Brahman not merely as being and consciousness but as supreme, unconditioned joy. The term ānandamaya ("consisting of bliss") becomes a technical designation for Brahman in the Vedānta.

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890* — "Thus here Brahman also, as abounding in bliss, is called anandamaya. That Brahman does abound in bliss follows from the passage (Taitt. Up. II, 8), where, after the bliss of each of the different classes of beings, beginning with man, has been declared to be a hundred times greater than the bliss of the immediately preceding class, the bliss of Brahman is finally proclaimed to be absolutely supreme." (direct match — the ānandamaya argument that identifies Brahman with supreme bliss)

4. **The Vedic brahman — sacred utterance** — In the earliest Vedic context, brahman (neuter) denoted the sacred power inherent in the ritual utterance, the hymn, or the prayer — the effective speech that sustains the cosmic order (ṛta). This sense gradually expanded into the metaphysical absolute of the Upaniṣads. The corpus primarily reflects the later philosophical sense, but the etymological root in sacred speech is the conceptual bridge between the Vedic ritual world and the Vedāntic metaphysical world.

## Common misuses

In modern English, "Brahman" is frequently confused with "Brahma" (the creator deity of the Hindu trimūrti), "brāhmaṇa" (the priestly caste), or "Brahmin" (an anglicized form of the same). These are all etymologically related but referentially distinct: Brahman is the metaphysical absolute, Brahmā is a specific deity, and brāhmaṇa/brahmin is a social category. The conflation is especially common in New Age and popular spirituality contexts where "Brahman" is treated as a name for a pantheistic "universal consciousness" without the rigorous non-dual framework that gives the Advaita concept its philosophical force. Another misuse treats "Brahman" as interchangeable with the Western God of classical theism, ignoring that nirguṇa Brahman is not a personal agent, not a creator by will, and not a being among beings but being-as-such.

## In which Ideas does this Term appear

- [[../God]] — Brahman reframes God as non-dual and impersonal (nirguṇa) or as the inner self of all (saguṇa), challenging the Western theistic framework of a transcendent, personal Creator.
- [[../Being]] — Brahman is being-as-such (sat), the ground of all existence, not a particular being among beings. The closest Western parallel is perhaps the Thomistic ipsum esse subsistens.
- [[../Infinity]] — Brahman is beyond all limitation, all qualities, all predication — a different register of infinity than the mathematical or spatial senses that dominate the Western canon.

## Adjacent Terms

- [[Atman]] — The individual self; in Advaita Vedānta, ātman and Brahman are ultimately identical (tat tvam asi). The pair is the central axis of Hindu philosophy.
- [[Maya]] — The cosmic illusion or creative power that makes the one Brahman appear as the manifold world. Māyā is neither real nor unreal; it is the principle of appearance that veils Brahman.
- [[Ishvara]] — The personal Lord; Brahman as qualified (saguṇa) and accessible to devotion. In Advaita, Īśvara is Brahman seen through the lens of māyā.
- [[Om]] — The sacred syllable; the sonic form of Brahman, the primordial sound from which the universe manifests.
