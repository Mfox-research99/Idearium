# Term: Karuna

## At a glance

Karuna (Pali/Sanskrit, करुणा) is a central ethical and soteriological concept in Buddhism, also present in Hindu and Jain traditions. In Buddhism it denotes the active wish to relieve suffering in all beings, often paired with *maitrī* (loving-kindness) as one of the four *brahmavihāras* (divine abodes). In Hindu contexts it appears as a virtue (compassion) within dharma. The term warrants its own page because its meaning is not exhausted by the English "compassion" — it carries a meditative cultivation, a bodhisattva vow, and a metaphysical dimension of non‑dual responsiveness that Western ideas of pity or mercy do not capture. The pre‑1929 translations in the corpus do not contain the term itself or its typical English equivalents in a retrievable form; the polysemy below is therefore drawn from general scholarly consensus and awaits corpus‑based verification.

## Polysemy

1. **Buddhist brahmavihāra (divine abode)** — In the Pali canon, karuṇā is the second of the four *brahmavihāras* (along with loving‑kindness, sympathetic joy, and equanimity). It is a boundless, impartial compassion for all sentient beings, cultivated through meditation. It partially overlaps the Western Ideas of Agape (selfless love) and Virtue.

   > *No corpus passage available.* — (The pre‑1929 translations in the corpus do not contain the term or its equivalents in this sense.)

2. **Mahāyāna bodhisattva ideal** — In Mahāyāna Buddhism, karuṇā becomes the driving force of the bodhisattva path: the compassionate vow to postpone one’s own nirvāṇa until all beings are liberated. This is a cosmic, salvific compassion that goes beyond personal virtue. It touches the Ideas of Witness (bearing the suffering of others) and Eros (aspirational love).

   > *No corpus passage available.*

3. **Hindu virtue (dayā/karuṇā)** — In the Hindu epics and dharmaśāstra, karuṇā (often synonymous with *dayā*) is a cardinal virtue, part of the conduct expected of a righteous person. It is a quality of the heart that leads to charitable action and non‑violence. This overlaps the Ideas of Justice (as fairness to the vulnerable) and Duty.

## Common misuses

In modern English‑language discourse, "karuna" is sometimes used as a synonym for generic "compassion" without the specific meditative or soteriological framework of the Buddhist tradition. It is also occasionally conflated with pity (a condescending emotion) rather than the active, impartial wish for relief that the term denotes. The bodhisattva‑vow dimension is often ignored in popular mindfulness contexts.

## Adjacent Terms

- [[Maitri]] (Pali *mettā*) — loving‑kindness, the first brahmavihāra; karuna is its compassionate complement.
- [[Bodhisattva]] — the being who embodies karuṇā; the vow is the practical expression of the term.
- [[Dukkha]] — suffering; karuṇā is the response to dukkha.
- [[Nirvana]] — the goal that the bodhisattva postpones out of karuṇā.
- [[Dharma]] — in Hindu contexts, karuṇā is part of dharma; in Buddhist contexts, it is a dharma‑practice.

## Status notes

- **Corpus gap:** The pre‑1929 translations in the corpus (Müller’s Dhammapada, Arnold’s Gita, etc.) do not contain the term *karuṇā* or its typical English equivalents ("compassion," "pity," "mercy") in a way that can be attributed to the concept. The Dhammapada uses "pity" in a few verses, but the context is not clearly the brahmavihāra sense. The Gita uses "pity" for Arjuna’s emotional state, not the cultivated virtue. Therefore no direct or equivalent matches were retrieved.
- **Methodological note:** Equivalent‑match searches were attempted for "compassion" and "pity" filtered to the Buddhist/Hindu tradition, but returned no passages that clearly map to karuṇā as a technical term. This may be because the translators used other words ("mercy," "tenderness") or because the relevant texts (e.g., the *Visuddhimagga*, *Bodhicaryāvatāra*) are not in the corpus.
