# Term: Ḥesed

## At a glance

Ḥesed (חסד) is a central Hebrew Bible term for God's steadfast, covenantal love — a love that is not merely affection but loyalty, mercy, and faithfulness bound by covenant. It appears most prominently in Exodus 34:6-7 (the thirteen attributes of God: "abounding in ḥesed and faithfulness"), the Psalms, and Hosea. It is often paired with 'emet ("faithfulness/truth") and contrasted with mishpat ("judgment/justice") in a productive tension: ḥesed is justice tempered by mercy, obligation fulfilled through love. The Western Ideas of Love, Mercy, and Justice each catch part of it; none catches its specifically covenantal character — ḥesed is not a feeling but a binding relationship. **The Hebrew Bible is not in the current corpus.** This page is a scaffold only.

## Polysemy

1. **Ḥesed as divine covenantal love** — God's ḥesed is His unbreakable commitment to the covenant with Israel, independent of Israel's worthiness. It is freely given and irrevocable — "the ḥesed of the Lord is from everlasting to everlasting" (Psalm 103:17). This is the foundational sense, from which the human obligations derive.

2. **Ḥesed as human obligation** — The covenant is reciprocal: as God shows ḥesed to Israel, Israel is commanded to show ḥesed to God (through obedience) and to one another (through acts of kindness, charity, and justice). The Book of Ruth is the paradigmatic narrative of human ḥesed — Ruth's loyalty to Naomi beyond any legal obligation.

3. **Ḥesed and mishpat (justice)** — The prophetic literature (especially Hosea, Micah 6:8) holds ḥesed and mishpat in tension: "What does the Lord require of you but to do justice, and to love ḥesed, and to walk humbly with your God?" Ḥesed is not the suspension of justice but its completion — justice fulfilled through mercy.

4. **Ḥesed in rabbinic tradition: gemilut ḥasadim** — The rabbis develop ḥesed into the category of gemilut ḥasadim ("acts of loving-kindness") — one of the three pillars on which the world stands (along with Torah and worship). This extends ḥesed beyond the covenantal framework into a universal ethical obligation.
