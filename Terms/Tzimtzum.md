# Term: Ṣimṣum

## At a glance

Ṣimṣum (צמצום, "contraction") is the central concept of Lurianic Kabbalah (Isaac Luria, 16th century): God's act of self-limitation or withdrawal by which the infinite (Ein Sof) created a "space" — a metaphysical void — within which a finite world could come into being. Before creation, the divine light filled all existence, leaving no room for anything other than God. For creation to occur, God had to contract, to withdraw into Himself, to make an empty space (ḥalal ha-panui) in which the world could exist. This is the original meaning, but the term has been widely adopted in modern Jewish thought (Rosenzweig, Levinas) and contemporary philosophy as a model for creation-through-self-limitation — God's power expressed not through expansion but through withdrawal. **The Hebrew Kabbalistic corpus is not in the current library.** This page is a scaffold.

## Polysemy

1. **Ṣimṣum as cosmogonic contraction** — Luria's answer to the question: how can a finite world exist if God is infinite and all-encompassing? The answer: God contracts. The first act of creation is not emanation outward but withdrawal inward. This reverses the standard theological logic: creation begins with absence, not presence; with concealment, not revelation.

2. **Ṣimṣum as the vessel-breaking (shevirat ha-kelim)** — In Luria's myth, the divine light that entered the empty space was too intense for the vessels (kelim) created to contain it. The vessels shattered, scattering sparks of divine light throughout creation. The task of humanity (tiqqun) is to gather these sparks and restore the broken world. Ṣimṣum is thus the first act of a cosmic drama that continues through history.

3. **Ṣimṣum in modern thought** — The concept has been widely adopted outside Kabbalah: in Rosenzweig's theology of creation, in Levinas's ethics (the self's contraction to make space for the Other), and in contemporary philosophy of religion. It has become a model for understanding creation, ethics, and even parenthood as acts of self-limitation rather than self-assertion.
