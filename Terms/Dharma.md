# Term: Dharma

## At a glance

Dharma (Sanskrit, धर्म, from √dhṛ "to hold/sustain") is the central operative concept of classical Hindu thought and a load-bearing term in Buddhism and Jainism with shifted meanings. Across the corpus it carries at least four distinct senses — cosmic order, individual duty, religious law, and the teachings of the Buddha — that are not interchangeable. The Western Ideas of Justice, Duty, Religion, and Truth each catch part of it; none catches the whole. This page draws on the Vedānta-sūtras (with Śaṅkara's commentary), the Bhagavad Gītā, and Jain doxography as represented in the pre-1929 English translations available in the corpus. Because many of these translations render dharma as "duty," "law," or "righteousness" rather than transliterating it, the equivalent-match passages below are flagged as the translator's English word, not the original term. This is a methodological necessity given the corpus's translation conventions, not a claim about the term's meaning.

## Polysemy

1. **Cosmic order / universal law** — The foundational sense in the Ṛg Veda (where *ṛta* is the term) and later absorbed into dharma: the principle that sustains the universe, keeps the stars in their courses, and governs the moral structure of reality. This meaning partially overlaps the Western Idea of Nature (as normative order) and Justice (as cosmic balance). The corpus does not contain a direct passage where dharma is explicitly glossed as cosmic order, but the equivalent-match material from the Vedānta-sūtras describes Brahman as the "support" (vidhṛti) that prevents the worlds from being confounded — a function later identified with dharma.

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890 (Sacred Books of the East, vol. 34)* — "That Self is a bank, a limitary support (vidhṛti), that these worlds may not be confounded." (E18, equivalent match — translator's word "support" for the sustaining function later called dharma)

2. **Individual duty (svadharma)** — The specific moral and social obligations of a person according to their caste, stage of life, and circumstances. This is the sense most prominent in the Bhagavad Gītā, where Kṛṣṇa repeatedly urges Arjuna to perform his svadharma as a warrior without attachment to results. Overlaps the Western Idea of Duty.

   > *Vyāsa (attrib.), Bhagavad Gītā, trans. Edwin Arnold, 1885* — "Let right deeds be / Thy motive, not the fruit which comes from them. / And live in action! Labour! Make thine acts / Thy piety, casting all self aside, / Contemning gain and merit; equable / In good or evil: equability / Is Yog, is piety!" (E14, equivalent match — Arnold's "right deeds" and "piety" render dharma)

   > *Vyāsa (attrib.), Bhagavad Gītā, trans. Edwin Arnold, 1885* — "When Righteousness / Declines, O Bharata! when Wickedness / Is strong, I rise, from age to age, and take / Visible shape, and move a man with men, / Succouring the good, thrusting the evil back, / And setting Virtue on her seat again." (E13, equivalent match — Arnold's "Righteousness" renders dharma in the sense of cosmic moral order, here embodied by Kṛṣṇa's avatāra)

3. **Religious duty / ritual law** — The body of prescribed actions (sacrifices, observances, caste rules) taught by the Veda and elaborated in the Dharmaśāstras. This is the sense operative in the Pūrva Mīmāṃsā and in the Vedānta-sūtras' contrast between dharma (action-based duty) and brahman (knowledge-based liberation). Overlaps the Western Idea of Religion (as law) and Law (as divine command).

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890* — "The enquiry into those actions, whether of body, speech, or mind, which are known from Śruti and Smṛti, and are comprised under the name 'religious duty' (dharma), is carried on in the Jaimini Sūtra, which begins with the words 'then therefore the enquiry into duty'; the opposite of duty also (adharma), such as doing harm, &c., forms an object of enquiry to the end that it may be avoided." (D7, direct match — the text explicitly glosses dharma as "religious duty" and adharma as its opposite)

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890* — "The fruit of the knowledge of Brahman must be of a different nature from the fruit of the knowledge of duty which depends on the performance of actions." (E1, equivalent match — "duty" renders dharma in the Mīmāṃsā sense)

4. **Merit / dharma as a category in Jainism** — In Jain ontology, dharma (along with adharma) is one of the five *astikāyas* (existing bodies/categories), denoting the principle of motion that makes movement possible. This is a technical metaphysical sense with no direct Western parallel.

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890* — "They teach that there are five so-called astikāyas ('existing bodies,' i.e. categories), viz. the categories of soul (jīva), body (pudgala), merit (dharma), demerit (adharma), and space (ākāśa)." (D5, direct match — the text explicitly translates dharma as "merit" in the Jain list of categories)

5. **Dharma as the Buddha's teaching (Buddha-dharma)** — In Buddhist usage, dharma (Pali: dhamma) shifts to mean the doctrine or truth proclaimed by the Buddha, the universal law of cause and effect, and the phenomena that constitute reality. This meaning is not represented in the current corpus (which lacks Buddhist primary sources in pre-1929 English translation), but it is a major sense that would need to be added when the corpus expands.

## Common misuses

In modern English-language spirituality, "dharma" is often reduced to "one's purpose" or "life path" — a personalized calling detached from caste, stage of life, and the cosmic order that anchored the classical term. This flattening loses the binding, obligatory character of dharma: it is not something one chooses but something one is born into and must uphold. The popular phrase "find your dharma" would be unintelligible to a classical Hindu thinker, for whom dharma is given, not discovered. Another misuse treats "dharma" as a synonym for "religion" in the modern Western sense of a voluntary belief system, whereas classical dharma is primarily about action and social obligation, not creed.

## In which Ideas does this Term appear

- [[../Duty]] — Dharma as svadharma and ritual duty reframes the Western concept of duty as context-dependent and cosmic, not merely moral.
- [[../Justice]] — Dharma as cosmic order and righteousness reframes justice as a sustaining principle of the universe, not merely a human institution.
- [[../Religion]] — Dharma as religious law and teaching reframes religion as orthopraxy (right action) rather than orthodoxy (right belief).
- [[../Truth]] — Dharma as the Buddha's teaching (Buddha-dharma) reframes truth as a path and a law, not merely a proposition.

## Adjacent Terms

- [[Karma]] — Action and its moral residue; dharma prescribes right action, karma tracks its consequences across lives. The two are inseparable in Hindu ethics.
- [[Brahman]] — The ultimate reality that dharma sustains and reflects; dharma is the phenomenal expression of Brahman's order.
- [[Moksha]] — Liberation from the cycle of rebirth; dharma is the path, moksha is the goal, and the two stand in tension (dharma binds one to the world; moksha releases one from it).
- [[Anatman]] — The Buddhist doctrine of no-self; dharma in Buddhism refers to the impersonal law of cause and effect that replaces the Hindu ātman as the locus of continuity.

## Status notes

The corpus is heavily weighted toward Vedānta and the Bhagavad Gītā, with no direct Buddhist or Jain sūtra texts. The Jain meaning (dharma as a category of motion) appears only in a doxographical summary within Śaṅkara's refutation. The Buddhist sense (Buddha-dharma) is entirely absent from the current corpus and should be added when Buddhist primary sources (e.g., Dhammapada, Milinda Pañha, or Mahāyāna sūtras in pre-1929 translation) become available. The cosmic-order sense is attested only through equivalent matches and commentary; a direct Ṛg Vedic passage using dharma in that sense would strengthen the page. The equivalent-match methodology is necessary here because the pre-1929 translators consistently rendered dharma as "duty" or "righteousness" rather than transliterating it; this is flagged in each citation.
