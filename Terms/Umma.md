# Term: Umma

## At a glance

Umma (أمة, plural umam) is the central Islamic concept of the religious community, carrying a polysemy that spans the universal body of Muslim believers, particular prophetic communities in salvation history, and (in modern usage) the political nation. It does not reduce cleanly to any single Western Idea: "Community" (not an Adler Idea) catches the social dimension, "Citizen" catches the legal membership, and "Church" catches the religious body, but umma is simultaneously theological, political, and eschatological in ways none of those terms capture. The corpus currently contains no pre-1929 English translations that use the term "umma" directly; the following analysis is therefore drawn from general knowledge of the Islamic tradition and will require supplementation when appropriate primary-source material becomes available.

## Polysemy

1. **The universal community of believers (al-umma al-islāmiyya)** — The trans-ethnic, trans-national body of all Muslims, bound by shared confession (shahāda) and law (sharīʿa), not by blood or territory. This is the most frequent classical meaning, rooted in the Qur’an and ḥadīth. Overlaps partially with the Idea of "Citizen" (membership in a polity) and "God" (the community as the object of divine covenant).

2. **A particular prophetic community (umma min al-umam)** — In Qur’anic salvation history, each prophet was sent to his own umma (e.g., the umma of Moses, the umma of Jesus, the umma of Muhammad). These are discrete communities defined by their response to revelation, and they will be judged separately. This meaning overlaps with the Idea of "History" (sacred history of peoples) and "Good_and_Evil" (communities as moral agents).

3. **The political nation (modern usage)** — From the late 19th century onward, "umma" was increasingly used to mean the Arab nation or the Islamic nation-state, partly under the influence of European nationalism. This is a semantic extension that classical usage would not have recognized. Overlaps with the Idea of "Government" and "Revolution" (pan-Islamic movements).

## Common misuses

In modern political discourse, "umma" is often treated as a synonym for "the Muslim world" or "the global Islamic population" in a purely demographic sense, losing the theological dimension of covenantal community. It is also sometimes conflated with "nation-state" (e.g., "the Egyptian umma"), which classical jurists would have rejected as a category error — the umma is one, not divisible by borders. A further misuse occurs in Western media, where "umma" is occasionally used as a vague term for "the enemy community" in security contexts, stripped of its internal legal and ethical content.

## In which Ideas does this Term appear

- [[../Citizen]] — Umma reframes citizenship as religious membership rather than territorial belonging.
- [[../Government]] — Umma challenges the Western separation of church and state; the community is itself a polity.
- [[../God]] — The umma is constituted by its relation to God; divine sovereignty defines its boundaries.
- [[../History]] — The succession of prophetic umam is a framework for sacred history.
- [[../Law]] — Sharīʿa is the law of the umma, not of a state; the community is the subject of law.

## Adjacent Terms

- [[Tawhid]] — The oneness of God is the theological foundation for the unity of the umma; a divided umma contradicts tawhid.
- [[Nafs]] — The individual self (nafs) is disciplined within the umma; the tension between personal and communal obligation is a recurring theme.
- [[Fana]] — In Sufi thought, annihilation of the self can lead to absorption in the community of the Beloved, though fana is primarily individual.
