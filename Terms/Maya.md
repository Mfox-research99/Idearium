# Term: Māyā

## At a glance

Māyā (माया) is the Advaita Vedānta term for the principle of cosmic illusion — the inexplicable power (śakti) through which the one, undifferentiated Brahman appears as the manifold world of names and forms. Māyā is neither real (sat) nor unreal (asat); it is anirvacanīya ("indescribable"), a principle of appearance that veils Brahman while simultaneously projecting the phenomenal world, as a magician creates an illusion that is experienced but not ultimately real. The doctrine reaches its definitive form in Śaṅkara (8th century CE); the Vedānta-sūtras themselves may not fully commit to the later technical sense — the translator's introduction and footnotes in Thibaut's edition flag this as a live scholarly question. The Western Ideas of Appearance (vs. Reality) and Illusion each catch part; neither catches the doctrine's distinctive claim that māyā is neither fully real nor fully unreal but a third category. The corpus source is the Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary (Thibaut, 1890); the text is mislabeled as "Rig Veda Griffith 1896."

## Polysemy

1. **Māyā as cosmic illusion** — The definitive Advaita sense: Brahman, associated with māyā, appears as the world without undergoing any real change or modification. Māyā is the upādāna (material cause) of the world, but a material cause that is itself neither real nor unreal — a category without Western parallel. The metaphor of the magician's illusion (indrajāla) is Śaṅkara's preferred illustration.

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890 (Sacred Books of the East, vol. 34)* — "This power cannot be called 'being' (sat), for 'being' is only Brahman; nor can it be called 'non-being' (asat) in the strict sense, for it at any rate produces the appearance of this world. It is in fact a principle of illusion; the undefinable cause owing to which there seems to exist a material world comprehending distinct individual existences. Being associated with this principle of illusion, Brahman is enabled to project the appearance of the world, in the same way as a magician is enabled by his incomprehensible magical power to produce illusory appearances of animate and inanimate beings." (direct match / translator commentary — the classic definition of māyā as anirvacanīya, neither real nor unreal)

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890* — "Māyā, under the guidance of the Lord, modifies itself by a progressive evolution into all the individual existences... In all those apparently individual forms of existence the one indivisible Brahman is present, but, owing to the particular adjuncts into which Māyā has specialised itself, it appears to be broken up into a multiplicity of intellectual or sentient principles, the so-called jīvas. What is real in each jīva is only the universal Brahman itself; the whole aggregate of individualising bodily organs and mental functions... is the offspring of Māyā and as such unreal." (direct match — māyā as the principle of individuation; jīvas are Brahman-plus-adjuncts)

2. **Māyā as a hermeneutical question** — Whether the Vedānta-sūtras themselves teach māyā in the full Śaṅkaran sense is contested. Thibaut's introduction notes: "It is well known that, with the exception of the Śvetāśvatara and Maitrāyaṇīya, none of the chief Upaniṣads exhibits the word 'māyā'" — and where it does appear in the Ṛg Veda, it means "creative power," not illusion. The māyā doctrine as a systematic philosophical position is largely Śaṅkara's development, and Rāmānuja's theistic Vedānta explicitly rejects it.

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890 (footnote)* — "It is well known that, with the exception of the Śvetāśvatara and Maitrāyaṇīya, none of the chief Upaniṣads exhibits the word 'māyā.' The term indeed occurs in one place in the Bṛhadāraṇyaka; but that passage is a quotation from the Ṛg Saṃhitā in which māyā means 'creative power.'" (direct match — the hermeneutical problem: the term's later sense is not present in the foundational texts)

3. **Māyā as the magician's trick** — The experiential register: the world appears real to the unenlightened, just as the magician's illusion appears real to the audience. The point is not that the world is a hallucination (prātibhāsika) but that it is less than ultimately real (vyāvahārika) — real for practical purposes, unreal sub specie aeternitatis. The snake that is actually a rope in dim light is the classic illustration: the snake is experienced, is not purely imaginary, but is not ultimately real.

## Common misuses

In modern English, "maya" is frequently used in New Age and pop-spirituality contexts as a synonym for "the world is an illusion" in the sense of "nothing matters" or "you create your own reality." This inverts the Advaita doctrine: māyā is not a license for solipsism but a diagnosis of why solipsism feels plausible — because the ego (ahaṃkāra) is itself a product of māyā. The doctrine does not say the world doesn't exist but that its mode of existence is less than ultimate — it is phenomenally real (vyāvahārika-satya) while being ontologically dependent on Brahman. Another misuse treats māyā as a synonym for the Buddhist concept of śūnyatā (emptiness). The two are radically different: māyā requires a substrate (Brahman) that appears as the world; śūnyatā is the absence of any such substrate.

## Adjacent Terms

- [[Brahman]] — The sole reality; māyā is the power through which Brahman appears as the world.
- [[Avidya]] — Ignorance; the individual correlate of māyā — māyā is the cosmic illusion, avidyā is the individual's failure to see through it.
- [[Jiva]] — The individual soul; the jīva is Brahman as limited by the adjuncts (upādhis) produced by māyā.
