# Term: Tawḥīd

## At a glance

Tawḥīd (توحيد) is the central doctrine of Islam: the absolute oneness and uniqueness of God (Allāh). It is the first half of the shahāda — "There is no god but God" (lā ilāha illā Allāh) — and the theological foundation from which all other Islamic doctrines derive. In kalām (scholastic theology), tawḥīd is defended through rational argument against shirk (associating partners with God); in Sufism, it is not merely a proposition to be affirmed but a reality to be realized — the annihilation (fanā') of all that is not God so that only God remains. The Western Ideas of God and Unity each catch part; neither catches the term's dual function as both the first article of faith and the goal of the entire spiritual path. The Islamic corpus is underpopulated. This page is a scaffold.

## Polysemy

1. **Tawḥīd as theological doctrine** — The assertion that God is one (wāḥid), unique (aḥad), without partner, without division, and without likeness. This is the tawḥīd of the theologians: defended through rational proofs, articulated through the divine attributes (ṣifāt), and guarded against anthropomorphism (tashbīh) and negation (ta'ṭīl).

2. **Tawḥīd as spiritual realization (Sufi)** — In Sufism, tawḥīd is not merely affirmed but experienced. The seeker passes through stages: tawḥīd of the tongue (verbal affirmation), tawḥīd of the heart (internal conviction), and tawḥīd of the spirit (existential realization — seeing only God in all things). The highest tawḥīd is fanā' an al-nafs: annihilation of the self so that nothing remains but the One.

3. **Tawḥīd al-rubūbiyya, al-ulūhiyya, al-asmā' wa'l-ṣifāt** — Classical theology distinguishes three aspects: tawḥīd of lordship (God alone creates and sustains), tawḥīd of divinity (God alone is worthy of worship), and tawḥīd of the names and attributes (God's attributes are unique and not comparable to creation).
