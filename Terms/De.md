# Term: De

## At a glance

De (德, dé) is the Daoist term for the particularized manifestation of the Tao in individual things — the Tao's "outflowing operation," its virtue or power expressed as the distinctive character and efficacy of each being. The classic is titled the Dao De Jing — the "Classic of the Way and its Power." If the Tao is the mother and source, de is what each child receives: the specific potency, virtue, or excellence that enables a thing to be fully what it is. In the Dao De Jing, de operates at three levels: the Tao's own nurturing operation (producing without possessing), the sage's embodiment of de (simplicity, humility, non-contention), and the natural efficacy of things when unforced by human artifice. Legge translates de inconsistently — as "characteristics," "virtue," "attributes," and "operation" — making equivalent-match retrieval the only option.

## Polysemy

1. **De as the Tao's outflowing operation** — The most fundamental sense: de is the Tao's power as it flows into and sustains individual beings. The Tao produces all things; de nourishes, matures, and completes them. De is the Tao "with a name" — the undifferentiated source become differentiated efficacy.

   > *Laozi, Dao De Jing, trans. James Legge, 1891 (Project Gutenberg #216, Ch. 51)* — "All things are produced by the Tao, and nourished by its outflowing operation. They receive their forms according to the nature of each, and are completed according to the circumstances of their condition. Therefore all things without exception honour the Tao, and exalt its outflowing operation. This honouring of the Tao and exalting of its operation is not the result of any ordination, but always a spontaneous tribute." (equivalent match — "outflowing operation" renders de; the passage describes de as the Tao's nurturing and completing function)

   > *Laozi, Dao De Jing, trans. James Legge, 1891 (Ch. 51)* — "It produces them and makes no claim to the possession of them; it carries them through their processes and does not vaunt its ability in doing so; it brings them to maturity and exercises no control over them;—this is called its mysterious operation." (equivalent match — "mysterious operation" renders de; the character of de is non-possessiveness, efficacy without assertion)

2. **De as the sage's virtue** — The sage who practices wu-wei embodies the highest de: simplicity, humility, non-contention, and the "unconditioned" quality of one who has returned to the root. This is de as the human attainment of the Tao's own mode.

   > *Laozi, Dao De Jing, trans. James Legge, 1891 (Ch. 65)* — "Deep and far-reaching is such mysterious excellence, showing indeed its possessor as opposite to others, but leading them to a great conformity to him." (equivalent match — "mysterious excellence" renders de; the sage's de is paradoxical: it appears opposite to worldly values but leads others to harmony)

3. **De as the natural efficacy of the unforced** — When things are allowed to follow their own nature without interference, they exhibit de: the spontaneous power that comes from being what one is, unforced and uncontrived. This is the de of water, which benefits all things without striving, and of the infant, whose vital power is complete because it has not yet been dissipated by desire and ambition.

## Common misuses

In modern English, "virtue" carries Christian-moral associations of self-denial and rule-following that are foreign to the Daoist de. De is not a moral achievement but a natural endowment — one already possesses it; the task is not to acquire it but to stop obstructing it. The sage's de is not distinct from the Tao's de — it is the same power flowing through the individual without the distortions of desire and artifice. Another misuse conflates de with the Confucian sense of de as moral charisma or the ruler's transforming influence through ritual conduct. The Daoist de shares the term but inverts the mechanism: the Confucian ruler's de operates through li (ritual); the Daoist sage's de operates through the absence of li, through return to simplicity and the dismantling of artifice.

## Zhuangzi (Chuang Tzŭ) — trans. Herbert Giles, 1889

> *Chapter I (Introduction by Giles, quoting Zhuangzi)* — "'The perfect man ignores self; the divine man ignores action; the true sage ignores reputation.' The Emperor Yao would have abdicated in favour of a hermit, but the hermit replies that 'reputation is but the shadow of reality,' and will not exchange the real for the seeming. But greater than Yao and the hermit is the divine being who dwells on the mysterious mountain in a state of pure, passionless inaction. For the sage, then, life means death to all that men think life, the life of seeming or reputation, of doing or action, of being or individual selfhood."
>
> *Gloss: Three ascending levels — perfect man (zhì rén), divine man (shén rén), true sage (shèng rén) — mapped to three renunciations: self, action, reputation. De is the power that emerges from each renunciation, not a quantity accumulated. The hermit who refuses the throne has grasped that reputation is the shadow of reality, but the divine being who dwells in "pure, passionless inaction" embodies de more fully still — he has let go even of the renunciation.*

> *Chapter II (Giles's exposition)* — "The sage 'blends everything into one harmonious whole, rejecting the comparison of contraries.' For him, 'a beam and a pillar are identical. So are ugliness and beauty, greatness, wickedness, perverseness, and strangeness. Separation is the same as construction: construction is the same as destruction.'"
>
> *Gloss: De as the power that perceives unity beneath distinction. This is not a moral claim but an epistemological one: the sage sees that opposites are not opposed in reality but only in the partial perspectives of human judgment. The "one harmonious whole" is not a dogma but a way of seeing — and that way of seeing is itself de.*

> *Giles, introducing the mirror metaphor* — "The perfect man employs his mind as a mirror. It grasps nothing, it refuses nothing. It receives, but does not keep."
>
> *Gloss: De figured as the mirror-mind — responsive but not accumulative. The perfect man's virtue is not a store of good deeds or cultivated habits but the clarity of a surface that reflects without distortion and without residue. This passage (from Zhuangzi ch. 7) is one of the most influential metaphors in all of Chinese philosophy.*

> *Chapter on "The True Sage"* — "He perfects his virtue, but does not involve himself. He guides himself by Tao, but makes no plans. He identifies himself with charity, but does not rely on it. He extends to duty towards his neighbour, but does not store it up. He responds to ceremony, without tabooing it. ... He undertakes affairs without declining them. He metes out law without confusion. He relies on his fellow-men and does not make light of them. He accommodates himself to matter and does not ignore it."
>
> *Gloss: De enacted — virtue that acts without entanglement. Each pair is a discipline of non-attachment: one engages fully with charity, duty, ceremony, affairs, law, people, and matter but without the clinging that turns engagement into burden. This is de as the power to be fully present without being captured.*
