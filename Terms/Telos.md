# Term: Telos

## At a glance

Telos (τέλος) is the Greek term for "end" in the sense of purpose, goal, or completion — that for the sake of which a thing exists or an action is performed. In Aristotle, telos is one of the four causes (the final cause, to hou heneka) and the organizing principle of both physics and ethics: the telos of an acorn is the oak, the telos of a human being is eudaimonia, and the telos of the polis is the good life. The Nicomachean Ethics opens by constructing a hierarchy of ends — each subordinate art pursued for the sake of a higher one, culminating in the "absolutely final" good, happiness. This teleological architecture is then operationalized through the ergon argument: the telos of a thing is realized in the excellent performance of its characteristic function. The Western Ideas of Purpose and Happiness depend on this concept; modern philosophy's rejection of natural teleology (from Bacon through Darwin) is one of the fault lines that separates the classical from the modern framework. The corpus has Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics and Metaphysics; the Kant corpus reframes telos as the "kingdom of ends."

## Polysemy

1. **Telos as final cause** — Aristotle's four-cause framework: material (that out of which), formal (the essence), efficient (the source of change), and final (that for the sake of which). The final cause is not an intention in a conscious mind (except in the case of human crafts) but the inherent directedness of a natural process toward its completion.

   > *Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, trans. R.W. Browne / W.D. Ross, Book I, Ch. 4* — "What then is the Chief Good in each? Is it not 'that for the sake of which the other things are done?' and this in the healing art is health, and in the art military victory, and in that of house-building a house... in short, in every action and moral choice the End, because in all cases men do everything else with a view to this." (equivalent match — "End" renders telos; the chain of purposes that structures all human activity)

2. **Telos and the hierarchy of ends** — Ends are arranged hierarchically: subordinate ends (wealth, instruments) are pursued for the sake of superior ends, which are in turn pursued for the sake of the "absolutely final" end — eudaimonia. This hierarchy is what prevents an infinite regress of purposes and gives ethical reasoning its architecture.

   > *Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, trans. R.W. Browne / W.D. Ross, Book I* — "Now since the ends are plainly many, and of these we choose some with a view to others (wealth, for instance, musical instruments, and, in general, all instruments), it is clear that all are not final: but the Chief Good is manifestly something final; and so, if there is some one only which is final, this must be the object of our search." (equivalent match — the regress argument: if every end were subordinate to another, desire would be "empty and vain")

3. **Telos as the absolutely final** — The highest telos is that which is chosen always for its own sake and never for the sake of anything further. For Aristotle, this is eudaimonia. Telos thus distinguishes between instrumental goods (chosen for something else) and intrinsic goods (chosen for themselves). The absolutely final good is both final and self-sufficient.

   > *Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, trans. R.W. Browne / W.D. Ross, Book I* — "Now that which is an object of pursuit in itself we call more final than that which is so with a view to something else; that again which is never an object of choice with a view to something else... and so by the term 'absolutely final,' we denote that which is an object of choice always in itself, and never with a view to any other. And of this nature Happiness is mostly thought to be." (equivalent match — the definition of the absolutely final telos)

4. **Telos in modern reframing: Kant's kingdom of ends** — Kant radicalizes the concept by transferring telos from nature to reason. Rational beings are ends-in-themselves, never merely means. The "kingdom of ends" is the systematic union of rational beings under common moral laws — a teleological order grounded not in nature but in autonomy.

   > *Kant, Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, trans. T.K. Abbott* — "Teleology considers nature as a kingdom of ends; ethics regards a possible kingdom of ends as a kingdom nature. In the first case, the kingdom of ends is a theoretical idea, adopted to explain what actually is. In the latter it is a practical idea, adopted to bring about that which is not yet." (equivalent match — the Kantian reframing: telos shifts from natural function to rational self-legislation)

## Common misuses

In modern English, "purpose" and "goal" are used interchangeably and often psychologically — "finding your purpose," "setting goals." This subjectivizes telos, making it a matter of individual choice rather than an objective feature of the thing's nature. For Aristotle, you do not choose your telos; you discover it through analysis of the human ergon. Another misuse is the reduction of teleology to intelligent design — the claim that because things have purposes, they must have a designer. Aristotle's teleology is natural, not theistic: the telos of an organism is internal to its form, not imposed by an external creator.
