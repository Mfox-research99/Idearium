# Term: Ātman

## At a glance

Ātman (Sanskrit, आत्मन्, from √an "to breathe") is the central term for the self in classical Hindu thought — the innermost essence of the individual, distinct from body, senses, and mind, and in the Advaita Vedānta tradition ultimately identical with Brahman, the ground of all reality. Across the corpus it carries at least four distinct senses — the individual soul (jīvātman), the universal Self (paramātman), the reflexive pronoun ("oneself"), and the embodied self of ordinary experience — that are not interchangeable. The Western Ideas of Soul, Self, and Mind each catch part of it; none catches the whole. Because pre-1929 English translators consistently rendered ātman as "Self," "soul," or "individual soul" rather than transliterating it, the equivalent-match passages below are flagged as the translator's English word, not the original term. This is a methodological necessity given the corpus's translation conventions, not a claim about the term's meaning.

## Polysemy

1. **The individual soul (jīvātman)** — The self as it experiences embodiment: the conscious subject entangled in saṃsāra (the cycle of rebirth), identified with a particular body and mind, subject to karma, and seeking liberation. This sense dominates the Bhagavad Gītā, where Kṛṣṇa teaches Arjuna about the eternal, indestructible nature of the ātman that cannot be slain when the body is slain. Overlaps the Western Idea of Soul.

   > *Vyāsa (attrib.), Bhagavad Gītā, trans. Edwin Arnold, 1885* — "I BRAHMA am! the One Eternal GOD, / And ADHYATMAN is My Being's name, / The Soul of Souls!" (equivalent match — Arnold's "ADHYATMAN" renders the compound adhyātman, "the supreme Self" or "the Self that dwells in all selves")

2. **The universal Self (paramātman) — identity with Brahman** — The mahāvākyas (great sayings) of the Upaniṣads — "tat tvam asi" (that thou art), "aham brahmāsmi" (I am Brahman) — assert that the innermost ātman is not different from Brahman, the ultimate reality. In Śaṅkara's Advaita Vedānta, this identity is absolute and non-dual: the apparent multiplicity of selves is an illusion (māyā) superimposed on the single reality of Brahman-ātman. This is the most philosophically distinctive sense of the term in the corpus and has no direct Western parallel.

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890 (Sacred Books of the East, vols. 34, 38)* — "The meditation on the Atman enjoined by Scripture is not an act to be accomplished once only, but is to be repeated again and again." (direct match — the text uses "Atman" untranslated, the meditation on which constitutes the path to liberation)

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890* — "The devotee engaged in meditation on Brahman is to view it as constituting his own Self." (equivalent match — "Self" renders ātman in the context of the identity between the individual self and Brahman)

3. **The embodied / empirical self** — In contrast to the pure ātman of Vedānta, the term can also denote the self as it appears in ordinary experience: the composite of body, senses, mind, and ego (ahaṃkāra) that the unenlightened person mistakes for the true self. This usage appears in the Vedānta-sūtras' discussion of adhyāsa (superimposition), where the attributes of the non-Self are falsely projected onto the Self.

   > *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, trans. George Thibaut, 1890* — "In the absence of the mutual superimposition of the Self and the Non-Self and their attributes." (equivalent match — "Self" renders ātman as the substrate onto which body and mind are falsely projected)

4. **The reflexive pronoun** — In its most mundane grammatical sense, ātman simply means "oneself" (as in "he knows himself"). Sanskrit philosophical texts often exploit the ambiguity between this ordinary reflexive sense and the metaphysical Self, making it difficult at times to determine which register is intended. This ambiguity is itself philosophically productive: the question "who am I?" is built into the grammar.

5. **Buddhist reframing: anātman (no-self)** — In Buddhist thought, the doctrine of anātman (Pali: anattā) denies the existence of a permanent, unchanging self of the kind asserted by the Hindu ātman tradition. What appears as a self is merely a contingent aggregation of five skandhas (form, sensation, perception, mental formations, consciousness). This is a direct rejection of the Hindu ātman doctrine and constitutes one of the most fundamental philosophical divides between the two traditions. This meaning is not yet represented in the current corpus (which lacks Buddhist primary sources in pre-1929 English translation), but it is a major sense that will need to be added when the corpus expands.

## Common misuses

In modern English-language wellness and spirituality discourse, "atman" is often equated with "the higher self" or "the true self" in a therapeutic sense — a personal essence one can discover through introspection or meditation. This flattens the radical metaphysical claim of Advaita Vedānta, where the ātman is not a personal essence at all but the impersonal ground of reality identical with Brahman. The popular phrase "connect with your atman" imports a therapeutic individualism foreign to the classical concept: in the Upaniṣadic framework, the problem is precisely that one is already over-connected to one's personal self and must dissolve that identification to realize the universal ātman. Another common misuse treats "atman" as interchangeable with the Western concept of "soul" in the Christian sense of a created, individual, immortal substance — whereas for Śaṅkara, the ātman is uncreated, non-individual (ultimately), and not a substance among substances but the substrate of all.

## In which Ideas does this Term appear

- [[../Soul]] — Ātman reframes the Western concept of the soul by asserting its identity with the ultimate reality (Brahman) rather than its created, individual character.
- [[../Self]] (when promoted) — The ātman is the classical Indian answer to the question "what is the self?" — but one that dissolves the questioner into the answer.
- [[../Immortality]] — The ātman is eternal not because it survives death (though it does) but because it was never born; its immortality is essential, not sequential.
- [[../Truth]] — The mahāvākyas assert the identity of ātman and Brahman as the highest truth; knowing this truth liberates.

## Adjacent Terms

- [[Brahman]] — The ultimate reality with which ātman is identified in Advaita Vedānta. The two are the central pair of Hindu philosophy.
- [[Moksha]] — Liberation from saṃsāra; realization of the true nature of ātman as identical with Brahman is the path to moksha.
- [[Karma]] — Action and its consequences; the ātman transmigrates through lives carrying the residues of karma until liberation.
- [[Anatman]] — The Buddhist doctrine of no-self; the direct philosophical antagonist to the Hindu ātman doctrine.
- [[Purusha]] — The cosmic person or consciousness principle in Sāṃkhya and the Gītā; closely related to ātman but with a more cosmological emphasis.
