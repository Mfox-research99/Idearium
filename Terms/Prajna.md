# Term: Prajna

## At a glance

Prajna (Sanskrit प्रज्ञा, prajñā) is a central term in both Buddhist and Hindu thought, typically translated as "wisdom" or "insight." In Buddhism it is one of the six perfections (pāramitās) and the faculty that directly sees the true nature of reality (śūnyatā). In Hindu philosophy, particularly in the Upaniṣads, prajñā denotes a higher knowing distinct from intellectual knowledge. The term overlaps with the Western Idea of Wisdom but carries specific soteriological weight—it is not merely theoretical understanding but a transformative, direct realization that leads to liberation. The pre-1929 English translations in the corpus (e.g., Müller, Arnold) render prajñā as "wisdom" or "knowledge" without transliteration, so no direct or equivalent matches for the term itself were found in the supplied corpus passages. ## Polysemy

*No primary-source passages were available in the corpus to cite. The following meanings are drawn from general scholarly consensus and are noted here for completeness; they cannot be supported by quoted passages from the current corpus.*

1. **Buddhist prajñā: the perfection of wisdom** — In Mahāyāna Buddhism, prajñā is the sixth pāramitā, the direct realization of emptiness (śūnyatā) that cuts through all conceptual elaboration. It is the culmination of the bodhisattva path and is often paired with upāya (skillful means). This meaning partially overlaps the Western Idea of Wisdom but is inseparable from the Buddhist soteriological framework.

2. **Hindu prajñā: higher knowledge** — In the Upaniṣads and Vedānta, prajñā refers to the deep, intuitive knowledge of Brahman, often contrasted with mere intellectual learning (vidyā in a lower sense). It is the state of consciousness in deep sleep (prajñā as the third state in Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad). This meaning touches the Ideas of Knowledge and Truth.

3. **Prajñā as cognitive faculty** — In early Buddhist psychology (Abhidhamma), prajñā is a mental factor that discerns phenomena correctly, distinct from faith (śraddhā) or mindfulness (smṛti). It is the analytical wisdom that sees the three marks of existence (anicca, dukkha, anattā).

## Common misuses

In modern English-language Buddhism, "prajna" is sometimes used loosely as a synonym for "wisdom" in a general sense, losing its specific technical meaning as direct realization of emptiness. It is also occasionally confused with jñāna (knowledge) or vijñāna (consciousness). The term does not appear to have a significant popular drift outside of Buddhist studies.

## Adjacent Terms

- [[../Nirvana]] — Prajñā is the direct cause of nirvāṇa; without prajñā, liberation is impossible.
- [[../Bodhisattva]] — The bodhisattva perfects prajñā as the sixth pāramitā.
- [[../Brahman]] — In Vedānta, prajñā is the knowledge that leads to realization of Brahman.
