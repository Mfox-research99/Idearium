# Term: Fanā'

## At a glance

Fanā' (فناء, "annihilation") is the Sufi term for the passing away of the individual self in the presence of the Divine — the station (maqām) in which the ego's boundaries dissolve and only God remains. It is the highest stage of the Sufi path before baqā' ("subsistence" — the return to the world with God, not the self, as the agent). In the Persian poetic tradition (Rumi, Hafiz, Attar, Jami), fanā' is figured through the imagery of the moth consumed in the candle's flame, the drop merged in the ocean, and "Annihilation's Waste" where the self is unmade. The corpus has limited Sufi primary sources — FitzGerald's Khayyām (1859) touches the concept through the imagery of self-dissolution, but Rumi and Hafiz are present only in fragmentary translations. The Western Ideas of Self (as negation) and Mysticism each catch part; neither catches the doctrine's pairing with baqā' — the return — which distinguishes fanā' from mere dissolution.

## Polysemy

1. **Fanā' as the annihilation of the self** — The core sense: the passing away of the ego (nafs), its desires, and its sense of separate identity in the overwhelming experience of divine presence. This is not the destruction of the soul but the extinction of the illusion that the self is an independent agent. "I" disappears; what remains is God's act.

   > *Omar Khayyām, Rubáiyát, trans. Edward FitzGerald, 1859 (Project Gutenberg)* — "One Moment in Annihilation's Waste, / One moment, of the Well of Life to taste— / The Stars are setting, and the Caravan / Starts for the dawn of Nothing—Oh, make haste!" (equivalent match — FitzGerald's "Annihilation's Waste" renders fanā'; the imagery of the caravan departing for "Nothing" captures the urgency and the extinction of self)

2. **Fanā' and baqā' — the return** — Fanā' is not the end of the path but its penultimate station. The perfected Sufi passes through fanā' into baqā': subsistence in God, where one returns to the world but acts through God rather than through the self. The self is not destroyed but transformed — emptied of ego and filled with the divine. This distinguishes Sufi fanā' from Buddhist nirvāṇa: the goal is not cessation but transformed agency.

3. **Fanā' as the moth and the flame** — The Persian poetic tradition renders fanā' through the image of the moth (parvāna) circling the candle flame until it plunges in and is consumed. The moth knows that the flame is its death and its beloved; it cannot not go. This is fanā' as love's consummation — the annihilation that is simultaneously union.

4. **Fanā' and the levels of the nafs** — Classical Sufi psychology distinguishes stages of the self (nafs): the commanding self (nafs al-ammāra), the self-accusing self (nafs al-lawwāma), the inspired self (nafs al-mulhama), the serene self (nafs al-muṭma'inna), the pleased self (nafs al-rāḍiya), and the pleasing self (nafs al-marḍiyya). Fanā' is the transition from the lower stages to the annihilation of even the purified self in God.

## Common misuses

In modern English, "ego death" is a common psychedelic-culture appropriation that flattens fanā' into a peak experience — a temporary dissolution of self-boundaries achievable through substances. This misses the ethical and disciplinary framework: fanā' is the fruit of a lifetime of spiritual practice (dhikr, murāqaba, the guidance of a shaykh), not an event one can trigger chemically. Another misuse conflates fanā' with nihilistic self-destruction or clinical depersonalization. Fanā' is not the loss of self in pathology but the loss of the *false* self in truth — an awakening, not a breakdown.

## Hafiz — Poems from the Divan (trans. Gertrude Bell, 1897)

> *Poem XXXIV* — "That, that is not the flame of Love's true fire / Which makes the torchlight shadows dance in rings, / But where the radiance draws the moth's desire / And sends him forth with scorched and drooping wings. / The heart of one who dwells retired shall break, / Rememb'ring a black mole and a red cheek, / And his life ebb, sapped at its secret springs."
>
> *Gloss: Fanā' figured through the moth (parvāna) and the flame. The distinction is crucial: not every fire is "Love's true fire." The moth that merely circles the torchlight has not yet reached fanā' — the true lover is the one who plunges in, wings scorched, life ebbing. The heart that "dwells retired" — withdrawn from the world — breaks not from ascetic discipline but from the memory of the beloved's beauty, figured in the intimate particularity of a mole and a cheek.*

> *Poem XXXVIII* — "I cease not from desire till my desire / Is satisfied; or let my mouth attain / My love's red mouth, or let my soul expire, / Sighed from those lips that sought her lips in vain. / Others may find another love as fair; / Upon her threshold I have laid my head, / The dust shall cover me, still lying there, / When from my body life and love have fled."
>
> *Gloss: The lover's ultimatum — union or annihilation. The soul on the threshold is already in the posture of fanā': head laid down, waiting for dust to cover it. The distinction between life and love collapses at the limit. "My soul is on my lips ready to fly" — the breath that would speak the beloved's name is already the breath of departure.*

## Jāmī — Salāmān and Absāl (trans. Edward FitzGerald, 1856)

> *The Second Vision* — "Soon as the maid from his lips had heard / That his answer was 'Yea' to her soul's demand, / She rose like the fragrance from off the rose / And stood before him with naked heart... 'Lo, I am she whom thou seekest afar: / Look on me, and know me, and make no stay. / What is near at hand that thou seekest afar, / Like a man who, with water up to his lip, / Cries, Alas that my thirst is so great! and dies.'"
>
> *Gloss: The beloved reveals that she was always present — the seeker's thirst was for what already surrounded him. This is the epistemological dimension of fanā': the annihilation is not of the self alone but of the illusion of distance. The beloved was never absent; the self's separateness was the veil.*

## Jāmī — Yūsuf and Zulaikhā (trans. Ralph T.H. Griffith, 1882)

> *On divine presence and the mirror* — "Thou art but the glass, his is the face reflected in the mirror; nay, if thou lookest steadfastly, thou shalt see that he is the mirror also. In a parable, Jami illustrates the universal presence of God, and the blind searching of man for that by which he is surrounded on every side. There was a frog which sat upon the shores of the ocean, and ceaselessly day and night he sang its praise... Some fish swimming in the shallow water heard the frog's song, and were filled with a desire to find that wonderful ocean of which he spoke, but go where they would they could not discover it."
>
> *Gloss: Fanā' reframed as the recognition that what one seeks is what one is already in. The frog on the ocean's shore cannot find the ocean because he has never been outside it. The seeker's problem is not distance but blindness. "Thou art but the glass" — the self is not a thing to be destroyed but a mirror that, when polished, reflects only the divine face. And even the mirror is ultimately He.*

## Al-Ghazālī — The Confessions (trans. Claud Field, 1909)

> *On the limits of definition* — "I saw that Sufism consists in experiences rather than in definitions, and that what I was lacking belonged to the domain, not of instruction, but of ecstasy and initiation. The researches to which I had devoted myself, the path which I had traversed in studying the speculative and the traditional branches of knowledge, had given me a firm faith in three things — God, Inspiration, and the Last Judgment."
>
> *Gloss: Al-Ghazālī marks the boundary between theology-as-knowledge and Sufism-as-experience. Fanā' cannot be defined; it must be undergone. This passage does not describe fanā' but explains why description fails — the concept belongs to "ecstasy and initiation," not to "instruction." The intellectual who exhausted the sciences must become the mystic who undergoes annihilation to know what he could not learn.*

## Copyrighted Passages — secondary sources

- **Ibn 'Arabī, *Fuṣūṣ al-Ḥikam*** — [[../../Copyrighted_Passages/Ibn_Arabi_Fusus_al-Hikam|Copyrighted Passages file]] with 5 key passages (Austin translation, 1980). Covers: the cosmos as divine mirror (Adam chapter), the Perfect Human Being as the locus of fanā' and baqā' (Seth chapter), the two commands and the convergence of will (Hūd chapter), annihilation through beauty (Joseph chapter), and the heart's infinite capacity (from the Tarjumān). Ibn 'Arabī provides the systematic metaphysical exposition of fanā' that the poetic sources evoke through imagery.

- **'Aṭṭār, *Manṭiq al-Ṭayr*** — [[../../Copyrighted_Passages/Attar_Conference_of_the_Birds|Copyrighted Passages file]] with 5 key passages (Darbandi-Davis translation, 1984). Covers: the hoopoe's call to the journey, the Valley of Poverty and Annihilation, the moth and flame parable, the thirty birds' self-recognition as the Simorgh, and the nightingale's attachment to the rose. 'Aṭṭār is the fountainhead of the Persian poetic tradition that Rumi, Hafez, and Jāmī later develop; the moth-and-flame parable is the emblematic Sufi image of fanā'.
