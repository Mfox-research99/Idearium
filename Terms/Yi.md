Yi (義, yì) is the Confucian term for righteousness — the moral judgment that discerns what is fitting and proper in a given situation. Unlike ren (仁, benevolence), which is the universal concern for others, yi is the discriminating virtue: the capacity to see that even a good act performed in the wrong circumstances is not right. The Analects consistently pair yi with li (ritual propriety) and ren (benevolence) as the three pillars of Confucian moral psychology: ren is the motive, yi is the judgment, li is the form. 1. **Yi as moral judgment** — Yi is the capacity to judge what is right in a particular case, the quality that prevents virtues from becoming mechanical. Courage without yi becomes insubordination or robbery; ren without yi can be misplaced.

## Common misuses

In modern English, "righteousness" carries biblical freight — self-righteousness, moral superiority. This is the opposite of Confucian yi, which is a capacity for situational judgment, not a posture of certainty. The person of yi does not proclaim virtue but exercises it through humility and sincerity. Another misuse conflates yi with li — mistaking the judgment of appropriateness for the form through which it is expressed. Yi judges when li should be adapted or even suspended; it is the meta-ritual virtue, and conflating the two removes the critical dimension from Confucian ethics.

## Mencius — The Sayings of Mencius (trans. James Legge, from *Chinese Literature*, 1900)

> *Book I, King Hwuy of Leang, Part I* — "Mencius replied, 'Why must your Majesty use that word "profit"? What I am likewise provided with are counsels to benevolence and righteousness; and these are my only topics.'"
>
> *Gloss: Yì (righteousness) paired with rén (benevolence) as the twin pillars of Mencian political philosophy. The pairing is crucial: rén provides the inner motive (compassion), yì provides the outer structure (what is fitting, appropriate, right). Neither is sufficient alone.*

> *Book I, King Hwuy of Leang, Part I (continued)* — "'If righteousness be put last and profit first, they will not be satisfied without snatching all. There never was a man trained to benevolence who neglected his parents. There never was a man trained to righteousness who made his ruler an after consideration. Let your Majesty likewise make benevolence and righteousness your only themes — Why must you speak of profit?'"
>
> *Gloss: Yì as the political expression of moral cultivation. The person "trained to righteousness" does not subordinate their ruler to private interest — yì is the virtue that orders priorities correctly, placing duty to the larger whole above personal gain. The claim is empirical as well as normative: Mencius asserts that neglect of parents and disloyalty to rulers never follow from genuine rén and yì, only from their absence.*

> *On the king's duty* — "King Hwuy of Lëang said, 'Small as my virtue is, in the government of my kingdom, I have devoted my whole heart thereto. When there was a famine in Ho-nuy, I removed the people to Ho-tung, and transported grain to Ho-nuy... I have not observed that the ruler of a neighbouring kingdom does not diminish his efforts as I diminish mine. How is it that the people of the neighbouring kingdoms do not decrease, and my people do not increase?' Mencius replied, 'Your Majesty loves war; allow me to take an illustration from war... If your Majesty will dispense a benevolent government to the people, being sparing in the use of punishments and fines, and making the taxes and levies light... then the people of the neighbouring kingdoms will turn to you as water flows downwards.'"
>
> *Gloss: Yì as the ruler's self-examination. The king believes he has done his duty — he moved grain during famine — but Mencius exposes the gap between the king's self-assessment and reality: he loves war, he taxes heavily, his people do not multiply. Yì is not self-reported virtue but the objective measure of whether a ruler's conduct actually conduces to the people's welfare. The test is the directional flow of population — people vote with their feet.*

> *King Hwuy on the quality of virtue* — "The king said, 'Of what kind must his virtue be who can attain to the throne of the whole kingdom?' Mencius replied, 'He who has no pleasure in killing men can unite the kingdom.'"
>
> *Gloss: Yì reduced to its simplest test — the aversion to killing. The ruler who can unite "all under heaven" (tiānxià) is not the one with the strongest army but the one for whom killing gives no pleasure. This is yì as the negative capability: the absence of the appetite for violence is the positive condition of legitimate rule.*
