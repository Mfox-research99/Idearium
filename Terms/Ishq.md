# Term: Ishq

## At a glance

Ishq (عشق) is the Arabic term for passionate, often overwhelming love, distinguished from milder forms such as *mahabba* (affection) or *hubb* (love). In classical Sufi thought, *ishq* is elevated to the central metaphor for the soul’s yearning for God — a consuming, ecstatic love that annihilates the lover’s separate self. The term warrants its own page because it cannot be reduced to the Western Idea of Eros without losing its specifically Sufi theological frame: *ishq* is not merely human desire but the very force that moves creation toward its Creator. The pre-1929 English translations in the corpus (e.g., Whinfield’s Rumi, Bell’s Hafiz) routinely render *ishq* as “love,” making direct or equivalent matches unavailable in the supplied corpus results. This page therefore draws on general knowledge of the term while noting the absence of primary-source quotations from the current corpus.

## Polysemy

1. **Human passionate love** — The ordinary sense of romantic or erotic desire, often with connotations of intensity and suffering. This meaning overlaps with the Idea of Eros but carries a stronger emphasis on the lover’s helplessness and the beloved’s inaccessibility.

   > *No corpus passage available.* The pre-1929 translations in the corpus do not preserve the term *ishq*; they translate it as “love.” A representative example from Rumi’s *Masnavi* (trans. Whinfield, 1898) would read “love” where the Persian has *ishq*.

2. **Divine love (Sufi)** — The mystical sense in which *ishq* is the soul’s longing for God, a love that consumes the lover’s ego and leads to *fanā* (annihilation in the Divine). This is the load-bearing meaning in Sufi poetry and metaphysics. It partially overlaps with the Idea of Eros (especially the Platonic ascent) but is distinct in its insistence on the lover’s utter dissolution and the beloved’s absolute transcendence.

   > *No corpus passage available.* The same translation loss applies. For example, in Rumi’s *Masnavi* I, 1–18 (trans. Whinfield), the famous reed-flute lament speaks of “love” that makes the reed cry; the original Persian uses *ishq*.

## Common misuses

In modern South Asian languages (Urdu, Hindi, Punjabi), *ishq* is often used colloquially to mean any romantic love, losing the Sufi theological weight. In Western New Age spirituality, “ishq” is sometimes romanticized as “divine love” without the rigorous ascetic and self-annihilating context of classical Sufism. Neither misuse captures the term’s full polysemy.
