# Term: Ren

## At a glance

Ren (仁, rén) is the central Confucian virtue — the quality of being fully and authentically human in relation to others. Etymologically composed of "person" (人) and "two" (二), it is inherently relational: one cannot be ren alone. In the Analects, Confucius defines it as "to love all men" (Book XII), traces its root to filial piety and fraternal respect (Book I), and characterizes it through the negative Golden Rule: "what you do not want done to yourself, do not do to others" (Book XV). It is simultaneously the highest virtue and an achievable practice — "is ren far away? When I desire ren, ren is at hand." The Western Ideas of Virtue, Love, and Humanity each catch part of it; none catches its rootedness in family relationships as the training ground for universal moral concern. Because James Legge consistently translated ren as "benevolence," "perfect virtue," or "virtue," all passages are equivalent matches.

## Polysemy

1. **Ren as love of others** — The most direct definition: ren is to love people. This is universal in scope but develops outward from the family. The Analects trace the logic: filial piety generates respect for elders; respect for elders generates loyalty to superiors; loyalty generates social order — and ren is the quality that animates the entire chain.

   > *Confucius, Analects, trans. James Legge, 1861 (Project Gutenberg #3330, Book XII)* — "Fan Ch'ih asked about benevolence. The Master said, 'It is to love all men.' He asked about knowledge. The Master said, 'It is to know all men.'" (equivalent match — Legge's "benevolence" renders ren; the definition is startlingly simple and universal)

2. **Ren as rooted in family** — Ren is not an abstract universalism but develops from concrete familial relationships. Filial piety and fraternal submission are "the root of all benevolent actions" — the family is the school of ren, where one first learns to subordinate self-interest to the needs of others.

   > *Confucius, Analects, trans. James Legge, 1861 (Book I)* — "The philosopher Yu said, 'They are few who, being filial and fraternal, are fond of offending against their superiors. There have been none, who, not liking to offend against their superiors, have been fond of stirring up confusion. ... Filial piety and fraternal submission!—are they not the root of all benevolent actions?'" (equivalent match — ren as the flowering of family ethics into social order)

3. **The Golden Rule** — Confucius formulates ren through the negative reciprocal principle: do not impose on others what you would not want for yourself. This is not a maxim of calculation but the spontaneous expression of ren — the person of ren naturally extends their own self-regard to others.

   > *Confucius, Analects, trans. James Legge, 1861 (Book XV)* — "Tsze-kung asked, saying, 'Is there one word which may serve as a rule of practice for all one's life?' The Master said, 'Is not RECIPROCITY such a word? What you do not want done to yourself, do not do to others.'" (equivalent match — "reciprocity" (shu) as the actionable expression of ren)

4. **Ren as achievable, not remote** — Unlike some conceptions of virtue as an ideal forever receding, Confucius insists ren is near: "Is ren far away? When I desire ren, ren is at hand." The practice of ren requires not extraordinary ability but the sincere direction of the will.

   > *Confucius, Analects, trans. James Legge, 1861 (Book VII)* — "The Master said, 'Is virtue a thing remote? I wish to be virtuous, and lo! virtue is at hand.'" (equivalent match — ren as immediately available to sincere intention)

5. **Ren balanced by learning** — Ren without learning degenerates into "foolish simplicity." The Analects warn that even the most central virtue must be cultivated through study and disciplined by li, or it becomes a liability.

   > *Confucius, Analects, trans. James Legge, 1861 (Book XVII)* — "The Master said, 'There is the love of being benevolent without the love of learning;—the beclouding here leads to a foolish simplicity.'" (equivalent match — ren requires the discipline of learning to avoid naïveté)

## Common misuses

In modern English, "benevolence" suggests a soft, sentimental quality — being "nice" or charitable. This misses the rigor of Confucian ren, which demands moral courage (the person of ren "will certainly have courage" — Book XIV), self-discipline (ren is achieved by "subduing the self and returning to propriety"), and the willingness to sacrifice one's life for ren ("the determined scholar and the man of ren will not seek to live at the expense of injuring their ren" — Book XV). Another misuse treats ren as interchangeable with Christian agape or Buddhist compassion, ignoring its distinctive grounding in graded familial love and its inseparability from li (ritual form). Ren without li is formless sentiment; li without ren is empty formalism. The Confucian synthesis is sui generis.

## Adjacent Terms

- [[Li]] — Ritual propriety; the form that ren takes. Without li, ren is amorphous; without ren, li is empty.
- [[Yi]] — Righteousness; the moral judgment that governs when li must be adapted. Ren is the motive; yi is the judgment.
- [[Xiao]] — Filial piety; the root (ben) of ren, the family relationship in which humane concern is first learned and practiced.

## Mencius — The Sayings of Mencius (trans. James Legge, from *Chinese Literature*, 1900)

> *Book I, King Hwuy of Leang, Part I* — "Mencius went to see King Hwuy of Lëang. The king said, 'Venerable Sir, since you have not counted it far to come here a distance of a thousand li, may I presume that you are likewise provided with counsels to profit my kingdom?' Mencius replied, 'Why must your Majesty use that word "profit"? What I am likewise provided with are counsels to benevolence and righteousness; and these are my only topics.'"
>
> *Gloss: The founding move of Mencian ethics — the displacement of profit (lì) by benevolence (rén) and righteousness (yì) as the sole proper topics of political discourse. The word "only" (而已) is the cutting edge: Mencius does not add benevolence to the king's existing concerns but replaces them entirely. Rén is not a supplement to profit-seeking governance; it is its alternative.*

> *Book I, King Hwuy of Leang, Part I (continued)* — "'If your Majesty say, "What is to be done to profit my kingdom?" the great officers will say, "What is to be done to profit our families?" and the inferior officers and the common people will say, "What is to be done to profit our persons?" Superiors and inferiors will try to take the profit the one from the other, and the kingdom will be endangered. ... If righteousness be put last and profit first, they will not be satisfied without snatching all.'"
>
> *Gloss: Rén and yì as the antidotes to a cascade of mutual predation. The argument is consequentialist in structure (profit-first leads to collapse) but the remedy is not a better calculus of profit — it is the cultivation of benevolence and righteousness as the framework within which all other considerations are weighed. The cascade begins at the top: the king's question determines the kingdom's orientation.*

> *King Hwuy of Leang, on the king's pleasure* — "When Mencius, another day, was seeing King Hwuy of Lëang, the King went and stood with him by a pond, and, looking round on the wild geese and deer, large and small, said, 'Do wise and good princes also take pleasure in these things?' Mencius replied, 'Being wise and good, they then have pleasure in these things. If they are not wise and good, though they have these things, they do not find pleasure.'"
>
> *Gloss: Rén as the condition of genuine enjoyment. The king's question assumes pleasure and virtue are separate domains; Mencius insists they are inseparable. The "wise and good" (the rén ruler) alone can truly enjoy the pond, the geese, the deer — because enjoyment detached from benevolence becomes grasping, and grasping precludes enjoyment. This is rén not as self-denial but as the condition of full human flourishing.*

> *On King Hwuy's compassion for the ox* — "The king said, '...I could not bear its frightened appearance, as if it were an innocent person going to the place of death. Therefore I changed it for a sheep.' Mencius said, '...The heart behind your action is sufficient to enable you to become a true king. ... You saw the ox, and had not seen the sheep. So is the superior man affected towards animals, that, having seen them alive, he cannot bear to see them die; having heard their dying cries, he cannot bear to eat their flesh.'"
>
> *Gloss: The canonical Mencian argument that rén is innate — the king's spontaneous compassion for the frightened ox is the "sprout" (duān) of benevolence that needs only cultivation, not creation. The shift from ox to sheep is not hypocrisy but the natural limit of a compassion not yet extended: the king felt for what he saw. The task of self-cultivation is to extend that feeling to what one does not yet see.*

> *On benevolent government* — "'If your Majesty wishes to carry out a benevolent government, why not turn back to the root? Let mulberry trees be planted about the homesteads... then those who are fifty may be clothed with silk. In keeping fowls, pigs, dogs, and swine, let not their times of breeding be neglected, and persons of seventy may eat flesh. Let there be no taking away the people's time for their hundred acres of land, and the family of eight mouths need not suffer from hunger.'"
>
> *Gloss: Rén as material policy, not sentiment. Mencius gives the king a concrete program: silk for the elderly, meat for the aged, food security for families. Rén is not a feeling of goodwill but a set of institutional arrangements that make human flourishing possible. The "root" (běn) is economic: benevolence without material foundation is empty.*
