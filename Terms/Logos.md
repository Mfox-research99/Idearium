# Term: Logos

## At a glance

Logos (λόγος, from λέγειν "to speak/gather/count") is the Greek term that spans the spectrum from ordinary speech to cosmic reason — one of the most semantically loaded words in the Western philosophical tradition. In Heraclitus it is the rational principle ordering the cosmos; in Plato and Aristotle it is reasoned discourse and definition; in Stoicism it is the divine fire that pervades and governs all things; in Philo of Alexandria it is the intermediary between the transcendent God and creation; and in the Gospel of John it is the pre-existent Word that "became flesh." The Western Ideas of Reason, Language, and God each catch a fragment; none catches the term's trajectory from pre-Socratic cosmology through Hellenistic theology to Christian incarnation. Because the term is often transliterated directly in philosophical commentary, the passages below are a mix of direct matches (where the Greek term or its transliteration appears) and equivalent matches (where the translator renders it as "word," "account," or "reason").

## Polysemy

1. **Logos as speech and discourse** — The most basic sense: articulate utterance, conversation, storytelling. This sense underlies all the others and provides the metaphor through which more abstract meanings are understood. The logos is what distinguishes human beings from animals: the capacity for reasoned speech.

   > *Plato, Theaetetus, trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1892* — "The first account of the meaning of the word is the reflection of thought in speech — a sort of nominalism 'La science est une langue bien faite.' But anybody who is not dumb can say what he thinks; therefore mere speech cannot be knowledge. And yet we may observe, that there is in this explanation an element of truth which is not recognized by Plato; viz. that truth and thought are inseparable from language, although mere expression in words is not truth." (equivalent match — Jowett translates logos as "account" and "speech"; the passage marks the boundary between logos-as-mere-utterance and logos-as-knowledge)

2. **Logos as rational account and definition** — In Plato's Theaetetus, the term logos becomes the crux of the definition of knowledge: knowledge is true opinion accompanied by logos. The dialogue explores three senses of logos in this context: (a) the expression of thought in speech, (b) the enumeration of elementary parts, and (c) the possession of a distinguishing mark. This is logos as the rational account that elevates true belief into knowledge.

   > *Plato, Theaetetus, trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1892* — "Three attempts to explain the new definition of knowledge still remain to be considered. They all of them turn on the explanation of logos. ... The third is the best explanation — the possession of a characteristic mark, which seems to answer to the logical definition by genus and difference." (direct match — the term logos is transliterated; the passage systematically examines the meaning of logos as it relates to knowledge)

3. **Logos as divine intermediary — Philo and the Gospel of John** — In Hellenistic Judaism, Philo of Alexandria developed a doctrine of the Logos as the intermediary between the transcendent, unknowable God and the created world: the Logos is the divine reason, the pattern of creation, and the agent of revelation. The Gospel of John radicalizes this: the Logos "became flesh" (John 1:14), identifying the divine principle with the historical person of Jesus.

   > *Edward Gibbon, Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire, Vol. II, 1776* — "This is the second logos of Philo, that which acts from the beginning of the world, alone in its kind, creator of the sensible world, formed by God according to the ideal world which he had in himself, and which was the first logos, the first-born of the Deity. The logos taken in this sense, then, was a created being, but, anterior to the creation of the world, near to God, and charged with his revelations to mankind." (direct match — Gibbon transliterates logos and distinguishes Philo's two logoi: the immanent divine reason and the creative-demirugic principle; the passage then connects this to the Johannine Logos)

4. **Logos as cosmic reason — Heraclitus and Stoicism** — For Heraclitus, the logos is the rational principle according to which all things come to pass — a cosmic law that most humans fail to comprehend even though it governs their lives. The Stoics inherited and expanded this: the logos is the active principle (the divine fire, creative reason, Zeus) that pervades matter and orders the universe. This sense has no direct equivalent in the corpus's pre-Socratic fragments (Heraclitus is not yet ingested) or Stoic texts (Marcus Aurelius is present but uses "reason" without transliteration), but it is the foundational sense from which the later theological uses derive.

5. **Logos as logic** — The derived sense: logos becomes the root of "logic" (λογική), the discipline of correct reasoning. In Locke's classification of the sciences, "logica" or "semiotica" is the doctrine of signs — the study of how the mind uses words to communicate ideas.

   > *John Locke, An Essay Concerning Human Understanding, 1690* — "The third branch may be called Σημειωτική, or THE DOCTRINE OF SIGNS; the most usual whereof being words, it is aptly enough termed also Λογική, LOGIC." (direct match — Locke uses the Greek term λογική to derive "logic," grounding the discipline in the logos as word and sign)

## Common misuses

In modern English, "logos" is frequently used as a synonym for "brand identity" or "visual mark" — a commercialized flattening of a term with two and a half millennia of philosophical weight. This usage preserves only the barest trace of the original sense (logos as word/expression) while losing everything that made the term philosophically significant. In theological contexts, "the Logos" is sometimes treated as a proper name for Jesus without awareness of the term's Greek philosophical background, making John 1:1 sound like a claim about nomenclature rather than a metaphysical claim about the rational structure of reality becoming incarnate in history. The term's Greek philosophical sense and its Hebrew wisdom-literature background (Proverbs 8, Wisdom of Solomon) are both essential to understanding what the Johannine Logos does and does not claim.

## Adjacent Terms

- [[Nous]] — Mind or intellect; in Plotinus and later Platonism, nous is the divine mind from which logos proceeds.
- [[Sophia]] — Wisdom; in the Hebrew wisdom tradition (Proverbs 8, Wisdom of Solomon), Wisdom is personified as God's companion in creation — a close parallel to the Logos doctrine in Philo and John.
- [[Rhema]] — Utterance or spoken word; a near-synonym distinguished from logos in some theological contexts (logos as the eternal Word, rhema as the spoken word).

## Heraclitus — Fragments (Burnet, *Early Greek Philosophy*, 3rd ed., 1920)

> *Fragment 1 (Burnet 1, Bywater)* — "It is wise to hearken, not to me, but to my Word, and to confess that all things are one."
>
> *Gloss: The founding move of Greek logos-philosophy. Heraclitus displaces authority from the speaker to the Word itself — the logos is not "my doctrine" but a principle independent of its human vehicle. "All things are one" is the content of the logos: the unity beneath the strife of opposites.*

> *Fragment 2 (Burnet 2, Bywater)* — "Though this Word is true evermore, yet men are as unable to understand it when they hear it for the first time as before they have heard it at all. For, though all things come to pass in accordance with this Word, men seem as if they had no experience of them, when they make trial of words and deeds such as I set forth, dividing each thing according to its nature and showing how it truly is. But other men know not what they are doing when awake, even as they forget what they do in sleep."
>
> *Gloss: The logos is public and universal — "all things come to pass in accordance with this Word" — yet most humans are asleep to it. The epistemological tragedy is that the logos governs their lives without their knowledge. This is not a hidden mystery but a manifest one: the problem is not the logos's obscurity but human inattention. The fragments link directly to the Gospel of John's claim that the Logos "was in the world, and the world knew him not."*

> *Fragment 19 (Burnet 19, Bywater)* — "Wisdom is one thing. It is to know the thought by which all things are steered through all things."
>
> *Gloss: Logos as the cosmic steering-thought — γνώμη (gnōmē, "thought" or "judgment"). Wisdom is not the accumulation of doctrines (cf. fr. 16: "the learning of many things teacheth not understanding") but the recognition of the single rational principle that directs all phenomena. The Stoic logos spermatikos and the Philonic logos as divine reason are direct descendants of this fragment.*

> *Fragment 20 (Burnet 20, Bywater)* — "This world, which is the same for all, no one of gods or men has made; but it was ever, is now, and ever shall be an ever-living Fire, with measures kindling, and measures going out."
>
> *Gloss: Logos as cosmic order, not creator. The cosmos is uncreated and eternal — "no one of gods or men has made" — and the logos is the "measures" (metra) by which fire kindles and extinguishes. This is the counterpoint to the Johannine Logos: for Heraclitus, the logos is immanent in an eternal world; for John, the Logos enters a created one. The conceptual distance between "ever-living Fire" and "the Word became flesh" measures the transformation Greek logos undergoes in its passage through Hellenistic Judaism.*

> *Fragment 65 (Burnet 65, Bywater)* — "The wise is one only. It is unwilling and willing to be called by the name of Zeus."
>
> *Gloss: The logos is theological but not comfortably so. The One Wise Thing can be called Zeus — but only with reservation ("unwilling and willing"). This ambivalence is the seed of later philosophical theology: the logos bears a divine name but exceeds it. The tension between the proper name of a god and the rational principle that cannot be named without remainder marks the boundary between myth and philosophy.*

## The Gospel According to John — King James Version, 1611

> *John 1:1–5* — "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. The same was in the beginning with God. All things were made by him; and without him was not any thing made that was made. In him was life; and the life was the light of men. And the light shineth in darkness; and the darkness comprehended it not."
>
> *Gloss: The Johannine Logos is the Heraclitean-Stoic logos fused with the Hebrew creation-theology of Genesis 1 ("In the beginning") and the wisdom tradition of Proverbs 8. The logos is now personal — a "him" rather than an "it" — and creative rather than merely ordering. But the Heraclitean echo is unmistakable: the light that "shineth in darkness" and that "the darkness comprehended it not" is a direct parallel to fragment 2, where men "know not what they are doing when awake."*

> *John 1:10–14* — "He was in the world, and the world was made by him, and the world knew him not. He came unto his own, and his own received him not. But as many as received him, to them gave he power to become the sons of God, even to them that believe on his name: Which were born, not of blood, nor of the will of the flesh, nor of the will of man, but of God. And the Word was made flesh, and dwelt among us, (and we beheld his glory, the glory as of the only begotten of the Father,) full of grace and truth."
>
> *Gloss: The radical Johannine move — the logos "became flesh" (σὰρξ ἐγένετο). The cosmic rational principle enters material, vulnerable, historical existence. This is not Heraclitean immanence (where fire pervades all things) but incarnation: the logos assumes a specific body, at a specific time, in a specific place. "The world knew him not" echoes Heraclitus fragment 2 almost verbatim — the logos is present and unrecognized. But the response is different: Heraclitus simply reports the fact; John offers sonship to those who receive him.*
