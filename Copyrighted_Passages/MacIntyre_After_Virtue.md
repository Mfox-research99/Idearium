## Key Passages

### 1. The Disquieting Suggestion (Chapter 1)

> "Imagine that the natural sciences were to suffer the effects of a catastrophe... What would happen if a widespread movement of reaction against the natural sciences led to the destruction of laboratories, the burning of books, and the execution of scientists? A later generation, seeking to revive science, would possess only fragments: a knowledge of experiments detached from any knowledge of the theoretical context which gave them significance... In such a culture, men would use expressions like 'neutrino,' 'mass,' and 'atomic weight' in systematic and often interrelated ways which would resemble the ways in which such expressions had been used in earlier times. But many of the beliefs presupposed by such usage would have been lost."

MacIntyre opens with a thought experiment: what if moral discourse in the modern world is in the condition of that imagined post-catastrophe science? We possess fragments of a tradition of moral reasoning — words like "virtue," "duty," "good" — but have lost the frameworks that gave them meaning. We speak the language of morality without inhabiting the form of life that made that language intelligible.

— MacIntyre, *After Virtue*, Chapter 1, University of Notre Dame Press, 1981

### 2. The Failure of the Enlightenment Project (Chapter 5)

> "The project of providing a rational vindication of morality had decisively failed; and from henceforward the morality of our predecessor culture — and subsequently of our own — lacked any public, shared rationale or justification. In a world of secular rationality, religion could no longer provide such a shared background and foundation for moral discourse and action; and the failure of philosophy to provide what religion could no longer furnish was an important cause of philosophy losing its central cultural role and of morality itself becoming subjective and private."

MacIntyre's historical thesis: the Enlightenment attempted to justify morality through reason alone, without teleology, without tradition, without a conception of the human good. Every major attempt — Hume, Kant, Kierkegaard — failed on its own terms. The result was not the abandonment of moral language but its survival as fragments, wielded emotively rather than rationally.

— MacIntyre, *After Virtue*, Chapter 5, University of Notre Dame Press, 1981

### 3. The Threefold Definition of a Practice (Chapter 14)

> "By a 'practice' I mean any coherent and complex form of socially established cooperative human activity through which goods internal to that form of activity are realized in the course of trying to achieve those standards of excellence which are appropriate to, and partially definitive of, that form of activity, with the result that human powers to achieve excellence, and human conceptions of the ends and goods involved, are systematically extended."

This is the constructive core of the book. A practice (chess, architecture, farming, inquiry) has internal goods — achievements that can only be recognized by participants in the practice — and external goods (money, prestige, power) that are only contingently attached. Virtues are those qualities that enable the pursuit of internal goods.

— MacIntyre, *After Virtue*, Chapter 14, University of Notre Dame Press, 1981

### 4. The Narrative Unity of a Human Life (Chapter 15)

> "I can only answer the question 'What am I to do?' if I can answer the prior question 'Of what story or stories do I find myself a part?'"

Against the modern conception of the self as a choosing sovereign, MacIntyre argues that we are always already embedded in narratives — not of our own making. We are born into families, communities, traditions that have histories. To ask what I ought to do is to ask how the story I am in should continue. This is not determinism; it is a recovery of the conditions under which moral deliberation makes sense.

— MacIntyre, *After Virtue*, Chapter 15, University of Notre Dame Press, 1981

### 5. The Concept of a Tradition (Chapter 15)

> "A living tradition is an historically extended, socially embodied argument, and an argument precisely in part about the goods which constitute that tradition."

Tradition, in MacIntyre's hands, is not the dead hand of the past but an ongoing argument — a conversation across generations about what goods are worth pursuing and how. This conception of tradition as a temporally extended debate is one of his most original contributions, dissolving the modern opposition between tradition and reason.

— MacIntyre, *After Virtue*, Chapter 15, University of Notre Dame Press, 1981

### 6. Emotivism (Chapter 2)

> "Emotivism is the doctrine that all evaluative judgments and more specifically all moral judgments are nothing but expressions of preference, expressions of attitude or feeling, insofar as they are moral or evaluative in character."

MacIntyre identifies emotivism — the view that moral statements merely express feelings — as the implicit moral theory of modern culture, even though almost no one explicitly holds it. In a world where moral disagreement seems rationally unresolvable, we default to treating moral claims as preferences, and the characters who populate our moral imagination (the therapist, the manager, the aesthete) embody this default.

— MacIntyre, *After Virtue*, Chapter 2, University of Notre Dame Press, 1981

### 7. Nietzsche or Aristotle (Chapter 9)

> "The choice is not between Aristotle and the Enlightenment; the Enlightenment project has already failed. The real choice is between Nietzsche and Aristotle."

MacIntyre's fork: if the Enlightenment project of justifying morality through reason alone has failed, then we are left with two alternatives. Nietzsche, who accepts the failure and draws the conclusion that morality is will to power. Or Aristotle, who recovers a teleological conception of human nature in which morality is the realization of a specifically human good. The book is an argument for Aristotle over Nietzsche — but it takes Nietzsche's challenge as the most serious one that virtue ethics must answer.

— MacIntyre, *After Virtue*, Chapter 9, University of Notre Dame Press, 1981

### 8. The New Dark Ages (Chapter 18)

> "What matters at this stage is the construction of local forms of community within which civility and the intellectual and moral life can be sustained through the new dark ages which are already upon us... We are waiting not for Godot, but for another — doubtless very different — St. Benedict."

The book closes with a startling image: we are entering a new dark age, and the appropriate response is not to try to reform the larger civilization but to build local communities — new monasteries — where the virtues can be lived and transmitted while the surrounding order collapses. This is not despair but strategic withdrawal, a patient cultivation of what can survive the failure of larger structures.

— MacIntyre, *After Virtue*, Chapter 18, University of Notre Dame Press, 1981
