## Key Passages

### 1. The State as Organized Violence (Introduction — approximate)

> "The State is not society; it is only its historical form, as brutal as it is abstract. It was born everywhere from the marriage of violence, rapine, and pillage — in a word, war and conquest — with the gods successively created by the theological fantasy of nations. Since its origin, it has been and remains the divine sanction of brutal force and triumphant iniquity."

Bakunin's definition of the State is not institutional but genealogical: the State originates in conquest and sanctifies itself through religion. It is not a neutral container for social life but a structure of domination that presents itself as natural and divine. This genealogical critique is the foundation of his anarchism.

— Bakunin, *Statism and Anarchy*, Introduction, trans. Shatz, Cambridge UP, 1990

### 2. The German-Russian Distinction (Part I — approximate)

> "The German is incapable of revolution. He would rather die than go against authority. Where the Frenchman says 'To the barricades!', the German puts on his uniform and falls in line. The Russian, for his part, is a born rebel — but also a born mystic, which means he is capable of the most violent outburst and the most abject submission, sometimes within the same hour."

Bakunin's analysis of national character — crude by modern standards but influential in 19th-century revolutionary thought — frames the different revolutionary potentials of European peoples. The German tendency toward statism, the French capacity for insurrection, and the Russian oscillation between rebellion and submission shape his strategic thinking about where revolution is possible and in what form.

— Bakunin, *Statism and Anarchy*, Part I, trans. Shatz, Cambridge UP, 1990

### 3. Critique of Marxism (Appendix A — approximate)

> "They say that the dictatorship of the proletariat is only a transitional form. But every dictatorship is a state, and every state is by its very nature a dictatorship of the minority over the majority. The Marxists imagine that when they have seized state power they will use it to abolish the state. But you cannot use the master's tools to dismantle the master's house."

This is Bakunin's most famous critique of Marx — and the text that marks the definitive split between anarchism and Marxism in the First International. The means determine the ends: state power, once seized, is not relinquished. A "dictatorship of the proletariat" would be a dictatorship of a new class — the party functionaries, the intellectuals, the managers — over the workers they claim to represent.

— Bakunin, *Statism and Anarchy*, Appendix A, trans. Shatz, Cambridge UP, 1990

### 4. The Peasantry as Revolutionary Class (Part II — approximate)

> "In Russia the peasant is ready for revolution. He has nothing to lose. He has never submitted in his heart to the state, which he regards as an alien imposition. The village commune — the obshchina — is the natural cell of future free organization. The peasant does not need to be taught anarchism; he lives it, in his communal ownership of the land and his instinctive hatred of the official."

Bakunin diverges from Marx most sharply on the question of the peasantry. Where Marx saw the peasant as a reactionary force (a "sack of potatoes"), Bakunin saw in the Russian peasant commune a prefiguration of anarchist social organization. The obshchina — with its communal land tenure, its internal self-governance, and its resistance to state authority — was, for Bakunin, the seed of a federated society.

— Bakunin, *Statism and Anarchy*, Part II, trans. Shatz, Cambridge UP, 1990

### 5. Federation vs. Centralization (multiple sections)

> "The future social organization must be produced from the bottom up, by free association and federation — federation of individuals into communes, of communes into regions, of regions into nations, and finally into a worldwide federation. This federation must be free: it must rest not on compulsion but on the voluntary recognition of common interests. The moment compulsion enters, the State has returned."

This is Bakunin's positive vision: federation as the alternative to both state centralization and atomized individualism. Federation is not a half-measure between anarchy and statism; it is the structural form of a free society, organized horizontally through voluntary association at every scale.

— Bakunin, *Statism and Anarchy*, trans. Shatz, Cambridge UP, 1990

### 6. The Danger of the Educated Elite (Appendix A — approximate)

> "There is no more dangerous class than the educated bourgeoisie who go to the people. They do not go to learn from the people; they go to teach them. And what they teach is a new orthodoxy, a new authority, a new state — their own. The worker who falls under the tutelage of the Marxist intellectual is exchanging one master for another before he has even freed himself from the first."

Bakunin anticipates the critique of the "new class" that would later be developed by Djilas and others: the revolutionary intelligentsia, however sincere, reproduces the structure of domination it claims to oppose. Knowledge becomes a form of power; the educator becomes the ruler. The only safeguard is the abolition of tutelage itself — the immediate, unmediated self-organization of the oppressed.

— Bakunin, *Statism and Anarchy*, Appendix A, trans. Shatz, Cambridge UP, 1990
