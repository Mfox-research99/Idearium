## Key Passages

### Attention as the Highest Form of Love

> "Attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity. The capacity to give one's attention to a sufferer is a very rare and difficult thing; it is almost a miracle; it is a miracle. Nearly all those who think they have this capacity do not possess it. Warmth of heart, impulsiveness, pity are not enough."

— Simone Weil, *Waiting for God*, "Reflections on the Right Use of School Studies," trans. Emma Craufurd, Putnam, 1951.

### On Affliction (*Malheur*)

> "Affliction is an uprooting of life, a more or less attenuated equivalent of death. In affliction, the soul is marked, branded by the violence that is inflicted on it. Affliction makes God appear absent for a time, more absent than a dead man, more absent than light in complete darkness. During this absence there is nothing to love."

— Simone Weil, *Waiting for God*, "The Love of God and Affliction," trans. Emma Craufurd, Putnam, 1951.

### Decreation

> "We participate in the creation of the world by decreasing ourselves. God created me as non-being which has the appearance of existence so that renouncing that apparent existence, I may be annihilated and yet continue to exist in God."

— Simone Weil, *Waiting for God*, trans. Emma Craufurd, Putnam, 1951. [Paraphrase.]

### The Obligation Toward Justice

> "The Gospel makes no distinction between the love of our neighbor and justice. For the Greeks, a distinction was possible. But for us, since the Gospel, there is no such distinction. Justice is a certain kind of attention to our neighbor. It is the supernatural virtue of justice which consists in behaving exactly as though there were equality when one is the stronger in an unequal relationship."

— Simone Weil, *Waiting for God*, "Forms of the Implicit Love of God," trans. Emma Craufurd, Putnam, 1951.

### Friendship as a Form of Love

> "Friendship is a miracle by which a person consents to view from a certain distance, and without coming any nearer, the very being who is necessary to him as food. Friendship has something universal about it. It consists in loving a human being as we should like to be able to love each soul in particular of all those who go to make up the human race."

— Simone Weil, *Waiting for God*, "Forms of the Implicit Love of God," trans. Emma Craufurd, Putnam, 1951.
