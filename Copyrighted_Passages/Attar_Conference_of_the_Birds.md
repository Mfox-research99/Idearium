# 'Aṭṭār — Manṭiq al-Ṭayr (The Conference of the Birds)

## Note: Reconstructed from model knowledge — verify against physical copy

The passages below are reconstructed from training data and represent the most-cited, most influential sections of the Manṭiq al-Ṭayr, particularly those bearing on fanā' and the annihilation of the self in the divine. Citations reference the Darbandi-Davis translation (1984, Penguin Classics) as the standard English edition. The earlier Nott translation (1954) differs considerably in wording and should be checked against the standard edition. All wording should be verified against: *The Conference of the Birds*, trans. Afkham Darbandi and Dick Davis, Penguin Classics, 1984. Pagination is approximate.

The Manṭiq al-Ṭayr is the quintessential Sufi allegory of the soul's journey to God. The birds of the world, led by the hoopoe, set out to find the Simorgh — the mythical king of birds. After crossing seven valleys (Quest, Love, Knowledge, Detachment, Unity, Bewilderment, and Poverty/Annihilation), only thirty birds (sī morgh in Persian — a pun on "Simorgh") reach the goal. What they find is themselves — for the Simorgh is the mirror in which the birds see their own true nature, which is nothing other than the divine.

## Key Passages

### The Hoopoe's Invitation — The Call to the Journey

> "'We have a king; he lives behind the mountains that are called Qaf. His name is Simorgh; he is the king of birds, and he is close to us — but we are far from him. The place where he dwells is inaccessible, and no tongue can pronounce his name. Before him hang a hundred thousand veils of light and darkness, and in both worlds no one can dispute his sovereignty. ... If you wish to set out on this road, you must be prepared to give up your life. Do not think the journey is short: one must have the heart of a lion to follow this strange road — it is very long and very deep. You will walk in blood for years, and when you reach the end, you will lose yourself.'"

— 'Aṭṭār, *The Conference of the Birds*, trans. Darbandi-Davis, Penguin, 1984, pp. 29–32 (adapted)

*Gloss: The hoopoe's call announces the central paradox of the journey: the Simorgh is close ("closer to you than your jugular vein," as the Qur'an says of God) yet separated by a hundred thousand veils. The journey is not a traversal of distance but a stripping away of veils. "You will lose yourself" is the promise and the warning: fanā' is both the condition of arrival and the cost of departure. Every bird who later makes excuses — the nightingale loves the rose, the duck loves water, the owl loves ruins — is offering a reason why the self cannot be surrendered.*

### The Seven Valleys — The Valley of Poverty and Annihilation

> "The seventh valley is the Valley of Poverty and Annihilation. Here, the wayfarer has become nothing, and yet everything. He is neither this nor that, neither here nor there. His tongue cannot speak, his mind cannot conceive — he is drowned in an ocean without shore. If you ask him who he is, he cannot answer, for he is no one. ... When you reach this valley, your being dissolves like a drop of water in the ocean. The drop remains — it has not ceased to exist — but now it is the ocean that is visible, and the drop is no longer seen."

— 'Aṭṭār, *The Conference of the Birds*, trans. Darbandi-Davis (adapted), Penguin, 1984

*Gloss: The seventh valley is fanā' itself — the station where the self is not destroyed but rendered transparent. The simile of the drop and the ocean is the classical Sufi image: the drop does not cease to be water, but it ceases to be distinguishable as "this drop." The paradox — "he has become nothing, and yet everything" — captures the transition from fanā' to baqā': the annihilation of the false self is the condition for the emergence of true being, which is not "his" being but Being itself acting through him.*

### The Moth and the Flame — The Parable of the Lovers

> "One night, moths gathered around a candle. They said: 'We must find someone to tell us about this flame for which we yearn.' One moth went to a distant castle and saw a candle burning there. He returned and described what he had seen — but the wise moth among them said: 'He has not truly seen the flame.' Another moth flew close enough to feel the heat, and returned with scorched wings. The wise moth said: 'He knows more, but still he has not entered.' Then one moth threw himself into the flame, and was consumed. The wise moth said: 'Now he knows. But he cannot return to tell us.'"

— 'Aṭṭār, *The Conference of the Birds*, trans. Darbandi-Davis (adapted), Penguin, 1984

*Gloss: The most famous parable in the Manṭiq al-Ṭayr — the moths and the flame as the stages of approach to fanā'. Three degrees: seeing from a distance (theory), feeling the heat (experience), and total immersion (annihilation). The crucial point is the third moth's silence: the one who has truly entered fanā' cannot report back. Knowledge of fanā' is incommunicable — not because it is esoteric but because there is no self left to communicate. This is why Sufism is "experiences rather than definitions" (Al-Ghazālī).*

### The Thirty Birds Arrive — The Mirror of the Simorgh

> "Then the thirty birds looked, and what they saw was themselves — thirty birds, and nothing more. And yet, when they looked more deeply, they saw the Simorgh, and the Simorgh was the thirty birds, and the thirty birds were the Simorgh. They looked at each other and saw that they were one. When they tried to distinguish the Simorgh from themselves, they could not. The Simorgh said: 'I am the mirror set before your eyes. You see yourselves in Me, and I see Myself in you. Come, annihilate yourselves in Me, that you may find yourselves in Me.'"

— 'Aṭṭār, *The Conference of the Birds*, trans. Darbandi-Davis (adapted), Penguin, 1984

*Gloss: The revelation at the journey's end — the pun that structures the entire poem: sī morgh means "thirty birds," and Sīmorgh is the name of the divine king. The thirty birds who survive the seven valleys discover that what they were seeking is what they already are: the Simorgh is not a separate being but the true nature of the thirty birds collectively. The mirror metaphor connects directly to Ibn 'Arabī: the world is the mirror in which God sees Himself; the birds are the mirror in which the Simorgh sees itself. Fanā' is the recognition that the seeker and the sought were never two — and that recognition is itself the annihilation of the illusion of separation.*

### The Nightingale's Excuse — The Love That Binds

> "'I am so drowned in love for the rose,' replied the nightingale, 'that I can think of nothing else. The rose is my beloved, and I cannot leave her for a journey to find a king I have never seen. For me, the rose is enough.' The hoopoe answered: 'The rose is beautiful, but her beauty lasts only a few days. She blooms, she withers, she falls. You love a veil, not what the veil conceals. The true Rose is eternal, and its beauty does not fade.'"

— 'Aṭṭār, *The Conference of the Birds*, trans. Darbandi-Davis (adapted), Penguin, 1984

*Gloss: The nightingale represents the soul attached to a partial, perishable beloved — the rose that blooms and dies. The hoopoe's response does not deny the beauty of the rose but recontextualizes it: the rose is a veil, a theophany of the divine Beauty it conceals. The nightingale's error is not love but the stopping-short of love — taking the sign for the signified. Fanā' is the refusal to stop at any beloved short of the Beloved who is the source of all belovedness. The nightingale's journey is from love of the rose to love of the Rose — from the particular to the universal, without losing the particular along the way.*
