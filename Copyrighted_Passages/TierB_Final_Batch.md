# Tier B Final Batch — Key Passages

## Robin Wall Kimmerer — *Braiding Sweetgrass* (2013)

### The Grammar of Animacy

> "In English, you are either a human being or an object. We don't have a pronoun to refer to the living world. In Potawatomi, one of the first things you learn is that most of what surrounds you is alive and must be spoken of as a person. The language animates the world. In Potawatomi, the word for 'hill' is a verb — 'to be a hill.' When you say 'hill,' you are saying that the hill is in the process of being a hill, that it has agency."

— Robin Wall Kimmerer, *Braiding Sweetgrass*, Milkweed Editions, 2013. [Paraphrase.]

### The Gift Economy of the Living World

> "Gifts from the earth or from each other establish a particular relationship. A gift is something for nothing, something that brings you into obligation. This is the opposite of a commodity, which asks nothing of you but payment. In a gift economy, wealth is understood as having enough to give away."

— Robin Wall Kimmerer, *Braiding Sweetgrass*, Milkweed Editions, 2013. [Paraphrase.]

### Reciprocal Maintenance

> "We are showered every day with gifts from the land, but they are not meant for us to keep. Their life is in their movement. They are meant to be passed on. The currency of a gift economy is, at its root, the pleasure of giving and the obligation of receiving. The earth gives us berries, and in return, we tend the meadow that feeds the berry bushes. The relationship is one of mutual care, not extraction."

— Robin Wall Kimmerer, *Braiding Sweetgrass*, Milkweed Editions, 2013. [Paraphrase.]

## Vine Deloria Jr. — *God is Red* (1973)

### Spatial vs. Temporal Religion

> "The fundamental difference between Western religion and Native American tradition is that Christianity is a time-based religion concerned with historical events — creation, the Exodus, the incarnation, the eschaton — while Native traditions are space-based, oriented around particular places where the sacred has revealed itself and where it remains present. When a people lose their land, they lose their religion, because their religion is inseparable from the place where it is practiced."

— Vine Deloria Jr., *God is Red*, Grosset & Dunlap, 1973. [Paraphrase of core thesis.]

### Treaty as Covenant

> "Treaties between Indian nations and the United States were not simply contracts; they were covenants, sacred agreements made before God. The United States chose to use the language of covenant, and the Indian nations took them at their word. The breaking of treaties is not merely a legal violation; it is a moral and spiritual violation, a breaking of faith that damages both parties."

— Vine Deloria Jr., *God is Red*, Grosset & Dunlap, 1973. [Paraphrase.]

## Thomas Merton — *New Seeds of Contemplation* (1961)

### The True Self and the False Self

> "Every one of us is shadowed by an illusory person: a false self. This is the man that I want myself to be but who cannot exist, because God does not know anything about him. My false and private self is the one who wants to exist outside the reach of God's will and God's love — outside of reality and outside of life. And such a self cannot help but be an illusion."

— Thomas Merton, *New Seeds of Contemplation*, Chapter 3, New Directions, 1961.

### Contemplation as Pure Attention

> "Contemplation is the highest expression of man's intellectual and spiritual life. It is that life itself, fully awake, fully active, fully aware that it is alive. It is spiritual wonder. It is spontaneous awe at the sacredness of life, of being. It is gratitude for life, for awareness and for being. It is a vivid realization of the fact that life and being in us proceed from an invisible, transcendent and infinitely abundant Source."

— Thomas Merton, *New Seeds of Contemplation*, Chapter 1, New Directions, 1961.

## Emmanuel Levinas — Key Passages

### The Face of the Other

> "The face is exposed, menaced, as if inviting us to an act of violence. At the same time, the face is what forbids us to kill. The face speaks. The face is a living presence; it is expression. The face says: 'You shall not kill.' The infinite in the face is the ethical resistance that paralyzes my powers."

— Emmanuel Levinas, *Totality and Infinity*, trans. Alphonso Lingis, Duquesne University Press, 1969. [Paraphrase of core thesis.]

### Responsibility Preceding Freedom

> "To be oneself is to be for the other. Responsibility for the Other is not an accident that happens to a subject, but precedes in it its essence. I am responsible for the Other without waiting for reciprocity. Responsibility is the essential, primary and fundamental structure of subjectivity. For in ethical terms, I am responsible for the Other without expecting the same in return."

— Emmanuel Levinas, *Ethics and Infinity*, trans. Richard Cohen, Duquesne University Press, 1985. [Paraphrase.]

## Jürgen Habermas — *Theory of Communicative Action* (1981)

### Communicative Rationality

> "In communicative action, participants coordinate their plans of action by achieving understanding about something in the world. Unlike strategic action, where participants try to influence each other's behavior through sanctions or rewards, communicative action is oriented toward reaching shared understanding through the force of the better argument. The structure of language itself presupposes the possibility of undistorted communication."

— Jürgen Habermas, *The Theory of Communicative Action*, trans. Thomas McCarthy, Beacon Press, 1984. [Paraphrase of core thesis.]

## Gregory Bateson — *Steps to an Ecology of Mind* (1972)

### Information as Difference

> "What we mean by information — the elementary unit of information — is a difference which makes a difference. A difference is a very peculiar and obscure concept. It is certainly not a thing or an event. This piece of paper is different from the wood of this lectern. There are many differences between them. But of these differences, only a few will become information — differences that make a difference to some perceiving entity."

— Gregory Bateson, *Steps to an Ecology of Mind*, "Form, Substance, and Difference," 1970 lecture, Chandler, 1972.
