## Key Passages

### 1. Berenger's Refusal (Act III)

> "I'm the last man left, and I'm staying that way! I'm not capitulating!"

Berenger, the everyman protagonist, watches his entire town turn into rhinoceroses — one by one, friends, colleagues, even his beloved Daisy. The transformation is not violent conquest but contagious conformity; people choose to join the herd. Berenger's refusal to capitulate is not heroic in the traditional sense — he is frightened, confused, unattractive — but it is absolute. He is the witness who will not become what he watches.

— Ionesco, *Rhinoceros*, Act III, trans. Derek Prouse, Grove Press, 1960

### 2. The Logic of the Epidemic (Act I — approximate)

> A man transforms into a rhinoceros in the street. The townspeople debate whether it had one horn or two, whether it was African or Asian. They argue about the zoological classification while ignoring the fact that a man has become a beast. The taxonomy of the horror becomes a way of domesticating it.

In the first act, Ionesco establishes the mechanism: conformity spreads not through force but through the collective refusal to name what is happening. The witnesses do not deny the transformation; they classify it, debate it, normalize it. By the time they recognize the epidemic, they are already inside it.

— Ionesco, *Rhinoceros*, Act I, trans. Derek Prouse, Grove Press, 1960

### 3. Jean's Transformation (Act II)

> Berenger visits his friend Jean, who is sick. As they talk, Jean's skin grows greener and thicker, a horn begins to protrude from his forehead, his voice drops to a growl. Berenger watches in horror as his friend becomes a rhinoceros before his eyes. Jean insists he feels fine — better than fine, in fact. The transformation feels like strength.

The most intimate horror of the play: watching someone you love choose to become something monstrous, and insisting while they do it that they have never felt better. This is the witness's ordeal — to see clearly while the one you see no longer recognizes you or your lucidity.

— Ionesco, *Rhinoceros*, Act II, trans. Derek Prouse, Grove Press, 1960

### 4. Daisy's Defection (Act III)

> Daisy, the last human companion, begins to find the rhinoceroses beautiful. Their trumpeting sounds like music to her. "After all, they're natural, they have the right to live," she says. Then she leaves to join them. Berenger is alone — the last human being in a world of beasts.

Daisy represents the seduction of conformity at its most intimate: it is not that she is forced; she is persuaded. The rhinoceroses are not monsters; they are natural. Her defection leaves Berenger as the sole witness — the last man who remembers what it was to be human.

— Ionesco, *Rhinoceros*, Act III, trans. Derek Prouse, Grove Press, 1960

### 5. Berenger's Final Monologue (Act III)

> "Against the whole world, I'll defend myself! I'm the last man, and I'll stay that way to the end! I'm not capitulating!"

The final words of the play. Berenger is alone, surrounded, without hope of reversal. His resistance is not strategic — it cannot achieve anything. It is existential: to be the one who does not become the thing, even when becoming the thing would be easier, more natural, more pleasurable. The witness as the last holdout — not because victory is possible but because capitulation is impossible.

— Ionesco, *Rhinoceros*, Act III, trans. Derek Prouse, Grove Press, 1960

### 6. The Theatre of the Absurd as Witness Form

> The Theatre of the Absurd (Beckett, Ionesco, early Pinter, Genet) is itself a mode of witnessing. It does not argue; it displays. It does not console; it persists. Its characters are not heroes but survivors — people who cannot stop seeing what is in front of them, even when what is in front of them makes no sense. This is the witness stripped of rhetoric, stripped of moralizing, stripped of hope: the witness who reports because reporting is all that remains.

Ionesco, like Beckett, refuses the consolations of meaning while insisting on the act of testimony. The absurd is not an excuse for silence; it is the condition under which testimony acquires its specific dignity — not because it changes anything, but because it refuses to look away.

— Ionesco, *Rhinoceros*, trans. Derek Prouse, Grove Press, 1960 (thematic synthesis)
