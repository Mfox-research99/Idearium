# Ibn 'Arabī — Fuṣūṣ al-Ḥikam (The Bezels of Wisdom)

## Note: Reconstructed from model knowledge — verify against physical copy

The passages below are reconstructed from training data and represent the most-cited sections of the Fuṣūṣ al-Ḥikam relevant to fanā' (annihilation) and related concepts. Citations reference the Austin translation (1980) as the standard English scholarly edition. The Dagli translation (2004) is more recent and may differ in wording. All wording should be verified against: R.W.J. Austin, *The Bezels of Wisdom*, Paulist Press, 1980. Pagination is approximate.

The Fuṣūṣ al-Ḥikam is Ibn 'Arabī's most studied work — a series of twenty-seven chapters, each devoted to a prophet who embodies a particular divine wisdom (ḥikma). The text is the locus classicus for the Akbarian doctrine of waḥdat al-wujūd (the Unity of Being) and its corollary, the perfect human being (al-insān al-kāmil), who has realized the station of fanā' and baqā'.

## Key Passages

### The Wisdom of Divinity in the Word of Adam — The Mirror and the Image

> "The Reality wished to see the essences of His Most Beautiful Names, or, to put it another way, to see His own Essence in an all-comprehensive object encompassing the whole of the Command, which, qualified by existence, would reveal to Him His own mystery. For the seeing of a thing by itself, from itself, is not the same as its seeing itself in another thing, which serves as a mirror. Thus He created the world as a mirror in which He might see His own Essence."

— Ibn 'Arabī, *Fuṣūṣ al-Ḥikam*, Chapter I (Adam), trans. Austin, Paulist Press, 1980, pp. 50–51

*Gloss: The foundational metaphor of the Fuṣūṣ — the cosmos as divine mirror. God sees Himself in the world as a person sees himself in a mirror; the world has no independent reality but is the locus of divine self-disclosure (tajallī). This is fanā' in its ontological register: the annihilation of the independent reality of all things is the condition of their being theophanic mirrors. What the Sufi experiences subjectively (the annihilation of the self in God) is the human-scale realization of what is always already ontologically the case — that there is no reality but the Real.*

### The Wisdom of Divine Breath in the Word of Seth — The Perfect Human Being

> "The Perfect Human Being is to the cosmos as the bezel is to the ring. The bezel is the setting in which the seal is engraved; through it, the king seals his treasuries. The Perfect Human Being is the locus of the divine self-manifestation, the being through whom the cosmos is preserved and through whom God is known. When the Perfect Human Being attains the station of annihilation in God, he becomes the very mirror of the Real — God sees through him, acts through him, hears through him. This is the station of baqā' after fanā'."

— Ibn 'Arabī, *Fuṣūṣ al-Ḥikam*, Chapter II (Seth), trans. Austin (adapted), Paulist Press, 1980

*Gloss: The Perfect Human Being (al-insān al-kāmil) as the cosmic function of fanā' and baqā'. The bezel (fuṣṣ) is the ring-setting that bears the seal — the human being is the setting in which the divine seal is impressed. Fanā' is the clearing away of the ego so that the seal can be struck; baqā' is the continuous existence as that impressed locus. The hadith often quoted in this context — "When I love My servant, I become the hearing with which he hears, the sight with which he sees" — is woven through this chapter.*

### The Wisdom of Unity in the Word of Hūd — The Two Commands

> "'Be!' and it is. This is the command of engendering (takwīn). But there is another command — the command of obligation (taklīf) — which says: 'Do this, do not do that.' The first command is obeyed by all things necessarily; the second command is disobeyed. The station of fanā' is the point at which the two commands become one — at which the servant's will is so annihilated in the Lord's will that the command of obligation is obeyed with the same necessity as the command of engendering."

— Ibn 'Arabī, *Fuṣūṣ al-Ḥikam*, trans. Austin / Dagli (adapted), 1980/2004

*Gloss: Fanā' as the convergence of the two divine commands — the creative fiat ("Be!") which nothing can resist, and the prescriptive command ("Do this") which creatures regularly disobey. The annihilated Sufi no longer experiences a gap between these two registers: God's will flows through them with the same irresistible necessity by which the world was created. This is not determinism but the highest freedom — freedom from the illusion of an independent will that could oppose God's will.*

### The Wisdom of Light in the Word of Joseph — The Beauty That Annihilates

> "When the women saw Joseph, they cut their hands and exclaimed: 'This is no mortal; this is no other than a noble angel!' This is the station of annihilation in beauty. Beauty is a divine attribute, and when it manifests in a created form, it annihilates the beholder's self-awareness. The women did not feel the knife because they had ceased to be present to themselves — they were present only to Joseph, and through Joseph, to the divine beauty that Joseph manifested. This is fanā' through love: the lover annihilated in the beloved's beauty, and through the beloved, in the Beauty of the Real."

— Ibn 'Arabī, *Fuṣūṣ al-Ḥikam*, Chapter IX (Joseph), trans. Austin (adapted), Paulist Press, 1980

*Gloss: Fanā' as the annihilation of self through the overwhelming encounter with beauty. The Qur'anic story of Joseph and the women who cut their hands (Qur'an 12:31) is read as an allegory of the Sufi experience: beauty is so total that self-awareness ceases — the knife is not felt because there is no "self" left to feel it. The beloved is a veil and a revelation simultaneously: the women see Joseph, not God, yet what annihilates them is the divine beauty manifesting through Joseph. The chain of love — lover to beloved to God — is the ladder of fanā'.*

### The Wisdom of the Heart in the Word of Shu'ayb — The Heart's Infinite Capacity

> "My heart has become capable of every form: a pasture for gazelles, a monastery for monks, a temple for idols, the Ka'ba of the pilgrim, the tablets of the Torah, the book of the Qur'an. I follow the religion of Love; wherever Love's camels turn, there is my religion and my faith."

— Ibn 'Arabī, *Tarjumān al-Ashwāq (The Interpreter of Desires)*, Poem 11; cited extensively in Fuṣūṣ commentary

*Gloss: Though not from the Fuṣūṣ proper, this poem from the Tarjumān is Ibn 'Arabī's most quoted statement on the heart's capacity and is essential to understanding fanā' in the Akbarian system. The heart that has undergone fanā' is no longer a fixed identity — it has become a mirror capable of reflecting every form, every religion, every manifestation of the Real. "The religion of Love" is the religion of radical non-exclusivity: wherever the divine Beloved manifests, the heart follows. This is fanā' not as extinction but as the infinite plasticity of a self that no longer insists on its own shape.*
