## Key Passages

### 1. The Eternal Consciousness (Preface)

> "If there were no eternal consciousness in a man, if at the bottom of everything there were only a wild ferment, a power that twisting in dark passions produced everything great and insignificant, if a bottomless void never-satiated lay hidden beneath everything — what would life be then but despair?"

Kierkegaard frames the existential stakes before the analysis even begins. Without an eternal dimension, all striving collapses into meaninglessness — a premise that undergirds the entire investigation of faith.

— Kierkegaard, *Fear and Trembling*, Preface, trans. Hong & Hong, Princeton UP, 1983

### 2. The Knight of Infinite Resignation and the Knight of Faith (Problema I)

> "The knight of infinite resignation does not need Abraham. He makes the movement of infinite resignation, and in that movement he renounces everything. The knight of faith also makes the movement of infinite resignation, but then he makes one more movement — the movement of faith — and by virtue of the absurd he gets everything back."

This is the structural core of the work. The knight of infinite resignation renounces the finite and finds peace in the eternal. The knight of faith renounces the finite, then receives it back — not as a replacement but as the very thing surrendered, now held differently. The distinction is not in what is possessed but in how it is held.

— Kierkegaard, *Fear and Trembling*, Problema I, trans. Hong & Hong, Princeton UP, 1983

### 3. The Teleological Suspension of the Ethical (Problema I)

> "The ethical as such is the universal, and as the universal it applies to everyone, which from another angle means that it applies at all times. It rests immanently in itself, has nothing outside itself that is its telos, but is itself the telos for everything outside itself... If this is the case, then Hegel is right... Faith is precisely the paradox that the single individual as the single individual is higher than the universal."

Kierkegaard introduces what may be his most radical thesis: that the demands of faith can suspend, without abolishing, the demands of the ethical. The single individual stands in an absolute relation to the absolute — a relation that cannot be mediated by the universal. This is not antinomianism; it is the terror of Abraham.

— Kierkegaard, *Fear and Trembling*, Problema I, trans. Hong & Hong, Princeton UP, 1983

### 4. The Trial of Abraham (Problema II)

> "The ethical expression for what Abraham did is that he was willing to murder Isaac; the religious expression is that he was willing to sacrifice Isaac. But in this contradiction lies the anxiety that can make a person sleepless, and yet without this anxiety Abraham is not the one he is."

The distinction between murder and sacrifice is not one of outcome — in both cases Isaac dies — but of category. Abraham acts within a religious category that the ethical cannot recognize, and the anxiety of this unrecognizability is constitutive of his trial.

— Kierkegaard, *Fear and Trembling*, Problema II, trans. Hong & Hong, Princeton UP, 1983

### 5. The Knight of Faith in Ordinary Life (Problema III)

> "I candidly admit that in my experience I have not found any authentic example, although I do not therefore deny that every second person may be such an example. I have searched in vain for years... He looks just like a tax collector."

This is Kierkegaard's most vivid image: the knight of faith is indistinguishable from anyone else. He walks home, eats dinner, tends his business. No visible heroism, no dramatic renunciation. He has absorbed the infinite into the finite so completely that the two are indistinguishable from the outside — and this is the highest achievement.

— Kierkegaard, *Fear and Trembling*, Problema III, trans. Hong & Hong, Princeton UP, 1983

### 6. Passion and Faith (Epilogue)

> "Faith is the highest passion in a human being. There are perhaps many in every generation who do not come to faith, but no one goes further."

Faith is not a station one passes through en route to something higher — it is the destination. Kierkegaard's anti-Hegelian thrust: there is no dialectical supersession of faith, no Aufhebung into pure concept. The passion of faith is terminal and sufficient.

— Kierkegaard, *Fear and Trembling*, Epilogue, trans. Hong & Hong, Princeton UP, 1983

### 7. The Paradox (Problema I)

> "The paradox of faith, then, is this: that the single individual is higher than the universal, that the single individual determines his relation to the universal through his relation to the absolute, not his relation to the absolute through his relation to the universal."

A restatement of the central paradox. The individual does not reach God through the ethical; the individual stands before God and then — only then — relates to the ethical from that prior relation. This inverts the Hegelian order entirely.

— Kierkegaard, *Fear and Trembling*, Problema I, trans. Hong & Hong, Princeton UP, 1983

### 8. Temporality and Finitude (Problema III)

> "Temporality, finitude — that is what it is all about. I can resign everything by my own strength and find peace and rest in the pain. I can push against the possibility of the absurd with everything in me. But I cannot by my own strength get the least little thing that belongs to finitude back."

The movement of faith is not purely interior. It concerns real finite goods — Isaac, the beloved, the kingdom — not merely spiritual equanimity. The scandal of faith is that it claims the return of precisely what was surrendered.

— Kierkegaard, *Fear and Trembling*, Problema III, trans. Hong & Hong, Princeton UP, 1983
