## Key Passages

### 1. Who Controls the Past Controls the Future (Part I, Chapter 3)

> "Who controls the past controls the future: who controls the present controls the past."

This is the Party's slogan of historical mutability. If the past can be rewritten at will — if records can be altered, memories destroyed, facts unmade — then the Party can claim any narrative and make it true. There is no external reality against which the Party's version can be checked, because the Party controls all access to reality. This passage distills totalitarianism's deepest epistemological ambition.

— Orwell, *Nineteen Eighty-Four*, Part I, Chapter 3, Secker & Warburg, 1949

### 2. Doublethink (Part I, Chapter 3)

> "To know and not to know, to be conscious of complete truthfulness while telling carefully constructed lies, to hold simultaneously two opinions which cancelled out, knowing them to be contradictory and believing in both of them, to use logic against logic, to repudiate morality while laying claim to it... that is doublethink."

Doublethink is not mere lying. The liar knows the truth and conceals it. The doublethinker has abolished the distinction between truth and falsehood within his own mind. He is not deceiving others while secretly knowing the truth — he has learned to believe the Party's version even while knowing it contradicts what he just erased. This is a cognitive achievement of a terrifying kind.

— Orwell, *Nineteen Eighty-Four*, Part I, Chapter 3, Secker & Warburg, 1949

### 3. Newspeak (Appendix)

> "The purpose of Newspeak was not only to provide a medium of expression for the world-view and mental habits proper to the devotees of Ingsoc, but to make all other modes of thought impossible. It was intended that when Newspeak had been adopted once and for all and Oldspeak forgotten, a heretical thought — that is, a thought diverging from the principles of Ingsoc — should be literally unthinkable, at least so far as thought is dependent on words."

The Appendix on Newspeak is one of Orwell's most penetrating insights: language is not merely a tool for expressing thought; it sets the boundaries of thinkable thought. By eliminating words like "freedom" or "justice," the Party does not merely suppress dissent — it makes dissent inconceivable. The final victory of totalitarianism is not the silencing of heresy but its impossibility.

— Orwell, *Nineteen Eighty-Four*, Appendix, Secker & Warburg, 1949

### 4. The Future Is a Boot Stamping on a Human Face (Part III, Chapter 3)

> "If you want a picture of the future, imagine a boot stamping on a human face — forever."

O'Brien delivers this image to Winston in the Ministry of Love. The key word is "forever." Totalitarianism is not a temporary expedient, not a transition to utopia — it is the permanent condition. The boot never lifts. The face never heals. There is no dialectical resolution, no redemption at the end of history. This is the horror that distinguishes *1984* from the progressive tyrannies of the left.

— Orwell, *Nineteen Eighty-Four*, Part III, Chapter 3, Secker & Warburg, 1949

### 5. 2 + 2 = 5 (Part III, Chapter 3)

> "In the end the Party would announce that two and two made five, and you would have to believe it. It was inevitable that they should make that claim sooner or later: the logic of their position demanded it. Not merely the validity of experience, but the very existence of external reality, was tacitly denied by their philosophy."

The final horror of Room 101 is not physical pain but the destruction of the capacity to trust one's own perception. When Winston is made to see five fingers when O'Brien holds up four, the victory is complete: reality is whatever the Party says it is, and the individual mind has no independent access to truth.

— Orwell, *Nineteen Eighty-Four*, Part III, Chapter 3, Secker & Warburg, 1949

### 6. Freedom Is the Freedom to Say Two Plus Two Makes Four (Part I, Chapter 7)

> "Freedom is the freedom to say that two plus two make four. If that is granted, all else follows."

Orwell's minimal definition of freedom: the right to state an obvious truth without fear. This is not freedom as self-government or public participation — it is something more fundamental. Before politics, before citizenship, before any collective project, there is the solitary individual standing before a fact and being permitted to name it.

— Orwell, *Nineteen Eighty-Four*, Part I, Chapter 7, Secker & Warburg, 1949

### 7. The Proles (Part I, Chapter 7)

> "If there was hope, it must lie in the proles."

Winston's desperate conviction — that the working classes, excluded from the Party's ideological machinery, retain some primal sanity — is the book's only gesture toward redemption. But it is never vindicated. The proles are not organized, not conscious, not agents. They are bodies, and the Party leaves them to their bodies while it colonizes minds. Hope lies in them only if one imagines a future the book never provides.

— Orwell, *Nineteen Eighty-Four*, Part I, Chapter 7, Secker & Warburg, 1949

### 8. He Loved Big Brother (Part III, Chapter 6)

> "He had won the victory over himself. He loved Big Brother."

The novel's final sentence. Winston is not executed; he is broken. He becomes a true believer. The victory of the Party is not the removal of the enemy but the conversion of the enemy into a friend — the elimination of the very possibility of opposition. This is totalitarianism's completed form: the victim who loves the executioner, who cannot remember ever having not loved him.

— Orwell, *Nineteen Eighty-Four*, Part III, Chapter 6, Secker & Warburg, 1949
