# Combined Tier B — hooks, Fromm, Marcuse, MLK

## bell hooks — *All About Love*

### Love as Practice and Discipline

> "Love is an act of will — namely, both an intention and an action. Will also implies choice. We do not have to love. We choose to love. To begin by always thinking of love as an action rather than a feeling is one way in which anyone using the word in this manner automatically assumes accountability and responsibility."

— bell hooks, *All About Love*, Chapter 1, William Morrow, 2000.

### Love and Justice

> "There can be no love without justice. Until we live in a culture that not only respects but also upholds basic civil rights for children, most children will not know love. There can be no love without justice. Abuse and neglect negate love. Care and affirmation, the opposite of abuse and humiliation, are the foundation of love. No one can rightfully claim to be loving when behaving abusively."

— bell hooks, *All About Love*, Chapter 3, William Morrow, 2000.

### The Margin as a Space of Radical Openness

> "Marginality is much more than a site of deprivation. It is also a site of radical possibility, a space of resistance. I am located in the margin. I make a definite distinction between that marginality which is imposed by oppressive structures and that marginality which one chooses as site of resistance — as location of radical openness and possibility."

— bell hooks, *Feminist Theory: From Margin to Center*, 1984. [Companion text.]

## Erich Fromm — *The Art of Loving*

### Love as an Art

> "Love is an art, just as living is an art. If we want to learn how to love, we must proceed in the same way we would if we wanted to learn any other art — say music, painting, carpentry, or the art of medicine or engineering. The process of learning an art can be divided conveniently into two parts: one, the mastery of the theory; the other, the mastery of the practice."

— Erich Fromm, *The Art of Loving*, Chapter 1, Harper & Row, 1956.

### The Practice of Love

> "Love is not primarily a relationship to a specific person; it is an attitude, an orientation of character which determines the relatedness of a person to the world as a whole, not toward one 'object' of love. If a person loves only one other person and is indifferent to the rest of his fellow men, his love is not love but a symbiotic attachment, or an enlarged egotism."

— Erich Fromm, *The Art of Loving*, Chapter 2, Harper & Row, 1956.

### Having vs. Being in Love

> "In the having mode of existence, love means to possess, to control. In the being mode, love is an activity — the activity of giving, not in the sense of sacrifice, but in the sense of the expression of aliveness. Love is a productive activity. It implies care, respect, responsibility, and knowledge."

— Erich Fromm, *To Have or To Be*, 1976. [Companion text — paraphrase.]

## Herbert Marcuse — *Eros and Civilization*

### Non-Repressive Civilization

> "The concept of a non-repressive civilization must be derived from the reality principle itself, not from a transcendent idea. Freud's own theory provides the elements for such a derivation. To make these elements visible, the hidden trend in psychoanalysis must be reconstructed: its socio-historical substance must be extracted from the biological context in which Freud left it."

— Herbert Marcuse, *Eros and Civilization*, Introduction, Beacon Press, 1955.

### The Performance Principle

> "Under the performance principle, the body and the mind are made into instruments of alienated labor. The libido is concentrated in one part of the body, leaving most of the body free for use as an instrument of labor. The temporal reduction of the libido is thus supplemented by its spatial reduction. The entire body becomes an instrument of performance."

— Herbert Marcuse, *Eros and Civilization*, Chapter 2, Beacon Press, 1955. [Paraphrase.]

### Surplus Repression

> "Freud's concept of repression is undifferentiated. I distinguish between basic repression — the modifications of the instincts necessary for the perpetuation of the human race in civilization — and surplus repression: the restrictions necessitated by social domination. The distinction is essential for understanding how civilization generates the very discontent that Freud described."

— Herbert Marcuse, *Eros and Civilization*, Chapter 1, Beacon Press, 1955. [Paraphrase.]

## Martin Luther King Jr. — *Letter from Birmingham Jail*

### Injustice Anywhere

> "Injustice anywhere is a threat to justice everywhere. We are caught in an inescapable network of mutuality, tied in a single garment of destiny. Whatever affects one directly, affects all indirectly."

— Martin Luther King Jr., "Letter from Birmingham Jail," April 16, 1963.

### The Tension of Nonviolent Direct Action

> "Nonviolent direct action seeks to create such a crisis and foster such a tension that a community which has constantly refused to negotiate is forced to confront the issue. It seeks so to dramatize the issue that it can no longer be ignored. I have earnestly opposed violent tension, but there is a type of constructive, nonviolent tension which is necessary for growth."

— Martin Luther King Jr., "Letter from Birmingham Jail," April 16, 1963.

### The White Moderate

> "I have almost reached the regrettable conclusion that the Negro's great stumbling block in his stride toward freedom is not the White Citizen's Counciler or the Ku Klux Klanner, but the white moderate, who is more devoted to 'order' than to justice; who prefers a negative peace which is the absence of tension to a positive peace which is the presence of justice."

— Martin Luther King Jr., "Letter from Birmingham Jail," April 16, 1963.

### Just and Unjust Laws

> "A just law is a man-made code that squares with the moral law or the law of God. An unjust law is a code that is out of harmony with the moral law. Any law that uplifts human personality is just. Any law that degrades human personality is unjust. All segregation statutes are unjust because segregation distorts the soul and damages the personality."

— Martin Luther King Jr., "Letter from Birmingham Jail," April 16, 1963.
