## Key Passages

### 1. The Opening (The Stranger, Part I, Chapter 1)

> "Maman died today. Or yesterday maybe, I don't know. I got a telegram from the home: 'Mother deceased. Funeral tomorrow. Faithfully yours.' That doesn't mean anything. Maybe it was yesterday."

Meursault's voice is the voice of radical detachment — a man who observes his own life from the outside, who reports the facts without the expected emotional accompaniment. He is a witness to his own experience, but a witness who cannot or will not integrate observation into a coherent moral self. This is witnessing at its most estranged: accurate, affectless, and ultimately unsustainable.

— Camus, *The Stranger*, Part I, Chapter 1, trans. Matthew Ward, Vintage, 1988

### 2. The Trial (The Stranger, Part II, Chapter 4)

> "He stated that I had no place in a society whose most fundamental rules I ignored, and that I could not appeal to the same human heart whose elementary response I knew nothing of. 'After all,' he said, turning toward me, 'he has said nothing to defend himself. Is it because he has nothing to say? I ask you to note his silence.'"

Meursault is condemned not for killing a man but for failing to perform the expected emotions — for not weeping at his mother's funeral, for not showing remorse in the expected way. The society that judges him cannot process a witness who refuses to participate in the emotional theater of moral life. His crime is not murder but honesty of the wrong kind.

— Camus, *The Stranger*, Part II, Chapter 4, trans. Matthew Ward, Vintage, 1988

### 3. The Absurd Revelation (The Myth of Sisyphus)

> "A world that can be explained even with bad reasons is a familiar world. But, on the other hand, in a universe suddenly divested of illusions and lights, man feels an alien, a stranger. His exile is without remedy since he is deprived of the memory of a lost home or the hope of a promised land. This divorce between man and his life, the actor and his setting, is properly the feeling of absurdity."

The absurd is not a property of the world or of the mind but of their collision — the meeting of the human demand for meaning and the universe's silent refusal to provide it. The witness who perceives this divorce without flinching is the absurd hero: one who sees clearly and persists.

— Camus, *The Myth of Sisyphus*, trans. Justin O'Brien, Vintage, 1955

### 4. Sisyphus at the Foot of the Mountain (The Myth of Sisyphus)

> "I leave Sisyphus at the foot of the mountain. One always finds one's burden again. But Sisyphus teaches the higher fidelity that negates the gods and raises rocks. He too concludes that all is well. This universe henceforth without a master seems to him neither sterile nor futile... The struggle itself toward the heights is enough to fill a man's heart. One must imagine Sisyphus happy."

The final image: the man who knows his labor is endless and futile, and yet — because he knows it — transcends it. Sisyphus is the witness to his own condition, and in the act of witnessing, he achieves a kind of dignity. Not hope, not meaning, not redemption — but lucidity. The absurd hero is the one who sees the rock, the hill, the eternal return, and chooses to push anyway.

— Camus, *The Myth of Sisyphus*, trans. Justin O'Brien, Vintage, 1955

### 5. Meursault's Final Illumination (The Stranger, Part II, Chapter 5)

> "As if that blind rage had washed me clean, rid me of hope; for the first time, in that night alive with signs and stars, I opened myself to the gentle indifference of the world. Finding it so much like myself — so like a brother, really — I felt that I had been happy and that I was happy again."

In his final moments, Meursault achieves what he could not achieve in life: integration. He accepts the world's indifference not as a wound but as a kinship. He is no longer the alien observer of his own existence; he is at home in the indifference. This is witnessing that finally becomes participation — not through belief but through acceptance of the terms.

— Camus, *The Stranger*, Part II, Chapter 5, trans. Matthew Ward, Vintage, 1988

### 6. The Absurd and Suicide (The Myth of Sisyphus)

> "There is but one truly serious philosophical problem, and that is suicide. Judging whether life is or is not worth living amounts to answering the fundamental question of philosophy. The rest — whether or not the world has three dimensions, whether the mind has nine or twelve categories — comes afterwards."

Camus opens The Myth of Sisyphus with this stark framing: the question of meaning is not academic; it is existential. The witness who perceives absurdity must decide whether to continue. Sisyphus does. Meursault, in his own way, does. This is the caesura that separates Camus from the nihilists: the perception of meaninglessness does not license surrender.

— Camus, *The Myth of Sisyphus*, trans. Justin O'Brien, Vintage, 1955
