## Key Passages

### 1. The Closing Exchange (Waiting for Godot, Act II)

> "VLADIMIR: Well? Shall we go?
> ESTRAGON: Yes, let's go.
> *They do not move.*"

The play's most famous stage direction. After pages of talk about leaving, about hope, about despair, about Godot who may or may not come — Vladimir and Estragon remain exactly where they are. They witness their own paralysis with perfect lucidity and perfect inaction. This is the absurd witness in its comic register: the gap between intention and act is not hidden but displayed, again and again, without resolution.

— Beckett, *Waiting for Godot*, Act II, Grove Press, 1954

### 2. I Can't Go On, I'll Go On (The Unnamable)

> "You must go on. I can't go on. I'll go on."

Three sentences that contain the entire Beckettian condition. The first acknowledges an imperative with no identifiable source. The second states the impossibility of compliance. The third — identical to the first but now freighted with the second — makes the choice to persist. This is not hope; it is not resolution; it is not meaning. It is the minimal act of a witness who sees no reason to continue and continues anyway. The gap between "I can't" and "I'll" is the space where Beckett's entire work lives.

— Beckett, *The Unnamable*, Grove Press, 1958

### 3. The Witness to the Void (The Unnamable)

> "Where now? Who now? When now? Unquestioning. I, say I. Unbelieving. Questions, hypotheses, call them that. Keep going, going on, call that going, call that on."

The voice in The Unnamable is pure witnessing — a consciousness stripped of body, of place, of identity, of time, but still producing language. It is the witness reduced to its minimal condition: not a person observing events but a voice observing its own production of voice. What remains when everything else is stripped away is not silence but testimony — testimony to what, exactly, is unclear.

— Beckett, *The Unnamable*, Grove Press, 1958

### 4. Pozzo on Time (Waiting for Godot, Act II)

> "Have you not done tormenting me with your accursed time? It's abominable. When! When! One day, is that not enough for you, one day like any other day, one day he went dumb, one day I went blind, one day we'll go deaf, one day we were born, one day we'll die, the same day, the same second, is that not enough for you? They give birth astride of a grave, the light gleams an instant, then it's night once more."

Pozzo's eruption is Beckett's most direct statement on human temporality. Birth and death collapse into a single moment; the interval is too brief to be called a life in any meaningful sense. Yet within that interval, the witness operates — noticing the light, feeling the darkness coming, and speaking both facts into the void.

— Beckett, *Waiting for Godot*, Act II, Grove Press, 1954

### 5. Hamm on Witnessing (Endgame)

> "HAMM: Have you not had enough?
> CLOV: Yes! (*Pause.*) Of what?
> HAMM: Of this... this... thing.
> CLOV: I always had. (*Pause.*) Not you?
> HAMM: (*gloomily*) Then there's no reason for it to change."

In Endgame, the characters inhabit a world already ended — a post-catastrophe shelter where nothing remains outside. Hamm and Clov witness the aftermath, not as observers of events but as survivors of an event that has already concluded. The question "Have you had enough?" presupposes that endurance has no purpose — and receives the answer that purpose was never on offer. They remain because leaving is no more meaningful than staying.

— Beckett, *Endgame*, Grove Press, 1958

### 6. The Diminishing Witness (Malone Dies)

> "Live and invent. I have tried. I must have tried. Invent. It is not the word. Neither is live. No matter. I have tried."

The witness in Malone Dies is not the confident observer but the failing one — memory collapsing, language thinning, the self dissolving even as it continues to narrate its own dissolution. This is witnessing at the edge of coherence: the voice that reports its own disappearance, watching itself go.

— Beckett, *Malone Dies*, Grove Press, 1956

## Status Notes

- All texts under copyright (Grove Press)
- Beckett's characters are cautionary witnesses: they see clearly, persist absurdly, but lack the integration into action and care that the Fair Witness (Heinlein) represents
- The Beckettian strand complements Camus's absurd witness and Ionesco's witness to conformity
- "I can't go on, I'll go on" is the anti-heroic motto of the witness who persists without illusions
- For the [[Witness]] Idea page: Beckett gives us the witness reduced to its minimal condition — voice without self, testimony without event, persistence without hope
