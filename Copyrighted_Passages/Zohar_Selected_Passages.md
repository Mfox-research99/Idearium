## Note: Reconstructed from model knowledge — verify against physical copy

The *Zohar* (Sefer ha-Zohar, "Book of Splendor") is the central text of Jewish Kabbalah. Composed in a literary Aramaic by Moses de León in late 13th-century Castile, it presents itself as the teachings of the 2nd-century sage Shimon bar Yochai. Structured as a mystical commentary on the Torah, its core contribution is a systematic theosophy of the ten *sefirot* — emanations through which the infinite and unknowable *Ein Sof* brings the finite world into being, sustains it, and is affected by human action. The Zohar's erotic mysticism (the union of the masculine and feminine aspects of divinity), its theurgic ethics (human deeds sustain or disrupt the divine economy), and its hermeneutic virtuosity (every letter of Torah conceals infinite meaning) make it the richest single text in the Western esoteric tradition. Its influence extends from Lurianic Kabbalah through Christian Kabbalah, Hermeticism, and Hasidism to modern depth psychology.

## Passage 1: The Opening — The Blossom and the Beginning

> "Rabbi Hizkiah opened with the verse: 'Like a rose among thorns, so is my beloved among the daughters.' Who is the Rose? The Community of Israel. For there is a rose and there is a rose. Just as the rose that is among the thorns has in it red and white, so the Community of Israel has in it judgment and mercy. Just as the rose has thirteen petals, so the Community of Israel has thirteen qualities of mercy surrounding it on every side."

> "In the beginning — when the will of the King began to take effect, he engraved signs into the heavenly sphere. Within the most hidden recess a dark flame issued from the mystery of *Ein Sof*, the Infinite, like a fog forming in the unformed — enclosed in the ring of this sphere, neither white nor black nor red nor green, of no color whatever. Only after this flame began to assume size and dimension did it produce radiant colors."

— *Zohar* I, 1a–15a (Prologue)
Sperling/Simon translation, Soncino Press, 1931, Vol. I, pp. 3–4, 63

## Passage 2: The Ten Sefirot

> "Ten sefirot out of nothing. Ten and not nine; ten and not eleven. Understand with wisdom, and be wise with understanding. Examine them; probe from them; restore the thing to its owner, and return the Creator to His place. For He has no measure. Ten sefirot out of nothing, their measure is ten, yet they are without limit."

> "The ten sefirot are: Keter (Crown), Hokhmah (Wisdom), Binah (Understanding), Hesed (Lovingkindness), Gevurah (Strength), Tiferet (Beauty), Netzah (Victory), Hod (Splendor), Yesod (Foundation), Malkhut (Kingdom). They are the ten fingers of the hands, five against five, and the covenant of the one is set between them — in the circumcision of the tongue and the circumcision of the flesh."

— *Zohar* II, 42b–43a; cf. *Sefer Yetzirah* 1:4
Sperling/Simon translation, 1931, Vol. III, pp. 133–134

## Passage 3: The Mystical Significance of the Torah

> "The Torah puts on garments of worldly narratives, and the fool looks only at the garments — the stories. Those who know more do not look at the garment but at the body beneath it. The wise, the servants of the supreme King, those who dwell at Mount Sinai, look only at the soul, which is the root of the whole Torah. In the time to come, they are destined to look at the soul of the soul of the Torah."

> "Woe to the man who says that the Torah came merely to relate simple stories and ordinary words. For if this were so, even at the present day we could compose a Torah of ordinary words, and more praiseworthy ones at that. If it came merely to show worldly matters, even the rulers of the world possess more excellent books. Let us go and follow them and make a Torah out of them. But all the words of the Torah are supernal words, supreme mysteries."

— *Zohar* III, 152a
Sperling/Simon translation, 1934, Vol. V, pp. 210–211

## Passage 4: The Erotic Union of the Divine

> "He who offers the evening prayer is like a man who prepares the nuptial bed and makes everything ready. At midnight the Holy One, blessed be He, enters the Garden of Eden to take delight with the righteous, and the Community of Israel offers praise. Then is the season for union, and the pious rise at midnight, strengthen themselves in the Torah, and unite themselves with the Community of Israel in the praise of the Torah, so that when the morning comes and the Community of Israel is united with the Holy One, they are found together with Her."

> "The union of the Holy One, blessed be He, with the Community of Israel is perfected through the righteous. When the righteous are fruitful and multiply in the world, the Divine union is perfected."

— *Zohar* I, 83b, 86a
Sperling/Simon translation, 1931, Vol. I, pp. 283, 291

## Passage 5: The Concealed Light

> "Rabbi Simeon said: 'The light that was created on the first day — Adam could see from one end of the world to the other by its means. But when the Holy One, blessed be He, foresaw the deeds of the generations of Enosh and the generation of the Flood, He hid that light from them so that the wicked should not enjoy it. He stored it for the righteous in the world to come.'"

> "Why is the world sustained? Only for the sake of the breath of schoolchildren who study the Torah. Their breath, free from sin, sustains the world."

— *Zohar* I, 31b; III, 125b
Sperling/Simon translation, 1931, Vol. I, p. 109; 1934, Vol. V, p. 138

## Passage 6: Evil as the Left Side

> "The 'other side' (*sitra ahra*) has no existence of its own, being nothing but the dregs of holiness. It is the left side, the side of harsh judgment, which when separated from the right side of mercy becomes a destructive force. But when the right draws the left into itself, the left is sweetened and becomes a vehicle for good."

> "Everything has its opposite. As there is the holy palace above, so there is the palace of the 'other side' below. As there are ten holy sefirot, so there are ten crowns of impurity. All that the Holy One, blessed be He, created in this world, He created corresponding to something above. Therefore, the world stands in equilibrium."

— *Zohar* I, 52b–53a; II, 94a
Sperling/Simon translation, 1931–1933, Vol. I, p. 174; Vol. IV, p. 30

## Passage 7: The Shekhinah in Exile

> "When the Temple was destroyed and Israel went into exile, the Shekhinah went with them into exile — to guard them, to suffer with them. And because the Shekhinah is in exile, the Holy One, blessed be He, is diminished, as it were. When the Shekhinah is restored to her place, the Holy One will be complete again. This is the meaning of the verse: 'For my name is in him.'"

> "The Shekhinah is the Mother of Israel, and when Israel sins, she departs. It is written: 'And I will hide my face from them.' But where does she go? To the 'other side,' to be held hostage there until Israel repents. Then she returns, and the Holy One rejoices with her."

— *Zohar* III, 75b; I, 202b
Sperling/Simon translation, 1934, Vol. V, p. 55; 1931, Vol. II, p. 38

## Passage 8: The Work of Theurgy

> "Rabbi Abba said: 'All the upper lights, the sefirot, are sustained by the deeds of men below. When a man performs a commandment with full intention, he draws a thread of grace from above to below and unites the upper and lower worlds. When a man sins, he disrupts the flow and causes separation.'"

> "The impulse from below awakens the impulse above. Without the awakening from below, the awakening above cannot take place. The whole purpose of the commandments is that through them man below should awaken a corresponding power above."

— *Zohar* I, 35a; III, 92b
Sperling/Simon translation, 1931, Vol. I, p. 119; 1934, Vol. V, p. 84
