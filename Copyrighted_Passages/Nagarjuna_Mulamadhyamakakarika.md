# Nāgārjuna — Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way)

## Key Passages

### The Dedication Verse — The Eight Negations

> "I prostrate to the Perfect Buddha, the best of teachers, who taught that whatever is dependently arisen is unceasing, unborn, unannihilated, not permanent, not coming, not going, without distinction, without identity, and free from conceptual construction."

— Nāgārjuna, *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā*, Dedication Verse, trans. Garfield, Oxford, 1995, p. 2

*Gloss: The dedication verse is a compressed statement of the entire Madhyamaka project. The eight negations — not arising, not ceasing, not annihilated, not permanent, not coming, not going, not different, not identical — are not metaphysical assertions but the removal of all metaphysical assertions. The Buddha is praised not for revealing a truth but for revealing that the nature of dependently arisen phenomena cannot be captured by any conceptual predicate.*

### Chapter 24, Verse 18 — The Identity of Emptiness and Dependent Arising

> "Whatever is dependently co-arisen, that is explained to be emptiness. That, being a dependent designation, is itself the middle way."

— Nāgārjuna, *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā*, XXIV.18, trans. Garfield, Oxford, 1995, p. 304

*Gloss: The most cited verse in the entire MMK — the identification of śūnyatā with pratītyasamutpāda (dependent arising). Emptiness is not a substance or a realm beyond phenomena; it is the fact that phenomena arise dependently. "Dependent designation" (prajñaptir upādāya) is the crucial third term: emptiness itself is dependently designated — it is not a thing. This is Nāgārjuna's refutation of both nihilism (emptiness does not mean non-existence) and eternalism (emptiness does not mean an underlying essence).*

### Chapter 24, Verse 19 — No Dependently Arisen Thing Without Emptiness

> "Something that is not dependently arisen — such a thing does not exist. Therefore a non-empty thing does not exist."

— Nāgārjuna, *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā*, XXIV.19, trans. Garfield, Oxford, 1995, p. 304

*Gloss: The corollary of verse 18, stated with uncompromising universality. Because everything that exists arises in dependence on causes and conditions, everything is empty. There is no exception. The phrase "non-empty thing does not exist" (nāsti aśūnyaḥ bhāvaḥ) is the Madhyamaka equivalent of a proof — not a proof that nothing exists, but that nothing exists in the way we habitually take things to exist (as independent, self-sufficient entities).*

### Chapter 13, Verse 8 — The Emptiness of Emptiness

> "The Victorious Ones have announced that emptiness is the relinquishing of all views. Those who take emptiness as a view are said to be incurable."

— Nāgārjuna, *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā*, XIII.8, trans. Garfield, Oxford, 1995, p. 212

*Gloss: The most radical move in the MMK — śūnyatā itself is empty. It is not a metaphysical theory, not a "view" (dṛṣṭi) to be adopted, not a hidden essence behind appearances. The danger is that śūnyatā becomes a new object of attachment: the person who clings to emptiness as a doctrine is "incurable" (asādhya) because they have mistaken the medicine for a new disease. This verse marks the boundary between Madhyamaka and all forms of substantialist philosophy, including some forms of Yogācāra that reify the "storehouse consciousness."*

### Chapter 25, Verse 19-20 — Saṃsāra and Nirvāṇa

> "There is not the slightest difference between saṃsāra and nirvāṇa. There is not the slightest difference between nirvāṇa and saṃsāra. Whatever is the limit of nirvāṇa, that is the limit of saṃsāra. There is not even the slightest difference between them, not even the subtlest thing."

— Nāgārjuna, *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā*, XXV.19–20, trans. Garfield, Oxford, 1995, pp. 331–332

*Gloss: The identity of saṃsāra and nirvāṇa — the most counterintuitive claim in the MMK and the summit of Nāgārjuna's śūnyatā analysis. If all phenomena are empty, then the distinction between the realm of suffering (saṃsāra) and the realm of liberation (nirvāṇa) cannot be a distinction between two different orders of reality. Nirvāṇa is saṃsāra correctly seen — as empty, dependently arisen, without intrinsic nature. This is not a denial of liberation but a redefinition: liberation is not departure from the world but the cessation of grasping at the world as substantially real.*

### Chapter 1, Verse 1 — The Four Conditions Refuted

> "Not from itself, not from another, not from both, nor without a cause — never in any way is there any existing thing that has arisen."

— Nāgārjuna, *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā*, I.1, trans. Garfield, Oxford, 1995, p. 105

*Gloss: The opening verse of the MMK — a tetralemma (catuṣkoṭi) that negates all four logical possibilities for causation. Nothing arises from itself (which would make causation pointless), from another (which would make causal connection impossible), from both (compounding the problems), or without cause (arbitrariness). The negation is total — "never in any way" — and the conclusion is that arising itself is an empty concept. This is not a denial of the dependently arisen world of experience but a denial that "arising" captures any ultimate truth about it.*

### Chapter 18, Verse 7 — The Cessation of Conceptual Proliferation

> "When the domain of thought has ceased, the object of speech has ceased. Like cessation itself, nothing arises and nothing ceases — this is the nature of things."

— Nāgārjuna, *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā*, XVIII.7, trans. Garfield (adapted), Oxford, 1995

*Gloss: The MMK does not end in a thesis but in the cessation of prapañca — conceptual proliferation, the mind's tendency to spin ever-more-elaborate metaphysical constructions. The goal of Madhyamaka analysis is not to arrive at a correct view but to exhaust the impulse to construct views. When thought ceases, the object of speech ceases — and what remains is not a thing but the silence in which the nature of things (dharmatā) is no longer occluded.*
