# Śūraṅgama Sūtra

## Note: Reconstructed from model knowledge — verify against physical copy

The passages below are reconstructed from training data and represent the most influential sections of the Śūraṅgama Sūtra, particularly those that bear on the concept of śūnyatā and the relationship between emptiness and perception. Citations reference the two standard English translations: Charles Luk (1966) and the Buddhist Text Translation Society edition (2009). All wording should be verified against a physical copy. Pagination is approximate.

The Śūraṅgama Sūtra is one of the most important texts in East Asian Mahāyāna Buddhism, particularly in the Chan/Zen and Huayan traditions. It is known for its detailed analysis of consciousness, its refutation of the mistaken identification of the self with the objects of perception, and its teaching that the true nature of mind is neither inside nor outside the body — a form of śūnyatā analysis applied to the problem of selfhood.

## Key Passages

### The Seven Inquiries into the Mind's Location — Introduction

> "The Buddha asked Ānanda: 'Where is your mind?' Ānanda replied: 'It is inside my body.' The Buddha said: 'If your mind is inside your body, it should first see what is inside, then see what is outside. But you do not see your internal organs; you see only what is outside. How then can the mind be inside?'"

— *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, trans. Charles Luk, Rider, 1966, pp. 8–10 (paraphrased)

*Gloss: The opening move of the Sūtra's famous "seven inquiries into the mind's location" — a phenomenological deconstruction of the ordinary assumption that the mind is located inside the body. The Buddha does not assert a correct location but demonstrates that every candidate (inside, outside, between, in the eyes, etc.) collapses under analysis. This is śūnyatā deployed not as a metaphysical thesis but as a method of inquiry: the mind, like all phenomena, cannot be located because it has no substantial, independent existence.*

### The Nature of Perception — Neither Inside Nor Outside

> "The Buddha said: 'Ānanda, when you see the Tathāgata's fist, what is it that sees? Is it your eye that sees, or is it your mind?' Ānanda replied: 'It is my mind that sees.' The Buddha said: 'If your mind sees, then when you close your eyes and see darkness, does that darkness belong to your mind or to the outside world? If to the outside world, then darkness should be visible even when your eyes are open. If to your mind, then darkness is inside your mind — but then why cannot you see your own mind as you see the darkness?'"

— *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, trans. Charles Luk (adapted), Rider, 1966

*Gloss: The perceptual analysis that extends the earlier inquiry. The Buddha presses Ānanda on the simplest possible experience — seeing a fist — and demonstrates that the distinction between "inner" (mind) and "outer" (world) cannot be maintained. The darkness behind closed eyes is the test case: it seems to be both "seen" and "not outside," yet it cannot be identified with the mind as a thing. The argument prefigures the phenomenological investigations of Husserl and Merleau-Ponty while grounding them in a soteriological purpose: liberation from the illusion of the self.*

### The Two False Views and the Middle Way

> "The Exalted One said: 'All living beings have from time without beginning been subject to the two inverted views — that the self exists, and that the self does not exist. Both are false. The true nature of the mind is neither existent nor non-existent, neither identical with phenomena nor separate from them, neither inside the body nor outside it. It is like the reflection of the moon in water — visible, undeniable, yet without substance.'"

— *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, trans. BTTS / Charles Luk (adapted), 2009/1966

*Gloss: The Śūraṅgama's formulation of the middle way — applied specifically to the self rather than to phenomena in general (as in Nāgārjuna). The "two inverted views" are the eternalist and nihilist extremes, now framed in terms of selfhood. The simile of the moon's reflection in water is one of the most quoted images in the Sūtra: the reflection is real enough to be seen, yet it has no independent substance — it exists dependently (on moon, water, observer) and is therefore empty. This is the Mahāyāna account of the self: not denied, not affirmed, but understood as dependently arisen and therefore empty.*

### The True Mind and the Distinguishing Mind

> "Ānanda, you have mistaken the thief for your son. The distinguishing mind that thinks, reasons, remembers, and plans — this is not your true mind. It is the conditioned, karmic consciousness that arises from contact between sense-organ and sense-object. Your true mind, which is the Tathāgata-garbha, is uncaused, unconditioned, and does not arise or cease. It is the ground of all experience, yet it is not any particular experience."

— *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, trans. Charles Luk (adapted), Rider, 1966

*Gloss: The distinction between the "distinguishing mind" (pravṛtti-vijñāna — the ordinary, conditioned consciousness) and the "true mind" (the Tathāgata-garbha, Buddha-nature). This is not a dualism of two minds but a recognition that what we ordinarily call "mind" is a construction — the product of sense-contact, karma, and conceptual proliferation. The "true mind" is not another thing behind the ordinary mind but the empty, luminous nature of mind itself when it is no longer grasped as a self. The passage connects śūnyatā to the doctrine of Buddha-nature: emptiness is not absence but the unconditioned openness that makes experience possible.*

### The Five Skandhas as Empty — the Fifty Demons

> "When you practice samādhi and the five skandhas begin to dissolve, you will encounter fifty demonic states — visions, voices, sensations of levitation, encounters with celestial beings. All these arise from the residual energy of the skandhas as they dissolve. Recognize them all as empty manifestations of your own mind. Do not grasp them, do not fear them, do not take them as signs of attainment. They are like clouds passing through an empty sky — they neither defile nor adorn it."

— *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, trans. BTTS (adapted), 2009

*Gloss: The famous "fifty demon states" section (or "fifty skandha-māras") — a practical manual for recognizing and not attaching to the experiences that arise during meditation as the five aggregates (skandhas) become transparent. The core teaching is śūnyatā applied to meditative experience: even the most sublime visionary states are "empty manifestations of your own mind" — they have no independent reality. To grasp them as attainments is to fall back into the very clinging the practice aims to dissolve. The "empty sky" simile recurs: phenomena drift through awareness without leaving a trace.*

The Śūraṅgama Sūtra is one of the most studied texts in East Asian Buddhism (especially Chan/Zen) but its Indian origins are debated — some scholars consider it a Chinese apocryphon composed in the Tang dynasty. The earliest complete English translation is Charles Luk (1966, Rider & Co., London), under copyright. This file supplements the existing Śūnyatā Term page with the Śūraṅgama's distinctive contribution: the application of emptiness analysis to the problem of perception and selfhood through phenomenological inquiry. The text also connects śūnyatā to Buddha-nature (Tathāgata-garbha) doctrine, which the Madhyamaka sources do not develop.
