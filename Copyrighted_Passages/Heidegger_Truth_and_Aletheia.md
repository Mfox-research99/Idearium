## Key Passages

### 1. Aletheia as Unconcealment (Being and Time, §44)

> "The 'being-true' of the logos as aletheia means: in legein as apophainesthai, the entities of which one is talking must be taken out of their hiddenness; one must let them be seen as something unhidden (alethes). That is, they must be discovered."

Heidegger recovers the pre-Socratic sense of truth — not as correctness of proposition but as *unconcealment*. The Greek a-letheia is a privative: truth is what has been wrested from hiddenness (lethe). This is not merely etymological archaeology; it reorients the entire question of truth away from the correspondence between statement and state of affairs toward the event of disclosure itself.

— Heidegger, *Being and Time*, §44, trans. Macquarrie & Robinson, Harper & Row, 1962

### 2. The Primordial Phenomenon of Truth (Being and Time, §44)

> "Dasein is 'in the truth.' This proposition has an ontological meaning. It does not mean that Dasein is always or even only sometimes factically 'initiated into all the truth.' It means that Dasein's disclosedness belongs to its state of Being."

Truth is not something Dasein occasionally achieves; it is constitutive of Dasein's mode of being. To exist is to be in a world that is already disclosed, already meaningful. This disclosedness is prior to any particular propositional truth. Even to err, one must already stand within a clearing.

— Heidegger, *Being and Time*, §44, trans. Macquarrie & Robinson, Harper & Row, 1962

### 3. The Essence of Truth as Freedom (On the Essence of Truth, §4)

> "The essence of truth is freedom. Freedom is not, however, the absence of constraint with respect to what we can or cannot do. Rather, freedom is engagement in the disclosure of beings as such. Freedom is not what common sense would have us believe: the occasional caprice to turn this way or that. Freedom is not the unboundedness of doing as we please. Freedom is the letting-be of beings."

Heidegger's most provocative thesis in the essay: truth, understood as unconcealment, is grounded in freedom — not freedom as license but freedom as the capacity to *let beings be* in their disclosure. The human being does not impose categories on a neutral world; the human being participates in the event in which beings show themselves as what they are.

— Heidegger, "On the Essence of Truth," trans. John Sallis, in *Basic Writings*, Harper & Row, 1977; 1930 original

### 4. The Open Region (The End of Philosophy)

> "The task of thinking would then be the surrender of previous thinking to the determination of the matter of thinking. The matter of thinking is not achieved by philosophy, but rather philosophy is surpassed by the matter of thinking — the clearing, the open region."

In his late essay, Heidegger calls for a thinking that is no longer philosophy — a thinking that attends to the clearing (Lichtung) in which anything can appear at all. This clearing is not produced by thought; thought finds itself always already within it. The task is not to explain the clearing but to remain attentive to it.

— Heidegger, "The End of Philosophy and the Task of Thinking," trans. Joan Stambaugh, in *Basic Writings*, Harper & Row, 1977; 1964 original

### 5. Transcendence as Being-in-the-World (On the Essence of Ground, 1929)

> "Truth as aletheia belongs to the essence of ground. Ground means possibility, essence, and account — all unified in the transcendence of Dasein. In transcendence, Dasein surpasses beings toward the world, and in this surpassing, beings first become manifest as beings."

Heidegger here connects truth, ground, and transcendence. Dasein always already transcends beings — it is already "beyond" them, in the world — and this transcendence is what allows beings to appear at all. Truth is not added to beings; it is the condition of their appearing.

— Heidegger, "On the Essence of Ground," trans. Terrence Malick, in *Pathmarks*, Cambridge UP, 1998; 1929 original

### 6. Truth and Art (The Origin of the Work of Art, 1936)

> "In the work, the happening of truth is at work. Art is the setting-itself-into-work of truth. In the work, the openness of beings — the truth — happens. This openness is not a property of beings but the lighting in the midst of beings."

Heidegger extends the scope of truth beyond proposition and assertion into the work of art. A Greek temple does not represent truth; it *opens a world* in which truth can occur. The poem, the painting, the building are not decorations on truth — they are events of truth itself.

— Heidegger, "The Origin of the Work of Art," trans. Albert Hofstadter, in *Poetry, Language, Thought*, Harper & Row, 1971; 1936 original

### 7. The Question of Technology (The Question Concerning Technology, 1954)

> "The essence of technology is by no means anything technological. Technology is a mode of revealing. Technology comes to presence in the realm where revealing and unconcealment take place, where aletheia, truth, happens."

Technology is not merely a set of instruments but a way in which beings are disclosed — as standing-reserve (Bestand), as resources to be ordered and optimized. This is a destiny of truth, not an optional technique. The danger is not the machines but that this mode of revealing becomes the only one, foreclosing other ways in which beings might show themselves.

— Heidegger, "The Question Concerning Technology," trans. William Lovitt, in *Basic Writings*, Harper & Row, 1977; 1954 original

### 8. The Clearing (The End of Philosophy)

> "What is clearing? The clearing is the open region for everything that becomes present and absent. The clearing is not the mere openness of space but the opening that first grants being-present and being-absent."

This is Heidegger's most compressed statement on the central image of his late thought. The clearing is not a container for beings; it is the event of openness itself, prior to any particular being, prior even to being and nothing. It grants presence, but is not itself present in the way beings are.

— Heidegger, "The End of Philosophy and the Task of Thinking," trans. Joan Stambaugh, in *Basic Writings*, Harper & Row, 1977; 1964 original
