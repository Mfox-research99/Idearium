## Key Passages

### Justice as Fairness — The Two Principles

> "First: each person is to have an equal right to the most extensive total system of equal basic liberties compatible with a similar system of liberty for all. Second: social and economic inequalities are to be arranged so that they are both (a) to the greatest benefit of the least advantaged, and (b) attached to offices and positions open to all under conditions of fair equality of opportunity."

— John Rawls, *A Theory of Justice*, Chapter 2, §11, Harvard University Press, 1971.

### The Original Position and the Veil of Ignorance

> "No one knows his place in society, his class position or social status, nor does anyone know his fortune in the distribution of natural assets and abilities, his intelligence, strength, and the like. I shall even assume that the parties do not know their conceptions of the good or their special psychological propensities. The principles of justice are chosen behind a veil of ignorance."

— John Rawls, *A Theory of Justice*, Chapter 3, §24, Harvard University Press, 1971.

### Reflective Equilibrium

> "By going back and forth, sometimes altering the conditions of the contractual circumstances, at others withdrawing our judgments and conforming them to principle, I assume that eventually we shall find a description of the initial situation that both expresses reasonable conditions and yields principles which match our considered judgments duly pruned and adjusted. This state of affairs I refer to as reflective equilibrium."

— John Rawls, *A Theory of Justice*, Chapter 1, §4, Harvard University Press, 1971.

### The Difference Principle

> "Social and economic inequalities are to be arranged so that they are to the greatest benefit of the least advantaged. Inequalities that do not benefit the worst off are unjust, regardless of how they arose or how51:47 the total social product."

— John Rawls, *A Theory of Justice*, Chapter 2, §13, Harvard University Press, 1971. [Paraphrased core.]

### The Priority of Liberty

> "Liberty can be restricted only for the sake of liberty. There are two cases: (a) a less extensive liberty must strengthen the total system of liberty shared by all, and (b) a less than equal liberty must be acceptable to those with the lesser liberty."

— John Rawls, *A Theory of Justice*, Chapter 4, §39, Harvard University Press, 1971.

### The Maximin Reasoning

> "The parties in the original position are conceived as following the maximin rule: they compare alternatives by their worst possible outcomes. When the worst outcome of one alternative is superior to the worst outcome of another, the former is chosen. Given the significant features of the original position, it is rational for the parties to adopt the conservative attitude expressed by this rule."

— John Rawls, *A Theory of Justice*, Chapter 3, §26, Harvard University Press, 1971.

### Public Reason and the Overlapping Consensus (from *Political Liberalism*, 1993)

> "A political conception of justice must be one that can be endorsed by widely different and opposing though reasonable comprehensive doctrines. An overlapping consensus, therefore, is not a mere modus vivendi but a moral consensus built on reasons internal to each doctrine."

— John Rawls, *Political Liberalism*, 1993. [From companion work.]

### Justice, Fairness, and the Basic Structure

> "The primary subject of justice is the basic structure of society, the way in which the major social institutions distribute fundamental rights and duties and determine the division of advantages from social cooperation."

— John Rawls, *A Theory of Justice*, Chapter 1, §2, Harvard University Press, 1971.
