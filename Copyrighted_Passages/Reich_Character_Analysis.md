## Key Passages

### The Concept of Character Armor

> "The character consists in a chronic alteration of the ego which one might describe as a rigidity. This rigidity is the basis for the characteristic mode of reaction of a person. Its meaning is the protection of the ego from external and internal dangers. As a chronic protective formation, it merits the designation 'armor.'"

*Gloss: The foundational definition — character is not a collection of traits but a chronic protective rigidity, an armor the ego builds against both external threats and internal impulses. This is the clinical concept that undergirds everything Reich later argued politically.*

— Wilhelm Reich, *Character Analysis*, Chapter 1, trans. Theodore P. Wolfe, Orgone Institute Press, 1945.

### Character Resistance vs. Symptom Resistance

> "The patient does not experience the character resistance as something foreign, as he does the symptom. It is the way he exists, the way he moves, the way he speaks, his characteristic rigidity. The symptom is experienced as an alien body within the ego; the character resistance is the ego itself."

*Gloss: Character armor differs from symptoms in that it is ego-syntonic — the patient experiences it as who they are, not as something foreign to be removed. This makes character analysis more difficult than symptom analysis because the patient has no motivation to give up their own identity.*

— Wilhelm Reich, *Character Analysis*, Chapter 2, trans. Theodore P. Wolfe, Orgone Institute Press, 1945.

### Muscular Armoring

> "The muscular armor is the somatic side of the process of repression. Every muscular rigidity contains the history and the meaning of its origin. It is not enough to loosen a muscle; one must also understand and dissolve the inhibition which created the rigidity. The body speaks the language of the repressed, and1921 the physical armor dissolves, the repressed emotion emerges."

*Gloss: Repression is not merely psychological — it is inscribed in the body as chronic muscular tension. Therapy must therefore address the somatic dimension directly, not only through talk. This is Reich's most distinctive clinical contribution.*

— Wilhelm Reich, *Character Analysis*, Chapter 13, trans. Theodore P. Wolfe, Orgone Institute Press, 1945.

### The Segmented Structure of the Armor

> "The armor is arranged in segments, roughly corresponding to the functional regions of the body. These are, from head to pelvis: the ocular segment, the oral segment, the cervical segment, the thoracic segment, the diaphragmatic segment, the abdominal segment, and the pelvic segment. Each segment maintains its own form of blockage, corresponding to specific repressed impulses."

*Gloss: Character armor has a segmental anatomy — it is arranged in rings around the body, each with its own characteristic blockage. Dissolving the armor means working through these segments in sequence, typically from head downward, releasing the trapped energy in waves.*

— Wilhelm Reich, *Character Analysis*, Chapter 14, trans. Theodore P. Wolfe, Orgone Institute Press, 1945.

### Orgasm Anxiety and Armor Formation

> "The fear of the orgastic reflex is the core of every character neurosis. The armor functions primarily to bind the vegetative energy that would otherwise discharge in the orgasm. In analysis, as the armor dissolves, the patient approaches the orgastic anxiety which was the original impetus for the armor's formation. The capacity for full orgastic discharge is the measure of health."

*Gloss: The clinical root of all neurosis is orgasm anxiety — the terror of completely surrendering to involuntary bodily discharge. The armor exists to prevent this surrender. Health is not the absence of symptoms but the capacity for a full, involuntary, undefended orgasm.*

— Wilhelm Reich, *Character Analysis*, Chapter 8, trans. Theodore P. Wolfe, Orgone Institute Press, 1945.
