## Key Passages

### The Threefold Distinction: Labor, Work, Action

> "Labor is the activity which corresponds to the biological process of the human body. Work provides an 'artificial' world of things, distinctly different from all natural surroundings. Action, the only activity that goes on directly between men without the intermediary of things or matter, corresponds to the human condition of plurality."

— Hannah Arendt, *The Human Condition*, Chapter 1, University of Chicago Press, 1958.

### Natality

> "The new beginning inherent in birth can make itself felt in the world only because the newcomer possesses the capacity of beginning something anew, that is, of acting. It is in the nature of beginning that something new is started which cannot be expected from whatever may have happened before. The miracle that saves the world, the realm of human affairs, from its normal, 'natural' ruin is ultimately the fact of natality."

— Hannah Arendt, *The Human Condition*, Chapter 5, University of Chicago Press, 1958.

### The Rise of the Social

> "Society is the form in which the fact of mutual dependence for the sake of life and nothing else assumes public significance and where the activities connected with sheer survival are permitted to appear in public."

— Hannah Arendt, *The Human Condition*, Chapter 2, University of Chicago Press, 1958.

### The Public Realm and Plurality

> "Human plurality, the basic condition of both action and speech, has the twofold character of equality and distinction. If men were not equal, they could neither understand each other nor understand those who came before them. If men were not distinct, they would have no need of speech or action to make themselves understood."

— Hannah Arendt, *The Human Condition*, Chapter 5, University of Chicago Press, 1958.

### Forgiveness and Promise

> "Without being forgiven, released from the consequences of what we have done, our capacity to act would be confined to one single deed from which we could never recover. Without being bound to the fulfillment of promises, we would never be able to keep our identities. Both faculties depend on plurality, on the presence and acting of others, for no one can forgive himself and no one can feel bound by a promise made only to himself."

— Hannah Arendt, *The Human Condition*, Chapter 5, University of Chicago Press, 1958.

### The Modern Alienation from the World

> "The modern age has carried with it a world alienation of unprecedented radicalism. It began with the discovery of the Archimedean point — the point from which the earth could be moved — and has ended in the point of view of the universe, from which all human activities appear as mere processes."

— Hannah Arendt, *The Human Condition*, Prologue, University of Chicago Press, 1958.

### The Permanence of the World

> "If the world is to contain a public space, it cannot be erected for one generation and planned for the living only; it must transcend the life-span of mortal men. Without this transcendence into a potential earthly immortality, no politics, strictly speaking, no common world and no public realm, is possible."

— Hannah Arendt, *The Human Condition*, Chapter 2, University of Chicago Press, 1958.

### The Danger of 'Worldlessness'

> "Worldlessness as a political phenomenon is possible only on the assumption that the world does not outlast the generation which brought it into being — that it is the product of fabrication and carries no weight of its own. The modern loss of the world finds its most extreme form in mass society, where men are thrown back on the sheer givenness of their own existence."

— Hannah Arendt, *The Human Condition*, Chapter 6, University of Chicago Press, 1958.
