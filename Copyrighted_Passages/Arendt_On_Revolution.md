## Key Passages

### 1. The Lost Treasure of Revolution (Introduction)

> "The revolutionary spirit — a new spirit of beginning something new, a spirit which is a treasure that must be saved from the wreck of the revolution — has been lost time and again, and the failure to remember it has been as fatal as the failure to provide for it institutionally."

Arendt's central thesis: revolutions carry within them a "lost treasure" — the experience of public freedom that erupts when people act in concert to found a new political order. This treasure is almost never institutionalized; it fades with the revolutionary moment itself, leaving behind only the structures of government, not the experience of freedom that gave them birth.

— Arendt, *On Revolution*, Introduction, Viking Press, 1963

### 2. The Social Question (Chapter 2)

> "The direction of the American Revolution remained committed to the foundation of freedom and the establishment of lasting institutions... The direction of the French Revolution was deflected from its original course almost from the beginning by the urgencies of the poor."

Arendt distinguishes the American and French Revolutions by the intrusion of the "social question" — mass poverty. The French revolutionaries were forced to address bodily necessity, which Arendt argues is inherently anti-political because it admits no deliberation, only urgency. The American founders, unburdened by mass immiseration, could focus on constitutional architecture.

— Arendt, *On Revolution*, Chapter 2, Viking Press, 1963

### 3. Public Happiness (Chapter 3)

> "The Americans knew that public freedom consists in having a share in public affairs, and that the activities connected with this business constitute a happiness of their own. They knew what the Romans called publica felicitas, a happiness that consists in the right to participate in government."

For the American founders, freedom was not merely freedom *from* government (the liberal conception) but freedom *to* participate in government — what Arendt calls "public happiness." This positive conception of freedom was the animating principle of the revolution, and its loss in modern political thought represents a narrowing of what freedom can mean.

— Arendt, *On Revolution*, Chapter 3, Viking Press, 1963

### 4. The Council System (Chapter 6)

> "The councils, which were always organs of order as much as organs of action, were the only political institutions that could have preserved the revolutionary spirit after the revolution was over. They were the only spaces where the treasure could be kept."

Throughout the book, Arendt returns to the spontaneous formation of councils — the Paris Commune, the Russian soviets, the Hungarian revolutionary councils of 1956 — as the institutional form most faithful to the revolutionary experience. These councils, emerging from below and organized around direct participation, represent the political form that the revolutionary spirit itself generates. Their defeat is the defeat of the revolution's deepest impulse.

— Arendt, *On Revolution*, Chapter 6, Viking Press, 1963

### 5. Foundation and Authority (Chapter 5)

> "The great, and in the long run perhaps the greatest, American innovation in politics was the consistent abolition of sovereignty within the body politic of the republic, the insight that in the realm of human affairs sovereignty and tyranny are the same."

Arendt reads the American constitutional innovation as a break with the European tradition of sovereignty. The American founders understood that power need not be concentrated; it can be federated, distributed, checked. This is the political correlate of her broader philosophical commitment to plurality — the irreducibility of many perspectives to a single one.

— Arendt, *On Revolution*, Chapter 5, Viking Press, 1963

### 6. The Act of Beginning (Chapter 5)

> "The revolutionary spirit contains two elements which are intimately connected: the act of founding the new body politic and the experience of beginning something new. The foundation is an act that contains within itself the principle of augmentation."

Foundation is not a single moment but an ongoing act. The American Constitution was designed not to freeze politics but to enable its continuation — "to form a more perfect union." Arendt contrasts this with the French Revolution, which consumed its own children in a spiral of purification.

— Arendt, *On Revolution*, Chapter 5, Viking Press, 1963

### 7. Poverty and Necessity (Chapter 2)

> "Nothing, we might say today, could be more obsolete than to attempt to liberate mankind from poverty by political means; nothing could be more futile and more dangerous."

A characteristically provocative claim. Arendt argues that poverty is a condition of the body, not of the polis, and that trying to address it through political means confuses two distinct spheres. The social realm requires administration, not deliberation; freedom requires politics, not economics. Whether one agrees or not, the distinction is central to her argument.

— Arendt, *On Revolution*, Chapter 2, Viking Press, 1963
