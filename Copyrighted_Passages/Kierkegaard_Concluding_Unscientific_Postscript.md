## Key Passages

### 1. Truth Is Subjectivity (Section II, Chapter 2)

> "When subjectivity is truth, the definition of truth must also contain an expression for the antithesis to objectivity... Here is such a definition of truth: An objective uncertainty held fast in the appropriation of the most passionate inwardness is the truth, the highest truth attainable for an existing individual."

This is the book's most famous — and most frequently misunderstood — claim. Kierkegaard is not asserting that truth is whatever one sincerely believes. He is defining truth as a *how*, not a *what*: the mode of relation, not the content. Truth is the way an existing individual holds an objective uncertainty with passion. The "what" (God, for instance) is uncertain; the "how" (the passion of faith) is what makes the relation true.

— Kierkegaard, *Concluding Unscientific Postscript*, Part II, Section 2, Chapter 2, trans. Hong & Hong, Princeton UP, 1992

### 2. The Existing Individual (Part II, Section 2)

> "An existing individual is constantly in a process of becoming; the actual existing subjective thinker, who is thinking, continually reproduces this in his existence and invests all his thinking in becoming."

Against the Hegelian system, which treats the thinker as a finished spectator surveying completed actuality, Kierkegaard insists that the thinker is unfinished, in motion, mortal. Existence is not a premise from which thought departs — it is the medium in which all thought occurs and which no thought transcends.

— Kierkegaard, *Concluding Unscientific Postscript*, Part II, Section 2, trans. Hong & Hong, Princeton UP, 1992

### 3. The System and Existence (Part II, Section 2)

> "A system of existence cannot be given. Is there, then, not such a system? That is not the case at all. This is not implied in what has been said. Existence itself is a system — for God, but it cannot be a system for any existing spirit. System and conclusiveness correspond to each other, but existence is precisely the opposite."

The systematic philosopher (Hegel) claims to have completed thought. Kierkegaard's retort: existence is open, unfinished, forward-facing. Only a being outside existence — God — could survey it as a closed system. The existing individual sees through a glass darkly and must act without the guarantee of system.

— Kierkegaard, *Concluding Unscientific Postscript*, Part II, Section 2, trans. Hong & Hong, Princeton UP, 1992

### 4. The Leap (Part II, Section 2)

> "The decision lies in the subject. The appropriation is the paradoxical inwardness that is specifically different from all other inwardness. Being a Christian is not defined by the 'what' of Christianity but by the 'how' of the Christian. This 'how' can fit only one thing, the absolute paradox."

The leap is not a blind jump but a qualitative transition that no quantity of reasoning can effect. One can assemble all the historical evidence, all the philosophical arguments, and still stand outside faith — because faith requires a decision that reason cannot supply. The "how" is everything.

— Kierkegaard, *Concluding Unscientific Postscript*, Part II, Section 2, trans. Hong & Hong, Princeton UP, 1992

### 5. The Speculative Point of View (Introduction)

> "The speculative point of view has canceled the difficulty by canceling the existing individual, by abstracting from his existence, by regarding him sub specie aeterni. But no one has ever existed sub specie aeterni, and if anyone ever did, he would not be an existing individual."

Kierkegaard's anti-Hegelian polemic is sharpest here. The speculative philosopher claims to view things from the standpoint of eternity — but no existing human being occupies that standpoint. The claim is a pretense that erases the very condition (finitude, temporality, mortality) under which all human thinking occurs.

— Kierkegaard, *Concluding Unscientific Postscript*, Introduction, trans. Hong & Hong, Princeton UP, 1992

### 6. Subjectivity and Untruth (Part II, Section 2)

> "Subjectivity is truth. The paradox is that subjectivity is untruth. This is the Socratic definition. But now subjectivity is untruth. In this way we have come back to the Socratic definition which requires that if one is to be in truth, one must first and foremost not be in truth."

This dense passage unfolds Kierkegaard's deepening of the Socratic position. Socrates held that subjectivity is untruth (ignorance) that can be recalled into truth. Christianity deepens this: subjectivity is not merely ignorant but *in* untruth — sin — and cannot recall itself. It requires a teacher who is also a savior.

— Kierkegaard, *Concluding Unscientific Postscript*, Part II, Section 2, trans. Hong & Hong, Princeton UP, 1992

### 7. The Abstract Thinker vs. the Subjective Thinker (Part II, Section 2)

> "The abstract thinker is himself abstracted from his own existence. He is not an existing thinker but a fantasy of pure thought. The subjective thinker is an existing person and yet a thinker; he does not abstract from existence and its contradiction, but he has them present with him in his thinking."

The subjective thinker does not escape finitude into thought; he thinks *within* finitude, with all its constraints, incompleteness, and urgency. The impersonality of the system is not a virtue — it is an evasion of what it means to be a human being who must think, decide, and die.

— Kierkegaard, *Concluding Unscientific Postscript*, Part II, Section 2, trans. Hong & Hong, Princeton UP, 1992

### 8. The Highest Thought (Part II, Section 2)

> "If I were to wish for anything, I should not wish for wealth and power but for the passion of possibility, for the eye which, everywhere young and ardent, sees possibility. Pleasure disappoints; possibility never. And what wine is so sparkling, so fragrant, so intoxicating, as possibility!"

Even in a work of rigorous philosophical argument, Kierkegaard's lyrical intensity breaks through. Possibility — the open horizon of becoming — is the oxygen of the existing individual. The system kills it; passion sustains it.

— Kierkegaard, *Concluding Unscientific Postscript*, Part II, Section 2, trans. Hong & Hong, Princeton UP, 1992
