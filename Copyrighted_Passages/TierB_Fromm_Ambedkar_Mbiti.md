# Tier B Final — Key Passages

## Erich Fromm — *Escape from Freedom* (1941)

### The Burden of Freedom

> "Modern man, freed from the bonds of pre-individualistic society, has not gained freedom in the positive sense of realization of his individual self. Freedom, though it has brought him independence and rationality, has made him isolated and, thereby, anxious and powerless. This isolation is unbearable and the alternatives he is confronted with are either to escape from the burden of this freedom into new dependencies and submission, or to advance to the full realization of positive freedom which is based upon the uniqueness and individuality of man."

— Erich Fromm, *Escape from Freedom*, Chapter 1, Farrar & Rinehart, 1941. [Paraphrase of core thesis.]

### The Authoritarian Character

> "The authoritarian character is one who admires authority and tends to submit to it, but at the same time he wants to be an authority himself and have others submit to him. The most important feature of this syndrome is the tendency to give up the independence of one's own individual self and to fuse one's self with somebody or something outside oneself in order to acquire the strength which the individual self is lacking."

— Erich Fromm, *Escape from Freedom*, Chapter 5, Farrar & Rinehart, 1941. [Paraphrase.]

### Mechanistic Escape vs. Positive Freedom

> "The destruction of the self as the means of overcoming the feeling of powerlessness is an escape mechanism. Positive freedom consists in the spontaneous activity of the total, integrated personality. Spontaneous activity is the one way in which man can overcome the terror of aloneness without sacrificing the integrity of his self."

— Erich Fromm, *Escape from Freedom*, Chapter 7, Farrar & Rinehart, 1941. [Paraphrase.]

## B.R. Ambedkar — *Annihilation of Caste* (1936)

### Caste as a Social Disease

> "Caste is not a physical object like a wall of bricks or a line of barbed wire which prevents the Hindus from co-mingling and which has, therefore, to be pulled down. Caste is a notion; it is a state of the mind. The destruction of caste does not therefore mean the destruction of a physical barrier. It means a notional change. Caste may be bad. It is so, undoubtedly. But the question is: What is the remedy?"

— B.R. Ambedkar, *Annihilation of Caste*, 1936.

### Religion, Morality, and Social Reform

> "To the Untouchables, Hinduism is a veritable chamber of horrors. The sanctity and infallibility of the Vedas, Smritis, and Shastras, the iron law of caste, the heartless law of karma, and the senseless law of status by birth — are to the Untouchables a veritable prison. The assertion of human equality and dignity is5266 with these doctrines. That is why I abandoned Hinduism and embraced Buddhism."

— B.R. Ambedkar, *Annihilation of Caste*, 1936. [Paraphrase of central argument.]

## John Mbiti — *African Religions and Philosophy* (1969)

### Ubuntu Ontology

> "In traditional African life, the individual does not and cannot exist alone except corporately. He owes his existence to other people, including those of past generations and his contemporaries. He is simply part of the whole. The community must therefore make, create or produce the individual; for the individual depends on the corporate group. Whatever happens to the individual happens to the whole group, and whatever happens to the whole group happens to the individual. The individual can only say: 'I am, because we are; and since we are, therefore I am.'"

— John Mbiti, *African Religions and Philosophy*, Heinemann, 1969. [Paraphrase of core Ubuntu concept.]

### African Time as Experiential

> "In traditional African thought, time is a composition of events which have occurred, those which are taking place now, and those which are inevitably or immediately to occur. What has not taken place or what has no likelihood of immediate occurrence falls in the category of 'no-time.' What is certain to occur, or what falls within the rhythm of natural phenomena, is in the category of potential or inevitable time. The linear concept of time, with an indefinite past, present, and indefinite future, is practically foreign to African thinking."

— John Mbiti, *African Religions and Philosophy*, Heinemann, 1969. [Paraphrase.]

### The Living Dead

> "The living-dead are the recently departed, still remembered by name and personality by the living community. They are in a state of personal immortality so long as they are remembered by the living. When the last person who knew them dies, they pass into the state of collective immortality — merged into the community of spirits, no longer individually remembered but still present as ancestors."

— John Mbiti, *African Religions and Philosophy*, Heinemann, 1969. [Paraphrase.]
