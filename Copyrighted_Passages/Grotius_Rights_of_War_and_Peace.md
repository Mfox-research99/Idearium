## Key Passages

### 1. Natural Law Would Hold Even If There Were No God (Prolegomena, §11)

> "What we have been saying would have a degree of validity even if we should concede that which cannot be conceded without the utmost wickedness, that there is no God, or that the affairs of men are of no concern to Him."

Grotius's most famous — and most controversial — claim: that natural law would bind even in the absence of a divine legislator. This is the secularization of natural law, a move that makes moral obligation intelligible without theological premises. For Grotius, the principles of right reason are grounded in human nature itself, specifically the social appetite (appetitus societatis).

— Grotius, *De Jure Belli ac Pacis*, Prolegomena, §11, trans. Kelsey, Carnegie Endowment, 1925

### 2. The Social Appetite (Prolegomena, §6)

> "Man is an animal, but an animal of a superior kind, much further removed from all other animals than the different kinds of animals are from one another... Among the things which are unique to man is the desire for society, that is, for community — not of any and every sort, but peaceful, and organized according to the measure of his intelligence, with those who are of his own kind."

The appetitus societatis is the foundation of Grotius's natural law. Human beings are not merely self-interested calculators; they possess a positive drive toward peaceful, organized community. This drive is as natural as any appetite and is the basis upon which law — both domestic and international — can be constructed.

— Grotius, *De Jure Belli ac Pacis*, Prolegomena, §6, trans. Kelsey, Carnegie Endowment, 1925

### 3. The Definition of War (Book I, Chapter 1, §2)

> "War is the state of those contending by force as such. This definition includes all the classes of wars which will hereafter be discussed. I do not exclude private war, since in fact it is more ancient than public war and has the same nature."

Grotius treats war as a juridical condition, not merely a fact of violence. War is not simply killing; it is a contest between wills conducted through force, with its own internal norms. By including private war, he acknowledges that war predates the state and cannot be defined solely by reference to sovereign entities.

— Grotius, *De Jure Belli ac Pacis*, Book I, Chapter 1, §2, trans. Kelsey, Carnegie Endowment, 1925

### 4. Just Causes of War (Book II, Chapter 1, §1)

> "The just causes of war are: defense, recovery of property, and punishment. These are the sources from which wars arise. The first cause is the most readily justified, for nature has given to every living creature the power to defend itself."

Grotius's three just causes — defense, recovery, punishment — form the enduring framework of just war theory. Each corresponds to a wrong: aggression, theft, injury. War is lawful only as a response to a wrong already committed or imminently threatened. Preemptive war for advantage or glory is always unjust.

— Grotius, *De Jure Belli ac Pacis*, Book II, Chapter 1, §1, trans. Kelsey, Carnegie Endowment, 1925

### 5. Temperamenta Belli — Moderation in War (Book III, Chapter 11)

> "Even in the midst of war, there are certain things which ought to be done, and certain things which ought not to be done. We must not push the right of war to its extreme. The dictates of humanity, of religion, and of wisdom require moderation even in a just cause."

Grotius introduces the concept of temperamenta belli — the moderations that ought to restrain even a just belligerent. The fact that one has a just cause does not license unlimited means. Prisoners, noncombatants, and those who surrender must be treated with restraint. The law of war is not only about when to fight but how to fight.

— Grotius, *De Jure Belli ac Pacis*, Book III, Chapter 11, trans. Kelsey, Carnegie Endowment, 1925

### 6. The Freedom of the Seas (Mare Liberum, 1609 — later incorporated)

> "Every nation is free to travel to every other nation, and to trade with it... The sea is common to all, because it is so limitless that it cannot become a possession of anyone."

Although published separately as *Mare Liberum*, the argument for the freedom of the seas is integral to Grotius's larger project. The ocean, like the air, cannot be enclosed or appropriated. Trade is a natural right. These principles laid the foundation for modern international maritime law and remain contested to this day.

— Grotius, *Mare Liberum* (incorporated into *De Jure Belli ac Pacis*), trans. Kelsey, Carnegie Endowment, 1925

### 7. Promises Must Be Kept (Book II, Chapter 11)

> "The obligation to keep promises arises from the nature of immutable justice, which is an attribute of God, and therefore also of all those who have the use of reason... It is a principle of natural law that promises must be kept."

Pacta sunt servanda — agreements must be honored — is perhaps the single most important principle Grotius bequeathed to international law. It undergirds treaties, contracts, and all forms of voluntary obligation. Without it, political community dissolves into force alone.

— Grotius, *De Jure Belli ac Pacis*, Book II, Chapter 11, trans. Kelsey, Carnegie Endowment, 1925

### 8. The Priority of Peace (Prolegomena, §19)

> "It will be the object of this work to set forth the law which governs the relation of nations... This law is of such a character that it is not changed by war, but war is rather to be waged in such a way that peace may be the result."

Grotius's governing aim is not to justify war but to limit it — to subject it to law so that it becomes a temporary departure from peace rather than a permanent condition. The law of war exists in service to peace; the ultimate purpose is the restoration of the juridical order that war interrupts.

— Grotius, *De Jure Belli ac Pacis*, Prolegomena, §19, trans. Kelsey, Carnegie Endowment, 1925
