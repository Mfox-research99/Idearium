## Key Passages

### 1. The Fetishism of Commodities (Chapter 1, §4)

> "A commodity appears at first sight an extremely obvious, trivial thing. But its analysis brings out that it is a very strange thing, abounding in metaphysical subtleties and theological niceties... The mysterious character of the commodity-form consists therefore simply in the fact that the commodity reflects the social characteristics of men's own labour as objective characteristics of the products of labour themselves, as the socio-natural properties of these things."

The fetishism of commodities is Marx's most enduring philosophical concept. In a market economy, relations between people appear as relations between things. The social character of labor — who works for whom, under what conditions, producing what surplus — is hidden behind the apparent equality of market exchange. A table is just wood until it becomes a commodity; then it "stands on its head" and appears to have value as a natural property.

— Marx, *Capital*, Vol. I, Chapter 1, §4, trans. Fowkes, Penguin, 1976

### 2. The Secret of Primitive Accumulation (Part VIII, Chapter 26)

> "The so-called primitive accumulation is nothing else than the historical process of divorcing the producer from the means of production. It appears as 'primitive' because it forms the pre-history of capital and of the mode of production corresponding to capital."

The myth of capitalism's origins — the thrifty saver who patiently accumulated capital — is, Marx argues, a fairy tale. The actual history is one of expropriation: the violent separation of peasants from their land, the enclosure of commons, the creation of a class with nothing to sell but their labor power. "Conquest, enslavement, robbery, murder — in short, force — play the greatest part."

— Marx, *Capital*, Vol. I, Part VIII, Chapter 26, trans. Fowkes, Penguin, 1976

### 3. The Working Day (Chapter 10)

> "Capital is dead labour which, vampire-like, lives only by sucking living labour, and lives the more, the more labour it sucks. The time during which the labourer works is the time during which the capitalist consumes the labour-power he has purchased."

The image of capital as a vampire — dead labor that feeds on living labor — is one of Marx's most vivid. The struggle over the length of the working day is a struggle over the boundary between profit and life. Capital, left to its own logic, would consume every hour; the limits on the working day are won only through sustained class struggle.

— Marx, *Capital*, Vol. I, Chapter 10, trans. Fowkes, Penguin, 1976

### 4. The Dual Character of Labor (Chapter 1, §2)

> "All labour is an expenditure of human labour-power, in the physiological sense, and it is in this quality of being equal, or abstract, human labour that it forms the value of commodities. On the other hand, all labour is an expenditure of human labour-power in a particular form and with a definite aim, and it is in this quality of concrete useful labour that it produces use-values."

This is arguably Marx's most important analytical innovation: the distinction between concrete labor (the specific activity that produces a coat, a chair, a line of code) and abstract labor (the expenditure of undifferentiated human energy that makes disparate products commensurable in the market). Value is the social expression of abstract labor.

— Marx, *Capital*, Vol. I, Chapter 1, §2, trans. Fowkes, Penguin, 1976

### 5. The General Formula for Capital (Chapter 4)

> "The simple circulation of commodities — selling in order to buy — is a means to a final goal which lies outside circulation, namely the satisfaction of needs. The circulation of money as capital is an end in itself, for the valorization of value takes place only within this constantly renewed movement. The movement of capital is therefore limitless."

M-C-M' (Money → Commodity → More Money) is the circuit of capital, distinguished from C-M-C (Commodity → Money → Commodity) which is the circuit of simple exchange. In the latter, one sells to buy; money is a means. In the former, one buys to sell; money is the end, and the process has no natural terminus. Capital is value in motion, driven by the infinite hunger for more.

— Marx, *Capital*, Vol. I, Chapter 4, trans. Fowkes, Penguin, 1976

### 6. The Realm of Necessity and the Realm of Freedom (Chapter 48 — from Vol. III, but thematically essential)

> "The realm of freedom really begins only where labour determined by necessity and external expediency ends; it lies by its very nature beyond the sphere of material production proper. The shortening of the working day is its fundamental prerequisite."

This passage from Volume III (often cited alongside Volume I) defines Marx's vision of freedom not as the abolition of necessity but as its containment. Freedom is not liberation from work but liberation from work's domination of life — time released from the compulsion of survival, in which genuinely human capacities can develop.

— Marx, *Capital*, Vol. III, Chapter 48, trans. David Fernbach, Penguin, 1981

### 7. The Reserve Army of Labor (Chapter 25)

> "The working population therefore produces both the accumulation of capital and the means by which it is itself made relatively superfluous; and it does this to an ever-increasing extent. This is a law of population peculiar to the capitalist mode of production."

Technological progress under capitalism does not liberate workers from toil; it renders them superfluous — a "reserve army" of the unemployed whose very existence disciplines those still employed. The Iron Law of Wages is not natural; it is produced by the dynamic of accumulation itself.

— Marx, *Capital*, Vol. I, Chapter 25, trans. Fowkes, Penguin, 1976

### 8. The Expropriators Expropriated (Chapter 32)

> "The knell of capitalist private property sounds. The expropriators are expropriated."

The famous prophecy at the end of the historical chapters. The concentration of capital, the socialization of labor, and the immiseration of the working class create the conditions for a revolution that will abolish private property in the means of production. "Capitalist production begets, with the inexorability of a natural process, its own negation."

— Marx, *Capital*, Vol. I, Chapter 32, trans. Fowkes, Penguin, 1976
