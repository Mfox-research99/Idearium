# Mencius — The Complete Seven Books

## Note: Reconstructed from model knowledge — verify against physical copy

The passages below are reconstructed from training data and represent the most-cited sections of the Mencius beyond Book I (which is available in the corpus as the "Sayings of Mencius" selection from Legge's *Chinese Literature*, PG 10056). These passages, primarily from Books II through VII, develop the doctrines the selection only introduces. Citations reference the Legge translation (1895) as the standard public-domain edition — though the complete Legge translation has not yet been acquired for the corpus. The D.C. Lau translation (1970, Penguin) is the standard modern edition and is under copyright. All wording should be verified against: James Legge, *The Works of Mencius*, The Chinese Classics, Vol. II, Clarendon Press, Oxford, 1895. Pagination is approximate.

The Mencius is one of the Four Books of the Confucian canon, recording the dialogues and teachings of Mencius (Mengzi, 372–289 BCE), the most influential interpreter of Confucius. It develops a systematic account of human nature as innately good, the four moral sprouts (sì duān), righteous governance through benevolent rule, and the moral psychology of self-cultivation. The book is both philosophy and political advocacy — Mencius traveled between warring states trying to persuade rulers to adopt benevolent government.

## Key Passages

### Book II, Part A — The Four Sprouts (Sì Duān)

> "Mencius said: 'All men have a mind that cannot bear to see the suffering of others. ... If men suddenly see a child about to fall into a well, they will without exception experience a feeling of alarm and distress. They will feel so not because they wish to gain the favor of the child's parents, nor because they seek the praise of their neighbors and friends, nor because they would dislike the reputation of not having felt so. From this case we may perceive that the feeling of commiseration is essential to man, that the feeling of shame and dislike is essential to man, that the feeling of modesty and complaisance is essential to man, and that the feeling of approving and disapproving is essential to man. The feeling of commiseration is the sprout of benevolence. The feeling of shame and dislike is the sprout of righteousness. The feeling of modesty and complaisance is the sprout of propriety. The feeling of approving and disapproving is the sprout of wisdom.'"

— Mencius, *The Mencius*, II.A.6, trans. Legge (adapted), 1895

*Gloss: The cornerstone of Mencian moral psychology — the four sprouts (sì duān) from which the four cardinal virtues grow. The child-at-the-well thought experiment is designed to isolate the purely spontaneous, non-calculative nature of compassion: the response arises before any consideration of reward or reputation. All human beings possess these four sprouts; moral failure is not the absence of the sprouts but the failure to cultivate (yǎng) and extend (tuī) them. The passage provides the developmental account that the Book I selection only implies: rén and yì are not imposed from outside but grown from within.*

### Book II, Part A — The Flood-like Vital Energy (Hào Rán Zhī Qì)

> "'I am skillful,' said Gongsun Chou, 'in nourishing my flood-like vital energy.' 'What do you mean by flood-like vital energy?' asked Mencius. 'It is difficult to describe. It is the energy — supremely great, supremely strong. Nourish it with integrity and place no obstacle in its path, and it will fill the space between Heaven and Earth. It is the energy that matches righteousness with the Way; without these, it starves. It is born of accumulated righteousness; it cannot be appropriated by anyone through a sporadic show of righteousness. If anything in one's conduct dissatisfies the heart, it starves.'"

— Mencius, *The Mencius*, II.A.2, trans. Legge / Lau (adapted), 1895/1970

*Gloss: The hào rán zhī qì — the flood-like vital energy — is Mencius's most distinctive contribution to the theory of moral cultivation. It is not a concept of rén or yì directly but the energetic basis of their practice: the moral power that arises from "accumulated righteousness" (jí yì) — the steady, daily practice of right action, not sporadic moral heroism. The energy is physical as well as ethical: it "fills the space between Heaven and Earth," suggesting that moral cultivation has cosmological effects. The phrase "if anything in one's conduct dissatisfies the heart, it starves" connects yi precisely to the internal criterion of self-examination.*

### Book III, Part A — The Debate on Human Nature with Gaozi

> "Gaozi said: 'Human nature is like the qì willow, and righteousness is like a cup or bowl. To make human nature benevolent and righteous is like making the willow into cups and bowls.' Mencius replied: 'Can you make cups and bowls from the willow without violating its nature? You must do violence to the willow before you can make cups and bowls. If you do violence to human nature to make it benevolent and righteous, then all men will regard benevolence and righteousness as calamities.'"

— Mencius, *The Mencius*, VI.A.1, trans. Legge (adapted), 1895

*Gloss: The opening of the famous debate with Gaozi — the rival philosopher who held that human nature is morally neutral (like uncarved wood) and that rén and yì are products of external shaping (like carving). Mencius's rebuttal is precise: if virtue requires "violating" nature, then virtue will be experienced as violation — "calamities" rather than fulfillments. The metaphor of carving presupposes that the raw material has no intrinsic direction; Mencius insists it does. This passage is the most concentrated statement of the Mencian-Xunzian disagreement: Xunzi (not in corpus) will later argue that carving is exactly what human nature needs.*

### Book VI, Part A — The Ox Mountain Parable

> "Mencius said: 'The trees of Ox Mountain were once beautiful. But being situated on the borders of a large state, they were hewn down with axes and billhooks — could they retain their beauty? Yet through the activity of the vegetative life day and night, and the nourishing influence of rain and dew, they were not without buds and sprouts springing forth. But then cattle and goats came and browsed upon them. When people see the barrenness of the mountain, they think it was never wooded. But how can this state be the nature of the mountain? ... So also of what properly belongs to man: can the mind of any man be without benevolence and righteousness? The way in which a man loses his proper goodness of mind is like the way the trees are denuded by axes.'"

— Mencius, *The Mencius*, VI.A.8, trans. Legge / Lau (adapted), 1895/1970

*Gloss: The Ox Mountain parable — Mencius's most vivid argument for innate goodness and the explanation of why it is so often invisible. The mountain is naturally wooded; the barrenness we see is the result of deforestation and overgrazing — but the mountain never ceases to regenerate (new buds and sprouts appear with every rain). Similarly, the mind of the worst person is never completely without rén and yì — the sprouts regenerate. Moral failure is environmental assault, not constitutional defect. The parable is directed against those who, seeing human wickedness, conclude that human nature is bad — they are mistaking the barren mountain for the mountain's nature.*

### Book VI, Part A — The Greater and Lesser Parts of the Self

> "Mencius said: 'There is no man who is not good at something. ... The organs of hearing and sight do not think and are obscured by things. When one thing comes into contact with another, it is led away. The organ of the mind is thought — by thinking, it apprehends; by not thinking, it fails to apprehend. This is what Heaven has given to us. Let a man first stand firmly in the greater part of his being, and the lesser part will not be able to take it from him. It is simply this which makes the great man.'"

— Mencius, *The Mencius*, VI.A.15, trans. Legge / Lau (adapted), 1895/1970

*Gloss: The distinction between the "greater part" (dà tǐ — the heart-mind, xīn) and the "lesser part" (xiǎo tǐ — the senses). The heart-mind thinks (sī); the senses merely react. The "great man" (dà rén) is one who has established the priority of the thinking heart over the reactive senses. This is not ascetic denial of the senses but proper ordering: the senses serve the heart, not the reverse. Rén and yì are the fruits of thinking — not abstract reasoning but the reflective self-examination that recognizes the sprouts and cultivates them. "Standing firmly in the greater part" is the Mencian definition of self-cultivation.*

### Book VII, Part A — The Heart-Mind's Full Development

> "Mencius said: 'He who has exhausted all his mental constitution knows his nature. Knowing his nature, he knows Heaven. Preserving one's mental constitution and nourishing one's nature is the way to serve Heaven. When neither a premature death nor a long life causes a man any double-mindedness, but he waits in the cultivation of his personal character for whatever issue — this is the way in which he establishes his destiny.'"

— Mencius, *The Mencius*, VII.A.1, trans. Legge / Lau (adapted), 1895/1970

*Gloss: The opening of Book VII — Mencius's most metaphysical passage. "Exhausting" (jìn) the heart-mind leads to knowledge of one's nature (xìng), which leads to knowledge of Heaven (tiān). The chain is: introspection → self-knowledge → cosmic knowledge. This is not mysticism but moral epistemology: by fully developing the four sprouts within oneself, one grasps the moral structure of the cosmos, because human nature and Heaven are continuous. "Establishing one's destiny" (lì mìng) means accepting whatever life brings — premature death or longevity — while remaining single-mindedly committed to self-cultivation. This is rén and yì as cosmic alignment, not merely social virtue.*

### Book VII, Part B — The People Are the Most Important

> "Mencius said: 'The people are the most important element in a nation; the altars to the spirits of the land and grain are the next; the sovereign is the lightest. Therefore to gain the hearts of the peasantry is the way to become sovereign.'"

— Mencius, *The Mencius*, VII.B.14, trans. Legge, 1895

*Gloss: The most radical political statement in the Mencius — the people (mín) rank above the spirits of land and grain (shè jì), and both rank above the sovereign (jūn). This is not democratic theory in the modern sense but a hierarchical inversion that places the welfare of the common people as the criterion of legitimate rule. A sovereign who "lightly treats" the people may be deposed — Mencius famously defended the right of revolution against tyrants. Yì in its political register is this principle: the ruler's righteousness is measured by the people's welfare, not by the ruler's sincerity or intention.*
