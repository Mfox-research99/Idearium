## Key Passages

### 1. The Definition: Shamanism as Technique of Ecstasy (Chapter 1)

> "Shamanism in the strict sense is pre-eminently a religious phenomenon of Siberia and Central Asia. The word itself comes to us, through the Russian, from the Tungusic *šaman*. But throughout the immense area comprising North and Central Asia, the magico-religious life of society centers on the shaman. This is not to say that he is the sole manipulator of the sacred, nor that religious activity is entirely absorbed by him. In many tribes the sacrificing priest coexists with the shaman, not to mention the fact that every head of a family is also the head of the domestic cult. Nevertheless, the shaman remains the dominating figure, for throughout this whole region, where the ecstatic experience is considered the religious experience par excellence, the shaman, and the shaman alone, is the great master of ecstasy."

Eliade's foundational claim: shamanism is not a grab-bag of primitive magic but a specific technique — the mastery of ecstatic trance for communal purposes. The shaman is distinguished from other religious practitioners (priests, healers, sorcerers) by this technique. The definition delimits the study's scope while establishing that ecstasy — not belief, not ritual office — is the defining feature.

— Eliade, *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy*, Chapter 1, trans. Trask, Princeton, 1964

### 2. Initiation: Sickness, Death, and Dismemberment (Chapter 2)

> "The future shaman is distinguished by a crisis that he sometimes goes so far as to call his madness. The initiatory sickness very closely follows the pattern of the initiatory death: the candidate's sufferings correspond to the tortures of death and dismemberment. He is cut up by the spirits; his bones are cleaned, the flesh scraped off, the body fluids thrown away, and his eyes torn from their sockets. The operation is performed by the spirits of the dead or by the souls of the old shamans, and it takes place in the other world."

The initiatory crisis is universal across shamanic cultures: the candidate is chosen (often unwillingly) by the spirits, falls into a sickness that resembles madness, and undergoes a visionary experience of death and remaking. This is not pathology but transformation. The shaman returns with new capacities precisely because they have been dismembered and reassembled — a pattern that reappears in ecstatic traditions worldwide.

— Eliade, *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy*, Chapter 2, trans. Trask, Princeton, 1964

### 3. The Cosmic Tree and the Shamanic Journey (Chapter 8)

> "The shamanic tree is the Cosmic Tree, the Tree of the World. It is by climbing it, or by flying to its summit, that the shaman undertakes his celestial journey. The birch tree that the Altai shaman climbs, the seven or nine notches cut in it representing the seven or nine heavens, is the concrete symbol of the World Tree. And the shaman's drum, made from the wood of this tree, is itself a microcosm: the drum-skin represents the world, the images painted on it represent the three cosmic zones, and the drumstick striking it represents the axis mundi."

The axis mundi — Eliade's central concept from *The Sacred and the Profane* — finds its most dynamic expression in shamanism. The cosmos is structured vertically (underworld, earth, heavens), and the shaman is the one who traverses these zones. The journey is not metaphorical: the shaman climbs, flies, descends. The tools of the journey — drum, costume, tree-pole — are material technologies of transcendence.

— Eliade, *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy*, Chapter 8, trans. Trask, Princeton, 1964

### 4. Possession vs. Ecstasy — The Crucial Distinction (Chapter 1)

> "A first definition of this complex phenomenon, and perhaps the least hazardous, will be: shamanism = technique of ecstasy. The shaman is a specialist in a trance during which his soul is believed to leave his body and ascend to the sky or descend to the underworld. This 'ecstatic journey' distinguishes the shaman from the 'possessed' person. The shaman controls the spirits; he does not become their instrument. It is the shaman who ascends or descends; spirits never take possession of him."

This distinction is among Eliade's most consequential contributions. The possessed person is a passive vessel — spirits act through them. The shaman is an active agent — they journey to the spirits, negotiate with them, command them. This is not a minor taxonomic point. It reframes shamanism as a discipline of consciousness rather than a surrender to forces, with implications for how we understand agency, technique, and the relationship between self and sacred.

— Eliade, *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy*, Chapter 1, trans. Trask, Princeton, 1964

### 5. The Shamanic Costume as Hierophany (Chapter 5)

> "The shamanic costume constitutes a religious hierophany and a cosmography in itself. It discloses not only a sacred presence but also cosmic symbols and the itinerary of the ecstatic journey. In donning it, the shaman transcends profane space and prepares to enter into contact with the spiritual world. Properly studied, the costume reveals the whole system of shamanism as clearly as the myths and techniques."

Eliade treats material culture as theology. The shaman's costume is not decoration or performance; it is a wearable cosmos. Each element — iron pendants representing the skeleton, bird feathers enabling flight, the headdress as a portal — encodes a complete religious system. The shaman does not merely believe; they dress, and in dressing, become what they wear.

— Eliade, *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy*, Chapter 5, trans. Trask, Princeton, 1964

### 6. The Drum as Axis Mundi (Chapter 5)

> "The drum has a role of the first importance in shamanic seances. Its symbolism is complex, and its magical functions are manifold. The drum is indispensable in conducting the shamanic seance. Whether it helps the shaman to reach the sky, or whether it summons and 'imprisons' the spirits, or whether the drumming itself makes possible the ecstatic experience, it is always the drum that carries the shaman to the Center of the World and enables him to effect the break-through in level."

The drum is the primary technology of shamanic ecstasy. Its rhythm alters consciousness; its construction (wood from the World Tree, skin from a sacrificed animal) makes it a microcosm; its sound is the voice of the spirits. Eliade treats it not as musical accompaniment but as a tool for ontological transformation — as essential to the shamanic technique as the journey itself.

— Eliade, *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy*, Chapter 5, trans. Trask, Princeton, 1964

### 7. Shamanism and Cosmogony — The Return to Origins (Chapter 11)

> "The shamanic seance always includes a return to the illud tempus, the mythical time of the beginning. The shaman goes back to the primordial situation when the world was created, when the bonds between heaven and earth were still intact, when communication with the gods was possible for all beings. The ecstatic journey is a reactualization of the mythical age."

The shaman does not merely visit the spirit world; they re-enter the time before time. This return to origins is not nostalgia — it is technique. By accessing the moment when the world was still malleable, the shaman can heal, retrieve lost souls, negotiate with the forces that govern the present. Eliade connects this to his broader theory of myth as eternal return: the shaman is the living practitioner of what ritual only commemorates.

— Eliade, *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy*, Chapter 11, trans. Trask, Princeton, 1964

### 8. The Shaman as Psychopomp — Guiding the Dead (Chapter 6)

> "The shaman is the great specialist in the human soul; he alone 'sees' it, for he knows its form and its destiny. The shaman is a psychopomp, and he may also be a psychotherapist: he can identify the soul's whereabouts and bring it back to the body. Wherever the 'disease' is considered a 'loss' of the soul, the shaman is indispensable, for he alone can undertake the ecstatic journey that results in finding and capturing the fugitive soul."

Soul-loss is the shamanic diagnosis of illness: the patient is sick because part of their soul has wandered away or been stolen. The shaman's journey is a rescue mission. Eliade frames this as a coherent medical system — one that treats the soul as an organ that can be lost, retrieved, and reintegrated. This is not superstition; it is a different ontology of illness, with its own logic, technique, and evidence.

— Eliade, *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy*, Chapter 6, trans. Trask, Princeton, 1964

### 9. The Decline of Shamanism and Its Modern Residues (Conclusion)

> "The 'decadence' of shamanism is a historical fact, but shamanic motifs and techniques are not thereby abolished. They are integrated into other religious systems, or they survive in the form of folklore and popular beliefs. What is more: certain shamanic techniques, 'purified' of their ethno-religious context, have been taken over by various modern spiritual movements. The shamanic ecstasy has not disappeared from the contemporary world."

Eliade's conclusion opens the door to contemporary relevance. Shamanism persists — in folklore, in the structures of other religions, and in the modern revival of ecstatic technique separated from its original cultural matrices. This is not mere survival; it is transmutation. The patterns Eliade identifies — ecstatic journey, soul retrieval, axis mundi, initiation through ordeal — recur because they answer to permanent human structures of experience.

— Eliade, *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy*, Conclusion, trans. Trask, Princeton, 1964
