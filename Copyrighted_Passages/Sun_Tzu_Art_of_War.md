## Key Passages

### 1. Supreme Excellence (Chapter 3: Attack by Stratagem)

> "To fight and conquer in all your battles is not supreme excellence; supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting."

The most famous line in the text, and the most radical. War, in Sun Tzu's framework, is a failure — the resort to violence when strategy has not sufficed. The highest general wins without bloodshed, through positioning, deception, and the manipulation of the adversary's perceptions. The art of war is the art of making war unnecessary.

— Sun Tzu, *The Art of War*, Chapter 3, trans. Giles, Luzac, 1910

### 2. Know Yourself and Your Enemy (Chapter 3: Attack by Stratagem)

> "If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result of a hundred battles. If you know yourself but not the enemy, for every victory gained you will also suffer a defeat. If you know neither the enemy nor yourself, you will succumb in every battle."

War is fundamentally an information problem. Victory depends not on courage or numbers primarily but on knowledge — of one's own capacities and the adversary's dispositions. The general who fights without intelligence fights blind.

— Sun Tzu, *The Art of War*, Chapter 3, trans. Giles, Luzac, 1910

### 3. The Five Constant Factors (Chapter 1: Laying Plans)

> "The art of war is governed by five constant factors, to be taken into account in one's deliberations: The Moral Law, Heaven, Earth, The Commander, and Method and Discipline."

Sun Tzu opens by enumerating the dimensions of strategic assessment. The Moral Law (Dao/道) is the alignment between ruler and people — whether they will follow him into danger. Heaven is weather and timing. Earth is terrain and distance. The Commander embodies wisdom, sincerity, benevolence, courage, and strictness. Method is organization and logistics. No battle is decided by one factor alone.

— Sun Tzu, *The Art of War*, Chapter 1, trans. Giles, Luzac, 1910

### 4. All Warfare Is Based on Deception (Chapter 1: Laying Plans)

> "All warfare is based on deception. Hence, when able to attack, seem unable; when using our forces, seem inactive; when near, make the enemy believe we are far away; when far away, make him believe we are near."

War is not a contest of strength but of perception. The general shapes what the enemy sees, and through what he sees, shapes what the enemy does. Appearing weak where one is strong, retreating to draw the enemy forward, feinting east to strike west — these are not tricks but the essence of strategy itself.

— Sun Tzu, *The Art of War*, Chapter 1, trans. Giles, Luzac, 1910

### 5. The General as Guardian of the State (Chapter 2: Waging War)

> "The general who advances without coveting fame and retreats without fearing disgrace, whose only thought is to protect his country and do good service for his sovereign, is the jewel of the kingdom."

Sun Tzu's ideal commander is not driven by personal glory but by duty to the state. This restraint — the subordination of ego to strategic purpose — is what distinguishes the great general from the merely aggressive one. The best commander is almost invisible; his victories seem inevitable only in retrospect.

— Sun Tzu, *The Art of War*, Chapter 2, trans. Giles, Luzac, 1910

### 6. The Best Victory (Chapter 4: Tactical Dispositions)

> "What the ancients called a clever fighter is one who not only wins, but excels in winning with ease. Hence his victories bring him neither reputation for wisdom nor credit for courage... He wins his battles by making no mistakes. Making no mistakes is what establishes the certainty of victory, for it means conquering an enemy that is already defeated."

The paradox of supreme competence: the general who never fights desperate last stands, who never needs a dramatic reversal, appears unremarkable. His victories look easy because he has done the unglamorous work of positioning, supply, and intelligence that makes the outcome inevitable before the battle begins.

— Sun Tzu, *The Art of War*, Chapter 4, trans. Giles, Luzac, 1910

### 7. Water Has No Constant Shape (Chapter 6: Weak Points and Strong)

> "As water has no constant form, so in war there are no constant conditions. He who can modify his tactics in relation to his opponent and thereby succeed in winning may be called a heaven-born captain."

Strategy is not the application of fixed rules but the capacity to respond to changing conditions. The five factors, the principles of deception, the maxims about terrain — all are starting points, not recipes. The general who cannot adapt is a general who will be defeated by one who can.

— Sun Tzu, *The Art of War*, Chapter 6, trans. Giles, Luzac, 1910

### 8. The Pinnacle of Military Deployment (Chapter 6: Weak Points and Strong)

> "Military tactics are like water; for water in its natural course runs away from high places and hastens downwards... Therefore, just as water retains no constant shape, so in warfare there are no constant conditions."

A restatement and extension: the formless commander is the one who cannot be anticipated. By refusing a fixed formation, by adapting to the shape of the adversary, the general becomes ungraspable. The enemy prepares for what is known; against what has no fixed form, there is no preparation.

— Sun Tzu, *The Art of War*, Chapter 6, trans. Giles, Luzac, 1910
