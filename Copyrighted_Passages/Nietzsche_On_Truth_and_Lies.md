## Key Passages

### 1. The Intellect's Purpose (Section 1)

> "In some remote corner of the universe, poured out and glittering in innumerable solar systems, there once was a star on which clever animals invented knowledge. That was the most arrogant and mendacious minute of 'world history,' but nevertheless, it was only a minute. After nature had drawn a few breaths, the star cooled and congealed, and the clever animals had to die."

Nietzsche opens with a cosmic deflation of human pretension. Knowledge — that which we take to be our species' highest achievement — is framed as an invention of animals who mistook their brief tenure for the meaning of the universe. The essay begins where epistemology usually ends: with the question of whether knowledge has any cosmic significance at all.

— Nietzsche, "On Truth and Lies in a Nonmoral Sense," §1, trans. Breazeale, Humanities Press, 1979

### 2. The Intellect as Means of Preservation (Section 1)

> "The intellect, as a means for the preservation of the individual, unfolds its chief powers in dissimulation. For this is the means by which the weaker, less robust individuals preserve themselves, since they are denied the horns and the sharp teeth of beasts with which to wage the struggle for existence."

Far from being the organ of truth, the intellect is, in its origin, the organ of deception. The human animal, physically weak, survives by cunning — by appearing other than it is. Truth is a secondary development, a social convention layered onto an apparatus designed for dissembling.

— Nietzsche, "On Truth and Lies in a Nonmoral Sense," §1, trans. Breazeale, Humanities Press, 1979

### 3. What Is Truth? A Mobile Army of Metaphors (Section 1)

> "What then is truth? A mobile army of metaphors, metonymies, and anthropomorphisms — in short, a sum of human relations which have been enhanced, transposed, and embellished poetically and rhetorically, and which after long use seem firm, canonical, and obligatory to a people: truths are illusions about which one has forgotten that this is what they are."

The most famous passage in the essay. Truth is not the correspondence of proposition to fact but a sedimentation of forgotten metaphors. A word like "tree" is a metaphor (this particular sensory bundle = that category of being) that has been worn smooth by use until its metaphorical character disappears. Truth is the illusion that has forgotten its origin.

— Nietzsche, "On Truth and Lies in a Nonmoral Sense," §1, trans. Breazeale, Humanities Press, 1979

### 4. The Obligation to Lie (Section 1)

> "The duty which society imposes in order to exist — to be truthful, i.e., to use the customary metaphors — means, in moral terms, the obligation to lie according to a fixed convention, to lie herd-like in a style obligatory for all. Now man of course forgets that this is the way things are for him. Thus he lies in the manner indicated, unconsciously and in accordance with habits which are centuries old; and precisely by means of this unconsciousness, by this forgetting, he arrives at his sense of truth."

The social contract, for Nietzsche, is a contract to lie in the same way. What we call "truthfulness" is conformity to shared metaphors. The person who refuses the shared illusion — who uses idiosyncratic metaphors — is not praised for accuracy but punished for deviance. Truth is herd morality applied to language.

— Nietzsche, "On Truth and Lies in a Nonmoral Sense," §1, trans. Breazeale, Humanities Press, 1979

### 5. The Drive to Form Metaphors (Section 2)

> "That drive to form metaphors, that fundamental human drive which one cannot dispense with for even a second, is not truly conquered by the construction of concepts. It seeks a new realm for its activity and another channel, and finds it in myth and in art generally."

Conceptual thought does not eliminate the metaphorical drive; it represses it into approved channels. But the drive resurfaces — in dreams, in art, in myth — where the rigid structures of language are relaxed and new configurations become possible. The artist is not the opposite of the rational thinker; the artist is the rational thinker's unconscious, returning.

— Nietzsche, "On Truth and Lies in a Nonmoral Sense," §2, trans. Breazeale, Humanities Press, 1979

### 6. The Building of Concepts (Section 1)

> "Every concept arises from the equation of unequal things. Just as it is certain that one leaf is never totally the same as another, so it is certain that the concept 'leaf' is formed by arbitrarily discarding these individual differences and by forgetting the distinguishing aspects."

A concept is an act of violence against the particular. In calling two things by the same name, we ignore their differences. The concept "leaf" erases the irreplaceable singularity of this leaf, here, now. Abstraction is the forgetting of difference. Science, built on concepts, is built on forgetting.

— Nietzsche, "On Truth and Lies in a Nonmoral Sense," §1, trans. Breazeale, Humanities Press, 1979

### 7. Man as Architectural Genius (Section 1)

> "One might invent such a fable and still not have illustrated sufficiently how wretched, how shadowy and flighty, how aimless and arbitrary, the human intellect appears in nature. There have been eternities when it did not exist; and when it is done for again, nothing will have happened."

The essay's framing returns in its closing movement. The human intellect is a local, temporary, cosmically insignificant phenomenon. It produces truths, but those truths have the same ontological status as a spider's web — a structure spun to catch what it needs, not a mirror of reality.

— Nietzsche, "On Truth and Lies in a Nonmoral Sense," §1, trans. Breazeale, Humanities Press, 1979
