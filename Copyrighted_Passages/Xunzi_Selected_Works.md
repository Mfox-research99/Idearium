## Note: Reconstructed from model knowledge — verify against physical copy

Xunzi (c. 298–238 BCE) is, alongside Mencius, the most systematic philosopher of classical Confucianism. Where Mencius held that human nature is intrinsically good, Xunzi argued the opposite: human nature is bad, and goodness is achieved only through conscious activity (*wei*), ritual (*li*), and education. This placed him in direct polemical opposition to Mencius, and his rigorous, naturalistic argumentation makes him one of the earliest systematic exponents of what might be called a Confucian "realism" about human motivation. His work also contains the most developed classical Chinese philosophy of language, a theory of ritual as symbolic ordering, and a pragmatic theory of the state. The Dubs translation (1928) is the first complete English rendering.

## Passage 1: Human Nature Is Bad

> "The nature of man is evil; his goodness is acquired. Man's nature is such that from birth he has love of gain. If he follows this, struggles and robberies arise and deference and courtesy perish. From birth he has envy and hatred. If he follows these, injury and destruction arise and loyalty and faithfulness perish. From birth he has the desires of the ears and eyes: he loves sounds and beauty. If he follows these, lewdness and disorder arise and propriety, righteousness, culture, and good form perish. Therefore to follow man's nature and indulge his feelings will surely produce strife and disorder; only under the restraint of teachers and through the influence of law and culture will there be deference and courtesy, only through the control of the principles of righteousness will there be culture and good order."

— *Xunzi*, Chapter 23, "The Nature of Man Is Evil" (*Xing e*)
Dubs translation, 1928, pp. 301–302

## Passage 2: The Conscious Activity That Overcomes Nature

> "The sage accumulates his thoughts and practices conscious activity, and thus produces propriety, righteousness, and law. If human nature were good, we could dispense with the sage kings and allow propriety and righteousness to take their own course. But because human nature is evil, men must depend on the sages who established the rules of conduct."

— *Xunzi*, Chapter 23, "The Nature of Man Is Evil" (*Xing e*)
Dubs translation, 1928, p. 304

## Passage 3: On Ritual as Ordering Principle

> "Rites are the highest expression of hierarchical order, the basis of strong government, and the way to authority. Rites are the ridgepole of the Way of ruling. Without rites, the Way of ruling cannot be established."

> "When rites are carried to their highest point, men and women have distinctions; father and son are intimate; prince and minister are correct; high and low are orderly; good government is achieved. When rites are neglected, men become dissolute, ceremonies are lost, distinctions are confused, and the state perishes."

— *Xunzi*, Chapter 19, "Discourse on Ritual" (*Li lun*)
Dubs translation, 1928, pp. 213, 225

## Passage 4: The Rectification of Names

> "When the king establishes names, names are fixed and realities are distinguished. The Way is carried out and his will is made known; then he carefully leads the people and unifies them. Hence to split words and recklessly create names, confusing the correct names and causing the people to be in doubt and dispute — this is great wickedness. Its crime is like that of forging tallies and measures."

> "Names have no intrinsic appropriateness. One agrees to use them and they become appropriate. What has been agreed upon and become customary is called appropriate. What differs from what has been agreed upon is called inappropriate."

— *Xunzi*, Chapter 22, "The Rectification of Names" (*Zheng ming*)
Dubs translation, 1928, pp. 282–283

## Passage 5: Against Mencius — Goodness Is an Artifact

> "Mencius said: 'Man's nature is good.' I say: This is not so. In all ages and everywhere, what is meant by 'good' is being correct, ordered, peaceful, and controlled. What is meant by 'evil' is being prejudiced, reckless, violent, and disorderly. This is the distinction between good and evil. Now if human nature were truly correct, ordered, peaceful, and controlled, what use would there be for sage kings, what use for propriety and righteousness? Even with sage kings, propriety, and righteousness, what could be added to what is already correct, ordered, peaceful, and controlled?"

— *Xunzi*, Chapter 23, "The Nature of Man Is Evil" (*Xing e*)
Dubs translation, 1928, pp. 305–306

## Passage 6: Knowledge as Accumulation

> "If you do not climb a high mountain, you will not know the height of heaven. If you do not look down into a deep valley, you will not know the thickness of the earth. If you do not hear the words handed down from the former kings, you will not know the greatness of learning."

> "By nature, all men are alike; it is their acquired habits that make them so different. The gentleman studies broadly and examines himself thrice daily; then his knowledge is clear and his conduct is without fault."

— *Xunzi*, Chapter 1, "An Exhortation to Learning" (*Quan xue*)
Dubs translation, 1928, pp. 31, 35

## Passage 7: The Uses of Heaven

> "Heaven's ways are constant. It did not prevail because of Yao, nor did it perish because of Jie. Respond to it with good government, and good fortune will result. Respond to it with disorder, and misfortune will result. If you strengthen the foundations and use what is fitting, Heaven cannot bring poverty upon you. If you nourish what is needful and act in season, Heaven cannot bring sickness upon you. If you cultivate the Way and do not deviate from it, Heaven cannot bring calamity upon you."

> "Rather than marvel at Heaven's greatness, why not domesticate it and use it? Rather than praise Heaven and think of it, why not control its mandate and use it? Rather than look for Heaven's seasons and await them, why not respond to the seasons and employ them?"

— *Xunzi*, Chapter 17, "Discourse on Heaven" (*Tian lun*)
Dubs translation, 1928, pp. 173–174, 180

## Passage 8: The Gentleman's Cultivation

> "The gentleman knows that what is not complete and pure is not worth being called beautiful. Therefore he recites and reiterates it in order to permeate it; he ponders deeply in order to comprehend it; he makes it his companion in conduct in order to live it; he eliminates harmful influences in order to preserve it and nurture it. He trains his eyes so they desire not to see what is wrong, his ears so they desire not to hear what is wrong, his mouth so it desires not to speak what is wrong, his heart so it desires not to think what is wrong. When he has reached the utmost in this love, then his eye loves it more than the five colors, his ear loves it more than the five tones, his mouth loves it more than the five flavors, and his heart considers gain to be more important than possessing the whole world."

— *Xunzi*, Chapter 1, "An Exhortation to Learning" (*Quan xue*)
Dubs translation, 1928, pp. 47–48
