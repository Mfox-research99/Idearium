# Idea: Witness

## Working definition

What does it mean to bear witness? The Western tradition frames this as a question of legal testimony, religious martyrdom, epistemological reliance on the reports of others, and theological attestation to divine truth. The term "martyr" itself derives from the Greek μάρτυς (witness), linking death for faith to the act of testifying. Other traditions approach witness through the confession of faith (shahada) or the concept of a silent inner witness (sakshi); see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Legal Witness

The rules governing who may testify, how many witnesses are required, and the weight of testimony in courts of law.

### Sub-question: Religious Witness (Martyrdom)

Bearing witness to faith through suffering and death, and the theological rewards attached to such testimony.

### Sub-question: Epistemological Witness

The role of testimony in human knowledge — how we assess the credibility of witnesses and the probability of reported events.

### Sub-question: Theological Witness

The divine testimony of the Holy Spirit, the Father, and the Son, and the inner witness of the believer.

## Western canon

### Plato — *Laws*

> *Book XI, 937a–d* — "If any man refuses to be a witness, he who wants him shall summon him, and he who is summoned shall come to the trial; and if he knows and is willing to bear witness, let him bear witness, but if he says he does not know let him swear by the three divinities Zeus, and Apollo, and Themis, that he does not, and have no more to do with the cause. … A slave of either sex and a child shall be allowed to give evidence and to plead, but only in cases of murder; and they must produce sufficient sureties that they will certainly remain until the trial."
>
> *Gloss: Plato codifies the legal obligations and limitations of witness testimony, including the oath and the exclusion of slaves and children except in capital cases.*

> *Book XI, 937b–c* — "If a man is twice convicted of perjury, he is not to be required, if three times, he is not to be allowed to bear witness, or, if he persists in bearing witness, is to be punished with death."
>
> *Gloss: The penalty for repeated perjury is loss of the right to testify, and ultimately death — the state treats false witness as a capital offense against justice.*

Trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1871.

### Thomas Hobbes — *Leviathan*

> *Chapter 42* — "A Martyr is, (to give the true definition of the word) a Witnesse of the Resurrection of Jesus the Messiah; which none can be but those that conversed with him on earth, and saw him after he was risen: For a Witnesse must have seen what he testifieth, or else his testimony is not good."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes restricts the title "martyr" to those who were eyewitnesses of the Resurrection, grounding the concept in the strict legal sense of testimony.*

> *Chapter 42* — "But what then shall we say of all those Martyrs we read of in the History of the Church, that they have needlessely cast away their lives? … If any man shall accuse this doctrine, as repugnant to true, and unfeigned Christianity; I ask him, in case there should be a subject in any Christian Common-wealth, that should be inwardly in his heart of the Mahometan Religion, whether if his Soveraign Command him to bee present at the divine service of the Christian Church, and that on pain of death, he think that Mamometan obliged in conscience to suffer death for that cause, rather than to obey that command of his lawful Prince."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes challenges the traditional veneration of martyrs by arguing that obedience to the sovereign takes precedence over private religious conviction.*

1651.

### Augustine — *City of God*

> *Book XXII, Chapter 9* — "To what do these miracles witness, but to this faith which preaches Christ risen in the flesh, and ascended with the same into heaven? For the martyrs themselves were martyrs, that is to say, witnesses of this faith, drawing upon themselves by their testimony the hatred of the world, and conquering the world not by resisting it, but by dying."
>
> *Gloss: Augustine defines martyrdom as testimony to the Resurrection, and the miracles performed at martyrs' tombs confirm that faith.*

> *Book VIII, Chapter 27* — "We do not build temples, and ordain priests, rites, and sacrifices for these same martyrs; for they are not our gods, but their God is our God. Certainly we honour their reliquaries, as the memorials of holy men of God who strove for the truth even to the death of their bodies, that the true religion might be made known."
>
> *Gloss: Christians honour martyrs as witnesses, not as gods; the distinction between worship and memorial is carefully maintained.*

Trans. Marcus Dods, 1872.

### David Hume — *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*

> *Section X, Part I* — "The plain consequence is (and it is a general maxim worthy of our attention), 'That no testimony is sufficient to establish a miracle, unless the testimony be of such a kind, that its falsehood would be more miraculous, than the fact, which it endeavours to establish; and even in that case there is a mutual destruction of arguments, and the superior only gives us an assurance suitable to that degree of force, which remains, after deducting the inferior.'"
>
> *Gloss: Hume's famous maxim sets a probabilistic standard for evaluating testimony about miracles: the credibility of the witness must outweigh the improbability of the event.*

1748.

### John Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*

> *Book IV, Chapter XVI, §9* — "As the arguments and proofs PRO and CON, upon due examination, nicely weighing every particular circumstance, shall to any one appear, upon the whole matter, in a greater or less degree to preponderate on either side; so they are fitted to produce in the mind such different entertainments, as we call BELIEF, CONJECTURE, GUESS, DOUBT, WAVERING, DISTRUST, DISBELIEF, &c."
>
> *Gloss: Locke treats testimony as one source of probability, subject to rational weighing of circumstances — the degree of assent varies with the evidence.*

1690.

### William Blackstone — *Commentaries on the Laws of England*

> *Book III, Chapter 29* — "One witneſs (if credible) is ſufficient evidence to a jury of any ſingle fact; though undoubtedly the concurrence of two or more corroborates the proof. Yet our law conſiders that there are many tranſactions to which only one perſon is privy; and therefore does not always demand the teſtimony of two, as the civil law univerſally requires."
>
> *Gloss: English common law departs from the civil law's two-witness rule, accepting a single credible witness where the nature of the transaction allows no more.*

> *Book IV, Chapter 27* — "Baron Montesquieu lays it down for a rule, that those laws which condemn a man to death in any case, on the deposition of a single witness, are fatal to liberty: and he adds this reason, that the witness who affirms, and the accused who denies, make an equal balance; there is a necessity therefore to call in a third man to incline the scale. But this seems to be carrying matters too far: for there are some crimes, in which the very privacy of their nature excludes the possibility of having more than one witness; must these therefore escape unpunished?"
>
> *Gloss: Blackstone defends the English practice against Montesquieu's objection, arguing that the nature of some crimes makes a single witness necessary.*

1765–1769.

### Edward Gibbon — *The Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire*

> *Chapter XVI* — "They inculcated with becoming diligence, that the fire of martyrdom supplied every defect and expiated every sin; that while the souls of ordinary Christians were obliged to pass through a slow and painful purification, the triumphant sufferers entered into the immediate fruition of eternal bliss, where, in the society of the patriarchs, the apostles, and the prophets, they reigned with Christ, and acted as his assessors in the universal judgment of mankind."
>
> *Gloss: Gibbon describes the early Church's teaching that martyrdom granted immediate salvation and a role in the final judgment — a powerful incentive to bear witness unto death.*

1776–1789.

### The Holy Bible (King James Version)

> *1 John 5:7–8* — "For there are three that bear record in heaven, the Father, the Word, and the Holy Ghost: and these three are one. And there are three that bear witness in earth, the Spirit, and the water, and the blood: and these three agree in one."
>
> *Gloss: The Johannine epistle presents a threefold divine witness in heaven and a threefold earthly witness, grounding Christian faith in a celestial testimony.*

> *John 5:31–36* — "If I bear witness of myself, my witness is not true. There is another that beareth witness of me; and I know that the witness which he witnesseth of me is true. … But I have greater witness than that of John: for the works which the Father hath given me to finish, the same works that I do, bear witness of me, that the Father hath sent me."
>
> *Gloss: Jesus distinguishes his self-testimony from the Father's witness through works, establishing a hierarchy of divine attestation.*

1611.

## Reframings

### Islamic — Shahada

The Islamic tradition centers witness in the *shahada* ("I bear witness that there is no god but God, and Muhammad is His messenger"), which is both the confession of faith and the foundational legal testimony. The Qur'an repeatedly emphasizes God as the ultimate witness between the Prophet and his people. In legal contexts, the Qur'an prescribes two male witnesses or one male and two females for contracts, and requires oaths when witnesses are unavailable.

> *Surah 6:19* — "SAY: What thing is weightiest in bearing witness? SAY: God is witness between me and you; and this Koran hath been revealed to me that I should warn you by it, and all whom it shall reach. What! will ye really bear witness that there are other gods with God? SAY: I bear no such witness."
>
> *Surah 5:106* — "O believers! let there be witnesses between you, when death draweth nigh to any of you, at the time of making the testament; two witnesses–just men from among yourselves, or two others of a different tribe from yourselves."
>
> Trans. J.M. Rodwell, 1861.

→ See also the Term page for [[Terms/Shahada]] (not yet created; the concept is closely tied to *Tawhid*).

### Sufi — The Inner Witness

In Persian Sufi poetry, witness takes on a mystical dimension: the beloved becomes a witness to divine truth, and even an infant can bear testimony to innocence. Jami's *Yusuf and Zulaikha* includes a scene where a three-month-old infant miraculously speaks to exonerate Yusuf, serving as an "infant witness" to divine justice.

> *Yusuf and Zulaikha* — "With her babe on her bosom but three months old / She seemed her own soul in her arms to hold. / No line in the volume of life had it read, / And its tongue like a lily's no word had said. … The Infant Witness."
>
> Trans. R.T.H. Griffith, 1882.

This motif resonates with the broader Sufi concept of the *shahid* (witness) as one who perceives the divine in creation, and with the idea that the heart itself is a witness to God's unity.

### Hindu / Vedantic — Sakshi (Witness Consciousness)

The corpus lacks pre-1929 primary-source passages that directly use the term *sakshi* (witness) in the Vedantic sense. However, the concept is central to Advaita Vedanta: the *Atman* is the silent witness of all mental and physical phenomena, distinct from the ego and the body. This inner witness is not a testifier in the legal or religious sense but the unchanging consciousness that observes the changing states of waking, dream, and deep sleep. The English translations of the Upanishads available in the corpus (e.g., Müller's translations) render *sakshi* as "witness" in passages such as the *Mundaka Upanishad* 3.1.8–9, but these passages were not retrieved in the current search. A future expansion of the corpus or a targeted search for "witness" in the Upanishads would supply direct material.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Truth]] — Witness is the act of testifying to what one holds to be true; the credibility of the witness depends on the truth of the testimony.
- [[Martyrdom]] — The Greek *martys* (witness) is the root of "martyr"; religious witness unto death is the paradigmatic form of martyrdom.
- [[Law]] — Legal systems depend on witness testimony; rules of evidence and perjury laws govern the reliability of witnesses.
- [[Faith]] — Religious witness is an act of faith, and the acceptance of testimony about miracles requires a prior commitment to the possibility of the supernatural.
- [[Miracle]] — Hume's critique of testimony about miracles is a central epistemological challenge to religious witness.
- [[Knowledge]] — Testimony is a source of probable knowledge; Locke and Hume analyze how we weigh the credibility of witnesses.

## Status notes

- The legal and epistemological dimensions of witness are well covered by Plato, Blackstone, Hume, and Locke.
- The religious martyrdom dimension is covered by Hobbes, Augustine, and Gibbon; the theological witness by the Johannine epistles and Gospel.
- The Islamic reframing uses Rodwell's translation of the Qur'an; the Sufi reframing uses Jami's poem.
- The Hindu/Vedantic reframing is noted as a gap: the corpus did not return direct passages on *sakshi* (witness consciousness) from the Upanishads. A targeted search for "witness" in Müller's Upanishad translations would likely yield material; this is deferred.
- The Blavatsky passage (Secret Doctrine) was considered but excluded as it is Theosophical rather than a primary Hindu source.
- The Shakespeare passage (As You Like It) and the Kern Lotus Sutra passage (on modern martyrdom) were excluded as not directly relevant to the core concept of witness.
- The Lilly (Christian Astrology) passages on "testimonies" in astrological judgment were excluded as they use the term in a technical astrological sense unrelated to the Idea.
