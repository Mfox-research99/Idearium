# Idea: World

## Working definition

What is the world—the physical cosmos, the human realm, or a spiritual order—and how does it relate to divine reality and human destiny? The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about creation, eschatology, and the distinction between the present age and the world to come, with additional threads treating the world as a living organism (macrocosm) or as a field of pragmatic resistance.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Cosmological world (physical universe)

The world as the ordered cosmos, its structure and material composition.

### Sub-question: Eschatological world (old / present / future)

The division of world-history into ages—the old world before the Flood, the present world, and the world to come.

### Sub-question: World as macrocosm / microcosm

The world as a living whole whose parts mirror the human being, or vice versa.

### Sub-question: World as illusion vs. reality

The world as deceptive, impermanent, or a veil over a truer reality.

### Sub-question: Plurality of worlds

The existence of multiple worlds, layered or interpenetrating, beyond the visible one.

## Western canon

### Manilius — *Astronomica*

> "God the World's Almighty Soul / By secret methods rules and guides the Whole; / By unseen passes He himself conveys / Through all the Mass, and every part obeys. / … / The World seems One, and all its parts ally'd."
>
> *Gloss: Manilius commits to a Stoic world-soul that unifies the cosmos; the world is a single, ensouled organism.*

### The Holy Bible (King James Version) — Gospel of John

> "I have given them thy word; and the world hath hated them, because they are not of the world, even as I am not of the world. I pray not that thou shouldest take them out of the world, but that thou shouldest keep them from the evil. They are not of the world, even as I am not of the world."
>
> *Gloss: Jesus establishes a dualism between the divine order and the worldly order; believers are in the world but not of it, marking the world as a domain of opposition to truth.*

### Thomas Hobbes — *Leviathan*

> "There are three worlds mentioned in Scripture, the Old World, the Present World, and the World to Come. … This is that WORLD, wherein Christ coming down from Heaven, in the clouds, with great power, and glory, shall send his Angels, and shall gather together his elect, from the four winds, and from the uttermost parts of the Earth, and thence forth reign over them, (under his Father) Everlastingly."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes divides world-history into three eschatological ages, grounding the concept of "world" in scriptural narrative and political theology.*

### William James — *Pragmatism*

> "In short the only fully rational world would be the world of wishing-caps, the world of telepathy, where every desire is fulfilled instanter, without having to consider or placate surrounding or intermediate powers. This is the Absolute's own world. … In our world, the wishes of the individual are only one condition. Other individuals are there with other wishes and they must be propitiated first. So Being grows under all sorts of resistances in this world of the many, and, from compromise to compromise, only gets organized gradually into what may be called secondarily rational shape."
>
> *Gloss: James rejects the idea of a pre‑packed rational world; the world is a field of resistances and compromises, its rationality achieved only secondarily through experience.*

## Reframings

### Buddhist — Sunyata / Dukkha

The Dhammapada treats the world as a bubble, a mirage, a dark place from which the wise are led out. The question is not "what is the world's structure" but "how does one relate to a world that is fundamentally impermanent and deceptive?" The wise do not touch it; they transcend it through sobriety and good deeds. This reframing shifts the focus from cosmology to soteriology.

→ See [[Terms/Sunyata]], [[Terms/Dukkha]]

### Indigenous Siberian (Chukchee) — Shamanism

The Chukchee conceive of multiple worlds (five, seven, or nine) arranged symmetrically above and below the earth, with a passage under the pole‑star. Seasons and day‑night cycles are reversed between worlds. Shamans and spirits travel between them. Here the world is not a single cosmos but a stack of realms, each with its own inhabitants and natural order.

→ See [[Ideas/Shamanism]]

### Theosophical (Blavatsky) — Interpenetrating worlds

Blavatsky describes "other worlds" as interpenetrating our own, invisible yet as objective to their inhabitants as ours is to us. This reframes the plurality of worlds not as separate locations in space but as co‑existing planes of existence.

→ See [[Ideas/Space]], [[Ideas/Magic]]

## Status notes

- Western canon section includes four authors (Manilius, John, Hobbes, James) covering cosmological, eschatological, macrocosmic, and pragmatic framings.
- Montaigne passage (Essays) was retrieved but dropped: it marvels at the concurrence of opinions across the world rather than committing to a position on what the world is.
- Blavatsky is included as a reframing note rather than a Western canon entry because her occult cosmology is not part of the classical Western canon; it serves as a bridge to the plurality-of-worlds theme.
- Indigenous Siberian material (Bogoras, Czaplicka) is used for the Shamanism reframing; the two sources are consistent and only one (Bogoras) is cited.
- No direct Term page exists for "World" in any tradition; the Buddhist reframing points to Sunyata and Dukkha as the closest conceptual neighbors.
- Future expansions could include Plato’s *Timaeus* (world as living creature), Augustine’s *City of God* (two cities/worlds), and Dante’s three‑world cosmology.
