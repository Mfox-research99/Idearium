# Idea: Courage

## Working definition

What enables a person to face danger, pain, or fear for the sake of a noble end. The Western tradition primarily frames this as a moral virtue (Aristotle's mean between cowardice and rashness) and as a civic virtue (Plato's guardians preserving correct opinion about what is to be feared). Nietzsche later re-frames it as the existential condition for self-overcoming — courage as the slayer of dejection itself. Other traditions approach the question through duty (dharma) or through the cultivation of fearless presence; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Civic Courage — the virtue of the guardian class

Plato locates courage in the warrior class of the ideal city: the preservation of lawful opinion about what is to be feared, under all circumstances.

### Sub-question: Courage as the Mean — the Aristotelian moral virtue

Aristotle defines courage as the mean state in respect of fear and confidence, choosing the noble line of action for its own sake, and distinguishes true courage from five spurious forms.

### Sub-question: Existential Courage — Nietzsche's revaluation

Nietzsche rejects the civic and mean-based models, presenting courage as the aggressive, self-overcoming force that slays dejection and enables the ascent to the Übermensch.

## Western canon

### Plato — *Republic*

> *Book IV, 429a–430c* — "The city will be courageous in virtue of a portion of herself which preserves under all circumstances that opinion about the nature of things to be feared and not to be feared in which our legislator educated them; and this is what you term courage."
>
> *Gloss: Courage is not a personal disposition but a civic property — the guardians' unshakeable adherence to the law's teaching on what merits fear.*

Trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1892.

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics*

> *Book III, Chapter XI* — "Courage is a mean state in respect of objects inspiring boldness or fear, in the circumstances which have been stated, and the Brave man chooses his line and withstands danger either because to do so is honourable, or because not to do so is base. But dying to escape from poverty, or the pangs of love, or anything that is simply painful, is not the act of a Brave man but of a coward."
>
> *Gloss: True courage is distinguished from mere endurance of pain by its motive — the noble (to kalon) — and by its position as the mean between the coward who fears too much and the rash man who is overconfident before danger but fails in it.*

> *Book III, Chapter XI* — "The man moreover who exceeds in feeling fear is a coward, since there attach to him the circumstances of fearing wrong objects, in wrong ways, and so forth. He is deficient also in feeling confidence, but he is most clearly seen as exceeding in the case of pains; he is a fainthearted kind of man, for he fears all things: the Brave man is just the contrary, for boldness is the property of the light-hearted and hopeful."
>
> *Gloss: The coward and the rash man share the same object-matter as the brave man but stand in excess or deficiency; the brave man is quiet before danger and sharp in action.*

> *Book III, Chapter XI* — "Courage proper is somewhat of the kind I have described, but there are dispositions, differing in five ways, which also bear in common parlance the name of Courage. We will take first that which bears most resemblance to the true, the Courage of Citizenship, so named because the motives which are thought to actuate the members of a community in braving danger are the penalties and disgrace held out by the laws to cowardice, and the dignities conferred on the Brave."
>
> *Gloss: Aristotle distinguishes civic courage (motivated by honour and shame) from true courage, and lists four other spurious forms: experience, passion, hope, and ignorance.*

Trans. W. D. Ross, 1908.

### Friedrich Nietzsche — *Thus Spake Zarathustra*

> *Part III, "The Vision and the Enigma"* — "But there is something in me which I call courage: it hath hitherto slain for me every dejection. This courage at last bade me stand still and say: 'Dwarf! Thou! Or I!' — For courage is the best slayer,—courage which ATTACKETH: for in every attack there is sound of triumph. Man, however, is the most courageous animal: thereby hath he overcome every animal. With sound of triumph hath he overcome every pain; human pain, however, is the sorest pain. Courage slayeth also giddiness at abysses: and where doth man not stand at abysses!"
>
> *Gloss: Courage is not a mean or a civic preservation but an aggressive, self-assertive force that overcomes dejection, pain, and the vertigo of the abyss — the condition for Zarathustra's ascent and the overcoming of the spirit of gravity.*

Trans. Thomas Common, 1909.

## Reframings

### Hindu — Dharma

The Bhagavad Gītā frames courage not as a mean or a civic preservation but as the willingness to act according to one's *dharma* (duty) even when the action is terrifying — Arjuna's crisis on the battlefield is a crisis of courage, and Kṛṣṇa's teaching is that the courageous act is the one that aligns with cosmic order, regardless of personal fear or outcome. The English translator of the Gītā (Arnold) renders the relevant concept as "duty" rather than "courage," but the underlying question — what makes a person stand and fight when every instinct says flee — is the same.

### Confucian — Yi (Righteousness)

The Confucian tradition does not treat courage as a separate virtue in the Aristotelian sense; rather, moral courage is an expression of *yi* (righteousness) — the disposition to do what is morally fitting regardless of personal cost. The *Analects* praise the person who, faced with injustice, acts from *yi* rather than from fear of consequences. The English translator (Legge) renders *yi* as "righteousness," but the concept includes the courage to stand alone against the crowd.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Virtue_and_Vice]] — Courage is one of the four cardinal virtues in the Platonic and Christian traditions, and one of the moral virtues in Aristotle's scheme.
- [[Fear]] — Courage is defined in relation to fear; the brave person feels fear but acts rightly despite it.
- [[Death]] — The paradigmatic test of courage is facing death in battle (Plato, Aristotle) or the death of the self (Nietzsche).
- [[Honor]] — Civic courage is often motivated by the desire for honor and the avoidance of shame (Aristotle's first spurious form).
- [[War_and_Peace]] — The primary domain of courage in the classical canon is the battlefield.
- [[Temperance]] — Along with courage, one of the two moral virtues concerned with non-rational appetites (fear for courage, pleasure for temperance).
- [[Prudence]] — Courage without prudence is rashness; Aristotle insists the brave man must have right judgment about what is truly fearful.

## Terms in play on this page

- [[Terms/Arete]] — Courage is one of the *aretai* (excellences) in the Greek tradition.
- [[Terms/Dharma]] — The Hindu reframing of courage as duty-based action.
- [[Terms/Yi]] — The Confucian reframing of courage as righteous action.
- [[Terms/Telos]] — Courage aims at the noble end (*to kalon*), which is the *telos* of virtuous action.
- [[Terms/Eudaimonia]] — Courage contributes to the flourishing life, since without it one cannot face the dangers that threaten a good life.
