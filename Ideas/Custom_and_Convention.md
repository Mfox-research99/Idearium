# Idea: Custom\_and\_Convention

## Working definition

What binds conduct without the force of law, and what authority does such binding have? The Western tradition asks whether custom originates in nature, utility, or tacit agreement, whether it should constrain individual judgment, and where the line falls between legitimate custom and tyranny of the majority. Other traditions reframe the question through ritual propriety and cosmic duty; see Reframings.

## Western canon

### Cicero — *De Officiis* (Duties)

> “But no rules need to be given about what is done in accordance with the established customs and conventions of a community; for these are in themselves rules; and no one ought to make the mistake of supposing that, because Socrates or Aristippus did or said something contrary to the manners and established customs of their city, he has a right to do the same; it was only by reason of their great and superhuman virtues that those famous men acquired this special privilege.”
> 
> *Gloss:* Cicero treats custom as self-enforcing rule but acknowledges that extraordinary virtue can justify deviation — a conservative framing that reserves exception for the heroic few.

### Francis Bacon — *Essays*

> “In other things, the predominancy of custom is everywhere visible, insomuch as a man would wonder to hear men profess, protest, engage, give great words, and then do just as they have done before, as if they were dead images and engines, moved only by the wheels of custom. We see, also, the reign or tyranny of custom, what it is.”
> 
> *Gloss:* Bacon emphasizes custom’s mechanical, almost tyrannical hold over human action, citing extreme examples (suttee, Spartan scourging) to show its power over life itself.

### David Hume — *A Treatise of Human Nature*

> “This convention is not of the nature of a promise: For even promises themselves, as we shall see afterwards, arise from human conventions. It is only a general sense of common interest; which sense all the members of the society express to one another, and which induces them to regulate their conduct by certain rules. … Two men, who pull the oars of a boat, do it by an agreement or convention, though they have never given promises to each other.”
> 
> *Gloss:* Hume derives convention from perceived common interest, not explicit contract — a foundational account of social order arising spontaneously through mutual expectation.

### Adam Smith — *Theory of Moral Sentiments*

> “All these effects of custom and fashion, however, upon the moral sentiments of mankind, are inconsiderable in comparison of those which they give occasion to in some other cases; and it is not concerning the general style of character and behaviour, that those principles produce the greatest perversion of judgment, but concerning the propriety or impropriety of particular usages.”
> 
> *Gloss:* Smith limits custom’s influence to particular usages rather than foundational moral sentiments, but acknowledges that it can pervert judgment in matters of propriety.

### William Blackstone — *Commentaries on the Laws of England*

> “For if both are really customs, then both are of equal antiquity, and both established by mutual consent: which to say of contradictory customs is absurd. Therefore, if one man prescribes that by custom he has a right to have windows looking into another’s garden; the other cannot claim a right by custom to stop up or obstruct those windows: for these two contradictory customs cannot both be good, nor both stand together.”
> 
> *Gloss:* Blackstone applies legal reason to custom: contradictory customs cannot coexist; custom must be certain, compulsory, and strictly construed when in derogation of common law.

### John Stuart Mill — *On Liberty*

> “The traditions and customs of other people are, to a certain extent, evidence of what their experience has taught *them*; presumptive evidence, and as such, have a claim to his deference: but, in the first place, their experience may be too narrow; or they may not have interpreted it rightly. … It is for him to find out what part of recorded experience is properly applicable to his own circumstances and character.”
> 
> *Gloss:* Mill grants custom presumptive authority as condensed experience but insists that the mature individual must interpret and overrule it when it fails to fit his own life — the liberal limit on custom’s claim.

## Reframings

### Confucian tradition — Li (禮, ritual propriety)

The English phrase “custom and convention” captures Li’s function as inherited social pattern but misses its cosmic dimension: Li is the concrete embodiment of the Way (Dao) in interpersonal conduct, not merely habitual practice. Observing Li cultivates virtue; violating it disrupts harmony. The Western emphasis on individual judgment versus customary constraint is displaced by a framework where custom *is* the medium of moral education.

→ See [[Terms/Li]]

### Hindu tradition — Dharma (धर्म)

Dharma encompasses custom, duty, law, and cosmic order simultaneously. What the Western tradition splits into “custom” (human, changeable) and “law” (rational, universal) is unified in Dharma: one’s duties vary by caste, stage of life, and circumstance, but all are expressions of a single sustaining order. The question of whether custom binds is answered categorically — it does, but the binding is contextual and hierarchical, not a matter of individual autonomy.
