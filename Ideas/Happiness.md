# Idea: Happiness

## Working definition

Happiness is the state of complete well-being that functions as the final end of human action. The Western tradition splits into three schools: Aristotle's eudaimonia (happiness as virtuous activity, the fulfillment of human potential), the Stoic view (happiness as inner freedom through correct judgment, untouched by externals), and the Epicurean vision (happiness as ataraxia — tranquility achieved by eliminating fear, especially fear of death and the gods). Aquinas synthesizes the Aristotelian and Christian claims: perfect happiness is the beatific vision, an activity of the soul whose object lies outside it. Other traditions reframe happiness through release from attachment; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Happiness as the final end

Whether happiness is the one end toward which all human action aims, and whether it is self-sufficient and choice-worthy for its own sake. Aristotle's foundational claim.

### Sub-question: What happiness is not

Eliminative arguments that rule out candidates — pleasure, honor, power, wealth — clearing the ground for the true account. Aristotle, Aquinas, and Epictetus all wage this negative campaign.

### Sub-question: Happiness, pleasure, and virtue

Whether the happy life is inherently pleasant, and whether pleasure in virtuous action is a mark of genuine virtue (Aristotle). Contrast with the Stoic claim that virtue alone suffices, and the Epicurean claim that pleasure — understood as absence of pain — IS the good.

### Sub-question: Happiness as inner freedom

The Stoic revolution: happiness is entirely within our power because it depends solely on judgment. Epictetus: "Men are disturbed not by things, but by the views which they take of things." External circumstances are "indifferent" (adiaphora); only virtue and vice matter for happiness.

### Sub-question: Happiness as liberation from fear

Lucretius' Epicurean thesis: the root of all unhappiness is fear — especially fear of death and divine punishment. Happiness is the tranquil state achieved when reason demonstrates that "death is nothing to us" and that the gods dwell in undisturbed peace, indifferent to human affairs.

### Sub-question: Happiness as attainment of God

The Christian Aristotelian claim that perfect happiness consists in the vision of God, and that the soul is the means, not the end. Aquinas: "that which constitutes happiness is something outside the soul."

### Sub-question: Happiness as conquest of difficulty

Alfred Adler's social-psychological view: happiness as the affect of overcoming obstacles, tied to social feeling and community.

## Western canon

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics* (trans. W. D. Ross)

> *X.6–8* — "Now that we have spoken about the Excellences of both kinds, and Friendship in its varieties, and Pleasures, it remains to sketch out Happiness, since we assume that to be the one End of all human things … we said that it is not a State merely; because, if it were, it might belong to one who slept all his life through and merely vegetated … it is plain we must rank Happiness among those choice-worthy for their own sakes and not among those which are so with a view to something further: because Happiness has no lack of anything but is self-sufficient."
>
> *Gloss: Happiness is not a state (hexis) but an activity (energeia) according to virtue, self-sufficient and choice-worthy for its own sake — the final end of all human action.*

> *I.8 / X.8* — "Their life too is in itself pleasant: for the feeling of pleasure is a mental sensation … a man is not a good man at all who feels no pleasure in noble actions, just as no one would call that man just who does not feel pleasure in acting justly, or liberal who does not in liberal actions."
>
> *Gloss: The happy life is inherently pleasant to the virtuous person; pleasure in noble actions is not an appendage to virtue but a criterion of it.*

### Thomas Aquinas — *Summa Theologica*

> *I-II, Q. 2, Art. 4* — "It is impossible for happiness to consist in power; and this for two reasons. First because power has the nature of principle … whereas happiness has the nature of last end. — Secondly, because power has relation to good and evil: whereas happiness is man's proper and perfect good."
>
> *Gloss: Power cannot be happiness because it is a principle, not an end, and indifferent to good and evil.*

> *I-II, Q. 2, Art. 2* — "It is impossible for happiness to consist in honor. … Happiness is in the happy. But honor is not in the honored, but rather in him who honors."
>
> *Gloss: Honor is external — it resides in the one who honors, not the one honored — and therefore cannot constitute the intrinsic good of happiness.*

> *I-II, Q. 2, Art. 7* — "The thing itself which is desired as end, is that which constitutes happiness, and makes man happy; but the attainment of this thing is called happiness. Consequently we must say that happiness is something belonging to the soul; but that which constitutes happiness is something outside the soul."
>
> *Gloss: The soul is the means by which happiness is attained, but the object of happiness is outside the soul — namely, God.*

### Epictetus — *Enchiridion* (trans. Thomas W. Higginson)

The Stoic account of happiness begins with Epictetus' razor-sharp distinction: everything is either within our power or not. Happiness and freedom are procured exclusively by attending to the former and disregarding the latter.

> *§I* — "There are things which are within our power, and there are things which are beyond our power. Within our power are opinion, aim, desire, aversion, and, in one word, whatever affairs are our own. Beyond our power are body, property, reputation, office, and, in one word, whatever are not properly our own affairs. … if you attribute freedom to things by nature dependent and take what belongs to others for your own, you will be hindered, you will lament, you will be disturbed, you will find fault both with gods and men. But if you take for your own only that which is your own and view what belongs to others just as it really is, then no one will ever compel you … Aiming, therefore, at such great things, remember that you must not allow yourself any inclination … toward the attainment of the others … you will certainly fail of that by which alone happiness and freedom are procured."
>
> *Gloss: The cornerstone of Stoic happiness: the line between what is "up to us" (judgment, desire, will) and everything else is absolute. To pursue externals while neglecting the inner citadel is to guarantee misery. "Happiness and freedom" are yoked: the free person is the happy person.*

> *§V and §XVI* — "Men are disturbed not by things, but by the views which they take of things. Thus death is nothing terrible, else it would have appeared so to Socrates. But the terror consists in our notion of death, that it is terrible. When, therefore, we are hindered or disturbed, or grieved, let us never impute it to others, but to ourselves — that is, to our own views. It is the action of an uninstructed person to reproach others for his own misfortunes; of one entering upon instruction, to reproach himself; and one perfectly instructed, to reproach neither others nor himself."
>
> *Gloss: Epictetus' cognitive theory of emotion: happiness consists in the progressive elimination of false judgment. Events are "indifferent"; it is our judgments about them that produce disturbance or tranquility. Socrates faced death calmly — proving the judgment "death is terrible" is not compulsory.*

> *§VIII and §XI* — "Demand not that events should happen as you wish; but wish them to happen as they do happen, and you will go on well. … Never say of anything, 'I have lost it,' but, 'I have restored it.' Has your child died? It is restored. Has your wife died? She is restored. … While he permits you to possess it, hold it as something not your own, as do travelers at an inn."
>
> *Gloss: Happiness is the "smooth flow of life" (euroia) achieved by aligning desire with reality rather than demanding reality conform to desire. The linguistic discipline — saying "I have restored it" rather than "I have lost it" — is a spiritual exercise, not a euphemism.*

> *§XIX* — "You can be unconquerable if you enter into no combat in which it is not in your own power to conquer. When, therefore, you see anyone eminent in honors or power … take heed not to be bewildered by appearances and to pronounce him happy; for if the essence of good consists in things within our own power, there will be no room for envy or emulation."
>
> *Gloss: Invincibility is not superior force but choosing the right battlefield. We "pronounce happy" those with power only because we have misplaced the essence of the good. True happiness eliminates envy — it is entirely within one's power.*

> *Discourses III.15* — "If, by parting with [external goods], you have a mind to purchase serenity, freedom, and tranquillity … approach. If not, do not come hither; do not, like children, be now a philosopher, then a publican, then an orator. … You must watch, you must labor, you must get the better of certain appetites. … When you have fully considered all these things … purchase serenity, freedom, and tranquillity."
>
> *Gloss: The philosophical life is not a pleasant hobby but total training (askēsis). The payoff — ataraxia, eleutheria, apatheia — cannot coexist with the pursuit of conventional rewards. Happiness is a wholesale reorientation, not an addition.*

> *§XLVIII* — "The condition and characteristic of a vulgar person is that he never looks for either help or harm from himself, but only from externals. The condition and characteristic of a philosopher is that he looks to himself for all help or harm. … he keeps watch over himself as over an enemy and one in ambush."
>
> *Gloss: The happy person has achieved radical self-sufficiency. The final line reveals Stoic realism: the enemy to happiness is within, in the form of habituated false judgments. Happiness is continuous vigilance, not passive enjoyment.*

### Marcus Aurelius — *Meditations* (trans. Meric Casaubon)

Marcus Aurelius, Roman emperor and Stoic, wrote his *Meditations* as private exercises in self-discipline. Happiness here is the fruit of right reason applied to the conditions of human life.

> *Book II, III–IV* — "Whatsoever I am, is either flesh, or life, or that which we call the mistress and queen of the rest. … Let it then be the part of a man, as long as he is a man, to esteem nothing for a good, that doth not belong to him as he is a man. And a true man cannot own that which is not the work of man's nature, nor is man's nature against that which is according to nature. But now, as a man, thy place is to live in this world amongst men. … Every man's happiness depends from himself."
>
> *Gloss: The Stoic thesis in its most compressed form: happiness is unconditionally self-derived. The phrase "every man's happiness depends from himself" annihilates the distinction between fortunate and unfortunate circumstances — neither can touch the essential thing.*

> *Book IV, XLI* — "Thou must comfort thyself in the expectation of thy natural dissolution, and in the meantime not grieve at the delay; but rest contented in those two things: first, that nothing shall befall thee which is not according to the nature of the universe; secondly, that it is in thy power to do nothing against thy God and inward spirit. For no man shall force thee to transgress. … To bear it generously, is certainly great happiness."
>
> *Gloss: Happiness here is reconceived as the capacity to bear what must be borne — not the presence of pleasure but the absence of complaint. The cosmic frame (nothing befalls us outside nature's order) and the ethical frame (nothing compels us to transgress) together create an unassailable inner ground.*

> *Book V, XXX* — "The things that are within thy power, are those which belong to the mind, and are from the mind as their source and original. … as for things that are without thy power, avoid all care and affection for them. And he that can do this, to him assuredly nothing is able to happen contrary to his will and desire. … He will never be hurt by another: he will never have an enemy. And what, think you, will this be, but a life altogether full of serenity, what can be happier?"
>
> *Gloss: Aurelius echoes Epictetus' formula: confine concern to the mind's domain, and the result is invulnerability — "a life altogether full of serenity." This is the Stoic wager: that radical limitation of the sphere of concern produces not impoverishment but a kind of perfect freedom.*

> *Book VI, XXXI* — "It is in thy power absolutely to live without all manner of distemper and perturbation. … Why should not such a one be esteemed to have led a happy life? But if neither riches, nor pleasures, nor powers, nor glorious fame … have any power in them to make a happy man: what is it then that can do it? … To live in the continual contemplation and observation of the nature of the universe … to do nothing against the truth and justice."
>
> *Gloss: Aurelius refutes hedonism with a direct argument: if vicious people can enjoy pleasures, pleasure cannot constitute happiness. The true happy life combines contemplation of nature's order with just action — the contemplative and the active life in synthesis.*

> *Book VIII, I* — "This also, among other things, may serve to keep thee from vainglory: that thou canst not now say, that thou hast lived all thy life, or at least from thy youth upwards, a philosopher; but both to others and to thyself, it is plain and apparent that thou art far from true philosophy. … If, therefore, thou dost truly observe what is the nature of the thing that is good, and what of the thing that is truly and really evil … thou shalt find that neither in thy experience, nor in thy want, nor in thy suffering, nor in thy affections, canst thou ever place true felicity."
>
> *Gloss: True felicity cannot be placed in experience, suffering, or affection — it resides only in virtue and right reason. This is Aurelius' most radical statement: happiness has nothing to do with how life feels.*

> *Book XII, XXII* — "Consider what a thing it is, and what it requires, that we call happiness. And if thou dost what is just, and speakest what is true, and thinkest what is good and holy: what occasion canst thou have for any further thing, as having mastered and attained the height of human wisdom; or why shouldst thou desire to enlarge the limits of thy life? … whereas to live longer, when it is in thy power to depart, what art thou but a fool and a coward?"
>
> *Gloss: Happiness is the fruit of just action, true speech, and right thought — nothing more. The brevity of life is not an obstacle to happiness but its proper frame. Aurelius suggests that wanting more time reveals a failure to grasp what happiness actually consists in.*

### Lucretius — *On the Nature of Things* (trans. W. E. Leonard)

Lucretius presents the Epicurean path to happiness: through understanding the material nature of the universe, we liberate ourselves from the two fears that poison life — fear of death and fear of divine punishment.

> *Book II, Proem* — "But yet to see / The nature of the world, and her to guard / In her own tranquil peace, the rest of men / Tire out their life-time, all in vain … O wretched minds of men! O blinded hearts! / In how great perils, in what darks of life / Is spent this little span ye have of years! / O not to see that nature for herself / Barks after nothing, save that pain keep off, / Severed from body, and that in mind she have / A feeling sweet, from every care aloof!"
>
> *Gloss: The Epicurean diagnosis: most people spend their lives in frantic but misguided pursuit, blind to the simple truth that nature requires only the absence of pain and a mind "aloof from every care." Happiness is essentially negative — the removal of disturbance — not the achievement of some positive state.*

> *Book III, Proem* — "When before our eyes man's life lay grovelling prone to view, crushed under the weight of Religion … a Greek, a mortal man, first was he who dared lift up his mortal eyes and stand forth to oppose her. Him neither fables of the gods, nor lightnings, nor the sky with its menacing roar held back, but all the more they stirred the eager courage of his soul … And so, in turn, Religion now is crushed beneath our feet, and we are made gods by the victory."
>
> *Gloss: Epicurus is presented as a liberator — the first to break the grip of religious fear that "crushed" human life. The claim is extraordinary: by demonstrating that the gods are indifferent and the soul mortal, Epicurus allows humans to live without the dread of eternal punishment, opening space for genuine happiness.*

> *Book III.59–93* — "For as soon as thy philosophy … begins to proclaim that the mind, once born, must die, and that the soul, created long before, can never stay immortal, then men, perceiving before their eyes the contradiction of two truths … fear lest in death eternal punishment await them. … Hence, moreover, 'tis that greed of money and blind lust of honour, which drive unhappy men to go beyond the bounds of right … and sometimes even to make their living by slaughter of their countrymen … And these same wounds of life in no small part are fostered by the dread of death."
>
> *Gloss: The most powerful passage in Lucretius's psychological diagnosis: the fear of death is not merely one unhappiness among others — it is the ROOT from which greed, ambition, crime, and civil violence grow. Knowing they will die, men grasp desperately at wealth, power, and pleasure, trampling others in the scramble. Happiness is impossible until this fundamental fear is eliminated.*

> *Book III.830–869* — "Death, then, is nothing to us, nor does it concern us the slightest bit, since the nature of the mind we now hold to be mortal. And just as in time gone by we felt no distress when from all sides the Carthaginians came to clash in battle … so when we shall be no more, when soul and body, out of which we are compounded, have been divorced, nothing at all will be able to happen to us, who then will be no more …"
>
> *Gloss: The Epicurean "symmetry argument": just as we were not troubled by events before our birth, so we will not be troubled by anything after our death. "Death is nothing to us" because where we are, death is not; where death is, we are not. This is the rational cure for the fear that Lucretius identified as the root of all unhappiness.*

> *Book III.931–977* — "Why does not each depart like a banqueter filled with life, and take with tranquil mind thy rest, thou fool? But if thy life past has been all a waste, and if thy soul is sick, why dost thou seek to add again more years …? Why not rather make an end of life and labour? For there is nothing more which I can contrive or invent to please thee: all things are the same forever. … Therefore, what now is left for us is that we see that all nature is self-sufficient."
>
> *Gloss: Nature's voice rebukes the dissatisfied: life is a banquet, not a quest for more. If it has been wasted, adding years will not cure it; if it has been rich, depart contented. The cosmos is self-sufficient — it offers what it offers, and happiness consists in accepting the terms, not in demanding better ones.*

> *Book V, Proem* — "And since he [Epicurus] has taught that … he was a god, a god indeed, O noble Memmius, who first found out that plan of life which now is called wisdom, and who by his skill brought life out of such great billows and such thick darkness, and settled it in so tranquil a haven and in light so clear. … Now following thee, by thy trace, I place my footmarks in the way, not so much desiring to be thy rival as that from love I yearn to imitate. … He saw that almost all things which necessity demands for subsistence had been already prepared for mortals … but yet he saw that men possessed a mind which could lay open in complaint about its great deserts. He understood, therefore, that the flaw was in the vessel itself … and that from this fault it was impossible that anything, even things of the best, not in some part corrupt, could ever give content. So with true words he purged the heart."
>
> *Gloss: Epicurus is deified — "a god, a god indeed" — for discovering "that plan of life which now is called wisdom." The crucial insight: the problem is not that life is insufficient but that the vessel is flawed. External goods can never satisfy because the mind that receives them is corrupt. True happiness requires purging the heart — the inner transformation that philosophy provides — not acquiring more things.*

### Alfred Adler — *Understanding Human Nature*

> *Ch. 5* — "Happiness, as a matter of fact, is probably the best expression for the conquest of difficulties. Laughter, with its liberating energy, its freedom-giving powers, goes hand in hand with happiness, and represents, so to speak, the keystone of this affect. It reaches out beyond the personality and entwines itself in the sympathies of others."
>
> *Gloss: Happiness is the affect of overcoming difficulty, rooted in social feeling; when severed from social feeling it becomes a tool of domination rather than genuine well-being.*

## Reframings

### Buddhist — Nirvana / Sukha

The Pali term *sukha* (rendered "happiness" by translator F. Max Müller) operates in a different register than the Western eudaimonia tradition. In the Dhammapada's "Happiness" chapter (Sukha Vagga), happiness is not the fulfillment of human potential through virtuous activity but the ease of one who has released attachment: "Let us live happily then, not hating those who hate us! among men who hate us let us dwell free from hatred! … Let us live happily then, though we call nothing our own! We shall be like the bright gods, feeding on happiness!" (XV.197–200, trans. F. Max Müller, 1881). This is the happiness of the awakened one (buddha), which points toward *nirvana* — the cessation of craving — rather than toward flourishing within the world.
