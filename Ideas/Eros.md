# Idea: Eros

## Working definition

Eros is the driving desire for beauty, completion, and transcendence — the force that pulls the lover upward from physical attraction through moral and intellectual beauty toward the form of Beauty itself. In the Western tradition, Eros is distinguished from Agape (selfless love) and Philia (brotherly love) by its desiring, lack-driven character. Other traditions reframe this through divine longing (Sufi *ishq*) or cosmic desire (Vedic *kama*); see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: The Object of Eros — from bodies to forms

The Platonic tradition asks what Eros truly desires: a particular beautiful body, all beautiful bodies, beauty of soul, or Beauty itself. The answer determines whether Eros is a ladder or a trap.

### Sub-question: Eros as Madness and Inspiration

Eros is often classed among the divine madnesses — a state that disrupts rational order but opens the soul to vision. This sub-question asks whether Eros is a pathology or a gift.

### Sub-question: Eros and the Ethical Life

Does Eros make the lover more virtuous? The Symposium’s Phaedrus argues that a lover and beloved together will outfight any army, because shame before the beloved is the strongest motive to honor.

### Sub-question: Divine Eros — from Augustine to the Sufis

When the object of Eros becomes God, the desiring structure of Eros is retained but purified. Augustine’s *Confessions* and the Sufi poetry of Jami both explore how earthly longing can be a school for divine love.

## Western canon

### Plato — *Symposium*

> *Diotima’s speech (210a–212a)* — "These are the lesser mysteries of love, into which even you, Socrates, may enter; to the greater and more hidden ones which are the crown of these, and to which, if you pursue them in a right spirit, they will lead, I know not whether you will be able to attain. But I will do my utmost to inform you, and do you follow if you can. For he who would proceed aright in this matter should begin in youth to visit beautiful forms; and first, if he be guided by his instructor aright, to love one such form only—out of that he should create fair thoughts; and soon he will of himself perceive that the beauty of one form is akin to the beauty of another; and then if beauty of form in general is his pursuit, how foolish would he be not to recognize that the beauty in every form is one and the same! And when he perceives this he will abate his violent love of the one, which he will despise and deem a small thing, and will become a lover of all beautiful forms; in the next stage he will consider that the beauty of the mind is more honourable than the beauty of the outward form."
>
> *Gloss: Plato commits Eros to a teleological ascent: the same desire that fastens on a beautiful body can, if rightly guided, lead the soul to the Form of Beauty itself. Eros is not an end but a ladder.*

> *Phaedrus’ speech (178e–179a)* — "For I know not any greater blessing to a young man who is beginning life than a virtuous lover, or to the lover than a beloved youth. For the principle which ought to be the guide of men who would nobly live—that principle, I say, neither kindred, nor honour, nor wealth, nor any other motive is able to implant so well as love. Of what am I speaking? Of the sense of honour and dishonour, without which neither states nor individuals ever do any good or great work. And I say that a lover who is detected in doing any dishonourable act, or submitting through cowardice when any dishonour is done to him by another, will be more pained at being detected by his beloved than at being seen by his father, or by his companions, or by any one else."
>
> *Gloss: Eros is here the source of ethical shame and heroic courage — the beloved’s gaze makes the lover want to be worthy. This is the civic and military face of Eros.*

Trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1871.

### Song of Solomon (Hebrew Bible)

> *Song 2:5–10* — "Stay me with flagons, comfort me with apples: for I am sick of love. His left hand is under my head, and his right hand doth embrace me. I charge you, O ye daughters of Jerusalem, by the roes, and by the hinds of the field, that ye stir not up, nor awake my love, till he please. The voice of my beloved! behold, he cometh leaping upon the mountains, skipping upon the hills. My beloved is like a roe or a young hart: behold, he standeth behind our wall, he looketh forth at the windows, shewing himself through the lattice. My beloved spake, and said unto me, Rise up, my love, my fair one, and come away."
>
> *Gloss: The Song gives Eros a voice within the biblical canon — embodied, mutual, and unashamed. The beloved’s body is celebrated without allegorical evasion.*

King James Version, 1611.

### Augustine — *Confessions*

> *Book IV, on the death of a friend* — "But that fable would not die to me, so oft as any of my friends died. There were other things which in them did more take my mind; to talk and jest together, to do kind offices by turns; to read together honied books; to play the fool or be earnest together; to dissent at times without discontent, as a man might with his own self; and even with the seldomness of these dissentings, to season our more frequent consentings; sometimes to teach, and sometimes learn; long for the absent with impatience; and welcome the coming with joy. These and the like expressions, proceeding out of the hearts of those that loved and were loved again, by the countenance, the tongue, the eyes, and a thousand pleasing gestures, were so much fuel to melt our souls together, and out of many make but one."
>
> *Gloss: Augustine describes the eros of friendship — a desire for union so intense that it melts separate souls into one. The passage is a prelude to his later reorientation of all desire toward God.*

Trans. E. B. Pusey, 1838.

### Augustine — *City of God*

> *Book XI, chapter 28* — "We have said as much as the scope of this work demands regarding these two things, to wit, our existence, and our knowledge of it, and how much they are loved by us, and how there is found even in the lower creatures a kind of likeness of these things, and yet with a difference. We have yet to speak of the love wherewith they are loved, to determine whether this love itself is loved. And doubtless it is; and this is the proof. Because in men who are justly loved, it is rather love itself that is loved; for he is not justly called a good man who knows what is good, but who loves it. Is it not then obvious that we love in ourselves the very love wherewith we love whatever good we love?"
>
> *Gloss: Augustine turns Eros inward: the love with which we love is itself the object of love. This self-reflexive structure anticipates the Trinitarian analogy and makes Eros a participation in God’s own life.*

Trans. Marcus Dods, 1871.

### Krafft-Ebing — *Psychopathia Sexualis*

> *General introduction* — "True love is rooted in the recognition of the moral and mental qualities of the beloved person, and is equally ready to share pleasures and sorrows and even to make sacrifices. True love shrinks from no dangers or obstacles in the struggle for the undisputed possession of the beloved. Deeds of daring and heroism lie in its wake. But unless the moral foundation is present, love is merely sensual, and never true and lasting."
>
> *Gloss: Krafft-Ebing, writing at the birth of sexology, distinguishes a "true" Eros grounded in moral recognition from a merely sensual drive. The passage marks the modern medicalization of the ancient distinction between heavenly and earthly Eros.*

Trans. F. J. Chaddock, 1894.

## Reframings

### Sufi — *Ishq*

The Persian Sufi tradition reframes Eros as *ishq* — a burning, annihilating longing for the Divine Beloved that uses earthly beauty as a mirror. In Jami’s *Yusuf and Zulaikha*, earthly love is explicitly a school for divine love: "Drink deep of earthly love, that so thy lip / May learn the wine of holier love to sip." Unlike the Platonic ladder, which the lover climbs by reason, the Sufi path requires the lover to be consumed. The English word "love" in the translation stands for *ishq*; the original term carries connotations of passionate excess that the Greek *eros* also bears.

### Hindu — *Kama*

The Vedic and classical Hindu tradition treats *kama* (desire) as one of the four proper aims of life (*purusharthas*), alongside *dharma*, *artha*, and *moksha*. Kama is not merely sensual — it is the cosmic force that drives creation itself (the *Kama Sutra* systematizes its expression). The English word "desire" in pre-1929 translations (e.g., Griffith’s *Rig Veda*) stands for *kama*; the original term does not carry the same suspicion of the body that late Platonism and Augustinianism introduced. The Idea of Eros overlaps with *kama* but lacks the latter’s positive, non-ascending place within a fourfold scheme of life aims.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Love]] — Eros is one species of love, distinguished from Agape and Philia by its desiring, lack-driven character.
- [[Beauty]] — The object of Eros is beauty in all its forms; the ascent of Eros is an education in beauty.
- [[Desire]] — Eros is the paradigmatic desire; Locke and Spinoza analyze desire as the engine of action, while Plato sees it as the engine of transcendence.
- [[Sexuality]] — Eros is not identical to sexuality, but the two intersect; Krafft-Ebing and Freud mark the modern attempt to map Eros onto sexual instinct.
- [[Soul]] — The ascent of Eros is an education of the soul; Augustine relocates Eros within the soul’s relation to God.
- [[Witness]] — The beloved’s gaze in Phaedrus’ speech makes the lover want to be honorable; Eros creates a witness to one’s actions.

## Status notes

- **Missing authors:** Sappho and Ovid — the two other canonical Western voices on Eros — are not represented in the supplied corpus. Their absence is noted; a future refresh should add Sappho’s fragments (e.g., the "He seems to me equal to gods" poem) and Ovid’s *Ars Amatoria* or *Metamorphoses* (the Narcissus and Pygmalion episodes).
- **Sufi material:** Jami’s *Yusuf and Zulaikha* provides the Sufi divine-eros lineage; Rumi and Hafiz would strengthen this section but were not in the corpus.
- **Modern psychoanalytic:** Freud’s *Totem and Taboo* passage (passage 4) touches on love and the death wish but was not dense enough to commit Freud to a position on Eros proper. The Freudian concept of Eros (life instinct vs. Thanatos) is a major modern reframing that should be added when corpus material permits.
- **Hume and Spinoza** passages on passion and desire were considered but dropped because they treat desire generically rather than Eros specifically. They are better placed under the Idea [[Desire]].
- **Shakespeare’s *Antony and Cleopatra*** passages (Eros as a character) were considered but are not conceptual — they use "Eros" as a proper name, not as a term for the Idea.
