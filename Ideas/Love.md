# Idea: Love

## Working definition

What binds one being to another, and what is the nature of that bond. The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about desire, will, and recognition — between persons, between the soul and God, and between the self and the good. Other traditions reframe it through annihilation of the lover in the beloved, or through the dissolution of the separate self; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Love as ascent toward Beauty

The Platonic tradition, established in the *Symposium*: love as an education of desire, from the beauty of one body through the beauty of souls, laws, and sciences to the vision of Beauty itself. Love is not a passion to be satisfied but a ladder to be climbed.

### Sub-question: Love as recognition and will

The Augustinian tradition: love as the will's movement toward its object, grounded in self-knowledge and the image of the Trinity.

### Sub-question: Love as the divided self seeking wholeness

Aristophanes' myth in the *Symposium*: we were once whole beings, the gods split us, and love is the longing to recover our lost half. This is desire that cannot be satisfied by any ordinary union — it wants identity, not merely intimacy.

### Sub-question: Love as friendship and shared life

Aristotle's *philia*: love not as passion but as the bond of shared virtue and common life. Friendships of utility and pleasure are impermanent; only the friendship of the good, where each loves the other for their own sake, is complete.

### Sub-question: Love as divine gift and commandment

The Christian agape tradition: love originates in God and descends to the creature. The creature loves in return because "he first loved us." Love is not ascent or acquisition but reception and return.

### Sub-question: Love as sanctifying perception

The Romantic and proto-feminist tradition: love as a mode of seeing that hallows the beloved and reveals the divine.

### Sub-question: Love as divine pedagogy

The Sufi tradition (entering the Western canon through Persian poetry): earthly love as apprenticeship for divine love, the lover's annihilation as the condition of union.

### Sub-question: Love as the cosmic principle

Dante's synthesis: love as the force that moves the sun and other stars, the theological principle from which all action — virtuous and sinful — derives, and the destination of the human journey.

## Western canon

### Augustine — *City of God*

> *Book XI, Chapter 28* — "By it I am assured both that I am, and that I know this; and these two I love, and in the same manner I am assured that I love them. […] Because in men who are justly loved, it is rather love itself that is loved; for he is not justly called a good man who knows what is good, but who loves it."
>
> *Gloss: Love is not merely an affection but the third term in a trinitarian structure of selfhood — being, knowing, and loving are co-present and mutually confirming. The love with which we love is itself loved, and this self-reflexive love is the image of the divine Trinity in the soul.*

> *Book XI, Chapter 28* — "Is it not then obvious that we love in ourselves the very love wherewith we love whatever is good? For there is a love which is loved, and this love cannot be loved unless it already loves itself."
>
> *Gloss: Love is self-justifying at the level of the will — the good man is defined not by what he knows but by what he loves, and the love itself is the object of a higher-order love.*

### Eckhart — *Meister Eckhart Sermons*

> *Sermon XI* — "What does God love? God loves nothing but himself and his own likes so far as he can find them in me and in him. […] God loves nothing in us except in so far as he finds us in him."
>
> *Gloss: Love is not a transaction between separate beings but a recognition of identity — God loves himself in the soul that has become like him. The lover's task is to become the kind of being God can love, which means becoming what God is.*

### Wollstonecraft — *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman*

> *Chapter 7* — "True love, likewise, spreads this kind of mysterious sanctity round the beloved object, making the lover most modest when in her presence. So reserved is affection, that, receiving or returning personal endearments, it wishes, not only to shun the human eye, as a kind of profanation; but to diffuse an encircling cloudy obscurity to shut out even the saucy sparkling sunbeams."
>
> *Gloss: Love sanctifies perception — the beloved becomes a sacred temple, and the lover's modesty before that temple is the mark of genuine affection.*

### Krafft-Ebing — *Psychopathia Sexualis*

> *Chapter 1* — "True love is rooted in the recognition of the moral and mental qualities of the beloved person, and is equally ready to share pleasures and sorrows and even to make sacrifices. True love shrinks from no dangers or obstacles in the struggle for the undisputed possession of the beloved. Deeds of daring and heroism lie in its wake."
>
> *Gloss: Love is here defined against its pathological distortions — the true form is a recognition of moral qualities that issues in sacrifice and heroism, not the "purely sensual" infatuation that is "never true and lasting."*

### Song of Solomon — *The Holy Bible* (King James Version)

> *7:10–8:4* — "I am my beloved's, and his desire is toward me. Come, my beloved, let us go forth into the field; let us lodge in the villages. Let us get up early to the vineyards; let us see if the vine flourish, whether the tender grape appear, and the pomegranates bud forth: there will I give thee my loves."
>
> *Gloss: The canonical Western poem of erotic love — desire is mutual, embodied, and unashamed. The beloved's body is a landscape to be explored, and the love is given freely in the natural world.*

### Plato — *Symposium* (trans. Benjamin Jowett)

#### Pausanias: The Two Loves and the Two Aphrodites

> "We all know that Love is inseparable from Aphrodite, and if there were only one Aphrodite there would be only one Love; but as there are two goddesses there must be two Loves. … The elder one, having no mother, who is called the heavenly Aphrodite — she is the daughter of Uranus; the younger, who is the daughter of Zeus and Dione — her we call common; and the Love who is her fellow-worker is rightly named common, as the other love is called heavenly. … The Love who is the offspring of the common Aphrodite is essentially common, and has no discrimination, being such as the meaner sort of men feel, and is apt to be of women as well as of youths, and is of the body rather than of the soul."
>
> *Gloss: Pausanias distinguishes the heavenly love (Uranian, born of the sky alone, directed toward the soul and virtue) from the common love (Pandemian, born of male and female, directed toward the body). The distinction is ethical, not merely taxonomic.*

> "Evil is the vulgar lover who loves the body rather than the soul, inasmuch as he is not even stable, because he loves a thing which is in itself unstable, and therefore when the bloom of youth which he was desiring is over, he takes wing and flies away, in spite of all his words and promises; whereas the love of the noble disposition is life-long, for it becomes one with the everlasting."
>
> *Gloss: The vulgar lover is ephemeral by definition — he loves what decays. The noble lover loves what endures: character, intellect, virtue.*

#### Aristophanes: The Myth of the Divided Self

> "The original human nature was not like the present, but different. The sexes were not two as they are now, but originally three in number; there was man, woman, and the union of the two … the primeval man was round, his back and sides forming a circle; and he had four hands and four feet, one head with two faces. … Terrible was their might and strength, and the thoughts of their hearts were great, and they made an attack upon the gods. … At last, after a good deal of reflection, Zeus discovered a way. He said: 'Methinks I have a plan which will humble their pride and improve their manners; men shall continue to exist, but I will cut them in two.' … After the division the two parts of man, each desiring his other half, came together, and throwing their arms about one another, entwined in mutual embraces, longing to grow into one."
>
> *Gloss: The most famous myth of love in the Western canon: we were once whole, the gods split us, and love is the longing to recover our lost half. Aristophanes makes love a search for completion, not an ascent toward beauty. This is the origin of the "soulmate" — and of the claim that love's intensity is proportional to the recognition of the other as oneself.*

> "Suppose Hephaestus, with his instruments, to come to the pair who are lying side by side and to say to them, 'What do you people want of one another?' they would be unable to explain. And suppose further, that when he saw their perplexity he said: 'Do you desire to be wholly one; always day and night to be in one another's company? … I am ready to melt you into one and let you grow together, so that being two you shall become one' … there is not a man of them who when he heard the proposal would deny or would not acknowledge that this meeting and melting into one another, this becoming one instead of two, was the very expression of his ancient need. And the reason is that human nature was originally one and we were a whole, and the desire and pursuit of the whole is called love."
>
> *Gloss: Love cannot name what it wants because what it wants is to dissolve the boundary of separate selfhood. This is desire that cannot be satisfied by any ordinary union — it wants identity, not merely intimacy.*

#### Diotima (via Socrates): The Ladder of Love

> "He who would proceed aright in this matter should begin in youth to visit beautiful forms; and first, if he be guided by his instructor aright, to love one such form only — out of that he should create fair thoughts; and soon he will of himself perceive that the beauty of one form is akin to the beauty of another. … And when he perceives this he will abate his violent love of the one, which he will despise and deem a small thing, and will become a lover of all beautiful forms; in the next stage he will consider that the beauty of the mind is more honourable than the beauty of the outward form. … after laws and institutions he will go on to the sciences, that he may see their beauty … until on that shore he grows and waxes strong, and at last the vision is revealed to him of a single science, which is the science of beauty everywhere."
>
> *Gloss: Diotima's ladder — the ascent from love of one body, to love of all bodies, to love of souls, to love of laws and institutions, to love of knowledge, and finally to the vision of Beauty itself. Each step builds on the last but transcends it. Love is not a single passion but a progressive education of desire.*

> "He who has been instructed thus far in the things of love, and who has learned to see the beautiful in due order and succession, when he comes toward the end will suddenly perceive a nature of wondrous beauty … a nature which in the first place is everlasting, not growing and decaying, or waxing and waning; secondly, not fair in one point of view and foul in another … but beauty absolute, separate, simple, and everlasting, which without diminution and without increase, or any change, is imparted to the ever-growing and perishing beauties of all other things."
>
> *Gloss: The Form of Beauty — the Platonic absolute that grounds all particular beauties. Love, properly educated, becomes philosophy, and philosophy becomes vision.*

> "What if man had eyes to see the true beauty — the divine beauty, I mean, pure and clear and unalloyed, not clogged with the pollutions of mortality and all the colours and vanities of human life — thither looking, and holding converse with the true beauty simple and divine? Remember how in that communion only, beholding beauty with the eye of the mind, he will be enabled to bring forth, not images of beauty, but realities … and bringing forth and nourishing true virtue to become the friend of God and be immortal, if mortal man may."
>
> *Gloss: The ladder's consummation is theological: the contemplation of divine Beauty makes the lover "the friend of God" and confers immortality. This passage is the direct ancestor of Plotinus, Augustine, and the entire Christian Neoplatonic tradition.*

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics* (trans. W. D. Ross)

Aristotle treats love primarily as *philia* — the bond of shared virtue and common life. Unlike Plato's erotic ascent, Aristotle anatomizes the varieties of human attachment and grounds them in the structure of character and community.

> *Book VIII, Chapter 1* — "Next would seem properly to follow a dissertation on Friendship: because, in the first place, it is either itself a virtue or connected with virtue; and next it is a thing most necessary for life, since no one would choose to live without friends though he should have all the other good things in the world. … Again, it seems to be implanted in us by Nature: as, for instance, in the parent towards the offspring and the offspring towards the parent (not merely in the human species, but likewise in birds and most animals). … Furthermore, Friendship seems to be the bond of Social Communities, and legislators seem to be more anxious to secure it than Justice even."
>
> *Gloss: Philia precedes justice in the order of political life — communities are held together by friendship before they are held together by law. Love is not a private sentiment but the most fundamental political bond.*

> *Book VIII, Chapters 3–4* — "There are then three causes from which men feel Friendship … they whose motive is utility have no Friendship for one another really, but only in so far as some good arises to them from one another. And they whose motive is pleasure are in like case … they love their friends for what is good to themselves. … Such Friendships are of course very liable to dissolution if the parties do not continue alike."
>
> *Gloss: The celebrated threefold typology — friendships of utility, pleasure, and virtue. The first two are accidental and impermanent; only the friendship of the good loves the friend for their own sake.*

> *Book VIII, Chapter 5* — "That then is perfect Friendship which subsists between those who are good and whose similarity consists in their goodness: for these men wish one another's good in similar ways; in so far as they are good … and those are specially friends who wish good to their friends for their sakes. … Rare it is probable Friendships of this kind will be, because men of this kind are rare. Besides, all requisite qualifications being presupposed, there is further required time and intimacy: for, as the proverb says, men cannot know one another 'till they have eaten the requisite quantity of salt together.'"
>
> *Gloss: Perfect philia is the rarest form of love — it requires time, tested intimacy, and the mutual recognition of virtue. This is not love at first sight but love that grows through shared life.*

> *Book IX, Chapter 4* — "Now the friendly feelings which are exhibited towards our friends, and by which Friendships are characterised, seem to have sprung out of those which we entertain toward ourselves. … the good man has [these feelings] towards himself, and all others have them in so far as they suppose themselves to be good. … it is then because each of these feelings are entertained by the good man towards his own Self and a friend feels towards a friend as towards himself (a friend being in fact another Self), that Friendship is thought to be some one of these things."
>
> *Gloss: The most compressed statement of Aristotle's theory: the friend is "another self" (allos autos). All friendship originates in self-relation — only the person at peace with himself can love another.*

> *Book IX, Chapter 4 (cont.)* — "The wicked man cannot be in the position of a friend even towards himself, because he has in himself nothing which can excite the sentiment of Friendship. … they are at variance with themselves and lust after different things from those which in cool reason they wish for … the wicked seek others in whose company to spend their time, but fly from themselves."
>
> *Gloss: The negative correlate: the vicious man, internally at war with himself, cannot be his own friend — and therefore cannot be a friend to another. Character is the condition of love.*

> *Book IX, Chapter 8* — "Of the good man it is true likewise that he does many things for the sake of his friends and his country, even to the extent of dying for them, if need be: for money and honours, and, in short, all the good things which others fight for, he will throw away while eager to secure to himself the noble: he will prefer a brief and great joy to a tame and enduring one, and to live nobly for one year rather than ordinarily for many."
>
> *Gloss: The paradox of noble self-love: by dying for friends or giving away honors, the good man actually secures the greater good — the kalon — for himself. The friend who dies for another is the supreme egoist in Aristotle's sense.*

> *Book IX, Chapter 9* — "A question is raised also respecting the Happy man, whether he will want Friends, or no? … perhaps absurd to make our Happy man a solitary, because no man would choose the possession of all goods in the world on the condition of solitariness, man being a social animal and formed by nature for living with others."
>
> *Gloss: Even the supremely happy man needs friends — not for utility but as objects of beneficence and shared contemplation. Human nature is irreducibly social, and love is its expression.*

### The Bible — King James Version

#### Agape: Love as Divine Gift and Commandment

The Christian agape tradition departs from both the Platonic erotic ascent and the Aristotelian philia of virtue. Here love originates in God and descends to the creature; the creature loves in return because "he first loved us."

> **1 Corinthians 13:1–13** — "Though I speak with the tongues of men and of angels, and have not charity, I am become as sounding brass, or a tinkling cymbal. And though I have the gift of prophecy, and understand all mysteries, and all knowledge; and though I have all faith, so that I could remove mountains, and have not charity, I am nothing. … Charity suffereth long, and is kind; charity envieth not; charity vaunteth not itself, is not puffed up, Doth not behave itself unseemly, seeketh not her own, is not easily provoked, thinketh no evil … And now abideth faith, hope, charity, these three; but the greatest of these is charity."
>
> *Gloss: Paul's definitive hymn to agape — love (rendered "charity" in KJV) is the sine qua non of all spiritual gifts, the permanent virtue that outlasts prophecy, knowledge, and tongues. Love is the greatest because it alone participates in the eternal mode of knowing — face to face.*

> **1 John 4:7–18** — "Beloved, let us love one another: for love is of God; and every one that loveth is born of God, and knoweth God. He that loveth not knoweth not God; for God is love. … Herein is love, not that we loved God, but that he loved us, and sent his Son to be the propitiation for our sins. … There is no fear in love; but perfect love casteth out fear: because fear hath torment."
>
> *Gloss: "God is love" — an ontological identification, not merely a predicate. Love originates in God and is manifested in the sending of the Son. Perfect love eliminates fear; eschatological confidence is the fruit of love's completion.*

> **John 15:9–13** — "As the Father hath loved me, so have I loved you: continue ye in my love. … This is my commandment, That ye love one another, as I have loved you. Greater love hath no man than this, that a man lay down his life for his friends."
>
> *Gloss: The standard of agape is raised beyond reciprocity to self-gift. The ultimate measure of love is kenotic: laying down one's life for friends — a proleptic description of the cross.*

> **Romans 8:35–39** — "Who shall separate us from the love of Christ? shall tribulation, or distress, or persecution, or famine, or nakedness, or peril, or sword? … Nay, in all these things we are more than conquerors through him that loved us. For I am persuaded, that neither death, nor life, nor angels, nor principalities, nor powers, nor things present, nor things to come, Nor height, nor depth, nor any other creature, shall be able to separate us from the love of God, which is in Christ Jesus our Lord."
>
> *Gloss: The climax of Romans 8 — the love of God in Christ is the unbreakable bond that no cosmic power, dimension, or created thing can sever.*

> **Song of Solomon 8:6–7** — "Set me as a seal upon thine heart, as a seal upon thine arm: for love is strong as death; jealousy is cruel as the grave: the coals thereof are coals of fire, which hath a most vehement flame. Many waters cannot quench love, neither can the floods drown it: if a man would give all the substance of his house for love, it would utterly be contemned."
>
> *Gloss: The canonical witness to love's unassailable power — stronger than death, fiercer than the grave, unquenchable, beyond all purchase. The "seal" imagery invokes permanence and indelible impression. This complements the Song of Solomon 7:10–8:4 passage above: together they present erotic love as both mutual delight and unquenchable force.*

> **Deuteronomy 6:4–5** — "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God is one LORD: And thou shalt love the LORD thy God with all thine heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy might."
>
> *Gloss: The Shema — the root commandment from which the entire agape tradition springs. Jesus cites this as the "first and great commandment." Love of God is total: heart, soul, and might.*

### Dante — *Divine Comedy: Inferno* (trans. H. W. Longfellow)

Dante's love theology is the most comprehensive in any single literary work. The following passages are from the Inferno only; the doctrinal center (Purgatorio XVII–XVIII on love as the root of all action) and the consummation (Paradiso XXXIII: "the Love that moves the sun and the other stars") await corpus acquisition of the remaining canticles.

> *Inferno I.131–135* — "The time was the beginning of the morning, / And up the sun was mounting with those stars / That with him were, what time the Love Divine // At first in motion set those beauteous things."
>
> *Gloss: The poem's first mention of love — "Amor divino" as the prime mover of creation. Love is not first a human passion but the cosmological force that ordered the universe.*

> *Inferno II.363–365* — "Beatrice am I, who do bid thee go; / I come from there, where I would fain return; / Love moved me, which compelleth me to speak."
>
> *Gloss: Beatrice's first words — "Amor mi mosse" — name love as the motive of her descent from Heaven to intercede for Dante. Courtly love is redeemed: the love of a mortal woman becomes the instrument of divine rescue.*

> *Inferno III.471–474* — "Justice incited my sublime Creator; / Created me divine Omnipotence, / The highest Wisdom and the primal Love."
>
> *Gloss: The inscription over Hell's gate names its architect as the Trinity, whose third person is "primal Love." The paradox is devastating: Hell itself was made by Love. Damnation, too, is an expression of divine love — the love that honors free choice.*

> *Inferno V.995–1004* — "Love, that on gentle heart doth swiftly seize, / Seized this man for the person beautiful / That was ta'en from me. … Love, that exempts no one beloved from loving, / Seized me with pleasure of this man so strongly, / That, as thou seest, it doth not yet desert me; // Love has conducted us unto one death."
>
> *Gloss: Francesca's triple anaphora — "Amor… Amor… Amor…" — is the theological center of Canto V. She speaks the language of the dolce stil novo; framed by damnation, the same doctrine is exposed as fatal rationalization. Francesca uses love's inevitability to displace her own agency — never does she say "I chose."*

> *Inferno V.1023–1045* — "There is no greater sorrow / Than to be mindful of the happy time / In misery. … One day we reading were for our delight / Of Launcelot, how Love did him enthral. / Alone we were and without any fear. … When as we read of the much-longed-for smile / Being by such a noble lover kissed, / This one, who ne'er from me shall be divided, // Kissed me upon the mouth all palpitating. / Galeotto was the book and he who wrote it. / That day no farther did we read therein."
>
> *Gloss: The most famous love scene in medieval literature. Reading a romance incites desire; the book is a "Galeotto" (pander). The kiss is gentle, mutual, literary — and leads to murder and damnation. "That day no farther did we read therein" is an eternal judgment.*

> *Inferno V.1047–1051* — "And all the while one spirit uttered this, / The other one did weep so, that, for pity, / I swooned away as if I had been dying, // And fell, even as a dead body falls."
>
> *Gloss: The pilgrim collapses — "e caddi come corpo morto cade." His pity for the damned is a moral crisis: Dante recognizes in Francesca's story the logic of his own youthful love poetry. The entire journey — through Purgatory, where love is reordered, and Paradise, where Beatrice transfigures eros — is the answer to this collapse.*

## Reframings

### Sufi / Persian — Ishq (divine love)

The Persian Sufi tradition, represented here by Jami (d. 1492), treats earthly love as a necessary apprenticeship for divine love. The lover must "drink deep of earthly love" so that the lip "may learn the wine of holier love to sip." But the goal is not satisfaction — it is annihilation. "Freedom from self is gained through love alone." The lover who has not been wounded by love has no heart; the soul that has not loved is "soulless clay." This is not the Augustinian will moving toward its object, nor the Romantic sanctification of the beloved — it is the dissolution of the lover as a separate self. The beloved is a bridge, not a destination: "Pass o'er the bridge: with rapid feet advance."

### Sufi / Persian — Nafs (the lower self)

Jami's insistence that love requires the death of the self connects directly to the Sufi concept of *nafs* — the ego-self that must be annihilated (*fana*) before union is possible. The lover who clings to his own existence cannot love. "Come, Jami, from dreams of thyself forbear; take for thy being no thought or care."

## Cross-cuts

- [[Eros]] — the Platonic tradition of love as ascent toward the Form of Beauty, distinct from the Augustinian will-tradition and the Sufi annihilation-tradition.
- [[Desire]] — love as a species of desire, or as distinct from desire (Augustine: we love the love itself, not merely the object).
- [[Soul]] — love as the third term in the soul's trinitarian structure (being, knowing, loving); the friend as "another self" (Aristotle).
- [[Sexuality]] — the embodied dimension of love, from the Song of Solomon to Krafft-Ebing's clinical framing.
- [[Sacred_and_Profane]] — Wollstonecraft's "mysterious sanctity" and the Sufi bridge from earthly to divine love.
- [[Witness]] — love as a mode of attention that hallows the beloved; Beatrice as intercessor (Dante).
- [[Beauty]] — the Form of Beauty as the object and engine of love (Plato, Diotima).
- [[Virtue_and_Vice]] — Aristotle's claim that only the virtuous can truly love; the vicious man cannot be his own friend.
- [[Death]] — love as stronger than death (Song of Solomon); the lover who dies for friends (Aristotle, John 15); love conducted unto one death (Dante).
- [[Justice]] — philia as more fundamental than justice for political life (Aristotle); the primal Love that built Hell's gate (Dante).
- [[Community]] — Aristotle's claim that friendship is the bond of all communities.
- [[Nihilism]] — love as the answer to the passive nihilism of the last man; Nietzsche's revaluation of love.
