# Idea: Logic

## Working definition

What are the necessary forms and rules of valid reasoning, and what are the limits of formal correctness as a criterion of truth? The Western tradition has treated logic both as a pure science of the laws of thought (the canon of the understanding) and as an instrument (organon) for the production of knowledge — a tension that recurs from Aristotle through Kant. Other traditions embed logical reasoning within broader epistemological or soteriological frameworks; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: The nature of general logic

Whether logic is a purely formal, a priori science with no empirical content, or whether it can serve as a substantive instrument for discovery.

### Sub-question: Logic and truth

Whether logical correctness is sufficient for truth, or only a necessary condition.

### Sub-question: Dialectic as misapplied logic

When logic is used as an organon for producing the *semblance* of knowledge — the ancient and Kantian sense of dialectic as a "logic of illusion."

### David Hume — *A Treatise of Human Nature*

> (6) "The following principle is founded on the same reason. The difference in the effects of two resembling objects must proceed from that particular, in which they differ. For as like causes always produce like effects, when in any instance we find our expectation to be disappointed, we must conclude that this irregularity proceeds from some difference in the causes."
>
> (7) "When any object encreases or diminishes with the encrease or diminution of its cause, it is to be regarded as a compounded effect, derived from the union of the several different effects, which arise from the several different parts of the cause. The absence or presence of one part of the cause is here supposed to be always attended with the absence or presence of a proportionable part of the effect. This constant conjunction sufficiently proves, that the one part is the cause of the other."
>
> — David Hume, *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–40), trans. T. H. Green and T. H. Grose, 1874. No location given in passage; the principles of causal reasoning.

*Gloss: Hume articulates the logical principles of causal inference, grounding them in constant conjunction rather than a priori necessity — an empirical rather than formal logic.*

### Immanuel Kant — *Critique of Pure Reason*

> "As pure logic, it has no empirical principles, and consequently draws nothing (contrary to the common persuasion) from psychology, which therefore has no influence on the canon of the understanding. It is a demonstrated doctrine, and everything in it must be certain completely à priori."
>
> — Immanuel Kant, *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781/1787), trans. J. M. D. Meiklejohn, 1855. Introduction, VII (or Transcendental Logic Introduction, I — passage does not specify further).

*Gloss: Kant insists on the a priori, non‑psychological status of pure logic, separating it from any empirical or applied treatment of thinking.*

> "Notwithstanding, there lies so seductive a charm in the possession of a specious art like this—an art which gives to all our cognitions the form of the understanding, although with respect to the content thereof we may be sadly deficient—that general logic, which is merely a canon of judgement, has been employed as an organon for the actual production, or rather for the semblance of production, of objective assertions, and has thus been grossly misapplied. Now general logic, in its assumed character of organon, is called dialectic."
>
> — Ibid.

*Gloss: Kant diagnoses the ancient misuse of logic as a tool for producing apparent knowledge — the genesis of dialectic as a "logic of illusion."*

> "Whatever contradicts these rules is false, because thereby the understanding is made to contradict its own universal laws of thought; that is, to contradict itself. These criteria, however, apply solely to the form of truth, that is, of thought in general, and in so far they are perfectly accurate, yet not sufficient. For although a cognition may be perfectly accurate as to logical form, that is, not self-contradictory, it is notwithstanding quite possible that it may not stand in agreement with its object. Consequently, the merely logical criterion of truth, namely, the accordance of a cognition with the universal and formal laws of understanding and reason, is nothing more than the conditio sine qua non, or negative condition of all truth. Farther than this logic cannot go, and the error which depends not on the form, but on the content of the cognition, it has no test to discover."
>
> — Ibid.

*Gloss: Kant states the classic limitation of formal logic: it can only detect formal inconsistency, not material error; logic is a necessary but not sufficient condition of truth.*

### G. W. Leibniz — *The Monadology and Other Philosophical Writings*

> "I hope, therefore, that he will kindly allow me to take this opportunity of explaining this principle, which is of great use in reasoning, and which does not yet appear to be sufficiently employed nor known in all its scope. It has its origin in the conception of the Infinite; it is absolutely necessary in Geometry, and it also holds good in Physics, inasmuch as the Supreme Wisdom, which is the source of all things, acts as a perfect geometrician, and according to a harmony which cannot be bettered. ... The principle may be stated thus: When the difference between two cases can be diminished below any given magnitude in datis or in the antecedents [ce qui est posé] it will necessarily also be diminished below every given magnitude in quaesitis or in the consequents [ce qui en résulte]."
>
> — G. W. Leibniz, *The Monadology and Other Philosophical Writings*, trans. Robert Latta, 1898. From the Appendix: "On the Principle of Continuity."

*Gloss: Leibniz introduces the principle of continuity as a logical‑mathematical axiom, grounding reasoning in the infinite — a metaphysical extension of formal logic.*

## Reframings

No non‑Western reframings are supported by the current corpus. The Chinese tradition developed sophisticated logical thought (Mohist *Mo Jing*, the logic of names and objects), and the Nyāya school of Indian philosophy produced an elaborate pramāṇa‐based logic. Both reframe the question within ethics, soteriology, and epistemology rather than as a purely formal discipline. Future corpus expansions may provide passages for these reframings.
