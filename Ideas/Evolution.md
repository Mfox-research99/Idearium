# Idea: Evolution

## Working definition

How do living forms arise, persist, and change over time? The Western tradition frames this as a question about the origin of species, the mechanisms of descent with modification, and the forces—natural selection, mutual aid, or cosmic law—that drive or constrain transformation. This Idea is shaped decisively by the 19th-century scientific revolution, but its roots reach back to ancient speculations on the chain of being and the transformation of kinds.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Mechanism of change

What drives the modification of species? Competition and natural selection? Cooperation and mutual aid? Internal striving or external necessity?

### Sub-question: Tempo and mode

Does change occur gradually through the accumulation of slight variations, or in bursts? Is the geological record a faithful chronicle or a fragmentary archive?

### Sub-question: Scope of evolution

Does evolution apply only to biological organisms, or to the cosmos, consciousness, and society as well? Is there a direction or telos to evolutionary change?

## Western canon

### Charles Darwin — *On the Origin of Species*

> "I have now recapitulated the chief facts and considerations which have thoroughly convinced me that species have changed, and are still slowly changing by the preservation and accumulation of successive slight favourable variations. Why, it may be asked, have all the most eminent living naturalists and geologists rejected this view of the mutability of species? It cannot be asserted that organic beings in a state of nature are subject to no variation; it cannot be proved that the amount of variation in the course of long ages is a limited quantity; no clear distinction has been, or can be, drawn between species and well-marked varieties. It cannot be maintained that species when intercrossed are invariably sterile, and varieties invariably fertile; or that sterility is a special endowment and sign of creation. The belief that species were immutable productions was almost unavoidable as long as the history of the world was thought to be of short duration."
>
> *Gloss: Darwin commits to the mutability of species and the gradual accumulation of variation as the engine of descent with modification, directly challenging the creationist assumption of fixed kinds.*

> "If we admit that the geological record is imperfect in an extreme degree, then such facts as the record gives, support the theory of descent with modification. New species have come on the stage slowly and at successive intervals; and the amount of change, after equal intervals of time, is widely different in different groups. The extinction of species and of whole groups of species, which has played so conspicuous a part in the history of the organic world, almost inevitably follows on the principle of natural selection; for old forms will be supplanted by new and improved forms."
>
> *Gloss: Darwin acknowledges the imperfection of the fossil record as a necessary condition for his theory, and links extinction to the competitive advantage conferred by natural selection.*

> "Nature may be said to have taken pains to reveal, by rudimentary organs and by homologous structures, her scheme of modification, which it seems that we wilfully will not understand."
>
> *Gloss: Darwin points to vestigial structures and anatomical homologies as evidence for common descent—a pattern that creationism cannot explain without special pleading.*

### Peter Kropotkin — *Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution*

> "Two aspects of animal life impressed me most during the journeys which I made in my youth in Eastern Siberia and Northern Manchuria. One of them was the extreme severity of the struggle for existence which most species of animals have to carry on against an inclement Nature; the enormous destruction of life which periodically results from natural agencies; and the consequent paucity of life over the vast territories which fell under my notice. And the other was, that even in those few spots where animal life was abundant, I failed to find—although I was eagerly looking for it—that bitter struggle for the means of existence, among animals belonging to the same species, which was considered by most Darwinists (though not always by Darwin himself) as the dominant characteristic of the struggle for life, and the main factor of evolution."
>
> *Gloss: Kropotkin challenges the Hobbesian reading of natural selection, arguing from field observation that cooperation within species is a more significant factor in survival and evolutionary success than competition.*

### H. P. Blavatsky — *The Secret Doctrine, Vol. 1*

> "The Monads of the present dissertation are treated, from the standpoint of their individuality, as *Atomic Souls*, before these Atoms descend into pure terrestrial form. For this descent into *concrete* Matter marks the medial point of their own individual pilgrimage. Here, losing in the mineral kingdom their individuality, they begin to ascend through the seven states of terrestrial evolution to that point where a correspondence is firmly established between the human and Deva (divine) consciousness."
>
> *Gloss: Blavatsky re-frames evolution as a spiritual descent into matter followed by an ascent through seven kingdoms—a cosmic, teleological process that includes but transcends biological transformation.*

## Reframings

### Hindu/Buddhist — Samsara

The Western Idea of evolution as a progressive, directional change in biological form over deep time is not the primary frame in South Asian traditions. The cycle of birth, death, and rebirth (*samsara*) describes a different kind of transformation—one that is cyclical rather than linear, driven by karma rather than natural selection, and concerned with the transmigration of consciousness rather than the modification of species. The biological evolution of forms is, in this frame, a secondary phenomenon within a much larger cosmic rhythm of emanation and return.

→ See [[Terms/Samsara]]

### Theosophical — Evolution as cosmic involution

Blavatsky's *The Secret Doctrine* presents a seven-kingdom model of evolution that includes elemental, mineral, vegetable, animal, human, and superhuman stages, all governed by a cyclic law of descent into matter and ascent back to spirit. This is not a competing scientific hypothesis but a metaphysical reframing: evolution is the progressive manifestation of consciousness, not merely the adaptation of organisms to environments.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Nature]] — Evolution is the process by which nature produces and transforms its forms; the two Ideas are inseparable in post-Darwinian thought.
- [[Time]] — Darwin's theory depends on the vast, uniformitarian timescale that Lyell established; evolution is unthinkable without deep time.
- [[Man]] — The descent of man from earlier primates is the most culturally disruptive implication of evolutionary theory.
- [[Progress]] — Evolution is often conflated with progress, but Darwin's mechanism (adaptation, not improvement) does not guarantee directional advancement.
- [[Cause]] — Natural selection is a causal mechanism for evolutionary change, distinct from final causes or divine design.

## Status notes

- **v1 draft.** The Western canon section is thin—only Darwin and Kropotkin are represented with substantive passages. Aristotle's *History of Animals* and *Generation of Animals* should be added for the pre-Darwinian lineage (the scala naturae, spontaneous generation, and the fixity of species). Lamarck's *Zoological Philosophy* (1809) is a critical missing voice for the mechanism of acquired characteristics. Herbert Spencer's *Principles of Biology* (1864) coined "survival of the fittest" and extended evolution to sociology—this should be included if the corpus permits.
- **Reframings are minimal.** The Samsara reframing is present but thin; a stronger passage from the Upanishads or the Bhagavad Gita on the cycle of birth and death would improve it. The Theosophical reframing is included because Blavatsky's passage was retrieved, but it is marginal to the main tradition and should be flagged as such.
- **Passage 3 (Confucian Texts Legge)** was a false positive—the match was to a publisher's advertisement for a book on evolution bound into the volume, not to the Confucian text itself. Dropped.
- **Passage 9 (Newton, *Principia*)** was a false positive—the term "evolute" refers to the geometric curve (the locus of centers of curvature), not to biological evolution. Dropped.
- **Passage 10 (Kropotkin, *Fields, Factories and Workshops*)** was a passing reference to Darwin's theory being "narrowed" by overemphasis on one factor; the substantive Kropotkin passage is from *Mutual Aid*, which is already included.
- **Missing:** The pre-Socratic materialists (Empedocles on chance variation and survival), Lucretius on the trial-and-error origin of species (*De Rerum Natura*), and the medieval chain-of-being tradition. These would fill out the pre-Darwinian history of the Idea.
