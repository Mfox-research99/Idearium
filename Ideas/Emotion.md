# Idea: Emotion

## Working definition

What are emotions, and what role do they play in human life? The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about the relation between feeling, reason, and the body: Are emotions cognitive judgments or irrational passions? Can they be governed by the will or by knowledge? The tradition oscillates between viewing emotions as obstacles to clear thought and as essential components of moral and social existence.

## Western canon

### Spinoza — *Ethics*

> *Part III, General Definition of the Emotions* — "Emotion, which is called a passivity of the soul, is a confused idea, whereby the mind affirms concerning its body, or any part thereof, a force for existence (existendi vis) greater or less than before, and by the presence of which the mind is determined to think of one thing rather than another."
>
> *Gloss: Spinoza defines emotion as a confused idea that registers a change in the body's power of acting — emotion is not a separate faculty but a mode of thought that is inadequate because it reflects the body's state rather than the external cause.*

> *Part IV, Proposition VII* — "An emotion can only be controlled or destroyed by another emotion contrary thereto, and with more power for controlling emotion."
>
> *Gloss: Reason alone cannot master emotion; only a stronger, contrary emotion can displace it. The mind's power over the emotions consists in understanding them and associating them with clear ideas, not in suppressing them by will.*

> *Part V, Scholium to Proposition XX* — "Whence it appears that the mind's power over the emotions consists: I. In the actual knowledge of the emotions… II. In the fact that it separates the emotions from the thought of an external cause… III. In the fact, that, in respect to time, the emotions referred to things, which we distinctly understand, surpass those referred to what we conceive in a confused and fragmentary manner… IV. In the number of causes whereby those modifications are fostered… V. Lastly, in the order wherein the mind can arrange and associate, one with another, its own emotions."
>
> *Gloss: Spinoza's therapeutic program: the mind gains power over emotion not by denying it but by knowing it clearly, detaching it from confused causes, and reordering it through adequate ideas.*

*Translator: R. H. M. Elwes, 1883.*

### Freud — *Group Psychology and the Analysis of the Ego*

> *Chapter 7* — "The perception of the signs of an emotional state is calculated automatically to arouse the same emotion in the person who perceives them. The greater the number of people in whom the same emotion can be simultaneously observed, the stronger does this automatic compulsion grow. The individual loses his power of criticism, and lets himself slip into the same emotion. But in so doing he increases the excitement of the other people, who had produced this effect upon him, and thus the emotional charge of the individuals becomes intensified by mutual interaction. Something is unmistakably at work in the nature of a compulsion to do the same as the others, to remain in harmony with the many."
>
> *Gloss: Freud describes emotional contagion as an automatic, reciprocal intensification that overrides individual critical judgment — the group amplifies emotion through mutual suggestion.*

*Translator: James Strachey, 1922.*

### Adler — *Understanding Human Nature*

> *Chapter on "Affects and Emotions"* — "Since the affects and emotions are closely bound up with the very essence of personality, they are not solitary characteristics of solitary individuals, but are to be found more or less regularly among all people. Every individual is capable of showing some particular emotion if he is brought into the proper situation."
>
> *Gloss: Adler treats emotions as universal, situationally triggered expressions of the individual's style of life and striving for superiority — they are not merely passive states but purposive movements toward a goal.*

> *Chapter on "Anger"* — "Anger is an affect which is the veritable epitome of a striving for power… Individuals who do not have sufficient confidence in their ability to achieve their goal, do not give up their purpose because of their feeling of insecurity, but attempt to approach it by dint of greater efforts, and with the aid of accessory affects and emotions."
>
> *Gloss: For Adler, emotions like anger are compensatory tools used by the individual to overcome inferiority and force recognition — they are not simply reactions but instruments of the will to power.*

*Translator: Walter Béran Wolfe, 1927.*

## Reframings

No non-Western reframings are available from the current corpus. The Western tradition's emphasis on emotion as a cognitive-bodily state to be understood and controlled contrasts with, for example, the Buddhist analysis of *vedanā* (feeling-tone) as a conditioned link in the chain of dependent origination, or the Confucian cultivation of *ren* (humaneness) as an affective attunement that is not opposed to reason. ## Cross-cuts

- [[Desire]] — Emotion is often defined in relation to desire; Spinoza treats desire as one of the three primary emotions.
- [[Pleasure_and_Pain]] — Many theories of emotion (e.g., Spinoza, Freud) ground emotion in the experience of pleasure and pain.
- [[Will]] — The question of whether emotion can be controlled by will or only by another emotion is a central axis of the tradition.
- [[Soul]] — Emotion is traditionally located in the soul, often as the "passive" part.
- [[Body]] — Emotion is inseparable from bodily modification in Spinoza, James, and Freud.
- [[Habit]] — The regulation of emotion through habituation is a theme from Aristotle onward.
