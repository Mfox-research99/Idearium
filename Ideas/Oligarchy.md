# Idea: Oligarchy

## Working definition

Rule by the few, typically the wealthy, in their own interest. The Western tradition, beginning with Plato and Aristotle, treats oligarchy as a corrupted form of aristocracy — a regime where the few govern not by virtue but by property, and where the many are excluded from power. The central question is whether any concentration of power in a minority can be just, and under what conditions it degenerates into faction, tyranny, or revolution.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Definition and species of oligarchy

What distinguishes oligarchy from aristocracy, and what internal variations exist?

### Sub-question: Causes of revolution in oligarchies

Why do oligarchies fall — through internal faction, popular uprising, or the ambition of individual oligarchs?

### Sub-question: Preservation of oligarchy

How can an oligarchy be stabilized, and what does its fragility reveal about the nature of political order?

## Western canon

### Aristotle — *Politics*

> "Of the different species of oligarchies one is, when the right to the offices is regulated by a certain census; so that the poor, although the majority, have no share in it; while all those who are included therein take part in the management of public affairs. Another sort is, when the magistrates are men of very small fortune, who upon any vacancy do themselves fill it up: and if they do this out of the community at large, the state approaches to an aristocracy; if out of any particular class of people, it will be an oligarchy. Another sort of oligarchy is, when the power is an hereditary nobility. The fourth is, when the power is in the same hands as the other, but not under the control of law; and this sort of oligarchy exactly corresponds to a tyranny in monarchies, and to that particular species of democracies which I last mentioned in treating of that state: this has the particular name of a dynasty."
>
> *Gloss: Aristotle classifies four species of oligarchy — from property-based exclusion, through self-perpetuating appointment, to hereditary nobility, and finally lawless rule by a narrow clique (dynasty), which he considers the worst.*

> "Many revolutions also have been brought about in oligarchies by those who could not brook the despotism which those persons assumed who were in power, as at Cnidus and Chios. Changes also may happen by accident in what we call a free state and in an oligarchy; wheresoever the senators, judges, and magistrates are chosen according to a certain census; for it often happens that the highest census is fixed at first; so that a few only could have a share in the government, in an oligarchy, or in a free state those of moderate fortunes only; when the city grows rich, through peace or some other happy cause, it becomes so little that every one's fortune is equal to the census, so that the whole community may partake of all the honours of government; and this change sometimes happens by little and little, and insensible approaches, sometimes quicker."
>
> *Gloss: Oligarchies are overturned when the ruling few become despotic, or when economic shifts make the property qualification accessible to many, transforming the regime into a broader polity.*

> "An oligarchy is also subject to revolutions when the nobility spend their fortunes by luxury; for such persons are desirous of innovations, and either endeavour to be tyrants themselves or to support others in being so, as Hypparinus supported Dionysius of Syracuse. … But if the members of an oligarchy agree among themselves the state is not very easily destroyed."
>
> *Gloss: Internal decay through extravagance and ambition among the oligarchs is a primary cause of revolution; internal unity is the oligarchy's best defense.*

> "Much in the same manner ought that oligarchy to be established which is next in order: but as to that which is most opposite to a pure democracy, and approaches nearest to a dynasty and a tyranny, as it is of all others the worst, so it requires the greatest care and caution to preserve it: for as bodies of sound and healthy constitutions and ships which are well manned and well found for sailing can bear many injuries without perishing, while a diseased body or a leaky ship with an indifferent crew cannot support the least shock; so the worst-established governments want most looking after. A number of citizens is the preservation of a democracy; for these are opposed to those rights which are founded in rank: on the contrary, the preservation of an oligarchy depends upon the due regulation of the different orders in the society."
>
> *Gloss: The worst form of oligarchy — lawless and narrow — is the most fragile; its preservation requires constant vigilance and careful ordering of social ranks, unlike democracy which is sustained by the multitude itself.*

All passages from Aristotle, *Politics*, trans. William Ellis, 1912.

## Reframings

No non-Western corpus passages were retrieved for this Idea. The Confucian tradition, however, has a concept of rule by the virtuous few (*ren* and *yi* guiding the junzi) that might be mistaken for oligarchy but is fundamentally different: it is rule by moral worth, not wealth or birth, and is meant to serve the whole. ## Cross-cuts

- [[Aristocracy]] — oligarchy is the corrupted form of aristocracy; the distinction turns on whether the few rule by virtue or by wealth.
- [[Democracy]] — the paired opposite in Aristotle's typology; each regime's instability drives it toward the other.
- [[Tyranny]] — the worst oligarchies (dynasties) are functionally tyrannies of a few; the same dynamics of lawless power apply.
- [[Revolution]] — Aristotle's analysis of stasis in oligarchies is a central case study in political change.
- [[Constitution]] — the classification of regimes (politeia) is the framework within which oligarchy is defined.
