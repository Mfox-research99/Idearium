# Idea: Nature

## Working definition

“Nature” in the Western tradition is a polysemous term carrying at least four distinct senses: (1) the essence or intrinsic character of a thing (Greek *physis*); (2) the totality of the physical universe, including its regularities; (3) a normative principle (natural law, natural right) that prescribes how things ought to be; (4) that which stands opposed to art, convention, or supernature. The central question is whether nature is a descriptive fact, a prescriptive norm, or both — and what happens when the two conflict.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Nature as Essence vs. Nature as Totality

Is “nature” the inner principle of motion and rest in a thing (Aristotle’s *physis*), or the sum total of all such things (the modern scientific sense)? The distinction governs whether one speaks of “the nature of X” or “Nature” as a proper noun.

### Sub-question: Nature vs. Convention

What is natural in human affairs, and what is merely customary? The line between *physis* and *nomos* (law/custom) is a persistent fault line from the Sophists through Pascal and Montaigne.

### Sub-question: Natural Law

If nature is normative, can its precepts change? Aquinas distinguishes immutable first principles from mutable secondary precepts; Augustine grounds the law in the heart that iniquity cannot efface.

### Sub-question: Human Nature and the Good

What does it mean to live “according to nature”? The Stoic and Epicurean answers differ, and Spinoza redefines the good as whatever is in harmony with our nature.

## Western canon

### Augustine — *City of God*

> *Book XII, ch. 1* — “Now every fault injures the nature, and is consequently contrary to the nature. The creature, therefore, which cleaves to God, differs from those who do not, not by nature, but by fault; and yet by this very fault the nature itself is proved to be very noble and admirable. For that nature is certainly praised, the fault of which is justly blamed.”
>
> *Gloss:* Augustine establishes that fault is not a second nature but a privation; the goodness of the original nature is the condition for the blameworthiness of its corruption. Nature remains fundamentally good even in the fallen creature.

### Aquinas — *Summa Theologica*

> *First Part of the Second Part, Question 94, Article 5* — “A change in the natural law may be understood in two ways. First, by way of addition. In this sense nothing hinders the natural law from being changed: since many things for the benefit of human life have been added over and above the natural law, both by the Divine law and by human laws. Secondly, a change in the natural law may be understood by way of subtraction, so that what previously was according to the natural law, ceases to be so. In this sense, the natural law is altogether unchangeable in its first principles: but in its secondary principles, which, as we have said (A. 4), are certain detailed proximate conclusions drawn from the first principles, the natural law … may be changed in some particular cases of rare occurrence, through some special causes hindering the observance of such precepts.”
>
> *Gloss:* Aquinas holds that nature’s normative core (the first principles of natural law) is immutable, but its application to concrete circumstances admits of change — a crucial concession that prevents natural law from collapsing into rigid legalism.

> *First Part of the Second Part, Question 94, Article 6* — “Augustine says (*Conf.* ii): ‘Thy law is written in the hearts of men, which iniquity itself effaces not.’ But the law which is written in men’s heart is the natural law. Therefore the natural law cannot be abolished from the heart of man.”
>
> *Gloss:* The natural law is ineradicable from human reason, even if sin obscures its application. Nature, for Aquinas, is never wholly lost.

### Montaigne — *Essays*

> *Book III, ch. 13 (“Of Experience”)* — “Nature is a gentle guide, but not more sweet and gentle than prudent and just. … I hunt after her foot throughout: we have confounded it with artificial traces; and that academic and peripatetic good, which is ‘to live according to it,’ becomes on this account hard to limit and explain; and that of the Stoics, neighbour to it, which is ‘to consent to nature.’”
>
> *Gloss:* Montaigne treats nature as a reliable but elusive guide, warning that human artifice has overlaid and confused her traces. The ancient ideal of “living according to nature” is harder to realize than the philosophers supposed.

### Pascal — *Pensées*

> *Fragment 89 (Brunschvicg)* — “Custom is our nature. He who is accustomed to the faith believes in it, can no longer fear hell, and believes in nothing else. … What are our natural principles but principles of custom? In children they are those which they have received from the habits of their fathers, as hunting in animals. A different custom will cause different natural principles.”
>
> *Gloss:* Pascal radicalizes the *physis/nomos* distinction: what we call “nature” in human beings is largely second nature — the sediment of custom. There is no fixed human nature beneath the layer of habit.

> *Fragment 91* — “When we see the same effect always recur, we infer a natural necessity in it, as that there will be a to-morrow, etc. But nature often deceives us, and does not subject herself to her own rules.”
>
> *Gloss:* Even the regularity we attribute to nature is an inference from custom, and nature can violate her own apparent rules. Pascal anticipates the problem of induction.

### Spinoza — *Ethics*

> *Part IV, Proposition 31, Proof* — “In so far as a thing is in harmony with our nature, it cannot be bad for it. It will therefore necessarily be either good or indifferent. … Therefore, in so far as a thing is in harmony with our nature, it is necessarily good.”
>
> *Gloss:* Spinoza identifies the good with whatever agrees with our nature — a thoroughly immanent ethics that makes nature the sole criterion of value, without appeal to transcendent norms.

## Reframings

### Chinese Daoist — Tao

The Daoist tradition does not oppose nature to convention in the Western manner. The *Tao* (Way) is the spontaneous, unforced pattern of the cosmos; human artifice and moral striving are seen as departures from it, not fulfillments. “Nature” in the Western sense of a normative essence is replaced by the concept of *wu wei* (non-forced action) — acting in such a way that one’s activity is continuous with the Dao rather than imposed upon it. The English word “nature” in pre-1929 translations (e.g., Legge’s *Tao Te Ching*) often renders *tzu-jan* (self-so), but the fit is partial: the Daoist “nature” is not a principle of identity but a field of spontaneous generation.

### Hindu — Dharma

In Hindu thought, the closest analogue to “nature” as normative order is *dharma* — the cosmic law that sustains the universe and prescribes the duties appropriate to each being and station. Unlike the Western natural law tradition, dharma is not a universal code written in reason but a differentiated order: what is natural (dharmic) for a warrior differs from what is natural for a priest. The pre-1929 translations (e.g., Arnold’s *Bhagavad Gītā*) render *dharma* as “duty,” which captures the prescriptive force but loses the cosmic scope. The English “nature” appears in these translations as a stand-in for *svabhāva* (one’s own nature or inherent disposition), as when Krishna tells Arjuna to follow his own nature as a warrior.
