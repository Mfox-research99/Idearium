# Idea: Metaphysics

## Working definition

What is ultimately real, and how can we know it? The Western tradition frames this as a science of first principles—inquiry into being as being, the nature of substance, cause, God, and the soul—that claims a special kind of knowledge not derived from empirical experience. The Kantian critical turn reframes the question entirely: metaphysics becomes an investigation into the conditions of possibility for a priori synthetic cognition, rather than a direct inquiry into transcendent realities. Other traditions reframe the question through non-substance ontologies, negative theology, or the identity of self and absolute; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: The Inevitability of Metaphysics

Why human reason cannot abandon the metaphysical impulse.

### Sub-question: The Nature of Metaphysical Judgments

Whether metaphysical claims are analytic or synthetic, a priori or empirical.

### Sub-question: The Division of Metaphysics

Speculative (nature) versus practical (ethics) metaphysics.

### Sub-question: Metaphysics and Mathematics

How philosophical and mathematical a priori cognition differ.

### Sub-question: The Need for Critique

Why a critical examination of reason's own powers must precede any metaphysical system.

## Western canon

### Immanuel Kant — *Prolegomena to Any Future Metaphysics* (trans. Paul Carus, 1912)

> "That the human mind will ever give up metaphysical researches is as little to be expected as that we should prefer to give up breathing altogether, to avoid inhaling impure air. There will therefore always be metaphysics in the world; nay, every one, especially every man of reflexion, will have it, and for want of a recognised standard, will shape it for himself after his own pattern. What has hitherto been called metaphysics, cannot satisfy any critical mind, but to forego it entirely is impossible; therefore a Critique of Pure Reason itself must now be attempted or, if one exists, investigated, and brought to the full test, because there is no other means of supplying this pressing want, which is something more than mere thirst for knowledge."

> *Gloss: Kant presents metaphysics as an ineradicable need of human reason—the impulse to ask ultimate questions is not a contingent curiosity but a structural feature of mind, and the failure of dogmatic metaphysics leaves us not with the option of abandoning the enterprise but with the necessity of critiquing reason itself.*

> "Metaphysical judgments, properly so called, are all synthetical. We must distinguish judgments pertaining to metaphysics from metaphysical judgments properly so called. Many of the former are analytical, but they only afford the means for metaphysical judgments, which are the whole end of the science, and which are always synthetical. For if there be concepts pertaining to metaphysics (as, for example, that of substance), the judgments springing from simple analysis of them also pertain to metaphysics, as, for example, substance is that which only exists as subject; and by means of several such analytical judgments, we seek to approach the definition of the concept. But as the analysis of a pure concept of the understanding pertaining to metaphysics, does not proceed in any different manner from the dissection of any other, even empirical, concepts, not pertaining to metaphysics (such as: air is an elastic fluid, the elasticity of which is not destroyed by any known degree of cold), it follows that the concept indeed, but not the analytical judgment, is properly metaphysical."

> *Gloss: Kant draws a sharp distinction between judgments that merely analyze metaphysical concepts (analytical, not distinctive) and genuinely metaphysical judgments, which must be synthetic—extending knowledge a priori beyond what is contained in the concept.*

### Immanuel Kant — *Critique of Pure Reason* (trans. J. M. D. Meiklejohn, 1855)

> "Metaphysic is divided into that of the speculative and that of the practical use of pure reason, and is, accordingly, either the metaphysic of nature, or the metaphysic of ethics. The former contains all the pure rational principles—based upon conceptions alone (and thus excluding mathematics)—of all theoretical cognition; the latter, the principles which determine and necessitate à priori all action."

> *Gloss: As a systematic discipline, metaphysics splits into two domains: the theoretical investigation of nature through a priori concepts, and the a priori grounding of moral action—both parts of a single science of pure reason.*

> "It is a stranger who has been long a guest; and we make it welcome to stay, until it can take up a more suitable abode in a complete system of anthropology—the pendant to empirical physics. The above is the general idea of metaphysics, which, as more was expected from it than could be looked for with justice, and as these pleasant expectations were unfortunately never realized, fell into general disrepute. Our Critique must have fully convinced the reader that, although metaphysics cannot form the foundation of religion, it must always be one of its most important bulwarks, and that human reason, which naturally pursues a dialectical course, cannot do without this science, which checks its tendencies towards dialectic and, by elevating reason to a scientific and clear self-knowledge, prevents the ravages which a lawless speculative reason would infallibly commit in the sphere of morals as well as in that of religion."

> *Gloss: Kant acknowledges the disrepute into which metaphysics has fallen—it has been a perennial guest overstaying its welcome in the household of the sciences—yet insists that a properly critiqued metaphysics remains indispensable as a bulwark of morality and religion, not as their foundation but as a check on the excesses of unaided speculative reason.*

> "The fundamental idea of metaphysics was obscured on another side by the fact that this kind of à priori cognition showed a certain similarity in character with the science of mathematics. Both have the property in common of possessing an à priori origin; but, in the one, our knowledge is based upon conceptions, in the other, on the construction of conceptions. Thus a decided dissimilarity between philosophical and mathematical cognition comes out—a dissimilarity which was always felt, but which could not be made distinct for want of an insight into the criteria of the difference."

> *Gloss: A key source of confusion in the history of metaphysics has been its superficial resemblance to mathematics—both are a priori, but mathematics constructs its concepts while philosophy analyzes them; failing to recognize this difference led to false expectations and methodological errors that discredited the science.*

## Reframings

### Hindu — Brahman / Atman

The Western metaphysical question "what is ultimately real" is reframed in Advaita Vedanta as the identity of the self (Atman) and the absolute reality (Brahman). This is not primarily a theoretical inquiry into substance or cause but a soteriological recognition—the phenomenal world is Maya (not unreal but not ultimately real), and liberation (Moksha) consists in knowing the non-duality of Atman and Brahman. The English "metaphysics" captures the scope but obscures the existential stakes: the Hindu inquiry is not a science of first principles separable from the transformation of the knower.

### Buddhist — Sunyata / Anatman

Buddhist thought refuses the grounding move of Western metaphysics: there is no permanent self (Anatman), no unchanging substance, no ground of being. The concept of Sunyata (emptiness) does not name a transcendent reality but the absence of inherent existence in all phenomena. This is not metaphysical nihilism but a middle way that rejects both eternalism (the view that anything permanent exists) and annihilationism (the view that nothing exists across moments). The Western metaphysical project of discovering what *ultimately* exists is here replaced by an analysis of dependently originated phenomena that are empty of svabhava (own-being).

→ See [[Terms/Sunyata]], [[Terms/Anatman]]

### Daoist — Tao

The Daoist tradition does not ask about being as being but about the Way (Tao) that cannot be named—the unforced pattern of reality that precedes and exceeds conceptual determination. Where Western metaphysics seeks definitions and categorial frameworks, the *Tao Te Ching* insists that the Tao that can be spoken is not the eternal Tao. This is not a skepticism about metaphysical knowledge but a different orientation: wisdom consists in attunement and non-action (Wu Wei), not in grasping first principles.

→ See [[Terms/Tao]], [[Terms/Wu_Wei]]

### Islamic / Sufi — Tawhid

In Islamic thought, the metaphysical question is radically unified: Tawhid (the oneness of God) is not a proposition among others but the single truth from which all reality derives. Philosophy (falsafa) engages Aristotle and Neoplatonism within this framework, while Sufi metaphysics (e.g., Ibn al-Arabi's *wahdat al-wujud*, unity of existence) pushes toward a non-dual understanding in which all multiplicity is a manifestation of the single Real. The Western division of metaphysics into ontology, theology, and cosmology is here collapsed into the discipline of tawhid.

→ See [[Terms/Tawhid]]

## Cross-cuts

- [[Being]] — Metaphysics is the science of being as being; this was its classical Aristotelian definition.
- [[Cause]] — The causal nexus is central to both Aristotelian and post-Kantian metaphysical inquiry.
- [[Form]] — The form/matter distinction is a core metaphysical tool from Aristotle through the Scholastics.
- [[God]] — Natural theology is traditionally a branch of metaphysics; Kant denies it can be a science while affirming its regulative necessity.
- [[Knowledge]] — The Kantian critique makes epistemology prior to metaphysics; the question "what can we know?" conditions the question "what is real?"
- [[Logic]] — Metaphysics is distinguished from logic by its material (real) content, even when both are a priori.
- [[Matter]] — Substance theory and the ontology of material objects are central metaphysical concerns.
- [[Mind]] — The mind-world relation is the axis of modern metaphysical debate from Descartes through Kant.
- [[Nature]] — The metaphysic of nature is one of Kant's two branches of the discipline.
- [[One_and_Many]] — The problem of the one and the many is a recurrent metaphysical structure from Parmenides to the Neoplatonists.
- [[Philosophy]] — Metaphysics is traditionally "first philosophy," the foundational discipline of the philosophical system.
- [[Physics]] — The boundary between physics and metaphysics is a contested site; Kant critiques the conflation.
- [[Religion]] — Kant holds that metaphysics cannot *found* religion but serves as its indispensable bulwark.
- [[Science]] — The scientific status of metaphysics is the central critical question; Kant attempts to secure metaphysics on the model of a science of a priori principles.
- [[Soul]] — Rational psychology (the metaphysics of the soul) is a traditional branch that Kant subjects to critique.
- [[Space]] — The status of space and time as a priori intuitions or real entities is a metaphysical question.
- [[Theology]] — Transcendental theology is the third branch of Kant's critique, following rational psychology and cosmology.
- [[Time]] — Together with space, time is a central Kantian metaphysical structure—an a priori form of intuition.
- [[Truth]] — Metaphysical truth has been understood as correspondence, coherence, or (in Kant) as the agreement of cognition with its a priori conditions.
- [[Universal_and_Particular]] — The problem of universals is a perennial metaphysical question about the reality of general concepts.
