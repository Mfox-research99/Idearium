# Idea: Form

## Working definition

What gives a thing its identity, structure, or intelligibility — the question of what makes something *this* rather than *that*. In the Western tradition, Form is central to metaphysics (Plato’s separate *eidos*, Aristotle’s immanent *morphē*), epistemology (Kant’s forms of intuition and understanding), and biology (the form of living kinds). It also appears in political economy as the structural expressions of value. This page curates only passages supplied in the current corpus build; major authors (Plato, Aristotle, Plotinus, Aquinas, Kant) are not yet attested.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Form as a principle of identity and variation

How a shared form persists across individuals while allowing for difference.

### Sub-question: Form of value in exchange

How value takes on distinct forms (elementary, expanded, general, money) as a social expression of abstract human labour.

## Western canon

### Hermes Trismegistus — *Hermetica*

> "...though each living being has in all respects the form which is proper to its kind, the individuals, while one and all have the same form, yet differ among themselves. For instance, though the human race has a common form, so that we can know from a man's appearance that he is a man, at the same time individual men, for all their sameness of form, yet differ one from another. For it is impossible that any single form should come into being which is exactly like a second, if they originate at different points of time, and at places differently situated; but the forms change at every moment in each hour of the revolution of that celestial circle in which resides the god whom we have named Pantomorphos."
>
> *Gloss: Form is here a species-typical structure that admits individual variation, driven by a celestial power that produces endless distinct copies of the same type. The problem of universals meets cosmic emanation.*
>
> Trans. Walter Scott, 1924.

### Karl Marx — *Capital, Volume I*

> "In passing from form A to form B, and from the latter to form C, the changes are fundamental. On the other hand, there is no difference between forms C and D, except that, in the latter, gold has assumed the equivalent form in the place of linen. Gold is in form D, what linen was in form C — the universal equivalent. The progress consists in this alone, that the character of direct and universal exchangeability — in other words, that the universal equivalent form — has now, by social custom, become finally identified with the substance, gold."
>
> *Gloss: The forms of value (elementary, expanded, general, money) are not Platonic essences but historical-social expressions of the same underlying substance — abstract labour. Form here is a structural stage in a developmental logic.*
>
> Translator not specified in source; standard English edition: Samuel Moore and Edward Aveling, 1887.
