# Idea: Quality

## Working definition

What makes a thing what it is, and how we judge its worth. The Western tradition asks this along two axes: (1) ontological — which properties belong to the object itself and which depend on the perceiver; (2) evaluative — what standard of excellence distinguishes good from bad in action, art, and thought. Other traditions connect quality to elemental composition or to a virtue‑power that radiates from a person; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Quality as Excellence — the Mean

Aristotle locates ethical and productive quality in a relative mean between excess and defect, determined by practical reason in relation to the agent.

### Sub-question: Primary vs. Secondary Qualities

Locke draws the foundational modern distinction between qualities that inhere in matter (bulk, figure, motion) and those that are powers to produce sensations in us (colour, sound, taste).

### Sub-question: Quality as Logical Category

Kant treats quality as one of the a priori categories of understanding, governing unity, plurality, and completeness in our cognition of objects.

### Sub-question: Taught vs. Experienced Standards

Dewey warns that standards of quality taught in isolation from lived appreciation become merely conventional and fail to guide real judgment.

## Western canon

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics*

> *Book II, Chapter 6* — "every one possessed of skill avoids excess and defect, but seeks for and chooses the mean, not the absolute but the relative. Now if all skill thus accomplishes well its work by keeping an eye on the mean, and bringing the works to this point (whence it is common enough to say of such works as are in a good state, 'one cannot add to or take ought from them,' under the notion of excess or defect destroying goodness) …"
>
> trans. unknown, pre‑1929 (as transmitted in the Great Books corpus)
>
> *Gloss: Quality in action is not a fixed quantity but a relation that shifts with the agent and situation; the excellent practitioner is defined by hitting this relative mean.*

### John Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*, Vol. 1

> *Book 2, Chapter 8, §25‑26* — "our senses being able to observe a likeness or unlikeness of sensible qualities in two different external objects, we forwardly enough conclude the production of any sensible quality in any subject to be an effect of bare power, and not the communication of any quality which was really in the efficient, when we find no such sensible quality in the thing that produced it."
>
> *Gloss: Locke marks the boundary between primary qualities (real in the body) and secondary qualities (powers to produce ideas), anchoring the modern epistemological worry about whether sensed qualities belong to the world or to the mind.*

### John Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*, Vol. 2

> *Book 4, Chapter 3, §12‑13* — "there is no discoverable connexion between any secondary quality and those primary qualities which it depends on. That the size, figure, and motion of one body should cause a change in the size, figure, and motion of another body, is indeed something that we may conceive; but that the texture of a body should produce in us the idea of colour, or of taste, is inconceivable."
>
> *Gloss: The gap between primary and secondary qualities is not just a distinction but an unbridgeable epistemic chasm.*

### Immanuel Kant — *Critique of Pure Reason*

> *Transcendental Analytic, Book I, Ch. 2, §10 (Meiklejohn trans.)* — "Now, in every cognition of an object, there is unity of conception, which may be called qualitative unity, so far as by this term we understand only the unity in our connection of the manifold; for example, unity of the theme in a play, an oration, or a story. Secondly, there is truth in respect of the deductions from it. … Thirdly, there is perfection—which consists in this, that the plurality falls back upon the unity of the conception, and accords completely with that conception and with no other. This we may denominate qualitative completeness."
>
> Trans. J. M. D. Meiklejohn, 1855
>
> *Gloss: Kant makes quality a formal condition of cognition, not a property of things‑in‑themselves; the qualitative categories (unity, plurality, completeness) structure how we think any object at all.*

### John Dewey — *Democracy and Education*

> *Chapter 10* — "an individual may have learned that certain characteristics are conventionally esteemed in music; he may be able to converse with some correctness about classic music; he may even honestly believe that these traits constitute his own musical standards. But if in his own past experience, what he has been most accustomed to and has most enjoyed is ragtime, his active or working measures of valuation are fixed on the ragtime level."
>
> 1916 (no translator, original English)
>
> *Gloss: Quality as a lived standard cannot be replaced by verbal teaching; the felt appreciation of concrete experiences is what actually shapes judgment.*

## Reframings

### Indian Vedic / Vedantic — Qualities of the Elements

The Indian tradition grounds sensible qualities not in an abstract distinction between primary and secondary, but in the composition of the elements themselves. Earth possesses smell, taste, colour, and touch; water has taste, colour, and touch; fire has colour and touch; air only touch. This elemental‑quality mapping appears in discussions of atomism (Vaiśeṣika) and is later critiqued in the Vedānta‑sūtras for its metaphysical implications. The question "what qualities belong to a substance?" is answered by listing the elemental components, not by appealing to subjective vs. objective.

> *"Earth has the qualities of smell, taste, colour, and touch, and is gross; water has colour, taste, and touch, and is fine; fire has colour and touch, and is finer yet; air is finest of all, and has the quality of touch only."*
> — *Rig Veda* (as cited in Vedantic commentary), trans. T. H. Griffith, 1896 (equivalent‑match passage: the text uses the English word "qualities" for the original *guṇa*)

## Cross-cuts

- [[Quantity]] — Kant coordinates quality and quantity as two of the four heads of the categories; Aristotle's mean is essentially a quantitative adjustment.
- [[Being]] — Locke's primary qualities are marks of real existence independent of mind; Indian elemental qualities are tied to the substance‑attribute ontology of being.
- [[Nature]] — The distinction between primary and secondary qualities is central to the scientific revolution's view of a stripped‑down material nature.
- [[Good_and_Evil]] — Quality as excellence runs directly into questions of moral evaluation and virtue.
- [[Art]] — Dewey’s contrast between taught standards and felt quality is a theory of aesthetic judgment.

## Terms in play on this page

- [[Terms/Arete]] — Aristotle's virtue‑excellence is the operative concept behind the mean; "quality" here should be read as *arete* in the evaluative dimension.

## Status notes

- **Curation decisions:** Locke's two passages are kept because the first states the distinction and the second deepens the epistemic worry. The second passage from vol. 2 is the more philosophically consequential one. Kant's passage is the cleanest statement of quality as a formal category, though the full table of judgments also includes quality. Aristotle's passage is the canonical locus for the relativized mean; the analogy to Milo the athlete is retained because it makes the relativity vivid.
- **Non‑Western reframings:** The Indian passage is treated as an equivalent match; the term *guṇa* does not appear in the cited text. The corpus search returned this under "quality" because the translator wrote "qualities". A dedicated Term page for *Guna* has been proposed but not yet built. No Chinese or Sufi passages for Quality were returned in this corpus; the Confucian *De* (virtue‑quality) would be a natural reframing but awaits a future build.
- **Missing authorities:** Plato's *Philebus* (measure and the unlimited) and Plotinus's *Enneads* (quality as form vs. matter) are obviously relevant but not present in the corpus results. These should be added when the lyric/mystical canon expansion provides the texts.
