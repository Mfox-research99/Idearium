# Idea: Infinity

## Working definition

What it means for something to be without limit — in magnitude, divisibility, duration, or number. The Western tradition has approached this primarily through mathematics (the infinite series, the continuum), metaphysics (the infinite regress of causes or demonstrations), and theology (the infinite nature of God). The concept is often treated as a negative idea — the negation of finitude — rather than a positive intuition. Other traditions reframe the question through emptiness, the unbounded Tao, or the infinite as a positive attribute of the divine; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

- **Mathematical infinity** — the infinite in number, space, and divisibility.
- **Metaphysical infinity** — infinite regress in causation, demonstration, and definition.
- **Theological infinity** — the infinite as an attribute of God.
- **Psychological infinity** — how the mind forms the idea of the infinite.

## Western canon

### Aristotle — *Posterior Analytics*

> *Book I, ch. 19-22* — "The first question is, must this series terminate, or can it proceed to infinity? The second question is as follows: Suppose nothing is essentially predicated of A, but A is predicated primarily of H and of no intermediate prior term, and suppose H similarly related to G and G to B; then must this series also terminate, or can it too proceed to infinity? … This is the equivalent of inquiring, do demonstrations proceed to infinity, i.e. is everything demonstrable?"
>
> *Gloss: Aristotle treats infinity as a problem for demonstrative science: if every proposition requires a prior demonstration, the chain either terminates in first principles or regresses infinitely. He argues for termination, rejecting the possibility of an infinite regress in demonstration.*

Trans. G.R.G. Mure, 1928.

### John Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*

> *Book II, ch. 17, §1* — "FINITE and INFINITE seem to me to be looked upon by the mind as the MODES OF QUANTITY, and to be attributed primarily in their first designation only to those things which have parts, and are capable of increase or diminution by the addition or subtraction of any the least part: and such are the ideas of space, duration, and number… It is true, that we cannot but be assured, that the great God, of whom and from whom are all things, is incomprehensibly infinite: but yet, when we apply to that first and supreme Being our idea of infinite, in our weak and narrow thoughts, we do it primarily in respect of his duration and ubiquity; and, I think, more figuratively than literally."
>
> *Gloss: Locke grounds the idea of infinity in the experience of endless addition — we have no positive idea of the infinite, only the power to add without end. Even divine infinity is conceived through the modes of duration and space.*

> *Book II, ch. 17, §11-12* — "The same happens also in space, wherein, conceiving ourselves to be, as it were, in the centre, we do on all sides pursue those indeterminable lines of number; and reckoning any way from ourselves, a yard, mile, diameter of the earth or orbis magnus,—by the infinity of number, we add others to them, as often as we will. And having no more reason to set bounds to those repeated ideas than we have to set bounds to number, we have that indeterminable idea of immensity. … And since in any bulk of matter our thoughts can never arrive at the utmost divisibility, therefore there is an apparent infinity to us also in that, which has the infinity also of number."
>
> *Gloss: Locke distinguishes two kinds of infinity: the additive infinity of space and duration (endless extension) and the divisive infinity of matter (endless division). Both are derived from the endless addibility of number.*

1689.

## Reframings

### Buddhist — Sunyata (Emptiness)

The Buddhist tradition does not posit an infinite substance or an infinite regress; instead, it analyzes all phenomena as empty of intrinsic nature (*sunyata*). This is not a negative limit but the absence of fixed essence — a kind of "infinity" that is not a quantity but a relational openness. The Western idea of infinity as endless addition or extension does not map cleanly onto *sunyata*, which is more akin to the unboundedness of dependent origination.

→ See [[Terms/Sunyata]]

### Daoist — Tao (The Way)

The *Tao Te Ching* describes the Tao as "the unnameable," "the beginning of heaven and earth," and "the mother of the ten thousand things." It is not infinite in the sense of endless number or extension, but as the source that cannot be exhausted or fully grasped. The Daoist approach reframes infinity as the generative ground that is both empty and inexhaustible — closer to a qualitative unboundedness than a quantitative one.
