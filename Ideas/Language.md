# Idea: Language

## Working definition

Language is the human capacity for symbolic communication — spoken, written, signed, or gestured — through which thought is expressed, knowledge transmitted, and community constituted. The Western tradition asks: Is language conventional or natural? Does it reflect thought or shape it? What is gained or lost in the diversity of tongues? The question is posed primarily through epistemology (how language relates to truth) and social theory (how language binds or divides peoples).

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Origin and nature of language

Whether language arises by convention, by nature, or by divine gift; whether it is fundamentally a tool of thought or a social institution.

### Sub-question: Language and thought

How language mediates, enables, or limits thinking — whether there can be thought without words, and whether the structure of a language shapes the mind of its speakers.

### Sub-question: Language and society

Language as the bond of community, the vehicle of tradition, and the marker of cultural identity — including the political and theological significance of linguistic diversity.

### Sub-question: Non-verbal language

Whether gesture, expression, and bodily motion constitute a language, and what this reveals about the nature of signification itself.

## Western canon

### Plato — Theaetetus

> "They have not the mental inheritance of thoughts and ideas handed down by tradition, 'the slow additions that build up the mind' of the human race. And language, which is the great educator of mankind, is wanting in them; whereas in us language is ever present—even in the infant the latent power of naming is almost immediately observable. … For simultaneous with their growth in man a growth of language must be supposed. The child of two years old sees the fire once and again, and the feeble observation of the same recurring object is associated with the feeble utterance of the name by which he is taught to call it. Soon he learns to utter the name when the object is no longer there, but the desire or imagination of it is present to him. At first in every use of the word there is a colour of sense, an indistinct picture of the object which accompanies it. But in later years he sees in the name only the universal or class word, and the more abstract the notion becomes, the more vacant is the image which is presented to him."
>
> *Gloss: Language is the "great educator" — the vehicle through which tradition and thought are transmitted across generations. Naming is the primordial act that bridges sense and abstraction, and the growth of language in the individual recapitulates the growth of mind itself.*

### Augustine — City of God, Vol. II

> "Some one will say: If the earth was divided by languages in the days of Peleg, Heber's son, that language, which was formerly common to all, should rather have been called after Peleg. But we are to understand that Heber himself gave to his son this name Peleg, which means Division; because he was born when the earth was divided, that is, at the very time of the division, and that this is the meaning of the words, 'In his days the earth was divided.' For unless Heber had been still alive when the languages were multiplied, the language which was preserved in his house would not have been called after him. We are induced to believe that this was the primitive and common language, because the multiplication and change of languages was introduced as a punishment, and it is fit to ascribe to the people of God an immunity from this punishment."
>
> *Gloss: Linguistic diversity is a divine punishment for human pride at Babel; the preservation of a single primitive language (Hebrew) in the line of Heber marks the continuity of God's covenant people. Language unity is the original state; division is the consequence of sin.*

### Montaigne — Essays (Cotton translation)

> "What with the hands? We require, promise, call, dismiss, threaten, pray, supplicate, deny, refuse, interrogate, admire, number, confess, repent, fear, express confusion, doubt, instruct, command, incite, encourage, swear, testify, accuse, condemn, absolve, abuse, despise, defy, provoke, flatter, applaud, bless, submit, mock, reconcile, recommend, exalt, entertain, congratulate, complain, grieve, despair, wonder, exclaim, and what not! And all this with a variety and multiplication, even emulating speech. With the head we invite, remand, confess, deny, give the lie, welcome, honour, reverence, disdain, demand, rejoice, lament, reject, caress, rebuke, submit, huff, encourage, threaten, assure, and inquire. What with the eyebrows?—what with the shoulders! There is not a motion that does not speak, and in an intelligible language without discipline, and a public language."
>
> *Gloss: Gesture and bodily motion constitute a universal language that requires no instruction — it is "public" and "intelligible" across all human communities, rivaling speech in its expressive range. Language is not confined to the tongue.*

### Voltaire — Philosophical Dictionary

> "The genius of this language is order and clarity; for each language has its genius, and this genius consists in the facility which the language gives for expressing oneself more or less happily, for using or rejecting the familiar twists of other languages. French having no declensions, and being always subject to the article, cannot adopt Greek and Latin inversions; it obliges words to arrange themselves in the natural order of ideas. Only in one way can one say 'Plancus a pris soin des affaires de Cesar.' That is the only arrangement one can give to these words. Express this phrase in Latin—Res Caesaris Plancus diligenter curavit: one can arrange these words in a hundred and twenty ways, without injuring the sense and without troubling the language."
>
> *Gloss: Each language has a distinct "genius" — a character rooted in its grammatical structure. French compels the natural order of ideas; Latin permits free inversion. The structure of a language is not neutral but shapes what can be said and how clearly.*

## Reframings

### Chinese Daoist — Zhuangzi on the limits of language

The Daoist tradition does not ask "What is language?" as an epistemological question but as a spiritual one: language is a provisional tool that must be released once the idea is grasped. Zhuangzi distinguishes "natural speech" that flows from the heart (in accord with the divine) from artificial speech that seeks persuasion or authority. The highest wisdom is to find someone who can "ignore language" and yet converse — a paradox that points to the Dao, which cannot be named. The Western framing of language as the vehicle of truth is here inverted: language is more often the veil.
