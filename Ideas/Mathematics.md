# Idea: Mathematics

## Working definition

What is the nature of mathematical knowledge, and how does it relate to other forms of inquiry? The Western tradition has asked whether mathematics discovers eternal truths or constructs them, whether its objects exist independently of the mind, and whether its method of demonstration can be extended to philosophy, theology, or ethics. The question is framed primarily through the contrast between the certainty of mathematical reasoning and the contingency of empirical or metaphysical knowledge.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Nature of mathematical objects

Are numbers and figures real entities, mental constructions, or convenient fictions? The Platonic tradition treats them as eternal forms; the Aristotelian tradition as abstractions from matter; the Kantian tradition as forms of sensible intuition.

### Sub-question: Method of mathematics

Does mathematics proceed by construction (Kant) or by deduction from axioms (Euclid)? Is it a purely formal science or does it require intuition?

### Sub-question: Relation to metaphysics and religion

Is mathematics a handmaid to philosophy, a neutral tool, or a rival claimant to truth? The question recurs from Plato’s Academy to Al-Ghazali’s critique of the philosophers.

## Western canon

### Nicomachus of Gerasa — *Introduction to Arithmetic*

> “For mathematical speculations are always to be interlocked and to be explained one by means of another … the germs of these ideas are taken over into arithmetic, as the science which is the mother of geometry and more elementary than it. For we recall that a short time ago we saw that arithmetic abolishes the other sciences with itself, but is not abolished by them, and conversely is of necessity implied by them but does not itself imply them.”
>
> *Gloss: Nicomachus, writing in the Pythagorean-Platonic tradition, asserts arithmetic as the foundational mathematical science, more fundamental than geometry, because it is presupposed by all others but presupposes none.*

— *Introduction to Arithmetic*, Book II, ch. 6. Trans. Martin Luther D’Ooge, 1926. (University of Michigan Studies, vol. 16.)

### Proclus — *Commentary on Euclid’s Elements*

> “He quotes Plato’s opinion to the effect that ‘mathematics, as making use of hypotheses, falls short of the non-hypothetical and perfect science.’ … ‘Let us then not say that Plato excludes mathematics from the sciences, but that he declares it to be secondary to the one supreme science.’ And again, while ‘mathematical science must be considered desirable in itself, though not with reference to the needs of daily life,’ ‘if it is necessary to refer the benefit …’”
>
> *Gloss: Proclus, transmitting the Platonic view, positions mathematics as a propaedeutic to dialectic — valuable but subordinate to the non-hypothetical knowledge of the Forms.*

— *Commentary on Euclid I*, Prologue. Trans. Thomas Taylor, 1792; as excerpted in T. L. Heath, *The Thirteen Books of Euclid’s Elements*, vol. 1, 1908. (Cambridge University Press.)

### Al-Ghazali — *The Confessions of Al-Ghazali*

> “Mathematics comprises the knowledge of calculation, geometry, and cosmography: it has no connection with the religious sciences, and proves nothing for or against religion; it rests on a foundation of proofs which, once known and understood, cannot be refuted. Mathematics tend, however, to produce two bad results. The first is this: Whoever studies this science admires the …”
>
> *Gloss: Al-Ghazali grants mathematics its demonstrative certainty and neutrality toward religion, but warns that its precision can lead students to dismiss revealed knowledge as equally uncertain — a danger he takes seriously enough to include in his critique of the philosophers.*

— *Al-Ghazali’s Confessions*. Trans. Claud Field, 1909. (Project Gutenberg.)

### Immanuel Kant — *Critique of Pure Reason*

> “But to determine à priori an intuition in space (its figure), to divide time into periods, or merely to cognize the quantity of an intuition in space and time, and to determine it by number—all this is an operation of reason by means of the construction of conceptions, and is called mathematical.”
>
> *Gloss: Kant distinguishes mathematical from philosophical cognition: mathematics constructs its concepts in pure intuition (space and time), giving it synthetic a priori status, while philosophy works discursively from concepts alone.*

— *Critique of Pure Reason*, Introduction, §V. Trans. J. M. D. Meiklejohn, 1855. (Project Gutenberg.)

### Immanuel Kant — *Prolegomena to Any Future Metaphysics*

> “Consequently, the basis of mathematics actually are pure intuitions, which make its synthetical and apodeictically valid propositions possible. Hence our transcendental deduction of the notions of space and of time explains at the same time the possibility of pure mathematics. Without some such deduction its truth may be granted, but its existence could by no means be understood …”
>
> *Gloss: Kant argues that the possibility of mathematics as a synthetic a priori science is explained only by his transcendental idealism: space and time are forms of our sensibility, not properties of things in themselves.*

— *Prolegomena to Any Future Metaphysics*, §12. Trans. Paul Carus, 1902. (Project Gutenberg.)

## Cross-cuts

- [[Number]] — mathematics is the science of number and magnitude; the ontology of number is a central sub-question.
- [[Logic]] — the demonstrative structure of mathematics has often been taken as the model for logical reasoning.
- [[Science]] — mathematics is the language of modern physical science; the question of whether it is discovered or imposed shapes scientific realism.
- [[Philosophy]] — the relation between mathematical and philosophical method is a recurring theme from Plato to Kant.
- [[Space]] and [[Time]] — Kant grounds mathematics in the pure intuitions of space (geometry) and time (arithmetic).
- [[Quantity]] — mathematics as the science of quantity; the distinction between discrete and continuous quantity.
- [[Quality]] — the Pythagorean tradition attempted to reduce quality to quantity; the resistance to this reduction is a philosophical fault line.
