# Idea: Ethics

## Working definition

What is the good life, what ought one to do, and how are moral judgments grounded? The Western tradition has treated ethics as a systematic inquiry into right action and character, typically centered on reason, virtue, duty, or utility. Other traditions frame the question through cosmic order, social propriety, or spiritual liberation — see Reframings below.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Meta‑ethics

What is the nature of moral truth? Are ethical judgments objective, relative, or expressions of emotion? The Western canon debates these foundations from Plato’s Forms to Hume’s sentiment to Kant’s categorical imperative.

### Sub-question: Normative ethics

What makes an action right or a character good? Virtue ethics (Aristotle), duty‑based deontology (Kant), consequentialism (Mill), and their predecessors and critics.

### Sub-question: Moral psychology

How do desire, reason, habit, and emotion produce moral action? The interplay of appetite, spiritedness, and reason in the soul; the role of sympathy and moral sentiment.

### Sub-question: Applied ethics

Do the general rules of ethics hold in particular cases of war, wealth, punishment, medicine, and family? The tradition often tests principles here.

## Western canon

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics*

> Book I, Chapter 1 — "Every art, and every science reduced to a teachable form, and in like manner every action and moral choice, aims, it is thought, at some good: for which reason a common and by no means a bad description of the Chief Good is, 'that which all things aim at'."
>
> *Gloss: Aristotle establishes ethics as the inquiry into the final end (eudaimonia) toward which all human action is directed, grounding the discipline in teleology rather than divine command or social convention.*

Translator unnamed in source (Project Gutenberg ed., 2005; likely 19th‑century translation).

### Adam Smith — *Theory of Moral Sentiments*

> Part VII, sec. iv — "In treating of the rules of morality, in this manner, consists the science which is properly called Ethics, a science, which though like criticism, it does not admit of the most accurate precision, is, however, both highly useful and agreeable. It is of all others the most susceptible of the embellishments of eloquence, and by means of them of bestowing, if that be possible, a new importance upon the smallest rules of duty."
>
> *Gloss: Smith defines ethics as a descriptive and persuasive science of moral rules, rooted in natural sentiment and rhetorical elegance — a departure from strict rationalist systems.*

Smith, Adam. *Theory of Moral Sentiments*. 1759.

### Carl Jung — *Psychology of the Unconscious*

> Chapter 2, "The Origin of the Hero‑Myth" — "The history of ethical development within philosophy represents a reflection of the repression of the gross, egoistic, violent and cruel impulses of man and that the struggle against these asocial impulses takes place in the field of ethics, as the struggle against the libidinous impulses does in the domain of metaphysics… The establishment of ethical standards comes about by repression of these impulses."
>
> *Gloss: Jung reinterprets ethics as a psychological process — sublimation and repression of primal instincts — rather than a rational or theological one, placing it alongside metaphysics as a domain of unconscious conflict.*

Jung, Carl. *Psychology of the Unconscious*. Trans. Beatrice M. Hinkle, 1916.

## Reframings

### Hindu/Buddhist tradition — Dharma

In Sanskrit traditions, the question "what ought one to do" is inseparable from dharma — the cosmic order (ṛta) that sustains the world, and the particular duty (svadharma) of one’s station, caste, and life‑stage. Where Western ethics asks for universal principles, dharma is plural, contextual, and tied to the maintenance of cosmic harmony. There is no single "good" separable from one’s role in the whole.

→ See [[Terms/Dharma]]

### Confucian tradition — Yi (義)

The Confucian counterpart to ethics is yi (rightness or appropriateness), which governs conduct not through abstract rules but through the concrete demands of relationship (roles in family, polity, and ritual). Right action is what is fitting given the specific social configuration; the moral life is cultivated through li (ritual propriety) and ren (humaneness) rather than through systematic moral theory.

→ See [[Terms/Yi]] and [[Terms/Ren]]

## Cross-cuts

- [[Justice]] — ethics and justice share the question of what is owed; ethics grounds it in character, justice in distribution and law.
- [[Virtue_and_Vice]] — the cardinal virtues (prudence, temperance, courage, justice) are the immediate objects of ethical cultivation.
- [[Duty]] — deontological ethics focuses on moral obligation as the central concept.
- [[Good_and_Evil]] — the scale on which ethical judgments are made; meta‑ethical debates about the reality of good.
- [[Happiness]] — eudaimonia (flourishing) as the end of ethics in the Aristotelian tradition.
- [[Pleasure_and_Pain]] — hedonist and Epicurean threads within normative ethics.
- [[Law]] — natural law ethics (Thomas Aquinas) bridges ethics and jurisprudence.
- [[Conscience]] — the internal tribunal that is a locus of ethical judgment in Christian and post‑Reformation thought.
