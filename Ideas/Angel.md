# Idea: Angel

## Working definition

What is an angel? The Western tradition frames this as a question about created spiritual beings intermediate between God and humanity — messengers, guardians, executors of divine will, ordered in hierarchies, and capable of both obedience and rebellion. The term derives from the Greek *angelos* ("messenger"), but the concept develops through Jewish, Christian, and Islamic scriptural and theological traditions into a complex cosmology of celestial beings with distinct natures, ranks, and functions.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Nature and materiality of angels

Are angels incorporeal spirits or thin bodies? The canon divides. Hobbes argues for materiality; Augustine and the scholastic tradition assert immateriality.

### Sub-question: Angelic hierarchy and ranks

Orders of angels — cherubim, seraphim, thrones, dominions, etc. — appear in scripture (Pseudo-Dionysius; Milton). The hierarchy reflects degrees of proximity to God.

### Sub-question: Fallen angels and the origin of evil

The rebellion of Satan and his angels, their fall and imprisonment, is a key narrative for understanding evil as a privation (Augustine) or as a permanent opposition (Milton).

### Sub-question: Angels as messengers and mediators

Angels deliver God's will (Gabriel to Mary, the angel in Revelation), interpret visions, and minister to humans (guardian angels). Their primary function is communication between heaven and earth.

## Western canon

### Augustine — *City of God*

> *Book XI, ch. 33 (Dods trans., 1871)* — "Let each one, then, take it as he pleases; for it is so profound a passage, that it may well suggest, for the exercise of the reader's tact, many opinions, and none of them widely departing from the rule of faith. At the same time, let none doubt that the holy angels in their heavenly abodes are, though not, indeed, co-eternal with God, yet secure and certain of eternal and true felicity. To their company the Lord teaches that His little ones belong; and not only says, 'They shall be equal to the angels of God,' but shows, too, what blessed contemplation the angels themselves enjoy, saying, 'Take heed that ye despise not one of these little ones: for I say unto you, that in heaven their angels do always behold the face of my Father which is in heaven.'"
>
> *Gloss: Augustine affirms the createdness of angels (not co-eternal) and their blessed contemplation of God, while grounding the doctrine of guardian angels in Matthew 18:10.*

> *Book XI, ch. 33 (Dods trans., 1871)* — "That certain angels sinned, and were thrust down to the lowest parts of this world, where they are, as it were, incarcerated till their final damnation in the day of judgment, the Apostle Peter very plainly declares, when he says that 'God spared not the angels that sinned.'"
>
> *Gloss: The two communities of angels — the faithful and the fallen — are the prototype of the two cities, and the fall is a historical event with eschatological consequences.*

### John Milton — *Paradise Lost*

> *Book III, lines 648–662 (1667)* — "Th’ Arch-Angel *Uriel*, one of the seav’n / Who in Gods presence, neerest to his Throne / Stand ready at command, and are his Eyes / That run through all the Heav’ns, or down to th’ Earth / Bear his swift errands over moist and dry, / O’re Sea and Land: him *Satan* thus accostes; / ... where all his Sons thy Embassie attend; / And here art likeliest by supream decree / Like honour to obtain, and as his Eye / To visit oft this new Creation round."
>
> *Gloss: Milton gives poetic form to the seven archangels who stand before God as "his Eyes," combining messenger function with cosmic surveillance — a hierarchy that Satan can exploit through disguise.*

> *Book III, lines 613–637 (1667)* — "Within kenn a glorious Angel stand, / The same whom *John* saw also in the Sun: / His back was turnd, but not his brightness hid; / Of beaming sunnie Raies, a golden tiar / Circl’d his Head, nor less his Locks behind / Illustrious on his Shoulders fledge with wings / Lay waving round; on som great charge imploy'd / Hee seemd, or fixt in cogitation deep. / ... And now a stripling Cherube he appeers, / Not of the prime, yet such as in his face / Youth smil’d Celestial, and to every Limb / Sutable grace diffus’d, so well he feignd."
>
> *Gloss: The angel's physical appearance — radiance, wings, golden crown — and Satan's ability to counterfeit it, raise the epistemological problem of discerning true angels from false ones.*

### Thomas Hobbes — *Leviathan*

> *Part III, ch. 34 (1651)* — "By the name of ANGEL, is signified generally, a Messenger; and most often, a Messenger of God: And by a Messenger of God, is signified, anything that makes known his extraordinary Presence; that is to say, the extraordinary manifestation of his power, especially by a Dream, or Vision. ... Concerning the creation of Angels, there is nothing delivered in the Scriptures. That they are Spirits, is often repeated: but by the name of Spirit, is signified both in Scripture, and vulgarly, both amongst Jews, and Gentiles, sometimes thin Bodies; as the Aire, the Wind, the Spirits Vitall, and Animall, of living creatures; and sometimes the Images that rise in the fancy in Dreams, and Visions; which are not reall Substances, but accidents of the brain; yet when God raiseth them supernaturally, to signifie his Will, they are not unproperly termed Gods Messengers, that is to say, his Angels."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes offers a materialist and nominalist reduction: "angel" means only a messenger, and its biblical referents are either thin bodies or supernatural phenomena in the brain. He denies permanent incorporeal substances, citing the absence of scriptural support for their creation.*

> *Part III, ch. 34 (1651)* — "To mention all the places of the Old Testament where the name of Angel is found, would be too long. Therefore to comprehend them all at once, I say, there is no text in that part of the Old Testament, which the Church of England holdeth for Canonicall, from which we can conclude, there is, or hath been created, any permanent thing (understood by the name of Spirit or Angel,) that hath not quantity; and that may not be, by the understanding divided; that is to say, considered by parts; so as one part may bee in one place, and the next part in the next place to it; and, in summe, which is not (taking Body for that, which is some what, or some where) Corporeall."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes attacks the immateriality of angels on scriptural and philosophical grounds: every biblical angel can be interpreted as a corporeal manifestation or a visionary sign.*

### Meister Eckhart — *Sermons*

> *Sermon "Ave" (Pfeiffer/Evans trans., 1924)* — "No angel can do anything to help, nothing but work of service. 'Ave.' That means, free from woe. Freedom from creature means freedom from sorrow, freedom from hell, and those who possess and like creatures least have the least sorrow. ... The smallest work of grace is of more exalted nature than any of the angels. St Augustine says, any act of grace that is wrought by God, the conversion of a sinner for example, and making him into a good man, is a thing of greater moment than God’s creating a new world."
>
> *Gloss: Eckhart subordinates angels to grace: their function is service, and a single act of grace surpasses all angelic nature. This reverses the usual hierarchy — grace elevates humanity above the angelic in moral worth.*

> *Sermon on angelic nature (Pfeiffer/Evans trans., 1924)* — "And the more so since an angel has his own specific nature and is a natural descent from God. Yet by reason of nearness to God each angel possesses all that is possible to his natural grade of perfection, and hence it comes about that to every grade of angelic nature there belongs one angel. So many grades so many angels, neither more nor less. And here we note the marvellous adornment of the angels and the perfection of virtues which is theirs owing to their natural proximity to God."
>
> *Gloss: Eckhart presents a graded angelic hierarchy where each grade is instantiated by a single angel, mirroring the divine perfection in a way that human nature cannot.*

### King James Bible — *Revelation*

> *Revelation 8:4–7 (KJV, 1611)* — "And the smoke of the incense, which came with the prayers of the saints, ascended up before God out of the angel's hand. And the angel took the censer, and filled it with fire of the altar, and cast it into the earth: and there were voices, and thunderings, and lightnings, and an earthquake. And the seven angels which had the seven trumpets prepared themselves to sound. The first angel sounded, and there followed hail and fire mingled with blood, and they were cast upon the earth: and the third part of trees was burnt up, and all green grass was burnt up."
>
> *Gloss: Angels in Revelation are agents of cosmic judgment, mediating prayer and unleashing plagues. Their role is liturgical and eschatological, not merely communicative.*

- [[Prophecy]] — angels often appear as messengers in prophetic visions (Daniel, Revelation).
- [[Eternity]] — Augustine stresses that angels, though not co-eternal with God, enjoy eternal felicity.
- [[Good_and_Evil]] — the fall of the angels is the origin of demonic evil; angelic obedience is the model of goodness.
- [[Body]] — the materiality/immateriality debate about angels is a key axis in Hobbes's critique.
