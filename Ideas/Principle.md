# Idea: Principle

## Working definition

A foundational proposition, rule, or starting point that governs reasoning, action, or being. The Western tradition asks: Are principles discovered or constructed? Are they necessary or contingent? Do they belong to logic, epistemology, ethics, or metaphysics? The question is complicated by the term’s ambiguity — it can mean a general proposition used as a premise, an a priori axiom, a moral maxim, or a metaphysical ground. Other traditions reframe the question through concepts like *archē*, *logos*, and *li*; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Logical principles — contradiction and sufficient reason

What are the ultimate formal constraints on thought and reality? Leibniz distinguishes two great principles that structure all reasoning.

### Sub-question: Epistemological principles — a priori, intuitive, empirical

How do we come to know principles? Are they given in pure intuition, derived from experience, or grasped by a special mental faculty?

### Sub-question: Moral principles — objective and subjective

What is the ground of moral obligation? Is the moral law a principle of reason or a subjective maxim?

### Sub-question: Metaphysical principles — first causes and grounds

What are the first principles of being? This sub-question is less represented in the supplied corpus but is implicit in Leibniz’s treatment of sufficient reason.

## Western canon

### Aristotle — *Metaphysics*

> *[Location uncertain; passage discusses practical syllogism and moral principles]* — "The major premisses may be stored up in the mind as rules of action, and this is what is commonly meant by having principles good or bad."
>
> *Gloss: Principles are here understood as stored rules of action, acquired through habituation rather than intellectual training. The passage ties principle to moral character, not abstract cognition.*
>
> Trans. unknown, pre-1900. (Attribution to *Metaphysics* is questionable; the content is closer to *Nicomachean Ethics*.)

### David Hume — *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*

> *Section IV, Part I* — "What that principle is may well be worth the pains of enquiry. … He would not, at first, by any reasoning, be able to reach the idea of cause and effect; since the particular powers, by which all natural operations are performed, never appear to the senses; nor is it reasonable to conclude, merely because one event, in one instance, precedes another, that therefore the one is the cause, the other the effect."
>
> *Gloss: Hume treats the principle of causation as an empirical generalization, not an a priori truth. The principle itself is the object of enquiry, not its foundation.*
>
> Hume, 1748/1777.

### Immanuel Kant — *Critique of Pure Reason*

> *Introduction, Section I* — "The term principle is ambiguous, and commonly signifies merely a cognition that may be employed as a principle, although it is not in itself, and as regards its proper origin, entitled to the distinction. … Mathematical axioms (for example, there can be only one straight line between two points) are general à priori cognitions, and are therefore rightly denominated principles, relatively to the cases which can be subsumed under them. But I cannot for this reason say that I cognize this property of a straight line from principles—I cognize it only in pure intuition."
>
> *Gloss: Kant distinguishes genuine a priori principles (mathematical axioms) from merely empirical generalizations that can serve as premises. A principle, properly speaking, is known from concepts, not from intuition.*
>
> Trans. J. M. D. Meiklejohn, 1855.

### Immanuel Kant — *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals*

> *Section II* — "In fact the objective principle of all practical legislation lies (according to the first principle) in the rule and its form of universality which makes it capable of being a law (say, e. g., a law of nature); but the subjective principle is in the end; … Hence follows the third practical principle of the will, which is the ultimate condition of its harmony with universal practical reason, viz.: the idea of the will of every rational being as a universally legislative will."
>
> *Gloss: Kant distinguishes objective principles (the categorical imperative as universal law) from subjective principles (maxims based on ends). The moral principle is not a rule imposed from outside but one the rational will gives to itself.*
>
> Trans. Thomas Kingsmill Abbott, 1873.

### Blaise Pascal — *Pensées*

> *Section I, 1* — "But in the intuitive mind the principles are found in common use, and are before the eyes of everybody. One has only to look, and no effort is necessary; it is only a question of good eyesight, but it must be good, for the principles are so subtle and so numerous, that it is almost impossible but that some escape notice. … The reason, therefore, that some intuitive minds are not mathematical is that they cannot at all turn their attention to the principles of mathematics. But the reason that mathematicians are not intuitive is that they do not see what is before them, and that, accustomed to the exact and plain principles of mathematics, and not reasoning till they have well inspected and arranged their principles…"
>
> *Gloss: Pascal contrasts two modes of knowing principles: the intuitive mind grasps principles directly from common experience; the mathematical mind requires explicit, exact principles. Both are valid, but each has its blind spot.*
>
> Trans. W. F. Trotter, 1910.

### Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz — *The Monadology and Other Philosophical Writings*

> *Section on the two great principles* — "There are certain eternal and necessary truths which are independent of the will of God, existing in His understanding alone, and these are subject to the principle of contradiction; but the reality of all individual substances and their changes is dependent on the will as well as the understanding of God, and they are all subject to the principle of sufficient reason. … the necessity of the principle of contradiction differs in kind from that of the principle of sufficient reason, the former being an absolute, compelling, or metaphysical necessity, whose opposite is impossible, involving self-contradiction, while the latter is a relative, inclining, or moral necessity, whose opposite is not impossible, but incompossible."
>
> *Gloss: Leibniz establishes two foundational logical principles that govern all truth and reality: the principle of contradiction (necessary truths) and the principle of sufficient reason (contingent truths). They differ in kind of necessity, not just degree.*
>
> Trans. Robert Latta, 1898.

## Reframings

### Chinese (Neo-Confucian) — Li (理)

The Chinese concept *li* (principle, pattern) does not separate logical, moral, and metaphysical principles into distinct domains. In Neo-Confucianism, *li* is the rational pattern inherent in all things, both the structure of reality and the moral order. It is not a proposition or a rule but an immanent coherence. The Western question "are principles discovered or constructed?" does not arise in the same form, because *li* is neither — it is the way things are, accessible through moral cultivation and investigation of things.

→ See [[Terms/Li]]

### Greek — Logos

*Logos* in Heraclitus and the Stoics functions as a principle of cosmic order and rational structure, akin to Leibniz's principle of sufficient reason but embedded in a living, fiery intelligence. In the Johannine tradition, *Logos* becomes the divine principle through which all things were made. The Western Idea of Principle, especially in its logical and metaphysical forms, overlaps significantly with *Logos*, but *Logos* carries connotations of speech, reason, and proportion that the English "principle" lacks.

→ See [[Terms/Logos]]

### Chinese (Daoist) — Tao

The *Tao* (Way) is the ultimate principle of the universe in Daoism, but it is explicitly not a principle in the Western sense — it cannot be named, defined, or used as a premise in a syllogism. The *Tao Te Ching* opens by denying that the *Tao* can be spoken of as a principle. This reframes the question: what if the foundational "principle" is precisely what escapes principled formulation?

→ See [[Terms/Tao]]
