# Idea: Sexuality

## Working definition

Sexuality as a fundamental human drive encompassing biological reproduction, erotic desire, pleasure, and identity. The Western tradition has debated its nature—from classical moral philosophy treating lust as an irrational passion, through Augustinian identification with original sin, to the medical and psychological frameworks of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries that sought to classify, explain, and treat its variations. The question is not merely what sexuality *is*, but how it relates to the self, society, and the sacred.

## Sub-questions

### Nature of Sexual Desire

Is sexual desire a natural appetite, a moral failing, or a fundamental psychic force? The canon moves from Aristotle's view of lust as an irrational feeling shared with animals to Freud's concept of libido as the energy of all love-instincts.

### Perversion and Normality

What counts as normal sexuality, and how are deviations to be understood? Krafft-Ebing's clinical taxonomy and Freud's theory of infantile polymorphous perversity both challenge simple boundaries.

### Sexual Development

How does sexuality emerge and transform across the lifespan? Freud's account of infantile sexuality and the transformations of puberty provides a developmental model.

### Sexuality and Society

How do social norms, laws, and institutions shape sexual expression? The tension between individual desire and social regulation runs through Ovid's erotic advice, Augustine's condemnation, and the modern psychiatric cataloguing of "perversions."

## Western canon

### Ovid — *Art of Love*

> *Book I* — "Now is the time come for some conversation: fly afar hence, coy bashfulness, let Chance and Venus befriend the daring. Let your eloquence not be subject to any laws of mine; only make a beginning, of your own accord you will prove fluent. You must act the lover, and wounds must be feigned in your words. Hence let confidence be sought by you, by means of any contrivances whatever. And 'tis no hard matter to be believed; each woman seems to herself worthy to be loved. Though she be ugly in the extreme, to no one are her own looks displeasing. Yet often, he that pretends to love, begins in reality: frequently he becomes that which he at first personated."
>
> *Gloss: Ovid treats sexual pursuit as a game of artifice and self-persuasion, where performance can become genuine desire—a pre-modern recognition of the plasticity of erotic feeling.*

Trans. H. T. Riley, 1885.

### Sigmund Freud — *Three Essays on the Theory of Sexuality*

> *The Sexual Life of Man* — "One might think we could take for granted what we are to understand by the term 'sexual.' Of course, the sexual is the indecent, which we must not talk about. … Perhaps the only accurate definition would be everything that is connected with the function of reproduction. But if you think of the function of reproduction as the nucleus of sexuality you are in danger of excluding a number of things that do not aim at reproduction but are certainly sexual, such as onanism or even kissing. … We may suspect that in the development of the concept 'sexual' something occurred which resulted in a false disguise."
>
> *Gloss: Freud refuses a narrow reproductive definition, opening sexuality to include all pleasure-seeking activities—a radical expansion that underpins his theory of infantile sexuality and perversion.*

Trans. A. A. Brill, 1910.

### Sigmund Freud — *Psychopathology of Everyday Life*

> *Deviation in Reference to the Sexual Aim* — "The union of the genitals in the characteristic act of copulation is taken as the normal sexual aim. … Yet even in the most normal sexual process those additions are distinguishable, the development of which leads to the aberrations described as *perversions*. Thus certain intermediary relations to the sexual object connected with copulation, such as touching and looking, are recognized as preliminary to the sexual aim. These activities are on the one hand themselves connected with pleasure and on the other hand they enhance the excitement which persists until the definite sexual aim is reached."
>
> *Gloss: Freud argues that perversions are not alien to normal sexuality but exaggerations of components present in every sexual act—blurring the line between health and pathology.*

Trans. A. A. Brill, 1914.

### Richard von Krafft-Ebing — *Psychopathia Sexualis*

> *Homo-Sexual Feeling in Both Sexes* — "With tainted individuals, the matter is quite different. The latent perverse sexuality is developed under the influence of neurasthenia induced by masturbation, abstinence, or otherwise. Gradually, in contact with persons of the same sex, sexual excitation by them is induced. Related ideas are coloured with lustful feelings, and awaken corresponding desires. This decidedly degenerate reaction is the beginning of a process of physical and mental transformation … which is one of the most interesting psychological phenomena that have been observed."
>
> *Gloss: Krafft-Ebing frames same-sex desire as a degenerative condition rooted in heredity and nervous exhaustion—the medical model that Freud would later challenge by universalizing perverse components.*

Trans. C. G. Chaddock, 1894.

### Carl Jung — *Analytical Psychology*

> *The Sexual Theory* — "I postulate a hypothetical fundamental striving which I designate libido. … In the classical use of the word libido never had an exclusively sexual connotation as it has in medicine. The word interest, as Claparède once suggested to me, could be used in this special sense, if this expression had to-day a less extensive application. Bergson's concept, *élan vital*, would also serve if this expression were less biological and more psychological. Libido is intended to be an energising expression for *psychological values*."
>
> *Gloss: Jung explicitly broadens libido beyond Freud's sexual meaning, turning it into a general psychic energy—a move that reframes sexuality as one expression of a deeper vital force.*

Trans. C. E. Long, 1916.

### Alfred Adler — *The Practice and Theory of Individual Psychology*

> *The Problem of Sexuality* — "Anyone who comes into touch with psychoanalytic literature will note at once that the word sexuality there has an implication other than the current one. … That the act in which the male and female germ-cells meet and mingle is the nucleus of the concept is obvious. And it is also obvious that all things concerned with the attraction of individuals to one another belong to the term in so far as this attraction makes for fusion; consequently also all things concerned with kindred feelings and with the awakening of psychic life to productive creation."
>
> *Gloss: Adler acknowledges the breadth of the Freudian concept but insists on the social and creative dimensions of sexuality, linking it to the drive for community and self-assertion.*

Trans. W. Béran Wolfe, 1924.

## Reframings

### Sufi tradition — Ishq (Divine Love)

The Sufi tradition reframes sexuality not as a biological or psychological drive but as a metaphor for the soul's longing for God. Erotic love between human beings is a finite echo of the infinite love (*ishq*) that draws the created back to the Creator. The English term "sexuality" captures only the physical and emotional dimensions, missing the theological ascent. In Jami's *Yusuf and Zulaikha*, the beloved's beauty is a theophany: "Then Beauty shone, from stranger glances free, / Seen of herself, with naught beside to see" (trans. E. H. Griffith, 1882). This is an *equivalent match*—the passage speaks of Beauty, not sexuality directly, but within the Sufi frame, erotic beauty is a sign of the divine.

### Hindu tradition — Kama (Desire)

The Hindu tradition treats desire (*kama*) as one of the four legitimate aims of life (*purusharthas*), alongside duty, wealth, and liberation. Unlike the Western tendency to oppose sexuality to spirituality, *kama* is a domain of human flourishing that can be cultivated through the *Kama Sutra* and related texts. The English "sexuality" overemphasizes the biological and pathological, whereas *kama* includes aesthetic, emotional, and relational dimensions. ## Cross-cuts

- [[Eros]] — overlaps with sexuality but emphasizes the philosophical and transcendent dimensions of desire, especially in Plato and the Sufi tradition.
- [[Love]] — sexuality is one form of love; the canon distinguishes *eros*, *philia*, *agape*, and *storge*.
- [[Desire]] — sexuality is a subset of desire; the broader concept includes ambition, curiosity, and appetite.
- [[Body]] — sexuality is embodied; debates about the body's role in pleasure and reproduction intersect with sexuality.
- [[Pleasure_and_Pain]] — sexual pleasure is a central case in hedonistic and ascetic traditions.
- [[Ethics]] — sexual acts are subject to moral evaluation; virtue ethics, natural law, and consequentialism all weigh in.
- [[Medicine]] — the medicalization of sexuality from Krafft-Ebing to Freud transforms moral categories into clinical diagnoses.

Their positions on lust (Aristotle as irrational passion, Augustine as original sin) are relevant but could not be cited per the spec's bibliographic discipline.

- **Adler's passage** bridges the psychoanalytic and social-psychological approaches, emphasizing the creative and communal aspects of sexuality.
