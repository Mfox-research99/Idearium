# Idea: Sacred_and_Profane

## Working definition

What distinguishes the set-apart from the ordinary, the holy from the common, the consecrated from the everyday. The Western tradition frames this primarily as a question of demarcation — by divine command, by ritual separation, by degrees of sanctity, or by the nature of the object or person set apart. Other traditions reframe it through purity and pollution, through the hierarchy of inner and outer sanctuaries, or through the distinction between the conditioned and the unconditioned; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Degrees of Holiness

How the sacred admits of gradation — from the wholly other to the merely set apart, from the Holy of Holies to the consecrated vessel.

### Sub-question: Sacred as Propriety

How the sacred is understood as property — something owned by God, appropriated by covenant, or reserved for divine use — and the profane as that which is common or unclaimed.

### Sub-question: Pollution and Contact

How contact with the profane (or the sacred) transmits or drains the quality, and how this is managed through taboo, ritual, and separation.

## Western canon

### Hobbes — *Leviathan*

> "Of Holinesse there be degrees: for of those things that are set apart for the service of God, there may bee some set apart again, for a neerer and more especial service. The whole Nation of the Israelites were a people Holy to God; yet the tribe of Levi was amongst the Israelites a Holy tribe; and amongst the Levites, the Priests were yet more Holy; and amongst the Priests, the High Priest was the most Holy. So the Land of Judea was the Holy Land; but the Holy City wherein God was to be worshipped, was more Holy; and again, the Temples more Holy than the City; and the Sanctum Sanctorum more Holy than the rest of the Temple."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes defines holiness as a matter of degree — a graduated separation from common use, with the Holy of Holies at the apex and the whole nation of Israel as the base.*

> "And the word Profane, is usually taken in the Scripture for the same with Common; and consequently their contraries, Holy, and Proper, in the Kingdome of God must be the same also. But figuratively, those men also are called Holy, that led such godly lives, as if they had forsaken all worldly designes, and wholly devoted, and given themselves to God. In the proper sense, that which is made Holy by Gods appropriating or separating it to his own use, is said to be Sanctified."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes grounds the sacred/profane distinction in property and covenant: the holy is what God has appropriated, the profane is what remains common.*

### Frazer — *The Golden Bough*

> "Apparently holiness, magical virtue, taboo, or whatever we may call that mysterious quality which is supposed to pervade sacred or tabooed persons, is conceived by the primitive philosopher as a physical substance or fluid, with which the sacred man is charged just as a Leyden jar is charged with electricity; and exactly as the electricity in the jar can be discharged by contact with a good conductor, so the holiness or magical virtue in the man can be discharged and drained away by contact with the earth, which on this theory is the best conductor of the mysterious fluid."
>
> *Gloss: Frazer treats the sacred as a quasi-physical force that can be transmitted, drained, or grounded — a materialist reframing of taboo as primitive electrical theory.*

### Augustine — *City of God*

> "Shall eternal life be hoped for from these, by which this short and temporal life is polluted? Does the society of wicked men pollute our life if they insinuate themselves into our affections, and win our assent? and does not the society of demons pollute the life, who are worshipped with their own crimes?--if with true crimes, how wicked the demons! if with false, how wicked the worship!"
>
> *Gloss: Augustine inverts the sacred/profane distinction of pagan cults: what they call sacred (worship of demons) is actually pollution, and the true sacred is found in the City of God, not in the temples.*

### Jami — *Salaman and Absal* (FitzGerald translation)

> "Not among the Jews alone was the holy of holies kept veiled, to temper the divine radiance to man's benighted understanding. Nor is the chancel-rail of Christianity the sole survivor of the more exclusive barriers of olden times, even in the Western world. In the Far East, where difficulty of access is deemed indispensable to dignity, the material approaches are still manifold and mysterious."
>
> *Gloss: Jami (via FitzGerald) identifies a universal architectural principle: the sacred is guarded by degrees of access, from outer court to inner sanctuary, across all traditions.*

## Reframings

### Hindu — *Brahman / Atman / Maya*

The Western sacred/profane distinction assumes a dualism: some things are set apart, others are common. The Hindu tradition, particularly in Advaita Vedanta, reframes this entirely. The distinction between sacred and profane is itself a product of *maya* — the phenomenal world of differentiation. What is ultimately real (*Brahman*) is neither sacred nor profane in the Western sense; it is the undifferentiated ground of all. The "profane" world of multiplicity is not opposed to the sacred but is its illusory appearance. Ritual purity (*shuddhi*) and impurity (*ashaucha*) operate at the level of *dharma* and social practice, not at the level of ultimate reality.

→ See [[Terms/Brahman]], [[Terms/Atman]], [[Terms/Maya]]

### Buddhist — *Nirvana / Samsara*

Buddhism collapses the sacred/profane binary in a different direction: *samsara* (the cycle of birth and death, the "profane" world) and *nirvana* (the unconditioned, the "sacred") are not two separate realms. The Mahayana tradition explicitly teaches that *samsara* is *nirvana* — the distinction is a function of ignorance (*avidya*), not of ontology. What the West calls "sacred" (set apart, consecrated) Buddhism treats as a skillful means (*upaya*): monastic discipline, relics, and stupas are useful but not ultimately real separations. The truly sacred is the realization of emptiness (*sunyata*), which pervades all phenomena equally.

→ See [[Terms/Nirvana]], [[Terms/Samsara]], [[Terms/Sunyata]], [[Terms/Upaya]]

### Daoist — *Tao / Wu Wei*

The Daoist tradition does not recognize a sharp sacred/profane boundary. The *Tao* (the Way) is not set apart from the world; it is the spontaneous order of all things. What the West calls "profane" — the ordinary, the everyday, the natural — is precisely where the *Tao* is most manifest. The sage does not seek a separate sacred realm but returns to the uncarved block (*pu*), the simplicity before distinction. Ritual separation (*li* in the Confucian sense) is seen as a fall from the *Tao*, not an approach to it.

→ See [[Terms/Tao]], [[Terms/Wu_Wei]]

## Cross-cuts

## Status notes

**v1 draft.** The Western canon section is thin — only four authors, and Frazer is the only modern voice. The corpus returned no passages from Durkheim's *Elementary Forms of Religious Life* (the foundational sociological treatment of sacred/profane), no Otto (*The Idea of the Holy*), no Eliade (*The Sacred and the Profane*). These are major gaps. The Frazer passage is useful as a materialist counterpoint but is not the strongest statement of the distinction. The Augustine passage is more about pollution than about the sacred/profane boundary per se. Hobbes is the strongest — his property/covenant theory of holiness is genuinely philosophically committed.

The Jami passage is an equivalent match (FitzGerald's English, not Jami's Persian) and is included for its architectural reframing of degrees of access. It is the only non-Western voice in the corpus results; the Reframings for Hindu, Buddhist, and Daoist traditions are written from general knowledge, not from corpus passages. They should be updated when corpus material becomes available.

**Queries tried and returned nothing useful:** "holy" (too diffuse), "taboo" (Frazer only), "consecration" (no committed passages), "sacred space" (no results). The Gibbon passages (4, 6, 8, 10) are incidental uses of the phrase "sacred and profane" as a rhetorical flourish, not substantive engagements with the concept — excluded.

**Missing:** Durkheim's sacred/profane as social categories; Otto's *mysterium tremendum et fascinans*; Eliade's hierophany and the distinction between sacred space/time and profane space/time; the Hebrew *kadosh* / *chol* distinction; the Greek *hieros* / *hosios* / *bebelos* vocabulary; the Latin *sacer* / *profanus* / *sanctus* distinctions.
