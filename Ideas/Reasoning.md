# Idea: Reasoning

## Working definition

What it means to move from known premises to new conclusions, and what kinds of inference are valid. The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about the faculty of reason, its scope, its limits, and its relation to faith, sense, and imagination. Other traditions reframe it through the epistemology of valid knowledge sources (pramāṇa) or through the priority of direct insight over discursive thought; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Types of reasoning

Demonstrative (relations of ideas) vs. moral (matter of fact); deductive vs. inductive; the role of probability.

### Sub-question: Reason and faith

Whether reason is the ground of faith, or faith transcends reason, or the two are distinct domains.

### Sub-question: The limits of reason

What reasoning cannot reach: the existence of God, the resurrection, the course of nature, the heart’s knowledge.

## Western canon

### Blaise Pascal — *Pensées*

> *277* — "The heart has its reasons, which reason does not know. We feel it in a thousand things. I say that the heart naturally loves the Universal Being, and also itself naturally, according as it gives itself to them; and it hardens itself against one or the other at its will. You have rejected the one, and kept the other. Is it by reason that you love yourself?"
>
> *Gloss: Pascal posits a faculty of the heart that grasps truths (God, love) inaccessible to discursive reasoning, establishing a boundary on reason’s domain.*

> *278* — "It is the heart which experiences God, and not the reason. This, then, is faith: God felt by the heart, not by the reason."
>
> *Gloss: Faith is not a conclusion of reasoning but an immediate experience of the heart, setting a limit to what reasoning can achieve in religion.*

### John Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*

> *Book IV, Chapter 17, §2* — "If general knowledge, as has been shown, consists in a perception of the agreement or disagreement of our own ideas, and the knowledge of the existence of all things without us (except only of a God, whose existence every man may certainly know and demonstrate to himself from his own existence), be had only by our senses, what room is there for the exercise of any other faculty, but OUTWARD SENSE and INWARD PERCEPTION? What need is there of REASON? Very much: both for the enlargement of our knowledge, and regulating our assent. For it hath to do both in knowledge and opinion, and is necessary and assisting to all our other intellectual faculties, and indeed contains two of them, viz. SAGACITY and ILLATION. By the one, it finds out; and by the other, it so orders the intermediate ideas as to discover what connexion there is in each link of the chain, whereby the extremes are held together; and thereby, as it were, to draw into view the truth sought for, which is that which we call ILLATION or INFERENCE, and consists in nothing but the perception of the connexion there is between the ideas, in each step of the deduction."
>
> *Gloss: Locke defines reasoning as the faculty that discovers intermediate ideas (sagacity) and perceives the connections between them (illation), making inference the core act of reason.*

> *Book IV, Chapter 17, §23* — "By what has been before said of reason, we may be able to make some guess at the distinction of things, into those that are according to, above, and contrary to reason. 1. ACCORDING TO REASON are such propositions whose truth we can discover by examining and tracing those ideas we have from sensation and reflection; and by natural deduction find to be true or probable. 2. ABOVE REASON are such propositions whose truth or probability we cannot by reason derive from those principles. 3. CONTRARY TO REASON are such propositions as are inconsistent with or irreconcilable to our clear and distinct ideas. Thus the existence of one God is according to reason; the existence of more than one God, contrary to reason; the resurrection of the dead, above reason."
>
> *Gloss: Locke maps the boundary of reason by classifying propositions into three categories, reserving a space for truths that reason cannot reach but that may be accepted on faith.*

### David Hume — *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*

> *Section IV, Part I* — "All reasonings may be divided into two kinds, namely, demonstrative reasoning, or that concerning relations of ideas, and moral reasoning, or that concerning matter of fact and existence. That there are no demonstrative arguments in the case seems evident; since it implies no contradiction that the course of nature may change, and that an object, seemingly like those which we have experienced, may be attended with different or contrary effects. May I not clearly and distinctly conceive that a body, falling from the clouds, and which, in all other respects, resembles snow, has yet the taste of salt or feeling of fire? Is there any more intelligible proposition than to affirm, that all the trees will flourish in December and January, and decay in May and June? Now whatever is intelligible, and can be distinctly conceived, implies no contradiction, and can never be proved false by any demonstrative argument or abstract reasoning *à priori*."
>
> *Gloss: Hume divides reasoning into two kinds — demonstrative (relations of ideas) and moral (matters of fact) — and argues that causal inferences about the future cannot be demonstrative because their negation is always conceivable.*

## Reframings

### Hindu (Vedānta) — Inference (anumāna) and the defense of reasoning

The Indian tradition, particularly the Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, engages reasoning (tarka) as a valid means of knowledge (pramāṇa) while also acknowledging its limits. The passage below defends reasoning against the charge of groundlessness by pointing out that the skeptic's own position relies on reasoning, and that without inference practical life would cease. This reframing treats reasoning not as a faculty opposed to faith but as a necessary tool for interpreting scripture and for navigating empirical life — though it remains subordinate to direct scriptural testimony (śruti) in ultimate matters.

> *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary, Introduction* — "But (our adversary may here be supposed to say), we will fashion our reasoning otherwise, i.e. in such a manner as not to lay it open to the charge of having no proper foundation. You cannot, after all, maintain that no reasoning whatever is well-founded; for you yourself can found your assertion that reasoning has no foundation on reasoning only; your assumption being that because some arguments are seen to be devoid of foundation other arguments as belonging to the same class are likewise devoid of foundation. Moreover, if all reasoning were unfounded, the whole course of practical human life would have to come to an end. For we see that men act, with a view to obtaining pleasure and avoiding pain in the future time, on the assumption that the past, the present, and the future are uniform."
>
> *Gloss: The Vedānta tradition defends inference as pragmatically indispensable and self-grounding, anticipating Hume's problem of induction while rejecting radical skepticism.*

## Cross-cuts

- [[Logic]] — reasoning is the psychological process; logic is the formal structure that governs valid inference.
- [[Knowledge]] — reasoning is the primary means of extending knowledge beyond immediate sense perception.
- [[Truth]] — reasoning aims at truth, but its conclusions are only as reliable as its premises and method.
- [[Faith]] — the boundary between what reason can demonstrate and what must be accepted on authority or revelation.
- [[Science]] — modern empirical science depends on moral (inductive) reasoning as defined by Hume.
- [[Philosophy]] — philosophy is itself the exercise of reasoning on first principles.

## Terms in play on this page

- [[Terms/Logos]] — the Greek term for reason as both human faculty and cosmic principle, overlapping with but broader than the English "reasoning."
