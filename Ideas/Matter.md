# Idea: Matter

## Working definition

What is the underlying stuff of physical reality, and how does it relate to form, mind, and spirit? The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about substance — whether matter is passive or active, whether it can think, and whether it is distinct from body. The Aristotelian tradition treats matter as pure potentiality, the principle of individuation; early modern philosophy redefines it as inert extended substance; materialist traditions affirm it as the sole reality. Other traditions reframe the question through emptiness or cosmic nature; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Matter and Body — Locke's distinction

Locke argues that "matter" and "body" are not synonymous: matter is an incomplete conception (substance and solidity without extension and figure), while body includes extension and figure. This clarifies a conceptual confusion that persists in ordinary language.

### Matter and Mind — the cogitative matter debate

Locke argues that matter, even with motion, cannot produce thought. The impossibility of cogitative matter is a central argument for the necessity of an eternal spiritual being.

### Matter and Spirit — the materialist defense

Bakunin rejects the idealist caricature of matter as "vile" and inert, instead defining matter as eternally mobile, active, productive, and capable of generating intelligence.

### Matter as Receptacle — Hermetic tradition

In the Hermetica, matter is the eternal receptacle of forms, originally without order, given life and order by God through spirit.

## Western canon

### John Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*

> *Book III, Chapter 10* — "How many intricate disputes have there been about MATTER, as if there were some such thing really in nature, distinct from BODY; as it is evident the word matter stands for an idea distinct from the idea of body? For if the ideas these two terms stood for were precisely the same, they might indifferently in all places be put for one another. But we see that though it be proper to say, There is one matter of all bodies, one cannot say, There is one body of all matters: we familiarly say one body is bigger than another; but it sounds harsh (and I think is never used) to say one matter is bigger than another. Whence comes this, then? Viz. from hence: that, though matter and body be not really distinct, but wherever there is the one there is the other; yet matter and body stand for two different conceptions, whereof the one is incomplete, and but a part of the other. For body stands for a solid extended figured substance, whereof matter is but a partial and more confused conception; it seeming to me to be used for the substance and solidity of body, without taking in its extension and figure."
>
> *Gloss: Locke distinguishes matter from body as a conceptual distinction: matter is the substratum of solidity alone, while body includes extension and figure — a distinction that explains why we speak of "one matter of all bodies" but not "one body of all matters."*

> *Book IV, Chapter 10* — "Is it possible to conceive it can add motion to itself, being purely matter, or produce anything? Matter, then, by its own strength, cannot produce in itself so much as motion: the motion it has must also be from eternity, or else be produced, and added to matter by some other being more powerful than matter; matter, as is evident, having not power to produce motion in itself. But let us suppose motion eternal too: yet matter, INCOGITATIVE matter and motion, whatever changes it might produce of figure and bulk, could never produce thought: knowledge will still be as far beyond the power of motion and matter to produce, as matter is beyond the power of nothing or nonentity to produce."
>
> *Gloss: Locke argues that matter, even with eternal motion, cannot generate thought — a key premise in his argument for the necessity of an eternal spiritual being.*

### Michael Bakunin — *God and the State*

> *Chapter 1* — "Idealists of all schools, aristocrats and *bourgeois*, theologians and metaphysicians, politicians and moralists, religionists, philosophers, or poets, not forgetting the liberal economists—unbounded worshippers of the ideal, as we know—are much offended when told that man, with his magnificent intelligence, his sublime ideas, and his boundless aspirations, is, like all else existing in the world, nothing but matter, only a product of *vile matter*. We may answer that the matter of which materialists speak, matter spontaneously and eternally mobile, active, productive, matter chemically or organically determined and manifested by the properties or forces, mechanical, physical, animal, and intelligent, which necessarily belong to it—that this matter has nothing in common with the *vile matter* of the idealists. The latter, a product of their false abstraction, is indeed a stupid, inanimate, immobile thing, incapable of giving birth to the smallest product, a *caput mortuum*, an *ugly* fancy in contrast to the..."
>
> *Gloss: Bakunin rejects the idealist caricature of matter as inert and vile, defining it instead as spontaneously active, productive, and capable of generating intelligence — a materialist counter to the mind-matter dualism of Locke and the spiritualist tradition.*
>
> *Note: Translator not specified in corpus; likely Benjamin R. Tucker (1883).*

### Hermes Trismegistus — *Hermetica*

> *Fragment from Scott translation, Vol. 1 (1924)* — "Matter is the recipient of all forms; and the changes and unbroken successions of the forms (are wrought by means of spirit). The process is directed by God, who distributes (life) to all things in the universe, giving to each one of them as much as it needs. Into all things he infuses spirit, assigning it to each in larger measure, in proportion as the thing stands higher in the scale of being."
>
> *Gloss: In the Hermetic tradition, matter is the passive receptacle of forms, animated by spirit infused by God — a view that preserves matter as eternal but dependent on divine agency for order and life.*
>
> *Trans. Walter Scott, 1924.*

> *From Chambers translation (1882)* — "Matter or Material, eternal, originally without order; Material World, beautiful, not good; Matter, how dealt with by God; from Matter differs; qualities generated from, ibid.; Matter is from four things, Earth, Air, Fire, Water, ibid.; made immortal by Eternity; order of, regulated by Providence; is One; full of Life from God; not energized by Him is mere mass; Energy of Materiality, ibid.; generated by God; receptacle of Generation and Ideas; is variable, dissoluble, and eternal; how moved and warmed at Creation."
>
> *Gloss: This summary of Hermetic doctrines presents matter as eternal yet dependent on God for life and order, a receptacle of generation and ideas — a synthesis of Platonic and Stoic elements.*
>
> *Trans. John David Chambers, 1882.*

## Reframings

### Buddhist — Emptiness (Śūnyatā)

The Buddhist tradition does not posit matter as an enduring substance. Instead, all phenomena, including what the West calls "matter," are empty of inherent existence — dependently arisen, impermanent, and without a fixed essence. This reframes the question from "what is matter made of?" to "how do material phenomena arise and cease in dependence on causes and conditions?"

### Hindu — Prakṛti (not yet a Term page)

In Sāṃkhya and Vedānta, the material principle is *prakṛti* — primordial nature, composed of three guṇas (qualities), distinct from *puruṣa* (consciousness). Matter is not inert but dynamic, evolving through the interplay of sattva, rajas, and tamas. This offers a non-dualistic alternative to the Western matter-spirit divide.
