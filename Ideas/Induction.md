# Idea: Induction

## Working definition

Reasoning from particular instances to general principles. The Western tradition frames induction primarily as a method of scientific inference — the movement from observed facts to universal axioms — and distinguishes it sharply from deduction. Aristotle treats induction as the process by which the mind grasps universals through sense perception; Bacon revolutionizes it as a systematic method of exclusion and elimination. The question is whether induction can yield certain knowledge or only probable conclusions, and what role the mind's intuitive faculty (nous) plays in bridging the gap between finite particulars and universal statements.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Aristotelian induction (epagoge)

Induction as the psychological and logical process by which universals are derived from repeated sensory experience. Aristotle locates the warrant for induction in the intuitive grasp (nous) that the observed particulars exhaust the kind.

### Sub-question: Baconian induction (eliminative)

Induction as a method of scientific discovery that proceeds by constructing tables of presence, absence, and degrees, then systematically rejecting false causes until only the true form remains. Bacon contrasts this with "induction by simple enumeration," which he dismisses as puerile.

### Sub-question: Induction vs. demonstration

The relationship between induction (from particulars to universals) and demonstration (from universals to particulars). Aristotle treats them as complementary: demonstration develops from universals, induction from particulars.

## Western canon

### Aristotle — *Posterior Analytics*

> *Part 18* — "It is also clear that the loss of any one of the senses entails the loss of a corresponding portion of knowledge, and that, since we learn either by induction or by demonstration, this knowledge cannot be acquired. Thus demonstration develops from universals, induction from particulars; but since it is possible to familiarize the pupil ..."
>
> *Gloss: Induction and demonstration are the two routes to knowledge; induction begins with particulars given through sense perception, demonstration with universals.*

### Aristotle — *Metaphysics*

> *Book I (from the commentary passage)* — "Induction is said by Aristotle to be [Greek: hoia phanton], but he says in the same place that for this reason we must *conceive* ([Greek: noehin]) the term containing the particular Instances (as A B C above) as composed of all the Individuals. If Induction implied actual examination of all particular instances it would cease to be Reasoning at all and sink into repeated acts of Simple Apprehension it is really the bridging over of a chasm, not the steps cut in the rock on either side to enable us to walk down into and again out of it."
>
> *Gloss: Induction is not exhaustive enumeration but an act of intuitive conception (nous) that grasps the universal from a finite set of particulars — a leap, not a mechanical tally.*

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics* (commentary passage)

> *Book VI (from the commentary)* — "All Magnets attract iron (Major premiss of the last Syllogism proved by taking the minor term of that for the middle term of this.) ... Induction is said by Aristotle to be [Greek: hoia phanton], but he says in the same place that for this reason we must *conceive* ([Greek: noehin]) the term containing the particular Instances (as A B C above) as composed of all the Individuals."
>
> *Gloss: The inductive step requires the mind to treat the observed instances as if they were the whole class — a subjective certainty that depends on the quality of the intellect performing it.*

### Francis Bacon — *Novum Organum*

> *Aphorism CV* — "In forming axioms, we must invent a different form of induction from that hitherto in use; not only for the proof and discovery of principles (as they are called), but also of minor, intermediate, and, in short, every kind of axioms. The induction which proceeds by simple enumeration is puerile, leads to uncertain conclusions, and is exposed to danger from one contradictory instance, deciding generally from too small a number of facts, and those only the most obvious. But a really useful induction for the discovery and demonstration of the arts and sciences, should separate nature by proper rejections and exclusions, and then conclude for the affirmative, after collecting a sufficient number of negatives."
>
> *Gloss: Bacon rejects simple enumeration as childish and proposes an eliminative induction that proceeds through negatives before affirming a true axiom.*

### Francis Bacon — *Novum Organum*

> *Aphorism XVI (from the tables section)* — "We must, therefore, effect a complete solution and separation of nature; not by fire, but by the mind, which is a kind of divine fire. The first work, therefore, of true induction (as regards the discovery of forms) is the rejection or exclusion of the several natures which are not found in some instance where the given nature is present, or are found in some instance where the given nature is absent, or are found to increase in some instance when the given nature decreases, or to decrease when the given nature increases. Then indeed after the rejection and exclusion has been duly made, there will remain at the bottom, all light opinions vanishing into smoke, a form affirmative, solid, and true and well defined."
>
> *Gloss: True induction is a process of elimination — rejecting what cannot be the cause until only the true form remains.*

### Francis Bacon — *Novum Organum*

> *Aphorism XXI* — "After our tables of first review, our rejection or exclusive table, and the first vintage derived from them, we must advance to the remaining helps of the understanding with regard to the interpretation of nature, and a true and perfect induction, in offering which we will take the examples of cold and heat where tables are necessary, but where fewer instances are required we will go through a variety of others, so as neither to confound investigation nor to narrow our doctrine."
>
> *Gloss: Bacon outlines the full program of induction: tables, exclusions, first vintage, and then further "helps" — prerogative instances, supports, and corrections.*

### William Blackstone — *Commentaries on the Laws of England, Book the First*

> *Section on ecclesiastical induction* — "INDUCTION is performed by a mandate from the bishop to the arch-deacon, who usually issues out a precept to other clergymen to perform it for him. It is done by giving the clerk corporal possession of the church, as by holding the ring of the door, tolling a bell, or the like; and is a form required by law, with intent to give all the parishioners due notice, and sufficient certainty of their new minister, to whom their tithes are to be paid."
>
> *Gloss: A separate, non-philosophical sense of "induction" — the formal installation of a clergyman into a benefice. This usage is unrelated to logical induction and is included here only to mark the homonym.*

## Reframings

No non-Western reframings are supplied in the current corpus. The concept of induction as a method of inference from particulars to universals is distinctively Western in its formal articulation. Other traditions (e.g., Nyāya in Indian logic) have theories of inference (anumāna) that involve generalization from observed cases, but they do not map cleanly onto the Western induction/deduction dichotomy. A future expansion could include Nyāya-sūtra passages on anumāna.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Logic]] — Induction is one of the two fundamental forms of inference, alongside deduction.
- [[Science]] — Baconian induction is the methodological foundation of modern experimental science.
- [[Knowledge]] — Aristotle treats induction as the route from sense perception to universal knowledge.
- [[Experience]] — Induction depends on repeated observation and experience.
- [[Cause]] — Bacon's eliminative induction is designed to discover the true forms (causes) of natural phenomena.

## Status notes

- The corpus passages for Aristotle are drawn from the standard Oxford translations; translator/year is assumed from those editions. The *Metaphysics* and *Nicomachean Ethics* passages are actually from a commentary (possibly Giphanius or Cardwell) embedded in the translation; they have been cited as Aristotle's text because they present Aristotelian doctrine, but the commentary layer is noted.
- Blackstone's passage is included to flag the homonymous legal/ecclesiastical sense of "induction," which is a common source of confusion for modern readers. It is not a philosophical contribution to the Idea.
