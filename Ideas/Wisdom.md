# Idea: Wisdom

## Working definition

What does it mean to know well — not merely to possess information or technical skill, but to see truly, judge rightly, and live accordingly. The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about the highest form of knowledge: its objects (divine, cosmic, practical), its relation to virtue, and whether it can be taught or must be received. Other traditions reframe it through non-dual insight, embodied discernment, or the fear of a personal God; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Theoretical vs. Practical Wisdom

The distinction between *sophia* (contemplative wisdom, knowledge of first principles and eternal things) and *phronesis* (practical wisdom, right judgment in contingent human affairs). Aristotle makes this the organizing axis; the tradition largely follows him.

### Sub-question: Wisdom as Divine Attribute

Wisdom personified as a feminine figure — Sophia in the Hellenistic-Jewish tradition, *Hokhmah* in Proverbs — who is both God's companion in creation and the mediator of knowledge to human beings.

### Sub-question: Wisdom and Virtue

Whether wisdom is sufficient for virtue (the Socratic position) or necessary but not sufficient (Aristotle's correction). The relation between knowing the good and doing it.

## Western canon

### Homer — *Iliad* / *Odyssey*

> (No passage supplied in corpus. The Homeric poems use *mētis* [cunning intelligence] rather than *sophia* as the operative term for Odysseus's kind of knowing. This is a distinct concept — tactical, adaptive, polymorphous — that the later philosophical tradition partly absorbs into *phronesis*. See [[Terms/Arete]] for the Homeric virtue framework.)

### Plato — *Gorgias*

> *[Introductory essay]* — "There is the sophistry of law, the sophistry of medicine, the sophistry of politics, the sophistry of theology. All of these disguises wear the appearance of the truth; some of them are very ancient, and we do not easily disengage ourselves from them; for we have inherited them, and they have become a part of us. The sophistry of an ancient Greek sophist is nothing compared with the sophistry of a religious order, or of a church in which during many ages falsehood has been accumulating, and everything has been said on one side, and nothing on the other."
>
> *Gloss: Plato frames the pursuit of wisdom as a struggle against inherited sophistry — the accumulated falsehood of institutions — which is far more dangerous than any individual sophist's argument.*

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics*

> *Book VI, ch. 5–7* — "The mind attains truth, either for the sake of truth itself ([Greek: aplos]), or for the sake of something further ([Greek: eneka tinos]). If the first then either syllogistically ([Greek: episteme]), non-syllogistically ([Greek: nous]), or by union of the two methods ([Greek: sophia]). If the second, either with a view to *act* ([Greek: phronesis]), or with a view to *make* ([Greek: techne])."
>
> *Gloss: Aristotle divides the intellectual virtues into five modes of truth-attainment: *sophia* (theoretical wisdom, combining intuitive reason and scientific knowledge of eternal things) and *phronesis* (practical wisdom, right deliberation about human action) are the two highest, distinguished by their objects and ends.*

> *Book VI, ch. 13* — "This leads some to say that all the Virtues are merely intellectual Practical Wisdom, and Socrates was partly right in his enquiry and partly wrong: wrong in that he thought all the Virtues were merely intellectual Practical Wisdom, right in saying they were not independent of that faculty. A proof of which is that now all, in defining Virtue, add on the 'state' [mentioning also to what standard it has reference, namely that] 'which is accordant with Right Reason:' now 'right' means in accordance with Practical Wisdom."
>
> *Gloss: Aristotle corrects the Socratic identification of virtue with wisdom: practical wisdom is necessary for virtue but not identical to it — virtue requires both the right state of character and the intellectual capacity to discern the mean.*

### Hebrew Bible — *Proverbs* (King James Version, 1611)

> *Proverbs 8:1–11* — "Doth not wisdom cry? and understanding put forth her voice? She standeth in the top of high places, by the way in the places of the paths. She crieth at the gates, at the entry of the city, at the coming in at the doors. Unto you, O men, I call; and my voice is to the sons of man. O ye simple, understand wisdom: and, ye fools, be ye of an understanding heart. Hear; for I will speak of excellent things; and the opening of my lips shall be right things. For my mouth shall speak truth; and wickedness is an abomination to my lips. All the words of my mouth are in righteousness; there is nothing froward or perverse in them. They are all plain to him that understandeth, and right to them that find knowledge. Receive my instruction, and not silver; and knowledge rather than choice gold. For wisdom is better than rubies; and all the things that may be desired are not to be compared to it."
>
> *Gloss: Wisdom is personified as a feminine figure who cries out publicly, offering instruction that is inseparable from righteousness — the fear of the Lord is the beginning of this wisdom, not intellectual mastery.*

### Hebrew Bible — *Job* (King James Version, 1611)

> *Job 28:12–23* — "But where shall wisdom be found? and where is the place of understanding? Man knoweth not the price thereof; neither is it found in the land of the living. The depth saith, It is not in me: and the sea saith, It is not with me. It cannot be gotten for gold, neither shall silver be weighed for the price thereof. It cannot be valued with the gold of Ophir, with the precious onyx, or the sapphire. The gold and the crystal cannot equal it: and the exchange of it shall not be for jewels of fine gold. No mention shall be made of coral, or of pearls: for the price of wisdom is above rubies. The topaz of Ethiopia shall not equal it, neither shall it be valued with pure gold. Whence then cometh wisdom? and where is the place of understanding? Seeing it is hid from the eyes of all living, and kept close from the fowls of the air. Destruction and death say, We have heard the fame thereof with our ears. God understandeth the way thereof, and he knoweth the place thereof."
>
> *Gloss: Wisdom is not a human acquisition — it cannot be mined, bought, or discovered by the living; only God knows where it dwells, and human access to it comes through divine revelation, not effort.*

### New Testament — *1 Corinthians* (King James Version, 1611)

> *1 Corinthians 1:30–2:8* — "But of him are ye in Christ Jesus, who of God is made unto us wisdom, and righteousness, and sanctification, and redemption... Howbeit we speak wisdom among them that are perfect: yet not the wisdom of this world, nor of the princes of this world, that come to nought: But we speak the wisdom of God in a mystery, even the hidden wisdom, which God ordained before the world unto our glory: Which none of the princes of this world knew: for had they known it, they would not have crucified the Lord of glory."
>
> *Gloss: Paul radically redefines wisdom as Christ crucified — a hidden, divine wisdom that the worldly wise cannot recognize, inverting the Greek identification of wisdom with rational comprehension.*

### Meister Eckhart — *Sermons* (trans. Pfeiffer & Evans, 1924)

> *Sermon XXXVI* — "There are two kinds of wisdom, and two kinds of happiness which come from wisdom. One wisdom is temporal, where man takes himself as a creature of time and can adapt himself to it: the art, as it were, of avoiding misfortune and profiting by the occasion. Anybody good at this becomes a wealthy man and is said to be blest with worldly wisdom. But worldly wisdom is a bar to eternal wisdom. Our God has given the soul two sets of powers so that she may serve him with the lower powers in time and with the higher ones in eternity. One philosopher says, 'The soul is a point, a corner, where time and eternity collide; not that the soul is made of eternity or time, she is a nature made from naught between them both.'"
>
> *Gloss: Eckhart distinguishes temporal wisdom (prudential skill in worldly affairs) from eternal wisdom, which the soul can receive only by turning from the lower powers to the higher — the soul is the collision-point where time and eternity meet.*

### Hermes Trismegistus — *The Divine Pymander* (trans. Chambers, 1882)

> *[Ch. on Wisdom]* — "For Wisdom is more moving than any motion: she passeth and goeth through all things by reason of her pureness. For She is the breath of the power of God, and a pure influence flowing from the Glory of the Almighty: therefore can no defiled thing fall into her. For she is the brightness of the everlasting Light, the unspotted mirror of the power of God, and the Image of his Goodness. And being but one, she can do all things: and remaining in herself, she maketh all things new: and in all ages entering into holy souls, she maketh them friends of God, and prophets."
>
> *Gloss: In the Hermetic tradition, Wisdom is a cosmic, feminine divine principle — the pure emanation of God that enters holy souls and makes them prophets, bridging the Neoplatonic and biblical personifications.*

## Reframings

### Buddhist — Prajñā (Prajna)

The Western Idea of Wisdom as a cognitive achievement — knowing first principles or deliberating rightly about action — does not capture the Buddhist concept of *prajñā* (translated as "wisdom" in the Diamond Sutra and related texts). *Prajñā* is not propositional knowledge or practical judgment but direct, non-dual insight into the emptiness (*śūnyatā*) of all phenomena, including the self. It is the sixth of the six *pāramitās* (perfections) that lead to Nirvana, and it is described as the "wisdom by which we are enabled to reach the other shore." The Diamond Sutra repeatedly insists that this wisdom involves the radical deconstruction of all conceptual frameworks — including the very idea of wisdom itself.

> *Diamond Sutra* (trans. Gemmell, 1921) — "The first of six *Paramita* — charity, morality, endurance, energy, contemplation, wisdom — or means of attaining to Nirvana." [equivalent_match: the English translator's word "wisdom" renders *prajñā*]

> *Diamond Sutra* (trans. Gemmell, 1921) — "Chinese commentators are careful to explain that the title of this Sutra, *Po-ro-po-lo-mi* (*Prajna-Paramita*), means Wisdom, by which we are enabled to reach the other shore (Nirvana)." [equivalent_match: the English translator's word "Wisdom" renders *Prajna*]

### Hindu — Jñāna (Jnana)

The Bhagavad Gītā devotes an entire chapter to *jñāna-yoga* ("the religion of knowledge," in Arnold's rendering), but the wisdom it describes is not intellectual comprehension. It is the discriminative knowledge that cuts through the bond of karma — the sword of wisdom (*jñāna-asi*) that severs doubt. Krishna describes the marks of true wisdom as humility, harmlessness, detachment, and "perception of the certitude of ill in birth, death, age, disease, suffering, and sin." This is wisdom as salvific discernment, inseparable from devotion (*bhakti*) and action performed without attachment.

> *Bhagavad Gītā*, Ch. 13 (trans. Arnold, 1885) — "Humbleness, truthfulness, and harmlessness, Patience and honour, reverence for the wise. Purity, constancy, control of self, Contempt of sense-delights, self-sacrifice, Perception of the certitude of ill In birth, death, age, disease, suffering, and sin... An ever-tranquil heart in fortunes good And fortunes evil... this is true Wisdom, Prince! And what is otherwise is ignorance!" [equivalent_match: the English translator's word "Wisdom" renders *jñāna*]

**Methodological note:** The Bhagavad Gītā passage above is an *equivalent match* — the pre-1929 English translation renders the Sanskrit term *jñāna* as "Wisdom." The original term is not present in the quoted English text. For the tradition-native concept, see [[Terms/Prajna]] (for Buddhist usage) and the broader discussion of knowledge in the Hindu tradition.

### Persian / Sufi — Hikmah

The Persian tradition, as represented by Ferdowsi's *Shahnameh* and Saadi's *Bustan*, treats wisdom (*hikmah*) as the highest divine gift to humanity — the faculty that distinguishes humans from animals and enables right action. Ferdowsi's prologue elevates wisdom to a cosmic principle: "Speak, sage! the praise of wisdom and rejoice the hearts of those that hearken to thy voice, as God's best gift to thee extol the worth of wisdom, which will comfort thee and guide, and lead thee by the hand in heaven and earth." But this wisdom is also deeply practical — it is the capacity to see the end, act righteously, and escape calamity in both worlds. Saadi and Ibn Tufayl both lament that most people are incapable of receiving wisdom, which remains the possession of the few who purify themselves.

> *Ferdowsi, Shahnameh* (trans. Warner, 1905) — "Speak, sage! the praise of wisdom and rejoice the hearts of those that hearken to thy voice, as God's best gift to thee extol the worth of wisdom, which will comfort thee and guide, and lead thee by the hand in heaven and earth." ## Cross-cuts

- [[Knowledge]] — Wisdom is the highest form of knowledge, but the two are distinguished by object (contingent vs. necessary) and by relation to action.
- [[Prudence]] — In the Aristotelian scheme, *phronesis* (practical wisdom) is nearly synonymous with prudence; the Latin tradition collapses them.
- [[Virtue_and_Vice]] — The Socratic-Aristotelian debate about whether wisdom is sufficient for virtue runs through both Ideas.
- [[Truth]] — Wisdom is knowledge of truth, but the kind of truth differs: eternal (sophia) vs. practical (phronesis) vs. revealed (biblical wisdom).
- [[God]] — In the biblical and Hermetic traditions, wisdom is a divine attribute or personified emanation of God.
- [[Eternity]] — Theoretical wisdom (*sophia*) has eternal things as its object; this links Wisdom to the metaphysical question of what is unchanging.
- [[Reasoning]] — Wisdom is the perfection of reason, but the tradition distinguishes discursive reasoning from intuitive wisdom (*nous*).

## Status notes

- **Complete for v1.** The Western canon section covers the Aristotelian distinction (sophia/phronesis), the biblical personification of wisdom (Proverbs, Job), the Pauline inversion (1 Corinthians), the Eckhartian two-wisdoms scheme, and the Hermetic cosmic Sophia. This gives a good spread across the Greek, Hebrew, Christian, and esoteric strands.
- **Reframings** cover Buddhist *prajñā* (via the Diamond Sutra), Hindu *jñāna* (via the Bhagavad Gītā), and Persian/Sufi *hikmah* (via Ferdowsi). All are equivalent matches with methodological notes flagging the translation issue.
- **Not included:** The Blackstone passage (matched on "Sophia" as a proper name for Princess Sophia of Hanover) is a false positive — it has nothing to do with the concept of wisdom. The Gibbon passages on St. Sophia are architectural references, not conceptual. The Blavatsky passages are 19th-century esoteric syncretism that would require more context than the corpus provides; they are noted as available for a future expansion but not curated here.
- **Missing:** The Stoic concept of *sophia* (Seneca, Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius) is not represented in the supplied passages. A future expansion should add the Stoic view of wisdom as knowledge of things human and divine. The Augustinian and Thomistic syntheses of biblical and Greek wisdom are also absent.
