# Idea: State

## Working definition

The state as a political entity: a sovereign body exercising authority over a defined territory and population, claiming a monopoly on legitimate force and the power to make and enforce law. The Western tradition debates its origin (natural, contractual, divine), its purpose (security, justice, the common good), and its proper form (centralized or federal, democratic or aristocratic). This page traces the concept from early modern legal theory through the American federalist experiment; classical Greek and medieval contributions are represented only indirectly in the supplied corpus.

## Sub-questions

### Nature and Purpose of the State

What the state is, why it exists, and what gives it authority.

### State and Local Administration

How the state relates to its subordinate territorial units — townships, counties, provinces — and the balance between central direction and local self-government.

### Federal and State Jurisdiction

The division of judicial and legislative power between a national government and constituent states, as worked out in the American constitutional tradition.

## Western canon

### William Blackstone — *Commentaries on the Laws of England*

> "For a state is a collective body, composed of a multitude of individuals, united for their safety and convenience, and intending to act together as one man. If it therefore is to act as one man, it ought to act by one uniform will. But, inasmuch as political communities are made up of many natural persons, each of whom has his particular will and inclination, these several wills cannot by any *natural* union be joined together, or tempered and disposed into a lasting harmony, so as to constitute and produce that one uniform will of the whole. It can therefore be no otherwise produced than by a *political* union; by the consent of all persons to submit their own private wills to the will of one man, or of one or more assemblies of men, to whom the supreme authority is entrusted: and this will of that one man, or assemblage of men, is in different states, according to their different constitutions, understood to be *law*."
>
> *Gloss: Blackstone defines the state as a collective body united by consent, whose unity of will is achieved through political submission to a sovereign authority — the foundation of law.*

### Alexis de Tocqueville — *Democracy in America*

> "The township and the county are therefore bound to take care of their special interests: the State governs, but it does not interfere with their administration. Exceptions to this rule may be met with, but not a contrary principle. The first consequence of this doctrine has been to cause all the magistrates to be chosen either by or at least from amongst the citizens. As the officers are everywhere elected or appointed for a certain period, it has been impossible to establish the rules of a dependent series of authorities; there are almost as many independent functionaries as there are functions, and the executive power is disseminated in a multitude of hands. Hence arose the indispensable necessity of introducing the control of the courts of justice over the administration, and the system of pecuniary penalties, by which the secondary bodies and their representatives are constrained to obey the laws."
>
> *Gloss: Tocqueville describes the American principle that the state governs but does not administer locally — a decentralized model that disperses executive power and relies on judicial oversight to enforce compliance.*

> "The extent of the township was too small to contain a system of judicial institutions; each county has, however, a court of justice, a sheriff to execute its decrees, and a prison for criminals. There are certain wants which are felt alike by all the townships of a county; it is therefore natural that they should be satisfied by a central authority. In the State of Massachusetts this authority is vested in the hands of several magistrates, who are appointed by the Governor of the State, with the advice of his council. The officers of the county have only a limited and occasional authority, which is applicable to certain predetermined cases. The State and the townships possess all the power requisite to conduct public business."
>
> *Gloss: The county serves as an intermediate unit with limited, delegated authority; the real political existence belongs to the state and the township.*

### *The Federalist Papers* (Hamilton, Madison, Jay)

> "It is to comprehend 'all cases in law and equity arising under the Constitution, the laws of the United States, and treaties made, or which shall be made, under their authority; to all cases affecting ambassadors, other public ministers, and consuls; to all cases of admiralty and maritime jurisdiction; to controversies to which the United States shall be a party; to controversies between two or more States; between a State and citizens of another State; between citizens of the same State claiming lands and grants of different States; and between a State or the citizens thereof and foreign states, citizens, and subjects.' This constitutes the entire mass of the judicial authority of the Union."
>
> *Gloss: The Federalist defines the scope of federal judicial power, delineating the boundary between state and national jurisdiction in the American constitutional order.*

## Reframings

No non-Western passages on the political state were present in the supplied corpus. The concept of the state as a sovereign territorial entity is a distinctly Western development; other traditions organize political authority through kinship, cosmic hierarchy, or decentralized networks. For the Chinese tradition, see the Term page for [[Terms/Tao]] (the Way of governance) and [[Terms/Li]] (ritual propriety as social order). For the Islamic tradition, see [[Terms/Umma]] (the community of believers, which transcends territorial state boundaries).

## Cross-cuts

- [[Law]] — the state as the source of positive law; Blackstone's definition makes law the expression of the sovereign will.
- [[Government]] — the institutional apparatus through which the state acts.
- [[Constitution]] — the fundamental law that structures state authority and its limits.
- [[Citizen]] — the individual in relation to the state; Tocqueville's emphasis on local self-government presupposes active citizenship.
- [[Sovereignty]] — the supreme authority within a state; Blackstone's "one uniform will" is an expression of sovereignty.
- [[Justice]] — the state's purpose includes the administration of justice; Tocqueville notes the necessity of judicial control over administration.

## Status notes

- **Aristotle passage excluded:** The single retrieved passage (from the introduction to the *Politics*) lacked translator and year information, making citation impossible under the project's bibliographic standards. The passage also appeared to be editorial commentary rather than Aristotle's own text.
- **Kant passages excluded:** Both retrieved passages from the *Critique of Pure Reason* use "state" in the metaphysical sense of a condition or mode of being (e.g., "change of state" of a moving body). They are not relevant to the political concept of the state.
- **Parker passage excluded:** The passage from *The Constitution of the Five Nations* is a bibliographic list of state publications, not substantive content.
- **No non-Western material:** The corpus returned no passages from Eastern, Islamic, or indigenous traditions that directly address the political state. The Reframings section is therefore minimal, pointing only to related Term pages.
- **Future additions needed:** The page would benefit from direct passages from Aristotle (*Politics*), Hobbes (*Leviathan*), Locke (*Second Treatise*), Rousseau (*Social Contract*), and Hegel (*Philosophy of Right*). These are absent from the supplied corpus and should be added in a future pass.
