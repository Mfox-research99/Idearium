# Idea: Prophecy

## Working definition

What does it mean for a human to speak for God, and how can such speech be known to be true? The Western tradition frames prophecy primarily as a mode of divine revelation — a supernatural communication of sure knowledge about future or hidden things, mediated through chosen individuals. The question turns on the nature of the prophet's authority, the certitude of the revelation, and the relation between prophecy and ordinary knowledge. Other traditions reframe the question through different cosmologies of inspiration; see Reframings.

## Western canon

### Spinoza — *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus*

> *Chapter I, §1–2* — "Prophecy, or revelation is sure knowledge revealed by God to man. A prophet is one who interprets the revelations of God to those who are unable to attain to sure knowledge of the matters revealed, and therefore can only apprehend them by simple faith. The Hebrew word for prophet is 'nabi,' i.e. speaker or interpreter, but in Scripture its meaning is restricted to interpreter of God."
>
> *Gloss: Spinoza defines prophecy as a species of knowledge — certain, but not demonstrative; the prophet is an interpreter, not an originator, and the term's Hebrew root grounds the definition in a specific scriptural tradition.*

> *Chapter I, §25* — "The whole question of the certitude of prophecy, was based on these three considerations: 1. That the things revealed were imagined very vividly, affecting the prophets in the same way as things seen when awake; 2. The presence of a sign; 3. Lastly, and chiefly, that the mind of the prophet was given wholly, to what was right and good."
>
> *Gloss: Certitude in prophecy is moral, not mathematical — it rests on vividness, external confirmation, and moral character, not on logical demonstration.*

> *Chapter I, §38–41* — "Lastly, prophecy varied according to the opinions held by the prophets; for instance, to the Magi, who believed in the follies of astrology, the birth of Christ was revealed through the vision of a star in the East. To the augurs of Nebuchadnezzar the destruction of Jerusalem was revealed through entrails... To prophets who believed that man acts from free choice and by his own power, God was revealed as standing apart from and ignorant of future human actions."
>
> *Gloss: The content and form of prophecy are conditioned by the prophet's prior beliefs and temperament — revelation accommodates itself to the recipient.*

### Pascal — *Pensées*

> *Fragment 705* — "The prophecies are the strongest proof of Jesus Christ. It is for them also that God has made most provision; for the event which has fulfilled them is a miracle existing since the birth of the Church to the end. So God has raised up prophets during sixteen hundred years, and, during four hundred years afterwards, He has scattered all these prophecies among all the Jews, who carried them into all parts of the world."
>
> *Gloss: For Pascal, prophecy is not merely prediction but a continuous providential argument — the historical dispersion of the Jewish people and the preservation of the prophetic texts constitute a standing miracle that authenticates Christ.*

### Hobbes — *Leviathan*

> *Chapter 36* — "Prophecy is not an art, nor (when it is taken for Praediction) a constant Vocation; but an extraordinary, and temporary Employment from God, most often of Good men, but sometimes also of the Wicked. The woman of Endor, who is said to have had a familiar spirit, and thereby to have raised a Phantasme of Samuel, and foretold Saul his death, was not therefore a Prophetesse; for neither had she any science, whereby she could raise such a Phantasme; nor does it appear that God commanded the raising of it."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes insists on the extraordinary, non-artificial character of prophecy — it cannot be learned or practiced as a skill, and even apparent instances (like the Witch of Endor) are not genuine prophecy unless God directly commands.*

> *Chapter 36* — "In the sense of speaking from man to God, Abraham is called a Prophet (Genes. 20.7.) where God in a Dream speaketh to Abimelech in this manner, 'Now therefore restore the man his wife, for he is a Prophet, and shall pray for thee'; whereby may be also gathered, that the name of Prophet may be given, not unproperly to them that in Christian Churches, have a Calling to say publique prayers for the Congregation."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes notes the broader biblical sense of "prophet" as one who speaks to God on behalf of others (intercessor), not only one who speaks from God to the people — a semantic range that includes public prayer.*

### Bacon — *Essays*

> *Essay XXXV: Of Prophecies* — "I mean not to speak of divine prophecies, nor of heathen oracles, nor of natural predictions; but only of prophecies that have been of certain memory, and from hidden causes."
>
> *Gloss: Bacon deliberately brackets divine prophecy, pagan oracles, and natural foresight to examine only those reported predictions whose causes are obscure — a skeptical, empirical approach that treats prophecy as a phenomenon to be explained rather than a mode of revelation.*

## Reframings

### Hebrew Bible — *Nabi*

The Hebrew term *nabi* (prophet) carries a range the English word "prophet" does not transparently convey: speaker, interpreter, intercessor, and sometimes ecstatic. Spinoza and Hobbes both note the root meaning of "speaker" or "interpreter," and Hobbes adds the intercessory sense (Abraham praying for Abimelech). The Hebrew tradition does not sharply separate foretelling from forth-telling; the prophet's primary office is to deliver God's word to the present moment, whether that word concerns judgment, repentance, or future events. This reframing is already partially visible in the Western canon passages above, but a full treatment would require a dedicated Term page.

### Islam — *Nubuwwa*

Islamic prophecy (nubuwwa) shares the Semitic root with Hebrew *nabi* but is systematically distinguished from *rasul* (messenger). The prophet (nabi) receives divine revelation without necessarily being charged with a new law; the messenger (rasul) brings a scripture and a law. The certitude of prophecy in Islam rests on the miracle of the Qur'an itself (i'jaz) rather than on fulfilled predictions or moral character alone. No corpus passages are available for this reframing in the current corpus; it is noted here for future expansion.
