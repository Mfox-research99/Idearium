# Idea: Knowledge

## Working definition

What it means to know, and how knowing is distinguished from believing, opining, guessing, or being ignorant. The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about the mind's relation to its objects: whether knowledge is perception of ideas, grasp of Forms, justified true belief, or practical competence. The tradition has debated the sources (reason, sense, revelation), the degrees (intuitive, demonstrative, sensitive), and the objects (ideas, things, God, the good). Other traditions reframe the question through a distinction between higher and lower knowing; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Sources of knowledge

Whether knowledge originates in sense experience, rational intuition, divine revelation, or practical activity — and whether these sources are compatible.

### Sub-question: Degrees of certainty

How knowledge admits of gradation: intuitive certainty, demonstrative proof, probable judgment, and the boundary where knowledge shades into opinion.

### Sub-question: Knowledge and action

Whether knowledge is primarily contemplative (knowing that) or practical (knowing how), and whether the two are separable.

### Sub-question: Higher and lower knowledge

Whether there is a kind of knowledge that transcends discursive reasoning and yields absolute bliss or liberation — a question that the Western tradition touches (Plato's Form of the Good, Aristotle's nous, Christian beatific vision) but that other traditions make central.

## Western canon

### Plato — *Republic*

> *Book IV, 428b–d* — "And good counsel is clearly a kind of knowledge, for not by ignorance, but by knowledge, do men counsel well? … And the kinds of knowledge in a State are many and diverse? … There is the knowledge of the carpenter; but is that the sort of knowledge which gives a city the title of wise and good in counsel? … Then a city is not to be called wise because possessing a knowledge which counsels for the best about wooden implements? … Nor yet by reason of a knowledge which cultivates the earth; that would give the city the name of agricultural? … Well, I said, and is there any knowledge in our recently-founded State among any of the citizens which advises, not about any particular thing in the State, but about the whole, and considers how a State can best deal with itself and with other States? … And what is this knowledge, and among whom is it found?"
>
> *Gloss:* Plato distinguishes technical knowledge (carpentry, farming) from the knowledge that governs the whole — the ruler's knowledge of the good, which is the only knowledge that makes a city wise in the full sense.

### John Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*

> *Book IV, Chapter I, §1–2* — "Since the mind, in all its thoughts and reasonings, hath no other immediate object but its own ideas, which it alone does or can contemplate, it is evident that our knowledge is only conversant about them. … KNOWLEDGE then seems to me to be nothing but THE PERCEPTION OF THE CONNEXION OF AND AGREEMENT, OR DISAGREEMENT AND REPUGNANCY OF ANY OF OUR IDEAS."
>
> *Gloss:* Locke defines knowledge as a relation between ideas, not a direct grasp of things — a foundational move for British empiricism.

> *Book IV, Chapter II, §1–5* — "Thus, having the ideas of an obtuse and an acute angled triangle, both drawn from equal bases, and between parallels, I can, by intuitive knowledge, perceive the one not to be the other, but cannot that way know whether they be equal or no … there is need of some intervening qualities to measure them by, which is demonstration, or rational knowledge. … Fourthly, It follows, also, from what is above observed, that our RATIONAL KNOWLEDGE cannot reach to the whole extent of our ideas … Fifthly, SENSITIVE KNOWLEDGE reaching no further than the existence of things actually present to our senses, is yet much narrower."
>
> *Gloss:* Locke distinguishes three degrees of knowledge — intuitive (the highest certainty), demonstrative (mediated by proof), and sensitive (limited to present existence) — and notes that none covers the full range of our ideas.

### John Dewey — *Democracy and Education*

> *Chapter 14, "The Nature of Subject Matter"* — "The knowledge which comes first to persons, and that remains most deeply ingrained, is knowledge of how to do; how to walk, talk, read, write, skate, ride a bicycle, manage a machine, calculate, drive a horse, sell goods, manage people, and so on indefinitely. … When education, under the influence of a scholastic conception of knowledge which ignores everything but scientifically formulated facts and truths, fails to recognize that primary or initial subject matter always exists as matter of an active doing, involving the use of the body and the handling of material, the …"
>
> *Gloss:* Dewey inverts the scholastic hierarchy: knowledge begins in doing, not in contemplation. "Knowledge how" is primary; "knowledge that" is derivative and abstract.

> *Chapter 14* — "To be thoughtful, considerate, means to heed the claims of others. Apprehension means dread of undesirable consequences, as well as intellectual grasp. To have good sense or judgment is to know the conduct a situation calls for; discernment is not making distinctions for the sake of making them … but is insight into an affair with reference to acting. Wisdom has never lost its association with the proper direction of life. Only in education, never in the life of farmer, sailor, merchant, physician, or laboratory experimenter, does knowledge mean primarily a store of information aloof from doing."
>
> *Gloss:* Dewey ties knowledge to intelligent action and social context; knowledge divorced from practice is an artifact of schooling, not of life.

> *Chapter 14* — "Science is a name for knowledge in its most characteristic form. It represents in its degree, the perfected outcome of learning,—its consummation. What is known, in a given case, is what is sure, certain, settled, disposed of; that which we think with rather than that which we think about. In its honorable sense, knowledge is distinguished from opinion, guesswork, speculation, and mere tradition."
>
> *Gloss:* Dewey grants science the status of consummate knowledge — settled, reliable, a tool for further thinking — but insists it grows out of practical engagement, not detached observation.

## Reframings

### Hindu tradition — Vidya (higher and lower knowledge)

The Western distinction between knowledge and opinion maps imperfectly onto the Hindu distinction between *para vidya* (higher knowledge) and *apara vidya* (lower knowledge). The higher knowledge is knowledge of Brahman, the imperishable ground of all, which yields absolute bliss (*moksha*). The lower knowledge includes the Vedas, the sciences, and all discursive knowing — it leads only to worldly exaltation. The two are not continuous: you cannot climb from lower to higher by adding more lower knowledge. The passage below (from the Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary) makes this explicit. Note: the cited text uses the English word "knowledge" for the Sanskrit *vidya*; the original term is not present in the translation.

> *Vedānta-sūtras, commentary on I.1.1 (Mundaka Upanishad reference)* — "If we now were to assume that the Indestructible distinguished by invisibility and like qualities is something different from the highest Lord, the knowledge referring to it would not be the higher one. For the distinction of lower and higher knowledge is made on account of the diversity of their results, the former leading to mere worldly exaltation, the latter to absolute bliss; and nobody would assume absolute bliss to result from the knowledge of the pradhana."
>
> *Gloss:* The commentator insists that higher knowledge is not just a more refined version of lower knowledge — it has a different object (Brahman, not the material cause) and a different result (liberation, not worldly success).

## Cross-cuts

- [[Truth]] — knowledge aims at truth, but the two are not identical; one can have true belief without knowledge.
- [[Opinion]] — the boundary between knowledge and opinion is a central problem in Plato and Locke.
- [[Science]] — Dewey treats science as the consummate form of knowledge; the Western tradition often identifies the two.
- [[Wisdom]] — practical knowledge of how to live well, distinguished from mere information.
- [[Education]] — the transmission of knowledge is the central concern of Dewey's *Democracy and Education*.
- [[Logic]] — the method by which demonstrative knowledge is built from intuitive premises.
- [[Metaphysics]] — the question of whether knowledge can reach beyond phenomena to things in themselves.
