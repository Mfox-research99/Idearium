# Idea: Will

## Working definition

Will as the faculty of rational appetite, choice, and self‑determination. The Western tradition asks whether the will is free, how it relates to intellect and desire, and how human will interacts with divine will. Other traditions may not isolate will as a separate faculty; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Free Will vs. Necessity

Whether the will is self‑determining or subject to external causation (divine, natural, or logical).

### Divine Will and Human Will

How God’s will relates to human volition — whether it predetermines, permits, or cooperates.

### Will and Intellect

Whether the will is a blind appetite or follows the intellect’s judgment; the medieval debate on voluntarism vs. intellectualism.

### Will and Action

The relation between willing and doing — the gap Augustine names in Romans 7.

## Western canon

### Augustine — *City of God*

> *Vol. II, Bk. XXII, ch. 2* — “But I remember that I discussed these modes of expression in the preceding books. According to this will, then, by which we say that God wills what He causes to be willed by others, from whom the future is hidden, He wills many things which He does not perform. … But if we speak of that will of His which is eternal as His foreknowledge, certainly He has already done all things in heaven and on earth that He has willed,—not only past and present things, but even things still future.”
>
> *Gloss: Augustine distinguishes two senses of divine will: the will by which God causes others to will (which may not be fulfilled) and the eternal will identical with foreknowledge (which is always fulfilled). This sets the terms for the later debate on grace and free will.*

### Thomas Aquinas — *Summa Theologica*

> *I‑II, Q. 83, Art. 1* — “Objection 1. It would seem that man has not free‑will. For whoever has free‑will does what he wills. But man does not what he wills; for it is written (Rom 7:19): *For the good which I will I do not, but the evil which I will not, that I do.* Therefore man has not free‑will. … On the contrary, It is …”
>
> *Gloss: Aquinas opens the free‑will question by confronting the Pauline gap between willing and doing, then proceeds to argue that man does have free‑will as a rational power of choice.*

### Meister Eckhart — *Sermons* (trans. C. de B. Evans, 1924)

> *Sermon on Resignation* — “See what a righteous man can do with God! It is of course true, is bound to be, that he who resigns his will wholly to God will catch God and bind him so that he can do nothing but what that man wills. He who makes over his will to God, to him God gives his will in return, so wholly, so really that God’s will becomes that man’s own, he having sworn by himself to do nothing but what that man will, but God will never be anyone’s own who has not become his own first.”
>
> *Gloss: Eckhart pushes the Augustinian logic to a mystical extreme: total resignation of the human will results in a reciprocal union where God’s will becomes the creature’s own, collapsing the distinction between divine and human agency.*

### Leibniz — *The Monadology and Other Philosophical Writings* (trans. Robert Latta, 1898)

> *Introduction, on moral and metaphysical necessity* — “Will is not to be identified with the abstract understanding, whose principle is that of contradiction. Will does not follow the speculative understanding alone … volition is not absolutely necessitated as the system of Spinoza requires.”
>
> *Gloss: Leibniz defends a middle ground: the will is not a blind faculty but is inclined by reasons without being necessitated, distinguishing his view from both intellectualist determinism and Spinoza’s absolute necessity.*

### Leibniz — *Discourse on Metaphysics* (trans. ?)

> *Section on the presumptive will of God* — “It is necessary to act conformably to the presumptive will of God as far as we are able to judge of it, trying with all our …”
>
> *Gloss: Leibniz introduces the concept of the “presumptive will” of God — what we can reasonably infer God wills given general providence — as a guide for human action when the revealed will is not explicit.*

## Reframings

### Buddhist — Anatman / Karma

Buddhist thought does not posit a substantial will as the Western tradition does. The doctrine of *anatman* (no‑self) denies a permanent subject that could be the bearer of volition. The question of choice is reframed in terms of *cetanā* (intention) and *karma* (action‑and‑consequence), where volitional acts are conditioned by past causes rather than issuing from an autonomous faculty. See [[Terms/Anatman]] and [[Terms/Karma]].

### Daoist — Wu Wei / Tao

Daoist philosophy emphasizes *wu wei* (non‑action or effortless action) over willful striving. The ideal is spontaneous alignment with the *Tao* (the Way), not the exercise of individual will. Deliberate willing is seen as a departure from the natural order. See [[Terms/Wu_Wei]] and [[Terms/Tao]].

## Status notes

- The legal/testament meaning of “will” (Shakespeare, Blackstone) was excluded as not relevant to the philosophical Idea.
- The Dewey passage (*Democracy and Education*) was considered but dropped because it treats “will” only indirectly through the concept of interest and means; it does not commit Dewey to a position on the faculty of will.
- The Blavatsky passage was excluded as it is a prophecy about the Kali Yuga, not a philosophical treatment of will.
- The Hermes Trismegistus passage (Asclepius) was considered but not used because the concept of divine will there is embedded in a cosmological restoration narrative that does not advance the sub‑questions of this page.
- Translator provenance for Augustine and Aquinas is based on standard editions; the corpus metadata does not specify translator/year for those passages. If Mike requires exact citations, these should be verified against the source texts.
- The Leibniz *Discourse* passage is a fragment; the full section would be needed for a proper gloss. The passage was included because it introduces the important concept of “presumptive will.”
