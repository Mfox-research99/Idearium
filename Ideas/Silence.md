# Idea: Silence

## Working definition

Silence is the absence of sound, but the Western tradition has rarely treated it as a mere void. It appears as a deliberate discipline (monastic quiet, contemplative stillness), a legal presumption (acquiescence through failure to speak), a rhetorical figure (the unsaid), a theological condition (the hidden God, the silent ground before creation), and a mystical practice (the soul's stillness before the divine Word). The question is not only *what silence is* but *what it means to be silent* — and whether silence can be a form of action, consent, knowledge, or union.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Silence as Legal Presumption

In law, silence can signify abandonment of a claim, consent, or the lapse of rights. Grotius treats prolonged silence as evidence that a proprietor has relinquished ownership, provided the silence is not founded on ignorance or fear.

### Sub-question: Silence as Virtue and Discipline

In moral and rhetorical traditions, silence is praised as restraint — the wise person speaks little and weighs words. This thread runs through Stoic, monastic, and sapiential literature.

### Sub-question: Silence as Mystical Ground

In theology and mysticism, silence precedes creation and is the precondition for hearing the divine. Eckhart teaches that the soul must become perfectly still for the Eternal Word to be born within. This is the "silent desert of the Godhead" — the ground beyond all distinction.

### Sub-question: Silence as Existential Terror

Pascal confronts silence as ontological dread: "The eternal silence of these infinite spaces frightens me." The cosmos does not answer. This is not the peaceful silence of contemplation but the void's refusal to respond to the human cry for meaning.

### Sub-question: Silence as Hiddenness of God

Pascal's Deus absconditus — God's silence is not absence but deliberate veiling. God hides so that faith requires the whole heart, not merely intellectual assent. Silence is the mode of divine pedagogy.

## Western canon

### Hugo Grotius — *The Rights of War and Peace*

> *Book II, Chapter IV* — "But if a person knows his property to be in the possession of another, and allows it to remain so for a length of time, without asserting his claim, unless there appear sufficient reasons for his silence, he is construed to have entirely abandoned all pretensions to the same."
>
> *Gloss: Grotius establishes silence as a legal act — prolonged failure to speak is taken as evidence of intent to abandon ownership, a presumption that can be rebutted only by showing ignorance or duress.*

> *Book II, Chapter IV* — "For a silence founded in ignorance can have no weight; and where any other reason appears, the presumption of free consent must fail."
>
> *Gloss: The legal force of silence depends on the speaker's knowledge and freedom; coerced or ignorant silence carries no presumption of consent.*

### Blaise Pascal — *Pensées* (trans. W. F. Trotter)

Pascal treats silence in three registers: the terror of cosmic indifference, the awe of reason before the infinite, and the hiddenness of God. Together they form the most profound treatment of silence in the early modern period.

> *Pensée 206* — "The eternal silence of these infinite spaces frightens me."
>
> *Gloss: The most famous single line on silence in the Western canon. The cosmos does not speak; it does not know us. This is the existential shudder of modern consciousness, anticipating the absurd. Silence here is ontological: the universe's indifference is a kind of speechlessness that overwhelms.*

> *Pensée 72 ("Disproportion of Man")* — "He who regards himself in this light will be afraid of himself, and observing himself sustained in the body given him by nature between those two abysses of the Infinite and Nothing, will tremble at the sight of these marvels; and I think that, as his curiosity changes into admiration, he will be more disposed to contemplate them in silence than to examine them with presumption."
>
> *Gloss: Silence is the only adequate response to the sublime. When reason reaches its limits before the two abysses (the infinitely large and the infinitely small), the proper posture shifts from curiosity (the will to examine) to admiration (the will to contemplate). Presumption speaks; wisdom falls silent.*

> *Pensée 242 — Deus Absconditus* — "It is not after this manner that Scripture speaks, which has a better knowledge of the things that are of God. It says, on the contrary, that God is a hidden God, and that, since the corruption of nature, He has left men in a darkness from which they can escape only through Jesus Christ. … So it tells us elsewhere: Vere tu es Deus absconditus."
>
> *Gloss: God's silence is not absence but hiddenness — a deliberate veiling that preserves human freedom. Pascal's 'hidden God' is the theological counterpart to the cosmic silence of Pensée 206: both cosmos and Creator refuse to coerce belief. God is found only by those who seek with their whole heart.*

> *Pensée 277–278* — "The heart has its reasons, which reason does not know. … It is the heart which experiences God, and not the reason. This, then, is faith: God felt by the heart, not by the reason."
>
> *Gloss: Reason must fall silent before the heart's knowledge. The heart knows in a way reason cannot articulate — its 'reasons' are incommensurable with logic. Faith is not against reason but beyond it. In matters of ultimate concern, reason must yield the floor to a deeper silence.*

> *Pensée 919* — "Silence is the greatest persecution; the saints were never silent. … We must cry out so much the louder, the more unjustly we are censured, and the more violently they would stifle speech."
>
> *Gloss: A necessary dialectical reversal: silence can be the weapon of oppression, and speech a duty. The saints 'were never silent' because truth demands public witness. This stands in tension with Pensée 72: silence before the infinite is wisdom; silence before injustice is cowardice.*

### Meister Eckhart — *Sermons* (ed. Pfeiffer/Evans, 1924)

Eckhart is the greatest theorist of silence in the Western mystical tradition. For him, silence is not the absence of speech but the condition for the birth of the divine Word in the soul.

> *Sermon XIX* — "If I am to hear the Eternal Word things must all be silent in me. Perfection in each creature means the silence of the rest. For God to speak in me creatures must hold their peace. If thou hast anything speaking in thee God will say nothing: everything must have stopped speaking. Where God is present things have all said their say."
>
> *Gloss: The soul must become perfectly still — all creaturely noise (desires, images, attachments) must cease — for the Eternal Word to be born within. Silence is the prerequisite of mystical union: God cannot speak into a soul already full of speech.*

> *Sermon XIX–XX* — "The Eternal Word must be silent in itself. The profoundest happiness lies in the mere beholding of the naked Godhead. … All creatures are silent in the Godhead. The Trinity is silent in itself. In the Eternal Word the Father is not speaking."
>
> *Gloss: Eckhart ascends from the silence of creatures to the silence within the Godhead itself. This is the 'silent desert': the ground beyond all distinction where even the Trinity falls into stillness. True beatitude is not speaking about God but beholding God in silence.*

> *Sermon XX* — "I will be still and let God work in me; I will be silent and hear what God says in me; I shall take refuge in detachment, so shall I find God more intimate with me. … He must be still and listen to what God is saying in him."
>
> *Gloss: The practical discipline of Gelassenheit (letting-go, detachment). Stillness is active surrender: 'I will be still and let God work in me.' Detachment (Abgeschiedenheit) is the refuge where God becomes intimate — silence is the house where the divine birth occurs.*

> *Sermon XXI* — "I entered into myself and knew that creatures in themselves are nothing but a passing show. … I realized that endless joy was locked within my breast and that there was in me a diapsalm and unbroken stillness of all interior things. … and by thy Holy Spirit I was led into the ground, wherein the Son is imaged, and I saw there God in God."
>
> *Gloss: A 'diapsalm' (selah, a musical rest) and 'unbroken stillness' — this is the silent ground of the soul (Grunt) where God and the soul are one. Images, means, and self-awareness dissolve. This is the still point where the soul rests in its uncreated source.*

> *Sermon XXIII–XXIV* — "She sat in the cell of recollection and dispassion. … In the darkness she saw a light. In the silence she heard a word. In passivity she was aware of activity. Her soul was all the time resting in eternity and abiding in the Godhead."
>
> *Gloss: The Virgin Mary as the archetype of the listening soul. Three luminous paradoxes: darkness → light, silence → Word, passivity → activity. This is the Marian posture: recollected, dispassionate, silent, and thereby perfectly receptive to the birth of God.*

## Reframings

### Persian / Sufi — *Samt* (silence, taciturnity)

Saadi's *Bustan* devotes a chapter to the excellence of taciturnity, treating silence as practical wisdom: the person who speaks little avoids reproach, guards secrets, and is compared to a grain of musk (valuable because concentrated). Silence is not absence but discipline — a choice that reflects self-knowledge.

> "Be silent, O thou who knowest many things! for he that speaketh little will be free from reproach on the Day of Judgment." — Saadi, *Bustan*, trans. H. G. Clarke, 1879.

→ See [[Terms/Nafs]] (the self that must be disciplined) and [[Terms/Fana]] (the silence of annihilation in the divine).

### Hindu / Buddhist — *Mauna* (silence) and *Nirvāṇa* (the silence of cessation)

The corpus lacks direct pre-1929 primary-source passages on silence from Hindu or Buddhist texts. The genuine tradition — the *mauna* of the sage, the *noble silence* of the Buddha on metaphysical questions — is not yet represented in the current corpus. The Buddhist *noble silence* (ariya-tuṇhībhāva) — the Buddha's refusal to answer metaphysical questions that do not conduce to liberation — is a distinct form of silence: not ignorance but a disciplined refusal to engage what does not lead to the end of suffering.

→ See [[Terms/Nirvana]] (the "blowing out" that is the silence of conditioned existence) and [[Terms/Sunyata]] (emptiness, which is not silence but the absence of intrinsic nature).

## Cross-cuts

- [[Attention]] — silence as the condition for sustained attention; the contemplative traditions treat silence as the ground of listening.
- [[Sacred_and_Profane]] — silence marks the boundary of the sacred; what cannot be spoken is often what is most holy.
- [[Witness]] — silence can be a form of bearing witness (refusal to speak under coercion) or its opposite (complicity through silence). Pascal's dialectic: silence as persecution vs. silence as contemplation.
- [[Truth]] — the unsaid can be truer than speech; apophatic theology holds that God is known only in silence.
- [[Nihilism]] — Pascal's terror before the "eternal silence of infinite spaces" as the threshold of nihilism.
- [[God]] — Pascal's hidden God; Eckhart's silent Godhead beyond the Trinity.
