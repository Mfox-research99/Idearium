# Idea: Pleasure_and_Pain

## Working definition

What pleasure and pain are, whether they are opposites or motions of the soul, and how they relate to the good life, virtue, and knowledge. The Western tradition frames them as fundamental experiences that drive action and shape character, but disagrees on whether pleasure is the mere cessation of pain, a positive state, or something to be managed by reason. Other traditions reframe the pair through concepts of suffering, detachment, and liberation; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Ontology of pleasure and pain

Are pleasure and pain genuine opposites, or are they motions of the soul with a neutral state between them? Is pleasure a replenishment of a lack, or can it be independent of pain?

### Sub-question: Ethical role

How do pleasure and pain relate to virtue? Is the virtuous person the one who feels pleasure and pain at the right things, at the right times, and in the right degree?

### Sub-question: Epistemological status

Are pleasure and pain simple ideas known only by experience, and do they define good and evil?

## Western canon

### Plato — *Republic*

> *Book IX (583c–584b)* — "And both pleasure and pain are motions of the soul, are they not? … How, then, can we be right in supposing that the absence of pain is pleasure, or that the absence of pleasure is pain? … This then is an appearance only and not a reality; that is to say, the rest is pleasure at the moment and in comparison of what is painful, and painful in comparison of what is pleasant; but all these representations, when tried by the test of true pleasure, are not real but a sort of imposition? … Look at the other class of pleasures which have no antecedent pains … take as an example the pleasures of smell, which are very great and have no antecedent pains."
>
> *Gloss:* Plato distinguishes between true pleasures (those without antecedent pain) and merely apparent pleasures (the relief of pain), arguing that the neutral state is mistakenly called pleasure or pain only by contrast.

Trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1892.

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics*

> *Book II, 1104b–1105a* — "Again: since Virtues have to do with actions and feelings, and on every feeling and every action pleasure and pain follow, here again is another proof that Virtue has for its object-matter pleasure and pain. … every habit of the Soul by its very nature has relation to, and exerts itself upon, things of the same kind as those by which it is naturally deteriorated or improved: now such habits do come to be vicious by reason of pleasures and pains, that is, by men pursuing or avoiding respectively, either such as they ought not, or at wrong times, or in wrong manner, and so forth … Virtue then is assumed to be that habit which is such, in relation to pleasures and pains, as to produce the best acts."
>
> *Gloss:* Aristotle makes pleasure and pain the material of virtue: the virtuous person is the one who feels them correctly, not the one who eliminates them.

Trans. W. D. Ross, 1908.

### Aristotle — *Metaphysics*

> *Book XI, 1072b* — "Again, they say that Pain is a lack of something suitable to nature and Pleasure a supply of it. … This notion would seem to have arisen out of the Pains and Pleasures connected with natural nourishment; because, when people have felt a lack and so have had Pain first, they, of course, are pleased with the supply of their lack. But this is not the case with all Pleasures: those attendant on mathematical studies, for instance, are unconnected with any Pain; and of such as attend on the senses those which arise through the sense of Smell; and again, many sounds, and sights, and memories, and hopes."
>
> *Gloss:* Aristotle rejects the view that all pleasure is the replenishment of a lack, pointing to pleasures of thought and sense that have no antecedent pain.

Trans. W. D. Ross, 1908.

### John Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*

> *Book II, Chapter XX, §1–2* — "Amongst the simple ideas which we receive both from sensation and reflection, PAIN and PLEASURE are two very considerable ones. … These, like other simple ideas, cannot be described, nor their names defined; the way of knowing them is, as of the simple ideas of the senses, only by experience. … Things then are good or evil, only in reference to pleasure or pain. That we call GOOD, which is apt to cause or increase pleasure, or diminish pain in us; or else to procure or preserve us the possession of any other good or a…"
>
> *Gloss:* Locke treats pleasure and pain as simple, indefinable ideas that ground all moral distinctions — good and evil are defined by their relation to pleasure and pain.

1690.

## Reframings

### Buddhist — Dukkha

The Buddhist tradition does not treat pleasure and pain as a symmetrical pair to be balanced. All conditioned experience, including pleasure, is marked by *dukkha* (unsatisfactoriness, suffering). The path to liberation is not the maximization of pleasure but the cessation of craving, which uproots the very cycle of pleasure and pain. Pleasure is not the opposite of pain but another form of attachment.

→ See [[Terms/Dukkha]], [[Terms/Nirvana]]

### Hindu — Moksha

In classical Hindu thought, pleasure (*kama*) and pain are both transient modifications of the body-mind complex. The pursuit of pleasure is a legitimate aim of life (*artha* and *kama*), but the ultimate goal (*moksha*) lies beyond the duality of pleasure and pain, in the realization of the self (*atman*) as identical with *brahman*.

→ See [[Terms/Moksha]], [[Terms/Atman]], [[Terms/Brahman]]
