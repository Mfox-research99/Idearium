# Idea: Covenant

## Working definition

A binding agreement between parties that creates obligations, often with moral or divine sanction. The Western tradition frames covenant primarily as a legal or political contract (Hobbes, Blackstone) and as a theological bond between God and a people (Hebrew Bible, New Testament). The question is: what makes a covenant binding, and what are its limits? Other traditions reframe this through relational fidelity or cosmic order; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Political covenant — foundation of sovereignty

How does a covenant among individuals create a commonwealth? Hobbes argues that the covenant establishing the sovereign is between subjects, not between subject and sovereign, so the sovereign cannot breach it.

### Biblical covenant — divine-human bond

The Old Testament presents covenant (berith) as God's unilateral promise to Abraham and the nation, renewed at Sinai and Moab. The New Testament reinterprets this as a new covenant written on hearts.

### Legal covenant — property and obligation

In common law, a covenant is a promise under seal, enforceable by writ of covenant, and may run with the land.

## Western canon

### Thomas Hobbes — *Leviathan*

> *Chapter 14* — "But in a civill estate, where there is a Power set up to constrain those that would otherwise violate their faith, that feare is no more reasonable; and for that cause, he which by the Covenant is to perform first, is obliged so to do."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes distinguishes the state of nature, where fear of non-performance voids a covenant, from civil society, where a sovereign power enforces performance.*

> *Chapter 18* — "Because the Right of bearing the Person of them all, is given to him they make Soveraigne, by Covenant onely of one to another, and not of him to any of them; there can happen no breach of Covenant on the part of the Soveraigne; and consequently none of his Subjects, by any pretence of forfeiture, can be freed from his Subjection."
>
> *Gloss: The sovereign is not a party to the covenant; therefore subjects cannot claim release from obedience due to sovereign breach.*

> *Chapter 35* — "This Covenant, at the Foot of Mount Sinai, was renewed by Moses (Exod. 19.5.) ... and it is of Abraham (not of Moses) St. Paul saith (Rom. 4.11.) that he is the 'Father of the Faithfull,' that is, of those that are loyall, and doe not violate their Allegiance sworn to God, then by Circumcision, and afterwards in the New Covenant by Baptisme."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes reads the biblical covenant as a political pact establishing God's peculiar sovereignty over Israel, continuous with his own contractual theory.*

### The Holy Bible (King James Version, 1611)

> *Hebrews 8:10* — "For this is the covenant that I will make with the house of Israel after those days, saith the Lord; I will put my laws into their mind, and write them in their hearts: and I will be to them a God, and they shall be to me a people."
>
> *Gloss: The New Covenant is internalized — law written on hearts, not on stone — and establishes a direct, universal knowledge of God.*

> *Deuteronomy 29:14-15* — "Neither with you only do I make this covenant and this oath; But with him that standeth here with us this day before the LORD our God, and also with him that is not here with us this day."
>
> *Gloss: The Mosaic covenant binds not only the present generation but all future descendants, making it a transgenerational obligation.*

### William Blackstone — *Commentaries on the Laws of England*

> *Book the Third, Chapter 9* — "Covenant also, contained in a deed, to do a direct act or to omit one, is another species of express contracts, the violation or breach of which is a civil injury. ... The remedy for this is by a writ of covenant; which directs the sheriff to command the defendant generally to keep his covenant with the plaintiff ... and if he continues refractory, or the covenant is already so broken that it cannot now be specifically performed, then the subsequent proceedings set forth with precision the covenant, the breach, and the loss which has happened thereby; whereupon the jury will give damages."
>
> *Gloss: Blackstone codifies covenant as a formal legal instrument under seal, with specific remedies for breach — the common law's practical counterpart to Hobbes's political theory.*

## Reframings

### Hebrew Bible — Berith

The Hebrew term *berith* (בְּרִית) encompasses not only contract but also kinship-like loyalty, divine promise, and ritual ratification (e.g., cutting animals). The English "covenant" captures the binding aspect but flattens the relational and liturgical dimensions. The Mosaic covenant at Sinai is both a legal constitution and a marriage-like bond.

→ See [[Terms/Covenant]]

### Islamic tradition — Mithaq / 'Ahd

The Qur'an uses *mithaq* (covenant) for God's covenant with the prophets and with humanity at creation (Q 7:172). It is a primordial acknowledgment of lordship, not a negotiated contract. The term *'ahd* (pledge) carries similar weight. Both resist reduction to the Western legal-political frame.
