# Idea: Progress

## Working definition

Whether human history, knowledge, institutions, or moral character move toward a better state — and if so, by what mechanism, at what cost, and toward what end. The Western tradition opens with Bacon's call for a total "instauration" of the sciences, laying the philosophical foundation for progress as cumulative, collaborative, and directed toward "the relief of man's estate." Mill ties progress to individuality and the diversity of social experiment. Dewey grounds it in education as continuous growth without a fixed terminus. Nietzsche inverts the entire valuation: what we call progress is the increase of power in the few at the expense of the many. The question is not whether change occurs, but whether change has direction and value.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Progress as the instauration of knowledge

Bacon's foundational claim: genuine progress requires a radical break with tradition and a correct method. Knowledge grows not by accumulation on rotten foundations but by rebuilding from the ground up.

### Sub-question: Progress as emancipation from custom

Whether progress consists in freeing individuals and societies from the dead hand of inherited practice (Mill), and whether such liberation is itself a good.

### Sub-question: Progress as education and continuous growth

Dewey's pragmatist vision: progress is not approximation to a fixed goal but the capacity for continuous learning and adaptation. "He learns to learn" — the meta-capacity for growth is progress itself.

### Sub-question: Progress as power and sacrifice

Nietzsche's inversion: what we call progress is the increase of power in a few at the expense of the many, measured by the greatness of the sacrifice it demands.

## Western canon

### Francis Bacon — *Novum Organum* (1620)

Bacon is the philosophical founder of the modern idea of progress. His central claim: knowledge is cumulative and can advance without limit — but only if built on correct foundations and directed toward the improvement of human life.

> *Book I, §31* — "It is in vain to expect any great progress in the sciences by the superinducing or ingrafting new matters upon old. An instauration must be made from the very foundations, if we do not wish to revolve forever in a circle, making only some slight and contemptible progress."
>
> *Gloss: Genuine progress requires a radical break — a total "instauration" — not incremental addition to a flawed framework. This is the methodological foundation: cumulative knowledge requires a sound starting point.*

> *Book I, §78–79* — "Out of twenty-five centuries, with which the memory and learning of man are conversant, scarcely six can be set apart and selected as fertile in science and favorable to its progress. For there are deserts and wastes in times as in countries … The first cause, then, of such insignificant progress in the sciences, is rightly referred to the small proportion of time which has been favorable thereto."
>
> *Gloss: Progress is not inevitable — it requires favorable conditions. Most of human history has been "deserts and wastes" for science. This is an early diagnosis of the institutional and cultural preconditions for progress.*

> *Book I, §81* — "It is impossible to advance properly in the course when the goal is not properly fixed. But the real and legitimate goal of the sciences is the endowment of human life with new inventions and riches."
>
> *Gloss: The most concentrated statement of Bacon's pragmatic vision. The purpose of knowledge is not contemplation but the improvement of material human existence — "the relief of man's estate." This reorientation from contemplation to power is the philosophical charter of technological civilization.*

> *Book I, §90–93* — "By far the greatest obstacle to the advancement of the sciences … is to be found in men's despair and the idea of impossibility … We must, therefore, disclose and prefix our reasons for not thinking the hope of success improbable, as Columbus, before his wonderful voyage over the Atlantic, gave the reasons of his conviction that new lands and continents might be discovered."
>
> *Gloss: Bacon identifies despair — the belief that knowledge has limits — as the greatest obstacle, countered by the Columbus analogy: unknown continents of knowledge await discovery. Progress is both human project and sacred destiny (citing Daniel's prophecy).*

> *Book I, §128–129* — "The introduction of great inventions appears one of the most distinguished of human actions … for the benefits derived from inventions may extend to mankind in general … We should notice the force, effect, and consequences of inventions, which are nowhere more conspicuous than in those three which were unknown to the ancients; namely, printing, gunpowder, and the compass. For these three have changed the appearance and state of the whole world. … Now the empire of man over things is founded on the arts and sciences alone, for nature is only to be commanded by obeying her."
>
> *Gloss: Printing, gunpowder, and the compass are Bacon's emblematic examples of technology as the engine of history — each remaking civilization more profoundly than any emperor. "Nature is only to be commanded by obeying her" encapsulates progress as mastery through understanding.*

### John Stuart Mill — *On Liberty* (1859)

> *Chapter III* — "A people, it may be, may be progressive for a certain length of time, and then stop: when does it stop? When it ceases to possess individuality. … The despotism of custom with which these nations are threatened is not precisely stationariness. It proscribes singularity, but it does not preclude change, provided all change together. … But we are progressive as well as changeable: we continually make new inventions in mechanical arts, and we keep them until they are again superseded by better."
>
> *Gloss: Mill ties progress to individuality and diversity of experiment; a society that changes only in lockstep is changeable but not progressive.*

### John Dewey — *Democracy and Education* (1916)

Dewey grounds progress in the biological and psychological facts of human development and ties it to democratic education.

> *Chapter 4 — Education as Growth* — "A possibility of continuing progress is opened up by the fact that in learning one act, methods are developed good for use in other situations. Still more important is the fact that the human being acquires a habit of learning. He learns to learn. … The prolongation of human infancy, far from being a weakness, is the biological foundation of social progress: it forces the development of nurture, foresight, and plasticity."
>
> *Gloss: "He learns to learn" — progress is the meta-capacity for continuous growth, rooted in the biological fact of prolonged human infancy. Social progress is not an external imposition but a natural outgrowth of the human condition.*

> *Chapter 21 — Physical and Social Studies* — "The coincidence of the ideal of progress with the advance of science is not a mere coincidence. Before this advance men placed the golden age in remote antiquity. Now they face the future with a firm belief that intelligence properly used can do away with evils once thought inevitable. … Science has familiarized men with the idea of development, taking effect practically in persistent gradual amelioration of the estate of our common humanity."
>
> *Gloss: Science reorients the human imagination from a backward-looking golden age to forward-looking hope. "Amelioration of the estate of our common humanity" consciously echoes and democratizes Bacon's vision.*

> *Chapter 22 — The Individual and the World* — "A society based on custom will utilize individual variations only up to a limit of conformity with usage … A progressive society counts individual variations as precious since it finds in them the means of its own growth. Hence a democratic society must … allow for intellectual freedom and the play of diverse gifts and interests."
>
> *Gloss: Progress, democracy, and individuality are linked: static societies suppress variation; progressive ones cultivate it. Individual differences are the raw material of social adaptation.*

> *Chapter 15 — Play and Work in the Curriculum* — "The most direct blow at the traditional separation of doing and knowing … has been given by the progress of experimental science. If this progress has demonstrated anything, it is that there is no such thing as genuine knowledge and fruitful understanding except as the offspring of doing."
>
> *Gloss: Dewey radicalizes Bacon: knowledge is the "offspring of doing" — active intervention in the world, not passive observation. The laboratory method embodies how genuine knowledge and progress grow.*

> *Chapter 4 (existing)* — "Because the need of preparation for a continually developing life is great, it is imperative that every energy should be bent to making the present experience as rich and significant as possible. … The conception that growth and progress are just approximations to a final unchanging goal is the last infirmity of the mind in its transition from a static to a dynamic understanding of life."
>
> *Gloss: Dewey rejects teleological progress toward a fixed perfection; genuine progress is continuous growth without a final terminus.*

### Friedrich Nietzsche — *On the Genealogy of Morality* (trans. Horace B. Samuel, 1913)

> *Second Essay, §12* — "What I mean is this: even partial loss of utility, decay, and degeneration, loss of function and purpose, in a word, death, appertain to the conditions of the genuine progressus; which always appears in the shape of a will and way to greater power, and is always realised at the expense of innumerable smaller powers. The magnitude of a 'progress' is gauged by the greatness of the sacrifice that it requires: humanity as a mass sacrificed to the prosperity of the one stronger species of Man — that would be a progress."
>
> *Gloss: Nietzsche inverts the entire moral valuation of progress: it is not the betterment of all but the increase of power in the few, measured by the cost to the many. Progress is not liberation but domination — the weak are sacrificed so the strong may advance.*

## Reframings

### Classical Chinese — Tao and Wu Wei

The Western idea of progress as directional, cumulative improvement has no direct analogue in classical Chinese thought. The *Tao Te Ching* and *Zhuangzi* do not ask whether history moves toward a better state; they ask how to align with the spontaneous course of the Tao, which is cyclical and self-correcting. Wu Wei (non-forced action) is the opposite of the progressive will to reshape the world. Progress, in this frame, is a symptom of dis-ease — a restless striving that disrupts the natural order.
