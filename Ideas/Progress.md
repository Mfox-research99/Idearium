# Idea: Progress

## Working definition

Whether human history, knowledge, institutions, or moral character move toward a better state — and if so, by what mechanism, at what cost, and toward what end. The Western tradition has debated progress as cumulative knowledge (Bacon, Condorcet), as moral and social improvement (Mill, Dewey), as an illusion masking the will to power (Nietzsche), and as a theological narrative (Gibbon’s “progress of Christianity”). The question is not whether change occurs, but whether change has direction and value.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Progress as emancipation from custom

Whether progress consists in freeing individuals and societies from the dead hand of inherited practice, and whether such liberation is itself a good.

### Sub-question: Progress as education and growth

Whether the development of human capacities through education is the primary vehicle of progress, and whether growth is an end in itself or a means to a fixed goal.

### Sub-question: Progress as power and sacrifice

Whether what we call progress is actually the increase of power in a few at the expense of the many, and whether the magnitude of progress is measured by the sacrifice it demands.

## Western canon

### John Stuart Mill — *On Liberty*

> *Chapter III* — "A people, it appears, may be progressive for a certain length of time, and then stop: when does it stop? When it ceases to possess individuality. If a similar change should befall the nations of Europe, it will not be in exactly the same shape: the despotism of custom with which these nations are threatened is not precisely stationariness. It proscribes singularity, but it does not preclude change, provided all change together. … But we are progressive as well as changeable: we continually make new inventions in mechanical arts, and we keep them until they are again superseded by better; we are eager for improvement in politics, in education, even in morals, though in this last our idea of improvement is chiefly to persuade others to be as good as we are."
>
> *Gloss: Mill ties progress to individuality and diversity of experiment; a society that changes only in lockstep is changeable but not progressive.*

### John Dewey — *Democracy and Education*

> *Chapter 4* — "Because the need of preparation for a continually developing life is great, it is imperative that every energy should be bent to making the present experience as rich and significant as possible. Then as the present merges insensibly into the future, the future is taken care of. … The conception that growth and progress are just approximations to a final unchanging goal is the last infirmity of the mind in its transition from a static to a dynamic understanding of life."
>
> *Gloss: Dewey rejects teleological progress toward a fixed perfection; genuine progress is continuous growth without a final terminus.*

### Friedrich Nietzsche — *On the Genealogy of Morality*

> *Second Essay, §12* — "What I mean is this: even partial *loss of utility*, decay, and degeneration, loss of function and purpose, in a word, death, appertain to the conditions of the genuine *progressus*; which always appears in the shape of a will and way to *greater* power, and is always realised at the expense of innumerable smaller powers. The magnitude of a 'progress' is gauged by the greatness of the sacrifice that it requires: humanity as a mass sacrificed to the prosperity of the one *stronger* species of Man—that *would be* a progress."
>
> *Gloss: Nietzsche inverts the moral valuation of progress: it is not the betterment of all but the increase of power in the few, measured by the cost to the many. Trans. Samuel, 1913.*

## Reframings

### Classical Chinese — Tao and Wu Wei

The Western idea of progress as directional, cumulative improvement has no direct analogue in classical Chinese thought. The *Tao Te Ching* and *Zhuangzi* do not ask whether history moves toward a better state; they ask how to align with the spontaneous course of the Tao, which is cyclical and self-correcting. Wu Wei (non-forced action) is the opposite of the progressive will to reshape the world. Progress, in this frame, is a symptom of dis-ease — a restless striving that disrupts the natural order.

## Status notes

- The Gibbon passages (“Progress of the Christian Religion,” “Progress of the Huns”) are chapter titles only and contribute no substantive argument; excluded.
- The Nicomachus passage is a Greek lexicon entry for “progression” in arithmetic series; excluded as irrelevant.
- The Jami passage (FitzGerald’s introduction) is a secondary source on racial progress, not Jami’s own voice; excluded. A proper Sufi reframing would require primary material from Rumi or Ibn Arabi on spiritual ascent (mi‘raj) as a non-linear, non-historical progress — not yet in corpus.
- The Nietzsche passage appears in two identical retrievals (different editions); cited once.
- No direct non-Western primary-source passages on progress were found in the corpus. The Reframing section relies on the absence of the concept and points to Tao and Wu Wei as alternative framings. This is honest but thin; a future corpus expansion with Daoist primary sources would strengthen it.
