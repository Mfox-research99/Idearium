# Idea: Mechanics

## Working definition

The study of motion and the forces that produce or resist it, and the broader question of whether all natural phenomena can be explained by such mechanical principles. The Western tradition frames mechanics both as a mathematical science of bodies in motion and as a philosophical stance (mechanism) that reduces causation to efficient, material interactions. Other traditions often do not separate mechanics from a more holistic or teleological view of nature; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Mechanics as a mathematical art — ancient roots

The earliest Western mechanics was a practical art of constructing machines, but also a method for demonstrating geometrical truths through physical models. Plato’s objection that mechanics corrupts geometry by appealing to the senses marks an early boundary between pure and applied knowledge.

### Sub-question: Mechanical vs. dynamical explanation — Leibniz’s reformulation

Leibniz argues that motion alone (change of place) is not fully real; force is the real ground of motion. Mechanical explanation is universal in extent but subordinate in importance to final causes and metaphysical principles.

### Sub-question: The limits of mechanism — vitalism and psychology

Adler and others challenge the absolute character of mechanization, especially in psychology, where renewal and purpose break mechanical patterns.

### Sub-question: Mechanics in education and craft — Kropotkin

Kropotkin sees the decomposition of all machinery into a few elements and motions as the foundation of mechanical handicraft, and advocates teaching this as a basis for integrated manual and intellectual education.

## Western canon

### Plutarch — *Parallel Lives* (Life of Marcellus)

> *Life of Marcellus, c. 75 AD* — "Eudoxus and Archytas had been the first originators of this far-famed and highly prized art of mechanics, which they employed as an elegant illustration of geometrical truths, and as a means of sustaining experimentally, to the satisfaction of the senses, conclusions too intricate for proof by words and diagrams. … But what with Plato's indignation at it, and his invectives against it as the mere corruption and annihilation of the one good of geometry, — which was thus shamefully turning its back upon the unembodied objects of pure intelligence to recur to sensation, and to ask help (not to be obtained without base subservience and depravation) from matter; so it was that mechanics came to be separated from geometry, and, repudiated and neglected by philosophers, took its place as a military art."
>
> *Gloss: Plutarch records the ancient tension between mechanics as a demonstrative aid to geometry and as a mere practical craft, a split Plato is said to have enforced.*

*Citation: Trans. John Dryden, 1683. (Translator not specified in corpus; Dryden is the standard early English version.)*

### Leibniz — *Discourse on Metaphysics*

> *Discourse on Metaphysics, §18 (c. 1686)* — "Because motion, if we regard only its exact and formal meaning, that is, change of place, is not something entirely real, and when several bodies change their places reciprocally, it is not possible to determine by considering the bodies alone to which among them movement or repose is to be attributed, as I could demonstrate geometrically, if I wished to stop for it now. But the force, or the proximate cause of these changes is something more real, and there are sufficient grounds for attributing it to one body rather than to another, and it is only through this latter investigation that we can determine to which one the movement must appertain. Now this force is something different from size, from form or from motion, and it can be seen from this consideration that the whole meaning of a body is not exhausted in its extension together with its modifications as our moderns persuade themselves. We are therefore obliged to restore certain beings or forms which they have banished. It appears more and more clear that although all the particular phenomena of nature can be explained mathematically or mechanically by those who understand them, …"
>
> *Gloss: Leibniz insists that mechanical explanation (motion, extension) is incomplete without the concept of force, which is real and metaphysical, and that final causes are not eliminated by mechanism.*

*Citation: Trans. George R. Montgomery, 1902.*

### Leibniz — *The Monadology and Other Philosophical Writings*

> *From the translator's introduction (Latta, 1898)* — "The function of mechanism in the construction of the world is, without an exception, universal in its extent. … But Lotze protests strongly against the view that mechanism gives us a final explanation of the reality of the world. The laws of science are laws of phenomena; they do not account for the things themselves. … Thus while ‘the function of mechanism in the construction of the world is universal in its extent, it is entirely subordinate in its importance’."
>
> *Gloss: Leibniz (via Latta's exposition) affirms the universality of mechanical law in the phenomenal realm while denying it ultimate explanatory power — a position that preserves teleology.*

*Citation: Trans. Robert Latta, 1898.*

### Adler — *Practice and Theory of Individual Psychology*

> *Chapter on "The Problem of the Psychology of Life" (1924)* — "For the process of renewal is in essence an overcoming of mechanization. In the field of psychology we see mechanical paths perpetually form and existent mechanism break up as we do not see in other fields of science. In psychology the postulate of the absolute character of mechanization has no validity. Here Giordano’s vision becomes reality. … The principle of purpose and use that underlies the creation of new symbols …"
>
> *Gloss: Adler argues that psychological life cannot be reduced to mechanical laws; renewal and purpose continually break mechanical patterns, a claim that limits the scope of mechanism.*

*Citation: Trans. P. Radin, 1924.*

### Kropotkin — *Fields, Factories and Workshops*

> *Chapter on "Manual Work" (1913)* — "Theoretische Kinematik, that there is, so to say, a philosophy of all possible machinery. Each machine, however complicated, can be reduced to a few elements — plates, cylinders, discs, cones, and so on — as well as to a few tools — chisels, saws, rollers, hammers, etc.; and, however complicated its movements, they can be decomposed into a few modifications of motion, such as the transformation of circular motion into a rectilinear, and the like, with a number of intermediate links. … This is the foundation of all mechanical handicrafts; so that the knowledge of how to make in wood those primary elements, how to manage the chief tools in wood-work, and how to transform various kinds of motion ought to be considered as the very basis for the subsequent teaching of all possible kinds of mechanical handicraft."
>
> *Gloss: Kropotkin presents mechanics as a universal grammar of motion and tools, advocating its teaching as the foundation of integrated manual and intellectual education.*

*Citation: Original English, 1913.*

## Reframings

### Chinese thought — Tao and Wu Wei

The Western concept of mechanics as a system of efficient causes operating on inert matter has no direct equivalent in classical Chinese thought. The Daoist tradition understands natural order as spontaneous and unforced (wu wei), not as a mechanism imposed from outside. The *Tao Te Ching* describes the Dao as the way things happen without deliberate action — a kind of organic regularity that is neither mechanical nor teleological in the Western sense. Mechanical explanation would be seen as an artificial abstraction from the living whole.

→ See [[Terms/Tao]], [[Terms/Wu_Wei]]

### Indian thought — Karma

The law of karma, understood as the moral and cosmic causation of actions and their fruits, might superficially resemble a mechanical law of cause and effect. However, karma is inseparable from ethical intention and the cycle of rebirth; it is not a blind physical force but a principle of justice and spiritual development. The Western separation of mechanics from ethics and metaphysics does not hold in this framework.
