# Idea: Agape

## Working definition

A Greek term for selfless, unconditional love — distinct from *eros* (desiring love) and *philia* (brotherly love). In the Christian tradition, *agape* becomes the highest form of love: the love of God for humanity, and the corresponding love of neighbor that flows from it. The Western canon asks whether such love is primarily an affection of the will, a communal practice, or a metaphysical force that draws the soul toward unity with God. The question is framed from within a theological and philosophical lineage that runs from Plato through Augustine to medieval mysticism and Enlightenment critique.

## Sub-questions

### Agape as Divine Love

How does selfless love relate to the will, to grace, and to the soul’s union with God?

### Agape as Communal Practice

The early Christian *agapē* (love-feast) as a ritual of fellowship — and the historical suspicions it attracted.

### Agape vs. Eros

The contrast between desiring love and self-giving love, and the question of whether they are ultimately distinct or convergent.

## Western canon

### Plato — *Symposium*

> *189d–193a* — “…this meeting and melting into one another, this becoming one instead of two, was the very expression of his ancient need… And the reason is that human nature was originally one and we were a whole, and the desire and pursuit of the whole is called love.”
>
> *Gloss: Plato’s Aristophanes presents love as the desire to recover an original wholeness — a longing that later Christian thought would reinterpret as the soul’s yearning for God, though Plato names it *eros*, not *agape*.*

### Augustine — *City of God*

> *Book X, ch. 3* — “To Him we offer the sweetest incense when we come before Him burning with holy and pious love… to Him we offer on the altar of our heart the sacrifice of humility and praise, kindled by the fire of burning love. It is that we may see Him, so far as He can be seen; it is that we may cleave to Him, that we are cleansed from all stain of sins and evil passions, and are consecrated in His name.”
>
> *Gloss: Augustine identifies true love (*amor*/*caritas*) with the soul’s cleaving to God, purifying the heart and making it a living altar — a love that is both gift and sacrifice.*

### Augustine — *City of God*

> *Book XIV, ch. 7* — “Some are of opinion that charity or regard (*dilectio*) is one thing, love (*amor*) another. They say that *dilectio* is used of a good affection, *amor* of an evil love. But it is very certain that even secular literature knows no such distinction… we wished to show that the Scriptures… make no distinction.”
>
> *Gloss: Augustine rejects a sharp lexical separation between *amor* and *dilectio*, insisting that the same word can refer to either good or evil love depending on the object — a move that anticipates the Christian appropriation of *agape* as the highest form of love without abandoning the term *amor*.*

### Eckhart — *Meister Eckhart Sermons*

> *Sermon on Love* — “This love lies wholly in the will: the stronger the will the deeper the love. Then there is the other thing, the outcome and effect of love, clearly apparent in the guise of spirituality, devotion, jubilation, and which, truth to tell, is not always of the highest… Suppose, again, it is entirely love, it will not for all that be the highest of the kind, and jubilation of this sort may well be left at times for something better in the way of love, perhaps to do some needed act of kindness.”
>
> *Gloss: Eckhart locates the essence of love in the will, not in emotional consolation; the highest love is expressed in practical charity, not in spiritual sweetness.*

### Voltaire — *Philosophical Dictionary*

> *“Agape”* — “Initiates kissed each other at the mysteries of Ceres, as a sign of concord. The early Christians, men and women, kissed each other on the mouth at their *agapae*. This word signified ‘love-feast.’ They gave each other the holy kiss, the kiss of peace… This custom lasted for more than four centuries, and was abolished at last on account of its consequences.”
>
> *Gloss: Voltaire treats the *agapē* as a historical practice that attracted scandal; his tone is skeptical, reducing the sacred kiss to a custom whose abolition was prudent.*

### Krafft-Ebing — *Psychopathia Sexualis*

> *Introductory* — “Religious love strives for the possession of an object that is absolutely ideal, and cannot be defined by experimental knowledge. Both [sexual love and religious love] are metaphysical processes which give unlimited scope to imagination… they converge, however, in a similar indefinite focus… But because neither of them can at times grasp the real object of their existence they easily degenerate into fanaticism.”
>
> *Gloss: Krafft-Ebing draws a structural parallel between sexual and religious love, noting that both can become fanatical when the object is unattainable — a modern psychological reframing of the *agape*/eros distinction.*

## Reframings

### Hebrew — *Hesed*

The Hebrew term *hesed* (covenant loyalty, steadfast love) overlaps with *agape* in its emphasis on unconditional commitment, but it is grounded in a concrete historical covenant between God and Israel rather than in a metaphysical or mystical union. *Hesed* is less about the soul’s ascent and more about fidelity within a relationship that includes law, community, and divine promise.

→ See [[Terms/Hesed]]

### Sufi — *Ishq*

In the Sufi tradition, divine love (*ishq*) is a passionate, annihilating longing for the Beloved that dissolves the self. This differs from *agape*’s emphasis on will and charity; *ishq* is closer to *eros* in its intensity, yet it is directed entirely toward God. The boundary between *agape* and *ishq* is porous, but the Western canon tends to separate them more sharply than the Sufi tradition does.

→ See [[Terms/Ishq]]

### Buddhist — *Karuna*

Buddhist compassion (*karuna*) is the wish to free all beings from suffering, cultivated through meditation and ethical practice. It is not grounded in a personal God or in a will-to-union, but in the recognition of interdependence and the absence of a fixed self. *Karuna* is active and universal, like *agape*, but its metaphysical basis is radically different.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Eros]] — the counterpart and contrast; the Western tradition often pairs *agape* and *eros* as two modes of love, one self-giving, one desiring.
- [[Love]] — the broader Idea under which *agape* is a species; the English term “love” collapses distinctions the Greek tradition kept separate.
- [[Will]] — *agape* is located in the will by Augustine and Eckhart, not primarily in emotion.
- [[God]] — the ultimate object of *agape* in the Christian tradition; the love of God is both the source and the goal.
- [[Ritual]] — the *agapē* as a communal practice connects *agape* to liturgy and social bonding.
