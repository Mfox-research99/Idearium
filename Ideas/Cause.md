# Idea: Cause

## Working definition

What brings something about—the relation between an event or entity and that which produces it. The Western tradition distinguishes four types of cause (material, formal, efficient, final) and debates whether causation is a necessary connection or a constant conjunction. Other traditions reframe causation through interdependence, correlative resonance, or moral retribution; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: The four causes (Aristotelian framework)

What are the distinct senses in which one thing can be said to be the cause of another—the matter it is made of, the form it takes, the agent that produces it, and the end for which it exists?

### Sub-question: Efficient cause and the first cause

Can the chain of efficient causes be infinite, or must there be a first cause? (Aquinas)

### Sub-question: The critique of necessary connection

Is causation a real power or merely a habit of expectation based on constant conjunction? (Hume)

## Western canon

### Aristotle — *Posterior Analytics*

> *Book II, Part 17* — "Thus, B is the cause of A's inherence in the species of D: consequently A must be of wider extent than B; otherwise why should B be the cause of A's inherence in D any more than A the cause of B's inherence in D? … We conclude, then, that the same effect may have more than one cause, but not in subjects specifically identical. For instance, the cause of longevity in quadrupeds is lack of bile, in birds a dry constitution—or certainly something different."
>
> *Gloss: Aristotle establishes that causation is a relation between a universal attribute and a subject, and that the same effect can have different causes in different species—a point that resists reduction to a single causal formula.*

### Thomas Aquinas — *Summa Theologica*

> *First Part, Question 2, Article 3* — "The second way is from the nature of the efficient cause. In the world of sense we find there is an order of efficient causes. There is no case known (neither is it, indeed, possible) in which a thing is found to be the efficient cause of itself; for so it would be prior to itself, which is impossible. Now in efficient causes it is not possible to go on to infinity, because in all efficient causes following in order, the first is the cause of the intermediate cause, and the intermediate is the cause of the ultimate cause, whether the intermediate be several or one only. … Therefore it is necessary to admit a first efficient cause, to which everyone gives the name of God."
>
> *Gloss: Aquinas argues that the chain of efficient causes cannot regress infinitely; a first, uncaused cause must exist, and this is identified with God.*

### David Hume — *A Treatise of Human Nature*

> *Book I, Part III, Section III* — "It is exactly the same case with the third argument, which has been employed to demonstrate the necessity of a cause. Whatever is produced without any cause, is produced by nothing; or in other words, has nothing for its cause. But nothing can never be a cause, no more than it can be something, or equal to two right angles. … I believe it will not be necessary to employ many words in shewing the weakness of this argument, after what I have said of the foregoing. They are all of them founded on the same fallacy, and are derived from the same turn of thought. It is sufficient only to observe, that when we exclude all causes we really do exclude them, and neither suppose nothing nor the object itself to be the causes of the existence; and consequently can draw no argument from the absurdity of these suppositions to prove the absurdity of that exclusion."
>
> *Gloss: Hume attacks the rationalist claim that every event must have a cause, arguing that the proposition is neither intuitively nor demonstratively certain—causation is a matter of experience, not a priori necessity.*

## Reframings

### Buddhist — Dependent Origination (Pratītyasamutpāda)

The Buddhist tradition reframes causation as a web of interdependent conditions (idappaccayatā) rather than a linear chain of efficient causes. Nothing arises from a single cause; every phenomenon is conditioned by a multiplicity of prior factors. This reframing rejects both a first cause and a substantial self that could be an agent of causation. The term [[Terms/Sunyata]] (emptiness) captures the absence of inherent existence that follows from this causal interdependence.

### Chinese — Correlative Cosmology (Gǎn-yìng)

Classical Chinese thought does not separate cause from effect as discrete events. Causation is understood as resonance (gǎn-yìng) within a patterned cosmos—things influence each other because they belong to the same category or share the same qi. The [[Terms/Tao]] is the overarching order that makes such resonance possible, not a first cause. The Western question "what caused the universe?" is reframed as "how does the Tao pattern itself?"

### Hindu — Karma as Moral Causation

The concept of [[Terms/Karma]] treats causation as a moral law: actions produce consequences that shape future circumstances across lifetimes. This is not merely efficient causation (an act producing a result) but also a kind of retributive justice built into the fabric of reality. The Western distinction between physical and moral causation is blurred; every event has a moral dimension.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Necessity_and_Contingency]] — whether causal relations are necessary or contingent is the central Humean challenge.
- [[Change]] — causation is the principle that explains change; Aristotle's four causes are an analysis of change.
- [[God]] — the cosmological argument for God's existence depends on the impossibility of an infinite regress of efficient causes.
- [[Science]] — modern science assumes efficient causation as its primary explanatory framework.
- [[Metaphysics]] — the nature of causation is a foundational question in Western metaphysics.
