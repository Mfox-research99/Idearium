# Idea: Sign_and_Symbol

## Working definition

What distinguishes a sign from a symbol, and how do both mediate meaning? The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about representation, reference, and interpretation — whether signs are conventional or motivated, whether symbols participate in what they signify or merely point to it. Astrology, alchemy, and depth psychology have each developed distinct theories of symbolic meaning that resist reduction to mere semiotics.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Sign vs. Symbol

The distinction between a conventional sign (e.g., a word, a numeral) and a symbol that carries deeper, often unconscious or cosmic significance.

### Sub-question: Astrological Signs

The zodiacal signs as a system of correspondences linking celestial configurations to terrestrial events, character, and fate.

### Sub-question: Symbolic Interpretation

How symbols are read — whether as allegories to be decoded, or as living images that transform the interpreter.

## Western canon

### Manilius — *Astronomica*

> *Book 2, lines 150–230 (approx.)* — "Signs, 45, 4+ Male and Female Signs: | 5. Human and Brute Signs. 6. Single and Double Signs. 7. Pairs. 8. Double Signs made up of different Species. 9. Signs Double by Place, viz. Those that immediately precede the Four Tropick Signs. 10. Signs of Natural or Unnatural Postures. 11. Day and Night Signs. 12. Earth and Water Signs. 13. Fruitful and Barren Signs. 14. Signs of different Postures. 15. Maim'd and intire Signs. 16. Season Signs. 17. He sings the various Configurations or aspects of the Signs: As, 18. Trines. 19. Quadrates or Squares; shews what are to be accounted Right and what Left in these Figures: And, 20. Adds several Cautions concerning Squares and Trines. 21, He describes the Intercourse or Agreement of Trines and Quadrates. 22. Of Hexagons or Sextiles, of which he gives a particular Account. 23. Of Contiguous Signs. 24. Of Unequal Signs, 25. Of Opposites. 26. He shews what Gods are the Guardians of each Sign. 27: The Signs for the several parts of the Body, 28. What Signs See, Hear, Love, or Hate each other. 29. He makes a short Digression about Friendship. 30. He treats of the Friendly and Unfriendly Aspects..."
>
> *Gloss: Manilius catalogues the zodiacal signs as a complex system of binary and analogical properties — male/female, human/brute, fruitful/barren — treating each sign as a node in a network of correspondences that govern astrological interpretation.*

### William Lilly — *Christian Astrology*

> *Chapter on "The Nature of the Signes"* — "Movable Signes, as ♈︎ ♎︎ ♋︎ ♑︎, conduce to such Arts or professions as require a witty capacity, in regard they are directed by contemplation of the Wit, viz. Geometry, Physick, Astrology, &c. Fixed Signes doe also require Learning or Education, except ♌︎. Humane Signes, viz. ♊︎ ♍︎ ♒︎ ♐︎, as they signifie humane affections, so doe they import such proper Arts unto a man as are liberall, and have especiall use in the life of man, and are taught with Authority. Watry and Earthly Signes as ♏︎ ♓︎ ♉︎ ♍︎, shewes professions conversant about the Water and Earth, as upon Meadows, Herbes, Medicine, Ships, Fish or Funerals. Fourfooted Signes, as ♈︎ ♌︎ ♐︎, shew Mechanicall professions, Butchers, Graziers, Builders, Curriers, Cutters of Stones, Diggers of Stones, Clothiers, Wool-winders."
>
> *Gloss: Lilly treats the zodiacal signs as a classificatory grid for human professions and aptitudes — each sign's elemental and modal nature determines the kind of work it signifies, making the sign a practical diagnostic tool.*

### H.P. Blavatsky — *The Secret Doctrine, Vol. 1*

> *Proem* — "As a learned Mason and Theosophist, the late Kenneth Mackenzie, has shown in his *Royal Masonic Cyclopædia*, there is a great difference between *emblem* and *symbol*. The former 'comprises a larger series of thoughts than a symbol, which may be said rather to illustrate some single special idea.' Hence, the symbols—lunar, or solar, for example—of several countries, each illustrating such a special idea, or series of ideas, form collectively an esoteric emblem. The latter is 'a concrete visible picture or sign representing principles, or a series of principles, *recognizable by those who have received certain instructions* [Initiates].' To put it still plainer, an emblem is usually *a series of graphic pictures* viewed and explained allegorically, and unfolding an idea in panoramic views, one after the other. Thus the *Purânas* are written emblems. So are the Mosaic and Christian *Testaments*, or the *Bible*, and all other exoteric Scriptures."
>
> *Gloss: Blavatsky, drawing on Masonic sources, distinguishes emblem (a panoramic series of allegorical pictures) from symbol (a single concrete sign whose meaning is reserved for initiates), asserting that exoteric scriptures are emblems while esoteric knowledge uses symbols.*

### C.G. Jung — *Analytical Psychology* (1916)

> *Author's Preface to First Edition* — "The Zurich School recognises the scientific feasibility of a conception, but denies its exclusive validity, for it does not interpret the psychological symbol semiotically only, but also symbolistically, that is, it attributes a positive value to the symbol. The value does not depend merely on historical causes; its chief importance lies in the fact that it has a meaning for the actual present, and for the future, in their psychological aspects. For to the Zurich School the symbol is not merely a sign of something repressed and concealed, but is at the same time an attempt to comprehend and to point out the way of the further psychological development of the individual. Thus we add a prospective import to the retrospective value of the symbol."
>
> *Gloss: Jung insists that the psychological symbol is not a mere sign (semiotic) pointing backward to repressed content, but a living image with prospective meaning — it guides future development and cannot be exhaustively decoded.*

### C.G. Jung — *Analytical Psychology* (1916)

> *Author's Preface to First Edition* — "The functional importance of the symbol is clearly shown in the history of civilisation. For thousands of years the religious symbol proved a most efficacious means in the moral education of mankind. Only a prejudiced mind could deny such an obvious fact. Concrete values cannot take the place of the symbol; only new and more efficient symbols can be substituted for those that are antiquated and outworn, such as have lost their efficacy through the progress of intellectual analysis and understanding. The further development of mankind can only be brought about by means of symbols which represent something far in advance of himself, and whose intellectual meanings cannot yet be grasped entirely. The individual unconscious produces such symbols, and they are of the greatest possible value in the moral development of the personality."
>
> *Gloss: Jung argues that symbols are irreplaceable for moral and cultural development — they outrun intellectual comprehension and cannot be replaced by concrete values, only by newer, more potent symbols.*

## Reframings

### Chinese — The Yijing (Book of Changes) and the notion of *xiang* (image/symbol)

The Chinese tradition, especially in the *Yijing*, treats the hexagram lines as *xiang* (象) — images that are not arbitrary signs but condensations of dynamic cosmic patterns. A hexagram is both a sign of a situation and a participatory symbol that, through divination, actively shapes the outcome. This collapses the Western sign/symbol distinction into a single operative category: the image that is also an event. The term *xiang* does not map neatly onto either "sign" or "symbol" in English.

→ See [[Terms/Yin-Yang]] (for the correlative cosmology that grounds *xiang*)

### Indian — The *bija mantra* as sonic symbol

In Tantric and Vedantic traditions, the *bija mantra* (seed syllable) such as *Om* is not a sign standing for something else but a vibrational embodiment of the deity or cosmic principle itself. The symbol is the reality, not a representation. This challenges the Western semiotic assumption that signs are always separate from their referents.
