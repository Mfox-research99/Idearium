# Idea: Constitution

## Working definition

What is the fundamental law that establishes the framework of government, distributes political powers, and limits authority. The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about a written or unwritten supreme law that binds both rulers and citizens, defines the organs of the state, and stands above ordinary legislation. The question of who holds the authority to constitute — and to alter — that fundamental law runs through the entire tradition. Other traditions reframe the question through divine covenant, cosmic order, or the constituted community of believers; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions (optional)

### Sub-question: The constitution as fundamental pact

What makes a constitution binding, and on whom? The tradition distinguishes the constituting act of the people from the ordinary operations of government.

### Sub-question: Constitutional supremacy and judicial enforcement

If the constitution is supreme law, who guards its boundaries against legislative or executive encroachment? The American tradition answers with judicial review; the European tradition has often answered with political safeguards.

### Sub-question: Written vs. unwritten constitutions

Is a constitution necessarily a single written document, or can it be the settled body of fundamental laws, customs, and precedents that define a regime?

## Western canon

### Jean-Jacques Rousseau — *Discourse on the Origin of Inequality*

> "The multitude having, on occasion of the social relations between them, concentered all their wills in one person, all the articles, in regard to which this will explains itself, become so many fundamental laws, which oblige without exception all the members of the state, and one of which laws regulates the choice and the power of the magistrates appointed to look to the execution of the rest. This power extends to everything that can maintain the constitution, but extends to nothing that can alter it."
>
> *Gloss: Rousseau commits to the constitution as a set of fundamental laws arising from the concentration of all wills, binding every member of the state, and to the principle that constituted magistrates may maintain but never alter the constitutional order — the constituting act belongs to the people alone.*

— Part II, trans. G. D. H. Cole, 1913.

### Publius (Hamilton, Madison, Jay) — *Federalist Papers*

> "If, then, the courts of justice are to be considered as the bulwarks of a limited Constitution against legislative encroachments, this consideration will afford a strong argument for the permanent tenure of judicial offices, since nothing will contribute so much as this to that independent spirit in the judges which must be essential to the faithful performance of so arduous a duty."
>
> *Gloss: Hamilton (No. 78) commits the American constitutional tradition to judicial review as the mechanism for enforcing constitutional limits on legislative power, grounding the authority of courts in their independence from popular and political pressure.*

— Alexander Hamilton, *Federalist* No. 78, 1788.

> "Nothing need be said to illustrate the importance of the prohibition of titles of nobility. This may truly be denominated the corner-stone of republican government; for so long as they are excluded, there can never be serious danger that the government will be any other than that of the people. … bills of rights are, in their origin, stipulations between kings and their subjects, abridgements of prerogative in favor of privilege … under our constitutions must be intended as limitations of the power of the government itself."
>
> *Gloss: Hamilton (No. 84) commits to the constitution as a set of affirmative limitations on governmental power, distinguishing the American bill of rights from the English tradition of grants from the crown.*

— Alexander Hamilton, *Federalist* No. 84, 1788.

> "In the first place, as these constitutions invest the State legislatures with absolute sovereignty, in all cases not excepted by the existing articles of Confederation, all the authorities contained in the proposed Constitution, so far as they exceed those enumerated in the Confederation, would have been annulled, and the new Congress would have been reduced to the same impotent condition with their predecessors."
>
> *Gloss: Hamilton (No. 33) commits to the supremacy of the federal Constitution over state constitutions as a necessary condition for effective national government — a hierarchy of fundamental law.*

— Alexander Hamilton, *Federalist* No. 33, 1788.

### Alexis de Tocqueville — *Democracy in America*

> "An American constitution is not supposed to be immutable as in France, nor is it susceptible of modification by the ordinary powers of society as in England. It constitutes a detached whole, which, as it represents the determination of the whole people, is no less binding on the legislator than on the private citizen, but which may be altered by the will of the people in predetermined cases, according to established rules. In America the constitution may therefore vary, but as long as it exists it is the origin of all authority, and the sole vehicle of the predominating force."
>
> *Gloss: Tocqueville identifies the American constitution as a distinct genre — neither immutable nor subject to ordinary legislative change, but a detached whole representing the people's will, alterable only through pre-established procedures.*

— Vol. I, ch. 8, trans. Henry Reeve, 1835.

> "The general principles which are the groundwork of modern constitutions—principles which were imperfectly known in Europe, and not completely triumphant even in Great Britain, in the seventeenth century—were all recognized and determined by the laws of New England: the intervention of the people in public affairs, the free voting of taxes, the responsibility of authorities, personal liberty, and trial by jury, were all positively established without discussion."
>
> *Gloss: Tocqueville traces the substantive content of modern constitutionalism — popular participation, taxation by consent, official accountability, individual liberty, jury trial — to the colonial laws of New England, arguing that these principles were operational there before they were theorized in Europe.*

— Vol. I, ch. 2, trans. Henry Reeve, 1835.

## Reframings

### Hebrew — Covenant (Brit)

The Hebrew tradition does not ask "who constitutes the state" but "who enters into covenant with whom, and under what divine law." The constitution is not a human artifact but a *brit* — a binding agreement between God and the people, and among the people themselves, that establishes law, obligation, and community prior to any political institution. The covenantal frame shifts the question from sovereignty to fidelity: the people are constituted not by an act of will but by a promise.

→ See [[Terms/Covenant]]

### Islamic — Umma

The Islamic tradition conceives the political community (*umma*) as constituted by submission to divine law (*sharia*) rather than by a social contract among individuals. The constitution is not a written document drafted by a constituent assembly but the revealed will of God as interpreted through the scholarly tradition. The question is not "what form of government do we establish" but "how do we order our collective life in accordance with the divine law that already binds us."

→ See [[Terms/Umma]]

### Chinese — Tao

The Daoist tradition reframes the question of constitution entirely: a well-ordered society does not need a written constitution because it follows the *Tao* — the spontaneous, unforced way of nature. The attempt to codify fundamental law is itself a symptom of the disorder it purports to cure. The constitution, in this view, is not a human document but the cosmic-natural order that underlies all governance; the wise ruler aligns the community with that order rather than imposing a human framework.

→ See [[Terms/Tao]]

## Cross-cuts

- [[Government]] — the constitution establishes the form, distribution, and limits of governmental powers
- [[Law]] — the constitution as fundamental law, superior to ordinary legislation and binding on all
- [[State]] — the constitution constitutes the state as a political entity with defined organs and jurisdiction
- [[Democracy]] — the constitution provides the framework within which democratic self-governance operates, while also limiting the reach of popular majorities
- [[Liberty]] — the constitution as a bulwark of liberty against governmental encroachment, especially through bills of rights and judicial review
- [[Covenant]] — the Hebrew covenantal tradition as a theological antecedent to Western constitutionalism, reframing the source of binding law from popular will to divine promise
