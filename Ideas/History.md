# Idea: History

## Working definition

What is the human past, how do we know it, and what is it for? The Western tradition has asked this as a question about method (is history a science or a narrative?), about subject (great men or common life?), and about purpose (moral instruction, social understanding, or critique of power). Other traditions frame historical consciousness differently — through dynastic cycles, moral exemplars, or prophetic time — and these reframings are noted below.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Definition and method

What distinguishes history from fable, and what are the foundations of historical knowledge?

### Sub-question: History as social education

How does historical knowledge serve present social life — as moral instruction, as context for democratic citizenship, or as an organ of social intelligence?

### Sub-question: Critique of traditional historiography

Who is left out of conventional history — the common people, mutual aid, the life of nations beyond monarchs and generals?

### Sub-question: Economic and industrial history

What does a history centered on work, invention, and the conditions of daily life reveal that political history obscures?

## Western canon

### Voltaire — *Philosophical Dictionary*

> *"History"* — "History is the recital of facts given as true, in contradistinction to the fable, which is the recital of facts given as false. … The history of the arts can be the most useful of all when it joins to the knowledge of the invention and the progress of the arts the description of their mechanism. … The first foundations of all history are the recitals of the fathers to the children, transmitted afterward from one generation to another; at their origin they are at the very most probable, when they do not shock common sense…"
>
> *Gloss: Voltaire defines history by its truth-claim against fable, distinguishes sacred from profane history, and locates the most useful history in the arts and mechanical progress — an Enlightenment confidence in reason and utility.*

### Leo Tolstoy — *War and Peace*

> *Epilogue, Part I, Chapter IV* — "And the history of the Godfreys and the Minnesingers has remained the history of Godfreys and Minnesingers, but the history of the life of the peoples and their impulses has remained unknown. … The history of culture explains to us the impulses and conditions of life and thought of a writer or a reformer. We learn that Luther had a hot temper and said such and such things; we learn that Rousseau was suspicious and wrote such and such books; but we do not learn why after the Reformation the peoples massacred one another, nor why during the French Revolution they guillotined one another. … The life of the nations is not contained in the lives of a few men, for the connection between those men and the nations has not been found."
>
> *Gloss: Tolstoy rejects both great-man history and cultural-intellectual history as failing to explain the actual life and impulses of peoples — a radical critique of the historian's conventional subject.*

### Peter Kropotkin — *Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution*

> *Introduction* — "Leaving aside the preconceived ideas of most historians and their pronounced predilection for the dramatic aspects of history, we see that the very documents they habitually peruse are such as to exaggerate the part of human life given to struggles and to underrate its peaceful moods. … They hand down to posterity the most minute descriptions of every war, every battle and skirmish, every contest and act of violence, every kind of individual suffering; but they hardly bear any trace of the countless acts of mutual support and devotion which every one of us knows from his own experience; they hardly take notice of what makes the very essence of our daily life—our social instincts and manners."
>
> *Gloss: Kropotkin indicts historiography for its bias toward conflict and its neglect of mutual aid — the cooperative substrate of daily life that the historical record systematically omits.*

### John Dewey — *Democracy and Education*

> *Chapter 17: History and Geography* — "The function of historical and geographical subject matter has been stated; it is to enrich and liberate the more direct and personal contacts of life by furnishing their context, their background and outlook. While geography emphasizes the physical side and history the social, these are only emphases in a common topic, namely, the associated life of men."
>
> *Gloss: Dewey frames history as the context for present social experience — not a dead record but an organ of social intelligence that makes the present intelligible.*

> *Chapter 17* — "The segregation which kills the vitality of history is divorce from present modes and concerns of social life. The past just as past is no longer our affair. … But knowledge of the past is the key to understanding the present. History deals with the past, but this past is the history of the present. … Genetic method was perhaps the chief scientific achievement of the latter half of the nineteenth century. Its principle is that the way to get insight into any complex product is to trace the process of its making."
>
> *Gloss: Dewey insists on the genetic method — history as the study of how the present came to be, not antiquarianism. The past is only intelligible as the history of the present.*

> *Chapter 17* — "Economic history is more human, more democratic, and hence more liberalizing than political history. It deals not with the rise and fall of principalities and powers, but with the growth of the common life in its productive and distributive activities. … We do not need to go into disputes regarding the economic interpretation of history to realize that the industrial history of mankind gives insight into two important phases of social life in a way which no other phase of history can possibly do. It presents us with knowledge of the successive inventions by which theoretical science has been applied to the control of nature in the interests of security and prosperity of social life. It thus reveals the successive causes of social progress."
>
> *Gloss: Dewey champions economic and industrial history as more democratic and more explanatory of social progress than political history — a pragmatic, progressive view of historical knowledge.*

> *Chapter 17* — "Historical knowledge helps provide such insight. It is an organ for analysis of the warp and woof of the present social fabric, of making known the forces which have woven the pattern. The use of history for cultivating a socialized intelligence constitutes its moral significance. … The assistance which may be given by history to a more intelligent sympathetic understanding of the social situations of the present in which individuals share is a permanent and constructive moral asset."
>
> *Gloss: History's moral value is not in anecdotal lessons but in developing socialized intelligence — understanding the present through its genetic development.*

## Reframings

### Chinese tradition — Historiography as moral judgment

The Chinese historiographical tradition (Sima Qian, *Records of the Grand Historian*, c. 94 BCE; the *Spring and Autumn Annals* attributed to Confucius) treats history primarily as a vehicle for moral judgment and the rectification of names. The historian's task is to record events accurately so that praise and blame fall where they are due — a function that merges history with ethics and statecraft. This tradition does not separate historical inquiry from moral-political education in the way the Western Enlightenment does. No passages from this tradition were returned by the corpus; the reframing is noted here as a known gap.

### Islamic tradition — Ibn Khaldun's cyclical history

The *Muqaddimah* of Ibn Khaldun (1377) offers a theory of history as the rise and fall of dynasties driven by *asabiyyah* (group solidarity) — a cyclical, sociological model that anticipates modern historical materialism. The corpus does not contain this work in pre-1929 translation; the reframing is noted as a gap.
