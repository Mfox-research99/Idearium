# Idea: Time

## Working definition

What is time — a real dimension of the cosmos, a subjective form of intuition, or a measure of change? The Western tradition asks this question through physics, metaphysics, psychology, and economics: whether time is created or eternal, linear or cyclical, objective or mind-dependent, and how it structures labor, history, and salvation. The framing begins with Augustine’s confession of ignorance and runs through Kant’s transcendental aesthetic to Marx’s critique of the working day.

## Sub-questions

- **Time as created vs. eternal** — Does time begin with the world, or is it co-eternal with God?
- **Time as measure** — Is time the measure of motion (Locke) or a pure form of intuition (Kant)?
- **Time as resource** — How does capitalism extract value by controlling labor time?
- **Time as season** — The poetic and liturgical framing of time as a cycle of appointed times.

## Western canon

### Ecclesiastes (Hebrew Bible)

> *Ecclesiastes 3:1–8* — "To every thing there is a season, and a time to every purpose under the heaven: A time to be born, and a time to die; a time to plant, and a time to pluck up that which is planted; a time to kill, and a time to heal; a time to break down, and a time to build up; a time to weep, and a time to laugh; a time to mourn, and a time to dance; a time to cast away stones, and a time to gather stones together; a time to embrace, and a time to refrain from embracing; a time to get, and a time to lose; a time to keep, and a time to cast away; a time to rend, and a time to sew; a time to keep silence, and a time to speak; a time to love, and a time to hate; a time of war, and a time of peace."
>
> *Gloss: Time is not a uniform medium but a divinely appointed order of opposites, each with its proper moment — a cyclical, liturgical view that resists reduction to mere duration.*

### Augustine — *Confessions*

> *Book XI, Chapter 14* — "What then is time? If no one asks me, I know: if I wish to explain it to one that asketh, I know not: yet I say boldly that I know, that if nothing passed away, time past were not; and if nothing were coming, a time to come were not; and if nothing were, time present were not. Those two times then, past and to come, how are they, when even the past now is not; and the future is not as yet? But if the present were always present, and should not pass into time past, it were not time, but eternity."
>
> *Gloss: Augustine establishes the central puzzle: only the present exists, yet it is vanishing; past and future have no being. Time is a distention of the soul, not a property of the world.*

### Augustine — *City of God*

> *Book XI, Chapter 6* — "For we can reasonably say there was a time when Rome was not; there was a time when Jerusalem was not; there was a time when Abraham was not; there was a time when man was not, and so on: in fine, if the world was not made at the commencement of time, but after some time had elapsed, we can say there was a time when the world was not. But to say there was a time when time was not, is as absurd as to say there was a man when there was no man; or, this world was when this world was not. … As, then, we say that time was created, though we also say that it always has been, since in all time time has been, so it does not follow that if the angels have always been, they were therefore not created."
>
> *Gloss: Time is created with the world; there was no "before" time because time itself began. This resolves the puzzle of a temporal creation without a pre-creation duration.*

### John Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*

> *Book II, Chapter 15, §5* — "Time in general is commonly taken for so much of infinite duration as is measured by, and co-existent with, the existence and motions of the great bodies of the universe, as far as we know anything of them: and in this sense time begins and ends with the frame of this sensible world, as in these phrases before mentioned, ‘Before all time,’ or, ‘When time shall be no more.’ … Within these two are confined, and by the observable parts of them are measured and determined, the particular time or duration, and the particular extension and place, of all corporeal beings."
>
> *Gloss: Locke defines time as measured duration, dependent on the motion of bodies — a finite portion of infinite duration, beginning and ending with the material world.*

### Immanuel Kant — *Critique of Pure Reason*

> *Transcendental Doctrine of the Power of Judgment, Schematism* — "The schema of reality is existence in a determined time. The schema of necessity is the existence of an object in all time. … It is clear, from all this, that the schema of the category of quantity contains and represents the generation (synthesis) of time itself, in the successive apprehension of an object; the schema of quality the synthesis of sensation with the representation of time."
>
> *Gloss: Time is the pure form of inner intuition; the schemata of the categories are a priori determinations of time, making experience possible by linking concepts to temporal structure.*

### Karl Marx — *Capital, Volume I*

> *Chapter 10, Section 3* — "Now, the same children and young persons were shifted from the spinning-room to the weaving-room, now, during 15 hours, from one factory to another. … How was it possible to control a system which, 'under the guise of relays, is some one of the many plans for shuffling 'the hands' about in endless variety, and shifting the hours of work and of rest for different individuals throughout the day, so that you may never have one complete set of hands working together in the same room at the same time.' … this so-called relay system was an offspring of capitalistic fantasy, such as Fourier, in his humorous sketches of 'Courses Seances,' has never surpassed, except that the 'attraction of labour' was changed into the attraction of capital."
>
> *Gloss: Capitalist production treats labor time as a resource to be maximally extracted; the relay system is a scheme to evade legal limits on the working day, revealing time as a site of class struggle.*

### Carl Jung — *Studies in Word Association*

> *Section 5: Time Measurements* — "As previously mentioned, 764 time measurements were taken in five subjects; the measurement was recorded on a one-fifth second stop-watch, set when the stimulus-word was spoken and stopped at the utterance of the reaction. The time was measured in patients 6, 5, 8, 11, 13. The stimulus-words were divided into concrete, abstract, adjective, and verb. The mean numbers, which I give individually for each of these groups, are the probable mean in seconds."
>
> *Gloss: Time becomes an object of empirical psychology — reaction time as a measurable index of mental association, stripped of metaphysical weight.*

## Reframings

### Hindu / Buddhist — Cyclical Time (Samsara, Kalpa)

The Western linear-eschatological model of time (creation → fall → redemption → end) is not universal. Hindu and Buddhist traditions conceive time as beginningless and cyclical, measured in vast kalpas (aeons) of cosmic creation and destruction. The individual experiences time as the round of rebirths (samsara), from which liberation (moksha, nirvana) is escape into the timeless. The English word "time" poorly captures the sense of repetitive, impersonal cosmic rhythm.

→ See [[Terms/Samsara]], [[Terms/Moksha]], [[Terms/Nirvana]]

### Chinese — Seasonal and Daoist Time (Tao, Wu Wei)

Classical Chinese thought does not posit a single, uniform time. The Daoist tradition emphasizes the natural rhythm of the Dao, which unfolds without hurry or delay; wu wei (non-action) is acting in accord with the moment. The Confucian tradition focuses on ritual timing (li) and the proper season for each action. The Western concept of time as a measurable, homogeneous medium is foreign to these frameworks.

→ See [[Terms/Tao]], [[Terms/Wu_Wei]], [[Terms/Li]]

## Cross-cuts

- [[Eternity]] — Time's opposite and ground; Augustine and Boethius distinguish the timelessness of God from temporal succession.
- [[Change]] — Time is the measure of change (Aristotle); without change, time is imperceptible.
- [[Memory]] — Augustine ties the experience of time to memory (past), attention (present), and expectation (future).
- [[History]] — Time as the medium of historical narrative and eschatological meaning.
- [[Space]] — Kant pairs time and space as the two pure forms of sensible intuition.
- [[Labor]] — Marx shows how time becomes a commodity under capitalism.
- [[Causality]] — Kant's schematism grounds causality in temporal succession.
