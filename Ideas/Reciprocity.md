# Idea: Reciprocity

## Working definition

What is owed in return, and what binds exchange into a stable relation. The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about justice in transactions, mutual causation in nature, and the structure of friendship. Other traditions reframe it through covenantal faithfulness or relational propriety; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Reciprocity as Justice in Exchange

How proportional return grounds fair dealing, and why simple tit-for-tat is insufficient.

### Sub-question: Reciprocity as Cosmic Principle

How mutual interaction defines coexistence and community among substances.

### Sub-question: Reciprocity in Biological Process

How reciprocal crossing reveals the limits and possibilities of species interbreeding.

## Western canon

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics*

> *Book V, Chapter 8* — "There are people who have a notion that Reciprocation is simply just, as the Pythagoreans said: for they defined the Just simply and without qualification as 'That which reciprocates with another.' But this simple Reciprocation will not fit on either to the Distributive Just, or the Corrective … But in dealings of exchange such a principle of Justice as this Reciprocation forms the bond of union, but then it must be Reciprocation according to proportion and not exact equality."
>
> *Gloss: Aristotle rejects the Pythagorean equation of justice with strict reciprocity, insisting that proportional reciprocity—not equal return—is what holds exchange together.*

> *Book IX, Chapter 1* — "In cases where no stipulation as to the respective services is made they who disinterestedly do the first service will not raise the question … because it is the nature of Friendship, based on mutual goodness to be reference to the intention of the other … And it would seem the same rule should be laid down for those who are connected with one another as teachers and learners of philosophy; for here the value of the commodity cannot be measured by money, and, in fact, an exactly equivalent price cannot be set upon it, but perhaps it is sufficient to do what one can, as in the case of the gods or one’s parents."
>
> *Gloss: In friendships of virtue, reciprocity is measured by intention, not by market equivalence—a principle that extends to the teacher-student relation and to the divine.*

### Immanuel Kant — *Critique of Pure Reason*

> *Third Analogy of Experience* — "Principle of Coexistence, According to the Law of Reciprocity or Community. All substances, in so far as they can be perceived in space at the same time, exist in a state of complete reciprocity of action."
>
> *Gloss: Kant makes reciprocity a transcendental condition for the perception of coexistence: substances are contemporaneous only if they stand in mutual causal interaction.*

### Charles Darwin — *On the Origin of Species*

> *Chapter 8 (Hybridism)* — "By a reciprocal cross between two species, I mean the case, for instance, of a stallion-horse being first crossed with a female-ass, and then a male-ass with a mare: these two species may then be said to have been reciprocally crossed. There is often the widest possible difference in the facility of making reciprocal crosses. Such cases are highly important, for they prove that the capacity in any two species to cross is often completely independent of their systematic affinity."
>
> *Gloss: Darwin uses reciprocal crossing as a test of species boundaries, showing that the ease of hybridization is not symmetrical and does not simply reflect taxonomic closeness.*

## Reframings

### Confucian — Ren (仁) and Shu (恕)

The Confucian tradition does not treat reciprocity as a principle of justice in exchange but as the concrete practice of humaneness (*ren*). The *Analects* (15.24) records Confucius’s single thread: "What you do not wish for yourself, do not do to others" — a negative formulation of reciprocity (*shu*) that grounds all virtuous action. This is not a calculation of proportional return but an immediate recognition of shared humanity. The term *ren* encompasses this relational responsiveness.

→ See [[Terms/Ren]]

### Hebrew — Hesed (חסד)

In the Hebrew Bible, *hesed* denotes a covenantal loyalty that is reciprocal but not contractual — a steadfast love that both parties owe each other within a binding relationship (God and Israel, or human friends). It is closer to "faithful kindness" than to balanced exchange. The prophetic literature (Hosea, Micah) calls for *hesed* as the proper response to divine deliverance, not as a quid pro quo.
