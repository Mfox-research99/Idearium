# Idea: Duty

## Working definition

What is owed, and to whom, and on what authority. The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about moral obligation — the binding force of a rule or command that a rational agent must obey regardless of inclination. Duty is distinguished from prudence, from mere conformity to law, and from the desirable but non-obligatory. Other traditions reframe the question through cosmic order (dharma), relational propriety (yi), or divine command; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Duty and the good will

Whether an action has moral worth only when done from duty, not merely in conformity with it.

### Sub-question: Duty and enforceability

Whether duty is defined by what may be exacted from a person, and how duty relates to punishment and blame.

### Sub-question: Duty and inclination

The tension between duty and natural desire — whether the two can coincide without corrupting the moral character of the act.

## Western canon

### Immanuel Kant — *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals*

> "In order to do this, we will take the notion of duty, which includes that of a good will, although implying certain subjective restrictions and hindrances. These, however, far from concealing it, or rendering it unrecognizable, rather bring it out by contrast and make it shine forth so much the brighter. … It is always a matter of duty that a dealer should not over charge an inexperienced purchaser; and wherever there is much commerce the prudent tradesman does not overcharge… But this is not enough to make us believe that the tradesman has so acted from duty and from principles of honesty: his own advantage required it… The action was done neither from duty nor from direct inclination, but merely from a selfish view."
>
> *Gloss: Kant distinguishes actions done from duty (the only source of moral worth) from actions that merely conform to duty but are motivated by self-interest or inclination.*

### John Stuart Mill — *Utilitarianism*

> "Duty is a thing which may be *exacted* from a person, as one exacts a debt. Unless we think that it might be exacted from him, we do not call it his duty. Reasons of prudence, or the interest of other people, may militate against actually exacting it; but the person himself, it is clearly understood, would not be entitled to complain. There are other things, on the contrary, which we wish that people should do, which we like or admire them for doing, perhaps dislike or despise them for not doing, but yet admit that they are not bound to do; it is not a case of moral obligation; we do not blame them, that is, we do not think that they are proper objects of punishment."
>
> *Gloss: Mill ties duty to enforceability — a duty is something the agent may be punished for failing to do, distinguishing moral obligation from mere commendability.*

## Reframings

### Hindu & Buddhist — Dharma

The Sanskrit term *dharma* is often translated as "duty" in pre-1929 English translations (e.g., Arnold's *Bhagavad Gītā* renders it as "duty" throughout), but the concept is far broader: it encompasses cosmic order, social and ritual obligations, truth, and the very law that sustains the universe. Where the Western idea of duty tends to be grounded in reason or contract, dharma is grounded in the nature of reality itself — one's duty is determined by one's station, stage of life, and the eternal order (*ṛta*). The term is polysemic and cannot be reduced to a single English equivalent.

→ See [[Terms/Dharma]]

### Confucian — Yi (義)

The Confucian concept *yi* (often translated as "righteousness" or "duty") denotes what is morally fitting in a relational context — the obligation to act appropriately toward others according to one's role, rather than from a universal moral law or from personal profit. Unlike Kantian duty, which is abstract and universal, *yi* is always situated: the duty of a ruler differs from that of a subject, a father from a son. It is the inner disposition to do what is right, not merely the external act.

→ See [[Terms/Yi]]

### Islamic — Farḍ / Wājib

In Islamic jurisprudence, *farḍ* (obligatory duty) and *wājib* (necessary duty) are categories of divine command that carry legal and eschatological consequences. Duty is not primarily a matter of rational self-legislation but of submission (*islām*) to God's revealed law (*sharīʿa*). The scope of duty covers ritual, social, and ethical obligations, and failure to perform a *farḍ* incurs punishment both in this world and the next. No Term page exists yet for these concepts; see the broader framework at [[Terms/Tawhid]].
