# Idea: Threshold

## Working definition

What separates one state, space, or condition from another, and what happens at the boundary. The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about limits — mathematical, psychological, architectural, and social — where crossing changes the nature of what is encountered. Other traditions reframe it through rites of passage and liminality; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Psychological threshold — the boundary between conscious and unconscious

### Sub-question: Mathematical threshold — the limit of a variable quantity

### Sub-question: Sacred threshold — the boundary of holy space

### Sub-question: Domestic threshold — the boundary of the home and its guardianship

## Western canon

### Freud — *Three Essays on the Theory of Sexuality* (trans. Brill, 1910)

> "We will compare the system of the unconscious to a large ante-chamber, in which the psychic impulses rub elbows with one another, as separate beings. There opens out of this ante-chamber another, a smaller room, a sort of parlor, which consciousness occupies. But on the threshold between the two rooms there stands a watchman; he passes on the individual psychic impulses, censors them, and will not let them into the parlor if they do not meet with his approval."
>
> *Gloss: Freud spatializes the unconscious/conscious boundary as a threshold guarded by censorship — the threshold is not a neutral line but an active filter that determines which impulses become conscious.*

### Jung — *Analytical Psychology* (trans. Long, 1916)

> "The admission of the sense impression is received unconsciously (because of the increase of threshold stimulus), in the perceptive centre just below the threshold of consciousness, and consequently is not incorporated in the attention and conducted back to the speech route."
>
> *Gloss: Jung treats the threshold as a variable stimulus level — perceptions below it remain unconscious, and the threshold itself can be raised or lowered by attention and distraction.*

### Newton — *Principia, Sections I, II, III* (trans. Motte, 1729, revised 1846)

> "When a variable quantity tends continually to equality with a certain fixed quantity, and approaches nearer to this quantity than by any assignable difference, as the hypothesis determining its variation is indefinitely continued, this fixed quantity is called the limit of the variable quantity."
>
> *Gloss: Newton defines the mathematical threshold — the limit — as a fixed quantity that a variable approaches arbitrarily closely, a concept foundational to calculus and the analysis of continuous change.*

### King James Bible — *Ezekiel 40:5–7* (1611)

> "And behold a wall on the outside of the house round about, and in the man's hand a measuring reed of six cubits long by the cubit and an hand breadth: so he measured the breadth of the building, one reed; and the height, one reed. Then came he unto the gate which looketh toward the east, and went up the stairs thereof, and measured the threshold of the gate, which was one reed broad; and the other threshold of the gate, which was one reed broad."
>
> *Gloss: The threshold of the temple gate is measured with exactitude — a sacred boundary whose dimensions are prescribed, marking the transition from profane to holy space.*

### Propertius — *Elegies* (trans. Phillimore, 1906)

> "Happy the wife of Admetus and the bed of Ulysses, and every such woman as loves her husband's threshold! What is the good for girls to found temples of Chastity when any bride may be anything you please?"
>
> *Gloss: Propertius uses the threshold as a synecdoche for the marital home and its fidelity — the threshold is the boundary that ought to be guarded, but art and desire have corrupted it.*

## Reframings

### Rites of passage — Liminality

The Western Idea of threshold as a static boundary (conscious/unconscious, sacred/profane) is complemented by the anthropological concept of liminality, where the threshold is a dynamic phase of transition. Arnold van Gennep's *Rites de Passage* (1909) and Victor Turner's later work frame the threshold as a state of betwixt-and-between, neither here nor there — a condition that is itself a space of transformation. This reframing is not directly represented in the pre-1929 Great Books corpus but is implicit in the ritual structures of many traditions.

→ See [[Terms/Liminality]] (proposed)

### Chinese — *Yi Jing* (the "threshold" of change)

The *Yi Jing* (Book of Changes) treats every situation as a threshold between states — the hexagrams represent moments of transition, and the "ridge" or "critical point" (ji) is the moment when a line changes from yin to yang or vice versa. This is not a spatial threshold but a temporal one, embedded in the dynamics of change. The pre-1929 Legge translation renders this as "the critical point" or "the hinge."
