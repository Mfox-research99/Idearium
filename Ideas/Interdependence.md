# Idea: Interdependence

## Working definition

The condition in which entities — substances, persons, systems, or cosmic principles — are mutually dependent, such that the existence, action, or state of one is conditioned by the other(s). The Western tradition frames this primarily as a metaphysical question (Kant's reciprocity of substances), a political-economic question (Marx's interdependent value-forms, Kropotkin's integration of labor), and a pragmatic question (James's "hangings-together" of the world's parts). Other traditions reframe it through dependent origination (Buddhist pratītyasamutpāda) or correlative cosmology (Chinese yin-yang); see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Metaphysical interdependence

How are coexisting substances related? Kant's Third Analogy posits that simultaneity in space requires complete reciprocity of action — interdependence as a condition of empirical reality itself.

### Sub-question: Economic and social interdependence

How does the division of labor and exchange create mutual dependence among individuals and nations? Marx and Kropotkin treat interdependence as both a fact of industrial society and a normative basis for communist organization.

### Sub-question: Pragmatic interdependence

How do systems of relation actually hang together in experience? James treats interdependence as a pluralistic network of overlapping systems, not a single cosmic unity.

### Sub-question: Cosmic interdependence

How are spirit and matter, or mind and world, mutually constitutive? Blavatsky's esoteric metaphysics posits that Cosmic Substance and Cosmic Ideation are "mutually interdependent" — neither can manifest without the other.

### Sub-question: Political interdependence

How do clans or nations constitute themselves through mutual dependence? The Iroquois Confederacy's constitution embeds political interdependence as a structural principle of governance.

## Western canon

### Immanuel Kant — *Critique of Pure Reason*

> "All substances, in so far as they can be perceived in space at the same time, exist in a state of complete reciprocity of action."
>
> *Gloss: Kant makes mutual interdependence a transcendental condition of coexistence: simultaneity in time is only knowable through reciprocal causal interaction among substances.*

> *Critique of Pure Reason*, "Third Analogy: Principle of Coexistence, According to the Law of Reciprocity or Community." Trans. J. M. D. Meiklejohn, 1855.

### William James — *Pragmatism*

> "Human efforts are daily unifying the world more and more in definite systematic ways. We found colonial, postal, consular, commercial systems, all the parts of which obey definite influences that propagate themselves within the system but not to facts outside of it. The result is innumerable little hangings-together of the world's parts within the larger hangings-together, little worlds, not only of discourse but of operation, within the wider universe."
>
> *Gloss: James treats interdependence as a pluralistic, empirical fact — the world is unified by overlapping systems of relation, not by a single cosmic principle.*

> *Pragmatism*, Lecture IV: "The One and the Many," 1907.

### Peter Kropotkin — *Fields, Factories and Workshops*

> "The needs of human agglomerations correspond thus to the needs of the individual; and while a temporary division of functions remains the surest guarantee of success in each separate undertaking, the permanent division is doomed to disappear, and to be substituted by a variety of pursuits — intellectual, industrial, and agricultural — corresponding to the different capacities of the individual, as well as to the variety of capacities within every human aggregate."
>
> *Gloss: Kropotkin argues for the integration of labor over permanent division — interdependence is realized through each individual and community engaging in a variety of pursuits, not through fixed specialization.*

> *Fields, Factories and Workshops*, Chapter 1: "The Decentralisation of Industries." 1901.

### H. P. Blavatsky — *The Secret Doctrine*

> "And as Matter existing apart from perception is a mere abstraction, both of these aspects of the Absolute—Cosmic Substance and Cosmic Ideation—are mutually interdependent. In strict accuracy, to avoid confusion and misconception, the term 'Matter' ought to be applied to the aggregate of objects of possible perception, and the term 'Spirit' to the capacity of perceiving such objects."
>
> *Gloss: Blavatsky posits a metaphysical interdependence of matter and spirit: neither can be conceived or manifest without the other, making interdependence a cosmic principle.*

> *The Secret Doctrine*, Vol. 1, "Cosmic Evolution." 1888.

### Arthur C. Parker — *The Constitution of the Five Nations*

> "These Algonkin clans themselves, however, seem to have been politically interdependent, as one clan could not elect a chief without the consent of all the others."
>
> *Gloss: The Iroquois Confederacy's constitutional structure embeds political interdependence at the clan level — no single clan can act unilaterally in leadership selection.*

> *The Constitution of the Five Nations*, "The League of the Iroquois." Ed. Arthur C. Parker, 1916. (Note: Parker is the recorder/editor of Iroquois oral tradition.)

## Reframings

### Buddhism — Pratītyasamutpāda (Dependent Origination)

The Buddhist tradition reframes interdependence as the fundamental nature of all conditioned phenomena: "When this exists, that comes to be; with the arising of this, that arises." This is not merely a metaphysical claim about coexistence but a soteriological analysis of suffering — interdependence is the chain of causation that binds beings to saṃsāra, and its understanding is the path to liberation. The English term "interdependence" captures the mutual conditioning but loses the dynamic, causal, and liberative dimensions.

→ See [[Terms/Sunyata]] (the emptiness of inherent existence that follows from dependent origination)

### Chinese Daoism — Yin-Yang and Qi

The Chinese correlative cosmology of yin and yang posits that all phenomena arise through the dynamic interdependence of complementary opposites — dark/light, receptive/active, yielding/assertive. This is not a metaphysical principle of reciprocity among substances (as in Kant) but a rhythmic, processual interdependence in which each pole contains the seed of the other and transforms into it. The concept of qi (vital energy) further grounds interdependence: all things are connected through the flow and transformation of a single energetic substrate.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Reciprocity]] — Kant's law of reciprocity is the metaphysical foundation of interdependence; reciprocity as a moral and economic principle extends the concept into human relations.
- [[Community]] — interdependence is the structural condition of community; Kropotkin's integration of labor presupposes communal organization.
- [[Relation]] — interdependence is a specific type of relation (mutual dependence); the broader category of relation includes many other forms.
- [[Unity]] — James's "hangings-together" addresses the unity of the world through interdependence without monism.
- [[Causality]] — Buddhist dependent origination is a theory of causality; Kant's reciprocity is a causal principle.
- [[Economics]] — Marx and Kropotkin treat economic interdependence as the basis for social organization.
