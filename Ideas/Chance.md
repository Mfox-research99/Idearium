# Idea: Chance

## Working definition

What is chance? In the Western tradition, chance is typically understood as the negation of causation — an event without a determining cause, or a measure of our ignorance of real causes. It is also treated mathematically as the ratio of favorable to possible outcomes in games of probability. The question of whether chance is real or merely epistemic (Hume) and whether it can be rationally wagered upon (Pascal) are central. Other traditions reframe chance as an element of practical decision-making under uncertainty; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Chance as negation of cause

Hume argues that chance is not a positive force but the absence of a cause, producing total indifference in the mind. Probability arises only when a mixture of causes constrains the possibilities.

### Sub-question: Chance as probability

The mathematical treatment of chance — the comparison of equal possibilities — is the foundation of probabilistic reasoning. Hume uses the example of a die with unequal sides to show how the mind is carried more frequently to the more numerous outcome.

### Sub-question: Chance as risk in decision

Pascal frames chance as the basis of a wager: when infinite gain is possible against finite risk, reason compels one to stake everything. This moves chance from epistemology to existential decision.

## Western canon

### David Hume — *A Treatise of Human Nature*

> *Book I, Part III, Section XI* — "Proceeding then in that reasoning, wherein we have advanced three steps; that chance is merely the negation of a cause, and produces a total indifference in the mind; that one negation of a cause and one total indifference can never be superior or inferior to another; and that there must always be a mixture of causes among the chances, in order to be the foundation of any reasoning…"
>
> *Gloss: Hume defines chance as the absence of causation, which leaves the mind in a state of indifference; probability requires a mixture of causes to create a superior number of equal chances.*

### David Hume — *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*

> *Section VI* — "Though there be no such thing as *Chance* in the world; our ignorance of the real cause of any event has the same influence on the understanding, and begets a like species of belief or opinion."
>
> *Gloss: Hume denies the objective reality of chance; it is merely a name for our ignorance of causes, yet it produces genuine belief and probability in the mind.*

### Blaise Pascal — *Pensées*

> *Fragment 233 (Wager)* — "But there is an eternity of life and happiness. And this being so, if there were an infinity of chances, of which one only would be for you, you would still be right in wagering one to win two, and you would act stupidly, being obliged to play, by refusing to stake one life against three at a game in which out of an infinity of chances there is one for you, if there were an infinity of an infinitely happy life to gain."
>
> *Gloss: Pascal transforms chance into the foundation of a rational wager: faced with infinite possible gain and finite risk, one must stake everything on the existence of God.*

## Reframings

### Indian — *Kama Sutra* (practical ethics)

In the *Kama Sutra*, chance is not a metaphysical or epistemological category but a factor in practical decision-making. The courtesan must weigh the chance of monetary gain against the chance of avoiding disaster, and the choice is guided by the relative importance of the outcomes. This is a pragmatic, situational ethics that treats chance as an opportunity to be managed rather than a principle of reality. The framing is embedded in the broader Indian framework of *artha* (wealth), *kama* (desire), and *dharma* (duty), where decisions under uncertainty are evaluated by their consequences for one's overall well-being.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Necessity_and_Contingency]] — chance is the contingent counterpart to necessity; the two are often opposed.
- [[Cause]] — chance is defined as the negation of cause in Hume's system.
- [[Fate]] — chance and fate are sometimes conflated, but fate implies a predetermined order, while chance implies indifference.
- [[Knowledge]] — Hume ties chance to ignorance of causes; probability is a form of belief based on limited knowledge.
- [[God]] — Pascal's wager uses chance to argue for belief in God.
- [[Ethics]] — practical decisions under uncertainty (as in the Kama Sutra) involve ethical reasoning about risk and gain.
