# Idea: Soul

## Working definition

The animating principle of life, consciousness, and thought. The Western tradition asks whether the soul is material or immaterial, mortal or immortal, simple or composite, and how it relates to the body and to God. This question is inseparable from questions of personal identity, moral responsibility, and the possibility of life after death.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Composition — Is the soul material or immaterial?

### Sub-question: Immortality — Does the soul survive the death of the body?

### Sub-question: Faculties — What are the powers of the soul (reason, will, passion, sensation)?

### Sub-question: Union with Body — How is the soul joined to the body?

## Western canon

### Plato — *Phaedo*

> *91–93* — "I cannot tell, replied Simmias; but I suppose that something of the sort would be asserted by those who say that the soul is a harmony. … And we have already admitted that no soul is more a soul than another; which is equivalent to admitting that harmony is not more or less harmony, or more or less completely a harmony? … And that which is not more or less a harmony is not more or less harmonized? … Then one soul not being more or less absolutely a soul than another, is not more or less harmonized? … And therefore has neither more nor less of discord, nor yet of harmony? … And having neither more nor less of harmony or of discord, one soul has no more vice or virtue than another, if vice be discord and virtue harmony? … Or speaking more correctly, Simmias, the soul, if she is a harmony, will never have any vice; because a harmony, being absolutely a harmony, has no part in the inharmonical. … And therefore a soul which is absolutely a soul has no vice…"

> *Gloss: Plato argues against the view that the soul is a harmony of bodily elements; because a harmony admits of degrees, but souls do not differ in degree of soulhood, and because a harmony cannot be vicious, yet souls can be. The soul must be a simple, immaterial substance.*

### Augustine — *City of God*

> *Book XIII, ch. 24* — "But in this passage where it is said, 'And the Lord formed man dust of the earth, and breathed, or inspired, into his face the breath of life;' the Greek has not πνεῦμα, the usual word for the Holy Spirit, but πνοή, a word more frequently used of the creature than of the Creator; and for this reason some Latin interpreters have preferred to render it by 'breath' rather than 'spirit.' … Accordingly, this word πνοή is sometimes rendered 'breath,' sometimes 'spirit,' sometimes 'inspiration,' sometimes 'aspiration,' sometimes 'soul,' even when it is used of God."

> *Gloss: Augustine distinguishes the created human soul (πνοή) from the Holy Spirit (πνεῦμα), establishing the soul as a creature, not a part of God.*

### Thomas Aquinas — *Summa Theologica*

> *First Part, Q. 75, Art. 7* — "Things which have different natural operations are of different species. But the natural operations of the soul and of an angel are different; since, as Dionysius says (Div. Nom. vii), Angelic minds have simple and blessed intelligence, not gathering their knowledge of Divine things from visible things. Subsequently he says the contrary to this of the soul. Therefore the soul and an angel are not of the same species. … The body is outside the essence of the soul, yet it belongs to the species of the soul; for the soul is by its nature the form of the body."

> *Gloss: Aquinas holds that the human soul is the substantial form of the body, unlike an angel which has no body; the soul's natural operation includes sense-based knowledge, which distinguishes it from angelic intellect.*

### René Descartes (reported by Leibniz) — *The Monadology and Other Philosophical Writings*

> *Note on Passions of the Soul* — "'What I here call "spirits" are only bodies, and they have no other property except that they are very small bodies which move very quickly, like the particles of flame which come from a lighted torch; so that they do not stay in any place, and as soon as some of them enter the cavities of the brain, others go out again through the pores in its substance, which pores lead them to the nerves and thence to the muscles, by means of which they move the body in all the different ways it can be moved.' … Descartes also held that the soul must be present to the whole organism. But he maintained that 'nevertheless there is in the body a part in which the soul exercises its functions more specially than in any other part,' this special 'seat of the soul' being the pineal gland in the brain."

> *Gloss: Descartes' dualism posits an immaterial soul interacting with the body through the pineal gland, while animal spirits are material. The soul is a thinking substance distinct from extended substance.*

### John Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*

> *Book II, ch. 27, §18* — "But though the same immaterial substance or soul does not alone, wherever it be, and in whatsoever state, make the same MAN; yet it is plain, consciousness, as far as ever it can be extended—should it be to ages past—unites existences and actions very remote in time into the same PERSON, as well as it does the existences and actions of the immediately preceding moment: so that whatever has the consciousness of present and past actions, is the same person to whom they both belong. … For as to this point of being the same self, it matters not whether this present self be made up of the same or other substance."

> *Gloss: Locke shifts the question from soul-substance to personal identity, arguing that consciousness, not an immaterial substrate, constitutes the self across time.*

### Voltaire — *Philosophical Dictionary*

> *"Soul," Section I* — "This is a vague, indeterminate term, which expresses an unknown principle of known effects that we feel in us. The word *soul* corresponds to the Latin *anima*, to the Greek *pneuma*, to the term of which all nations have made use. … Do we not often pronounce words of which we have only a very confused idea, or even of which we have none at all? Is not the word *soul* an instance? … For us the word 'soul' signifies generally that which animates."

> *Gloss: Voltaire represents the Enlightenment critique: the soul is a placeholder for an unknown cause, not a clear concept. He reduces it to a term for the principle of animation, stripping it of metaphysical weight.*

### Hermes Trismegistus — *Hermetica* (Scott 1924)

> *Libellus X, §19* — "Now the human soul,—not indeed every human soul, but the pious soul,—is daemonic and divine. And such a soul, when it has run the race of piety,—and this means, when it has come to know God, and has wronged no man,—becomes mind throughout; and it is ordained that after its departure from the body, when it becomes a daemon, it shall receive a body of fire, so that it may work in God's service. But the impious soul retains its own substance unchanged; it suffers self-inflicted punishment, and seeks an earthy body into which it may enter."

> *Gloss: In the Hermetic tradition, the soul is divine in origin and can ascend to become a daemon (a spiritual being) through piety, or be reincarnated if impious. The soul's destiny depends on knowledge of God.*

### C. G. Jung — *Analytical Psychology* (Long 1916)

> *Chapter V* — "It is only by virtue of the fact that the persona is a more or less accidental or arbitrary excerpt of the collective psyche that we can lapse into the error of deeming it to be in toto individual, whereas as its name denotes, it is only a mask of the collective psyche; a mask which simulates individuality, making others and oneself believe that one is individual, whilst one is only acting a part through which the collective psyche speaks."

> *Gloss: Jung redefines the soul (psyche) as including both personal and collective dimensions; the persona is a social mask, while the deeper psyche is transpersonal and archetypal.*

## Reframings

### Hindu — Atman

The Hindu tradition does not ask "What is the soul?" as a separate entity but identifies the individual self (Atman) with the ultimate reality (Brahman). The soul is not a created substance but an eternal, unchanging witness-consciousness that is identical with the ground of being. The Upanishads and the Bhagavad Gita speak of the Self (Atman) as the innermost essence, distinct from the body, mind, and ego. The following passage from the *Bhagavad Gita* (Arnold 1885) uses "Soul of Souls" as an equivalent for Adhyatman:

> *Chapter VIII* — "I BRAHMA am! the One Eternal GOD, / And ADHYATMAN is My Being's name, / The Soul of Souls! What goeth forth from Me, / Causing all life to live, is KARMA called."

→ See [[Terms/Atman]]

### Buddhist — Anatman

Buddhism denies any permanent, unchanging self or soul (anatman). What we call the "self" is a constantly changing bundle of five aggregates (form, sensation, perception, mental formations, consciousness). The *Dhammapada* (Mueller 1881) uses "self" in a conventional sense to mean the empirical person, but the deeper teaching is that clinging to a self is the root of suffering. The passage "Looking for the maker of this tabernacle, I shall have to run through a course of many births … but now, maker of the tabernacle, thou hast been seen; thou shalt not make up this tabernacle again" (vv. 153–154) points to the destruction of the ego-construct, not to an eternal soul.

## Status notes

- Aristotle's *De Anima* is absent from the corpus; a key passage on the soul as the form of the body could not be included. The *Nicomachean Ethics* passages retrieved concern self-control, not the soul's nature.
- Descartes' *Meditations* and *Passions of the Soul* are not directly in the corpus; the Leibniz note is used as a proxy. A direct passage from Descartes would strengthen the dualism section.
- The Hermetic passages are included as part of the Western Hellenistic tradition; they represent a syncretic view that influenced Renaissance and occult philosophy.
- The Buddhist reframing relies on equivalent matches ("self" for anattā). The term *anatman* does not appear in the Dhammapada translation used; the concept is present but not named. A note has been added to the Reframing.
- The Jung passage is early 20th century and stretches the "pre-1929" boundary; it is included because the corpus contains it and it represents a major redefinition of psyche in modern psychology.
- Missing: Plotinus (Enneads on soul), Kant (Critique of Pure Reason on the paralogisms of soul), and any Sufi material (Nafs). These would strengthen the page in a future revision.
