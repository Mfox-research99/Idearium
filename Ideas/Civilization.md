# Idea: Civilization

## Working definition

What constitutes a "civilized" society, and by what criteria is it judged. The Western tradition has typically framed this as a progression from "savage" or "barbaric" states toward ordered political life under law, technological mastery over nature, and refinement of manners and arts. The question is never merely descriptive — it carries normative weight, distinguishing those societies worthy of the name from those deemed backward or degenerate. Other traditions reframe the question through moral and spiritual criteria that challenge the very equation of civilization with material and technological advance.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Civilization as political order under law

The Hobbesian tradition grounds civilization in the sovereign's command — civil law is what transforms natural-law dispositions into binding obligations, and the commonwealth is the condition under which justice and equity become actual rather than aspirational.

### Sub-question: Civilization as technological mastery and material progress

The Deweyan tradition sees civilization as the progressive transformation of natural forces into instrumentalities for human ends — the advance consists not in superior innate capacity but in superior stimuli and accumulated means.

### Sub-question: Civilization as moral elevation vs. material comfort

A counter-tradition, articulated most sharply by Gandhi, denies that technological sophistication or bodily comfort constitutes civilization. True civilization is measured by moral conduct, self-restraint, and the path of duty — and by this standard, modern Western civilization is a disease.

## Western canon

### Thomas Hobbes — *Leviathan*

> *Chapter XXVI: Of Civill Lawes* — "The Law of Nature, and the Civill Law, contain each other, and are of equall extent. For the Lawes of Nature, which consist in Equity, Justice, Gratitude, and other morall Vertues on these depending, in the condition of meer Nature (as I have said before in the end of the 15th Chapter,) are not properly Lawes, but qualities that dispose men to peace, and to obedience. When a Common-wealth is once settled, then are they actually Lawes, and not before; as being then the commands of the Common-wealth; and therefore also Civill Lawes: for it is the Soveraign Power that obliges men to obey them."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes makes civil law the actualizing condition of natural law — civilization is the settled commonwealth within which moral virtues become binding commands, not mere dispositions.*

> *Chapter XXVI: Of Civill Lawes* — "By CIVILL LAWES, I understand the Lawes, that men are therefore bound to observe, because they are Members, not of this, or that Common-wealth in particular, but of a Common-wealth."
>
> *Gloss: Civil law is defined by membership in a commonwealth as such, not by any particular national code — civilization is the condition of being subject to law rather than to mere counsel or command.*

### John Dewey — *Democracy and Education*

> "The advance of civilization means that a larger number of natural forces and objects have been transformed into instrumentalities of action, into means for securing ends. We start not so much with superior capacities as with superior stimuli for evocation and direction of our capacities. The savage deals largely with crude stimuli; we have weighted stimuli. Prior human efforts have made over natural conditions."
>
> *Gloss: Dewey defines civilization not by innate racial or cultural superiority but by the accumulated transformation of nature into means — a progressive, instrumentalist view that makes technology and social inheritance the engines of civilizational advance.*

## Reframings

### Indian — Dharma (as the criterion of true civilization)

Gandhi's *Hind Swaraj* mounts a wholesale rejection of the Western identification of civilization with material progress, technological convenience, and political dominion. For Gandhi, the true test of civilization is moral: "Civilization is that mode of conduct which points out to man the path of duty." By this standard, modern European civilization is not civilization at all but a disease — it makes bodily welfare the object of life, enslaves through luxury rather than chains, and is godless at its core. Indian civilization, by contrast, elevates the moral being and rests on a belief in God. This reframing shifts the question from *what a society has built* to *what it requires of the soul* — a move that aligns closely with the concept of **Dharma** as the cosmic-moral order that binds conduct to duty rather than to desire.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Law]] — Civilization is the condition under which natural law becomes civil law, binding through sovereign command rather than mere moral disposition.
- [[State]] — The commonwealth is the political form that civilization takes; without it, the law of nature remains aspirational.
- [[Progress]] — Dewey's instrumentalist view of civilization as the accumulation of transformed natural forces is a classic statement of the progressive narrative.
- [[Nature]] — Civilization is defined against the "state of nature" (Hobbes) or "crude stimuli" (Dewey); the boundary between the natural and the civil is the question.
- [[Technology]] — For Dewey, technology is the engine of civilization; for Gandhi, it is a snare that substitutes bodily comfort for moral elevation.
- [[Ethics]] — Gandhi's reframing makes ethics the criterion of civilization, not its byproduct.
