# Idea: Education

## Working definition

The process by which a society transmits its knowledge, values, skills, and norms to its young, forming individuals into members of the community. The Western tradition has debated the proper aim of education—whether it is the cultivation of virtue, the development of individual autonomy, the acquisition of useful knowledge, or the reconstruction of experience—and the proper relation between the state, the family, and the individual in directing it. Other traditions reframe the question through moral formation rooted in traditional life or through the cultivation of the whole person in harmony with cosmic order.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Aims of education

What is education *for*? Virtue, knowledge, social efficiency, individual flourishing, or something else?

### Sub-question: State vs. individual

Who should control education—the state, the family, the individual, or competing private institutions?

### Sub-question: Liberal vs. vocational

Should education be oriented toward the free development of the mind or toward preparation for a specific calling?

## Western canon

### Plato — *Laws*

> *Book I (Jowett, 1871)* — "The most important part of education is right training in the nursery. The soul of the child in his play should be guided to the love of that sort of excellence in which when he grows up to manhood he will have to be perfected. … we call one man educated and another uneducated, although the uneducated man may be sometimes very well educated for the calling of a retail trader, or of a captain of a ship, and the like. For we are not speaking of education in this narrower sense, but of that other education in virtue from youth upwards, which makes a man eagerly pursue the ideal perfection of citizenship."

> *Gloss:* Plato distinguishes education proper—the formation of virtue and the love of excellence—from mere vocational training. The aim is the whole citizen, not the skilled specialist.

### John Stuart Mill — *On Liberty*

> *Chapter 5 (1859)* — "All that has been said of the importance of individuality of character, and diversity in opinions and modes of conduct, involves, as of the same unspeakable importance, diversity of education. A general State education is a mere contrivance for moulding people to be exactly like one another; and as the mould in which it casts them is that which pleases the predominant power in the government, whether this be a monarch, a priesthood, an aristocracy, or the majority of the existing generation, in proportion as it is efficient and successful, it establishes a despotism over the mind, leading by natural tendency to one over the body."

> *Gloss:* Mill argues that state control of education is a form of despotism; the state may compel education but must not direct its content, leaving room for diversity and individuality.

### John Dewey — *Democracy and Education*

> *Chapter 1 (1916)* — "By various agencies, unintentional and designed, a society transforms uninitiated and seemingly alien beings into robust trustees of its own resources and ideals. Education is thus a fostering, a nurturing, a cultivating, process. All of these words mean that it implies attention to the conditions of growth. … Since what is required is a transformation of the quality of experience till it partakes in the interests, purposes, and ideas current in the social group, the problem is evidently not one of mere physical forming."

> *Gloss:* Education is the continuous transformation of experience, not the imposition of external forms; it is growth under social conditions.

> *Chapter 6 (1916)* — "We thus reach a technical definition of education: It is that reconstruction or reorganization of experience which adds to the meaning of experience, and which increases ability to direct the course of subsequent experience. (1) The increment of meaning corresponds to the increased perception of the connections and continuities of the activities in which we are engaged."

> *Gloss:* Dewey's pragmatic definition: education is the reconstruction of experience that deepens meaning and enhances the capacity to guide future action.

## Reframings

### Indian — Gandhi's critique of Western education

Gandhi, in *Hind Swaraj* (1909), rejects the equation of education with literacy and the Western model of schooling. He argues that a peasant who knows his duties to family and village and observes morality is truly educated, while the man who has mastered geography and algebra but lost connection to his community is not. Education, for Gandhi, is moral formation rooted in traditional life, not the acquisition of letters or the emulation of European civilization. This reframing challenges the Western assumption that formal schooling is the primary vehicle of education.
