# Idea: Justice

## Working definition

What is owed to whom, and on what authority. The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about persons, contracts, states, and divine law — the right ordering of the soul, the distribution of goods, the correction of wrongs, and the ground of obligation. The tradition distinguishes civic from divine justice, distributive from corrective, and law from equity. Other traditions reframe it through cosmic order or relational propriety; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

- **Civic Justice** — the right ordering of the soul and the city; what each part owes the whole.
- **Corrective Justice** — restoring balance after a wrong, in courts and in exchange.
- **Equity** — the correction of law's generality; justice beyond the rule.
- **Divine Justice** — God's judgment, the gift of righteousness, the universal order.
- **Justice and Power** — whether justice can stand without force, or force without justice.

## Western canon

### Plato — *Gorgias*

> "He who has learned what is just is just… And he who is just may be supposed to do what is just… Must not the just man always desire to do what is just? … Surely, then, the just man will never consent to do injustice."
> *Trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1892*

**Gloss:** Plato commits to justice as a form of knowledge that necessarily produces just action; the just person, possessing this knowledge, cannot willingly act unjustly.

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics*

> "The Equitable is Just, being also better than one form of Just, but is not better than the Just as though it were different from it in kind: Just and Equitable then are identical, and both being good, the Equitable is preferable. The real cause of the difficulty is that the Equitable is Just, not indeed the Just according to law, but a correction of it."
> *Trans. D.P. Chase, 1847*

**Gloss:** Aristotle commits to equity as a correction of legal justice — not a rival virtue but the very form justice takes when law's generality falls short.

### The Holy Bible — *Romans 2:6-11*

> "Who will render to every man according to his deeds: To them who by patient continuance in well doing seek for glory and honour and immortality, eternal life: But unto them that are contentious, and do not obey the truth, but obey unrighteousness, indignation and wrath… For there is no respect of persons with God."
> *King James Version, 1611*

**Gloss:** The Pauline tradition commits to divine retributive justice as impartial, universal, and grounded in the character of God — while introducing a tension between righteousness through law and righteousness through faith.

### Augustine — *City of God*

> "The righteousness of God, which is the gift of grace without merits, is not known by those who go about to establish their own righteousness, and are therefore not subject to the righteousness of God."
> *Trans. Marcus Dods, 1872*

**Gloss:** Augustine commits to a radical reframing: true justice is not a human achievement but a divine gift; the attempt to establish one's own righteousness is the very obstacle to it.

### Pascal — *Pensées*

> "Justice without might is helpless; might without justice is tyrannical. Justice without might is gainsaid, because there are always offenders; might without justice is condemned. We must then combine justice and might, and for this end make what is just strong, or what is strong just."
> *Trans. W.F. Trotter, 1909*

**Gloss:** Pascal commits to the inescapable entanglement of justice and power; neither can stand alone, and their combination is the permanent political problem.

### Grotius — *The Rights of War and Peace*

> "In a PARTICULAR acceptation of the word justice, with regard to a matter in dispute, it cannot in war, any more than in legal proceedings, apply to both sides. For there can be no moral principle, commanding us, under the circumstances of the case, to do two opposite things."
> *Trans. A.C. Campbell, 1814*

**Gloss:** Grotius commits to the position that in a strict legal sense, only one side in a war can have justice — though he allows broader senses of the term that may admit ambiguity.

### Leibniz — *The Monadology and Other Philosophical Writings*

> "While justice is only a particular virtue, when we make abstraction from God or from a government which imitates that of God; and while this virtue, thus limited, includes only what is called commutative and distributive justice, we may say that as soon as it is founded upon God or upon the imitation of God, it becomes universal justice and contains all the virtues."
> *Trans. Robert Latta, 1898*

**Gloss:** Leibniz commits to a hierarchical conception of justice: limited to human affairs it is one virtue among others; founded on God it becomes the whole of virtue.

### John Stuart Mill — *Utilitarianism*

> "Justice is a name for certain classes of moral rules, which concern the essentials of human well-being more nearly, and are therefore of more absolute obligation, than any other rules for the guidance of life; and the notion which we have found to be of the essence of the idea of justice, that of a right residing in an individual, implies and testifies to this more binding obligation."
> *1863*

**Gloss:** Mill commits to justice as the subset of moral rules most vital to human well-being, grounded in the protection of individual rights — utility is the test, but the rules themselves are absolute in practice.

### Blackstone — *Commentaries on the Laws of England, Book the Third*

> "Equity then, in its true and genuine meaning, is the soul and spirit of all law: positive law is construed, and rational law is made, by it. In this, equity is synonymous to justice."
> *1765*

**Gloss:** Blackstone commits to equity not as a rival system to law but as the animating principle of all law — ultimately identical with justice itself.

## Reframings

### Hindu tradition — Dharma

The Sanskrit term *dharma* overlaps with Western "justice" but extends far beyond it: cosmic order, natural law, social duty, religious observance, and the truth that sustains the universe. Where the Western tradition asks "what is owed," the dharmic tradition asks "what sustains the whole." The individual's *svadharma* (own duty) is determined by birth and stage of life, not by abstract right. English translators of the *Bhagavad Gītā* (Arnold, 1885) often render *dharma* as "duty" — an equivalent match that captures only a fraction of the concept. See the Term page for the full polysemy.

→ See [[Terms/Dharma]]

### Confucian tradition — Yi

▲ Yi (義) is usually translated as "righteousness" or "rightness," but it is not a matter of abstract rights or proportional desert. It is the disposition to act appropriately in concrete relationships, guided by the situation and one's role. Where the Western tradition asks "what is owed to whom as a matter of right," the Confucian tradition asks "what is fitting for this person in this relationship." Yi is inseparable from *ren* (humaneness) and *li* (ritual propriety). English translators of the *Analects* (Legge, 1861) use "righteousness" for *yi* — an equivalent match that flattens the contextual, relational dimension of the term.

→ See [[Terms/Yi]]

### Chinese tradition — Li

礼 (Lǐ, ritual propriety) is not justice, but it is the concrete form through which justice is enacted in social life. Without the rituals that structure roles and relationships — between ruler and subject, parent and child, husband and wife — there is no clear answer to the question "what is owed." Li provides the choreography; justice names the quality of the performance.

→ See [[Terms/Li]]

## Cross-cuts

- [[Law]] — Justice is the content of law; law is the form justice takes in a political community.
- [[Ethics]] — Justice is the social virtue, the one that faces outward toward the other.
- [[Government]] — The just state is the nominal end of all political philosophy in the Western canon.
- [[Punishment]] — Retributive justice proportioning penalty to wrong.
- [[Religion]] — Divine justice as the ultimate guarantee that what is owed will be paid.
- [[Equality]] — Justice requires equality before the law; distributive justice asks what equal treatment means.
- [[Virtue_and_Vice]] — For Plato and Aristotle, justice is the cardinal virtue that orders all the others.

## Status notes

**What is present:** A solid Western canon core from Plato through Mill, covering civic, corrective, equitable, divine, and utilitarian framings of justice. The Grotius and Pascal passages add the justice-and-power axis that complicates ideal theory. Reframings for Dharma, Yi, and Li point to existing Term pages.

**What was considered and dropped:**
- *Cicero, Duties* (Passage 7) — concerns generosity and the selection of worthy recipients, not justice directly. Dropped as peripheral.
- *Eckhart, Sermons* (Passage 25) — speaks of being "right son of righteousness," but the passage is about mystical identity, not justice as a social or political question. Dropped.
- *Dante, Divine Comedy* (Passages 41, 42) — "dike" in the Dante passages refers to a physical ditch/barrier in the topography of Hell (translating Italian *argine*), not the Greek concept of justice (δικη). Dropped as a false positive.
- *Pindar Odes* (Passages 6, 12, 13, 19) — "dike" here refers to a physical dam structure in an engineering context, not the Greek concept of justice. Dropped as a false positive.
- *Gibbon, Decline and Fall* (Passages 33, 35, 39) — "dike" again refers to physical water barriers. Dropped.
- *Shakespeare* (Passages 18, 30) — "fair" in the sense of beauty or courtesy, not justice. Dropped.
- *Freud, Krafft-Ebing, Adler, Saadi* (Passages 46–50) — returned only login HTML or empty search frames. No usable content.

**Missing:** Direct passages from the *Republic* (the ring of Gyges, the city-soul analogy) and from Hobbes on justice as keeping of covenants. A Hebrew Bible passage using *mishpat* or *tzedek* directly (the current Romans passage is Pauline Greek, not Hebrew). These should be added when better corpus matches become available. A possible future Term page for *Tzedek* (Hebrew) would strengthen the Divine Justice sub-section.
