# Idea: Eternity

## Working definition

What is eternity — an infinite duration stretching without beginning or end, or a timeless mode of existence entirely outside the stream of temporal succession? The Western tradition frames this question at the intersection of metaphysics, theology, and epistemology: does eternity belong to God alone, or can created things — the world, the soul, the mind — participate in it? The following passages trace a lineage from the Hermetic identification of eternity with the divine mind, through Augustine's wrestling with pre-creation time, to Spinoza's vision of eternity as the intellectual intuition of things under the aspect of eternity (*sub specie aeternitatis*), and Locke's analysis of eternity as an idea derived from the infinity of successive duration.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Eternity as timelessness vs. everlasting duration

Is eternity a stable, present whole (the *nunc stans* of Boethius and the medieval tradition) or an endless series of successive moments? Hermes Trismegistus and Spinoza lean toward the first; Augustine and Locke wrestle with the second.

### Sub-question: The eternity of God

Is God's eternity an attribute of his nature, and can it be conceived separately from his essence? Spinoza and Hermes treat it as coextensive with the divine essence itself.

### Sub-question: Human knowledge of eternity

Can the human mind grasp eternity, or does it only approach it through negation and addition? Locke argues the latter; Spinoza claims the mind can attain a third kind of knowledge that sees things under the form of eternity.

## Western canon

### Hermes Trismegistus — *Hermetica*

> "Eternity then is not limited by the conditions of time; and time, which admits of numerical limitations, is eternal in virtue of its cyclic recurrence. Thus time as well as eternity is infinite, and is thought to be eternal. But eternity is rightly held to rank above time, in virtue of its fixity; for it is firmly fixed, so as to be able, by its rigid immobility, to sustain those things which are in motion. God and eternity then are the first principles of all things which exist." — *Asclepius III*, trans. Walter Scott, 1924.

*Gloss: Eternity is placed above time as a stable, motionless principle, yet time participates in eternity through its cyclic recurrence; God and eternity are the twin first principles.*

> "The Eternity then provides (a) the Immortality, imparting also permanence to the Matter. For the generation of that is dependent from the Eternity, as also the Eternity from The God. ... And The God indeed is in the Mind but the Mind in the Soul, but the Soul in the Matter, but all these through the Eternity." — *Poemandres*, trans. John Chambers, 1882.

*Gloss: A hierarchical chain: God → Eternity → Soul → Matter; Eternity functions as the medium through which God animates and sustains the cosmos.*

### Augustine — *City of God*, Vol. I

> "I own that I do not know what ages passed before the human race was created, yet I have no doubt that no created thing is co-eternal with the Creator. But even the apostle speaks of time as eternal, and this with reference, not to the future, but, which is more surprising, to the past. For he says, 'In hope of eternal life, which God that cannot lie promised before the eternal times, but hath in due times manifested His word.' You see he says that in the past there have been eternal times, which, however, were not co-eternal with God." — Book XI, Chapter 16, trans. Marcus Dods, 1887.

*Gloss: Augustine wrestles with the scriptural phrase 'before eternal times' — arguing that past 'eternal times' are neither co-eternal with God nor a contradiction, but a way of speaking about temporal orders beyond human reckoning.*

### Spinoza — *Ethics*

> "Things are conceived by us as actual in two ways; either as existing in relation to a given time and place, or as contained in God and following from the necessity of the divine nature. Whatsoever we conceive in this second way as true or real, we conceive under the form of eternity, and their ideas involve the eternal and infinite essence of God." — Part II, Prop. XLIV, Note, trans. R.H.M. Elwes, 1883.

*Gloss: Spinoza distinguishes two modes of conceiving reality — temporal and eternal — and locates the eternal in the intellectual vision of things as necessary consequences of God's essence.*

> "God, and all the attributes of God, are eternal. ... Eternity appertains to the nature of substance ... therefore, eternity must appertain to each of the attributes, and thus all are eternal." — Part I, Prop. XIX and Note, trans. R.H.M. Elwes, 1883.

*Gloss: Eternity is not an external property added to God but follows necessarily from the definition of substance as self-existent; it is coextensive with each attribute.*

### Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*

> "We ... suppose a duration exceeding as many such periods as I can reckon, let me add whilst I will, which I think is the notion we have of eternity; of whose infinity we have no other notion than we have of the infinity of number, to which we can add for ever without end." — Book II, Chapter 14, Section 30, trans. P.H. Nidditch (1975), based on 1689 original.

*Gloss: Locke derives the idea of eternity from reflection on the capacity to endlessly add durations of time, making it a negative concept — the negation of a terminus — rather than a positive intuition of timeless being.*

> "From those two fountains of all knowledge before mentioned, viz. reflection and sensation, we got the ideas of duration, and the measures of it. For, First, by observing what passes in our minds, how our ideas there in train constantly some vanish and others begin to appear, we come by the idea of SUCCESSION. ... by being able to repeat those measures of time, or ideas of stated lengths, we come by the idea of eternity." — Book II, Chapter 14, Sections 31–34 (paraphrased, precise quotation from the 1689/1975 edition not reproduced here). [Note: the passage supplied confirms the same point.]

*Gloss: The idea of eternity is built stepwise from the empirical observation of succession; it is a constructed, not an innate, idea.*

## Reframings

No direct corpus passages were retrieved for non-Western reframings of eternity. The following pointers are provided based on the known conceptual landscape of the Great Books tradition; they await future corpus-expansion with appropriate primary sources.

### Hindu — *Brahman*

In the Upanishads, *Brahman* is the eternal, unchanging ground of all existence, not as an infinite temporal extension but as the timeless reality (*satya*) that underlies the flux of *māyā*. The concept resists the Western dichotomy of everlasting duration vs. timelessness because *Brahman* is described as both beyond time and as the support of time. See the Term page for the full polysemy.

→ See [[Terms/Brahman]]

### Buddhist — *Nirvāṇa*

Nirvāṇa is often described as "the Unconditioned" (*asaṃskṛta*), utterly beyond the cycle of birth-death-rebirth (*saṃsāra*) that defines temporal existence. It is not an eternal duration but the cessation of the conditions that generate time itself. The Western framing of eternity as either infinite time or timelessness maps imperfectly onto this concept.

→ See [[Terms/Nirvana]]

### Daoist — *Tao*

The *Tao Te Ching* presents the Tao as "eternal" (*ch'ang*) but also as the nameless source that gives rise to the ten thousand things, which have names and are subject to change. The Tao's eternity is less a property and more a name for its unnameable persistence beyond the cycle of birth and decay.

→ See [[Terms/Tao]]

## Cross-cuts

- [[Time]] — eternity is the conceptual counterpart to time; much of the Western tradition defines one through the other.
- [[Infinity]] — the infinite duration of eternity is often confused with mathematical infinity; Locke explicitly compares them.
- [[God]] — in most Western theism, eternity is an attribute of God.
- [[Immortality]] — the soul's eternity (or eternal life) is a distinct but related question.
- [[Soul]] — Spinoza ties the mind's knowledge of eternity to the part of the mind that is eternal.
