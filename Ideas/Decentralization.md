# Idea: Decentralization

## Working definition

How should power, authority, and decision-making be distributed across a political or economic system? The Western tradition frames this primarily as a question about the proper relation between central government and local administration, and about the concentration or dispersal of industrial production. The term carries a normative charge: decentralization is often associated with liberty, local initiative, and resistance to tyranny, but also with inefficiency, disorder, and fragmentation. Other traditions approach the question through different categories (e.g., subsidiarity, federalism, anarchist communism); see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Administrative vs. Political Centralization

Tocqueville’s foundational distinction: centralization of *government* (the concentration of general legislative and foreign-policy power) is compatible with decentralization of *administration* (local management of local affairs). The two are not the same, and their confusion leads to bad policy.

### Sub-question: Economic Decentralization

Kropotkin’s argument that industrial monopoly is breaking down under its own weight, and that the future lies in a variety of industries integrated with agriculture in each region — a permanent division of labor is neither necessary nor desirable.

### Sub-question: The Principle of Dissemination

Mill’s practical rule: the greatest dissemination of power consistent with efficiency, but the greatest possible centralization of information and its diffusion from the centre.

## Western canon

### Alexis de Tocqueville — *Democracy in America*

> "Centralization is become a word of general and daily use, without any precise meaning being attached to it. Nevertheless, there exist two distinct kinds of centralization, which it is necessary to discriminate with accuracy. Certain interests are common to all parts of a nation, such as the enactment of its general laws and the maintenance of its foreign relations. Other interests are peculiar to certain parts of the nation, such, for instance, as the business of the several townships. When the power which directs the former is concentrated in one place or in the same persons, it constitutes a centralization of government. To concentrate the direction of the latter in like manner is to create a centralization of administration."
>
> *Gloss: Tocqueville insists that confusing the two kinds of centralization — government vs. administration — is the source of much political error; the United States combines strong central government with highly decentralized local administration.*

> "The system of local administration produces several different effects in America. The Americans seem to me to have outstepped the limits of sound policy in isolating the administration of the Government; for order, even in second-rate affairs, is a matter of national importance. As the State has no administrative functionaries of its own, stationed on different points of its territory, to whom it can give a common impulse, the consequence is that it rarely attempts to issue any general police regulations."
>
> *Gloss: Tocqueville warns that extreme administrative decentralization can lead to disorder and a lack of uniform regulation — a cost that must be weighed against the political benefits of local liberty.*

> "If we pay proper attention, we shall find that whenever it is said that a State cannot act because it has no central point, it is the centralization of the government in which it is deficient. ... The same remark is applicable to the Middle Ages; the cause of all the confusion of feudal society was that the control, not only of local but of general interests, was divided amongst a thousand hands, and broken up in a thousand different ways; the absence of a central government prevented the nations of Europe from advancing with energy in any straightforward course."
>
> *Gloss: Tocqueville clarifies that the feudal disorder was not decentralization of administration but a failure of governmental centralization — a crucial distinction for evaluating historical cases.*

### John Stuart Mill — *On Liberty*

> "I believe that the practical principle in which safety resides, the ideal to be kept in view, the standard by which to test all arrangements intended for overcoming the difficulty, may be conveyed in these words: the greatest dissemination of power consistent with efficiency; but the greatest possible centralisation of information, and diffusion of it from the centre."
>
> *Gloss: Mill formulates a balancing principle: power should be dispersed as far as efficiency allows, but knowledge and coordination should be centralized and then broadcast outward — a proto‑information‑age insight.*

### Peter Kropotkin — *The Conquest of Bread*

> "After the Napoleonic wars Britain had nearly succeeded in ruining the main industries which had sprung up in France at the end of the preceding century. She also became mistress of the seas and had no rivals of importance. She took in the situation, and knew how to turn its privileges and advantages to account. She established an industrial monopoly, and, imposing upon her neighbours her prices for the goods she alone could manufacture, accumulated riches upon riches. But a new current of thought induced bye and bye all civilized nations to manufacture for themselves. ... And now we see already that this industrial revolution strikes a crushing blow at the theory of the division of labour which for a long time was supposed to be so sound."
>
> *Gloss: Kropotkin argues that the historical trend toward industrial monopoly is reversing — each nation and region is developing its own productive capacity, undermining the classical economic doctrine of permanent division of labor.*

### Peter Kropotkin — *Fields, Factories and Workshops*

> "The present tendency of humanity is to have the greatest possible variety of industries gathered in each country, in each separate region, side by side with agriculture. ... while a temporary division of functions remains the surest guarantee of success in each separate undertaking, the permanent division is doomed to disappear, and to be substituted by a variety of pursuits — intellectual, industrial, and agricultural — corresponding to the different capacities of the individual, as well as to the variety of capacities within every human aggregate."
>
> *Gloss: Kropotkin extends the decentralization argument to the individual level: the permanent division of labor is replaced by an integration of pursuits, allowing each person to develop multiple capacities.*

## Reframings

No non-Western reframings are present in the current corpus. The question of decentralization as a political-administrative concept does not map neatly onto traditions organized around cosmic order, lineage, or divine mandate. For example, Confucian governance emphasizes hierarchical responsibility (see [[Terms/Li]] and [[Terms/Ren]]) rather than a distribution of power away from a centre; the Daoist ideal of *wu wei* (see [[Terms/Wu_Wei]]) might be read as a form of non‑interference that could resonate with decentralization, but it is not framed as a structural principle of administration. Future corpus expansion may yield relevant material from federalist thinkers, Islamic governance theory, or indigenous confederacy models.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Government]] — the central institution whose power is being distributed or concentrated.
- [[State]] — the entity whose administrative apparatus is the subject of decentralization debates.
- [[Democracy]] — local administration is often seen as a school of democratic participation.
- [[Liberty]] — Mill and Tocqueville both link decentralization to the protection of individual and local freedom.
- [[Community]] — the scale at which decentralized decision-making is supposed to operate.
- [[Anarchism]] — Kropotkin’s vision of a stateless, federated society is the most radical form of decentralization in the Western canon.
