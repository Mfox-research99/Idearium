# Idea: Same_and_Other

## Working definition

What does it mean for two things to be the same, and what does it mean for them to be other? The Western tradition has treated this as a metaphysical and logical question about identity, difference, and the principles that structure reality. In Pythagorean and Platonic thought, Sameness and Otherness are cosmic principles—the One and the Indefinite Dyad—that generate number, form, and change. In later philosophy, the question shifts to personal identity over time and the criteria by which we judge a thing to persist as the same despite alteration. Other traditions reframe the question through complementarity or non-duality; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

- **Same and Other as metaphysical principles** — How do Sameness and Otherness function as foundational elements of reality, especially in Pythagorean number theory and Platonic cosmology?
- **Same and Other in identity over time** — What constitutes the identity of a thing that undergoes change? When is a changing thing still "the same"?
- **Same and Other in number and mathematics** — How do odd/even, square/oblong numbers instantiate the principles of sameness and otherness?

## Western canon

### Nicomachus of Gerasa — *Introduction to Arithmetic*

> *Book II, Chapter XVIII* — "The physical philosophers, however, and those that take their start with mathematics, call 'the same' and 'the other' the principles of the universe, and it has been shown that 'the same' inheres in unity and the odd numbers, to which unity gives specific form, and to an even greater degree in the squares, made by the continued addition of odd numbers, because in their sides they share in equality; while 'the other' inheres in 2 and the whole even series, which is given specific form by 2, and particularly in the heteromecic numbers, which are made by the continued addition of the even numbers."
>
> *Gloss: Nicomachus identifies Sameness with the monad and odd numbers, Otherness with the dyad and even numbers, making them the formal principles of all things.*

> *Book II, Chapter XX* — "Thus, 'the other' is conceived of as being both greater and smaller than 'the same,' since it is produced, both by addition and by subtraction, in the same way that the two kinds of inequality also, the greater and the less, have their origin from the application of addition or subtraction to equality. This also is sufficient evidence that the two forms partake of sameness and otherness, of otherness in an indefinite fashion, but of sameness definitely."
>
> *Gloss: Otherness is inherently indefinite and relational, while Sameness is definite and self-identical.*

> *Book II, Chapter XVIII (commentary)* — "The origins of number, and indeed of all things, are the 'same' and the 'other,' or 'sameness' and 'otherness.' They are the formal principles, which, when they enter into the composition of things, cause them either to persist in the same fashion, preserving their identity, or, in the case of 'otherness,' to change from their original forms and assume others."
>
> *Gloss: Sameness and Otherness are not merely mathematical but ontological principles governing persistence and change.*

> *Book II, Chapter XVIII (on the dyad)* — "Thus 'the other' is fundamentally 'other' by I, and by no other number; and for this reason customarily 'other' is used, among those who speak correctly, of two things and not of more than two."
>
> *Gloss: Otherness is intrinsically binary—it always involves a pair, not a plurality.*

### David Hume — *A Treatise of Human Nature*

> *Book I, Part IV, Section vi* — "An oak, that grows from a small plant to a large tree, is still the same oak; though there be not one particle of matter, or figure of its parts the same. An infant becomes a man-, and is sometimes fat, sometimes lean, without any change in his identity. … though we commonly be able to distinguish pretty exactly betwixt numerical and specific identity, yet it sometimes happens, that we confound them, and in our thinking and reasoning employ the one for the other. Thus a man, who bears a noise, that is frequently interrupted and renewed, says, it is still the same noise; though it is evident the sounds have only a specific identity."
>
> *Gloss: Hume distinguishes numerical identity (one thing persisting through change) from specific identity (two things of the same kind), and notes that we often conflate them due to the imagination's tendency to smooth over interruptions.*

## Reframings

### Chinese — Yin-Yang

The Chinese tradition does not frame the question as "same vs. other" but as the dynamic interplay of complementary opposites. Yin and Yang are not substances with fixed identities; they are phases of a single process. Sameness and Otherness are not opposed principles but moments within a whole. The English framing of "same and other" as a binary opposition is a poor fit for this relational cosmology.

→ See [[Terms/Yin-Yang]]

### Buddhist — Sunyata (Emptiness)

Buddhist philosophy, particularly Madhyamaka, challenges the very notion of fixed identity (svabhava). All phenomena are empty of inherent self-nature and arise dependently. The question "same or other?" is rejected as a false dichotomy; things are neither identical nor different in any ultimate sense. The term *Sunyata* points to this middle way beyond identity and difference.

→ See [[Terms/Sunyata]]

### Greek — Logos

In Heraclitus and the Stoics, the Logos is the rational principle that unifies opposites. Sameness and Otherness are not separate principles but aspects of a single cosmic order. The Logos is "the same for all" yet manifests as the tension of opposites. This reframing is already partly within the Western canon but is better captured by the Term page.

## Status notes

- The corpus supplied only two authors: Nicomachus (eight passages) and Hume (one passage). This is a thin representation of the Western tradition. Ideally, this page would include Plato (*Timaeus*, *Parmenides*, *Sophist*), Aristotle (*Categories*, *Metaphysics*), Plotinus (*Enneads*), and later figures such as Leibniz (identity of indiscernibles) and Hegel (identity and difference in the Logic). These were not available in the retrieved corpus.
- The Nicomachus passages are rich but heavily technical; they were curated to show the metaphysical commitment to Sameness and Otherness as principles, not just number theory.
- The Hume passage was the only one on personal/object identity; it is included as a representative of the empiricist approach.
- Reframings were written without direct corpus passages; they point to existing Term pages for further reading. The corpus does not contain explicit discussions of "same and other" from Chinese or Buddhist sources under that English phrase.
- Future expansion should add Plato's *Timaeus* 35A–36B (the mixing of Same and Different in the world-soul) and Aristotle's discussion of identity and difference in *Metaphysics* Delta.
