# Idea: Shamanism

## Working definition

The Western ethnographic category for a set of practices centered on a specialist who enters altered states of consciousness to interact with spirits, diagnose and heal illness, and mediate between the human and non-human worlds. The term originates from the Tungusic *šaman* and was generalized by 19th‑ and early‑20th‑century anthropology to describe analogous figures across Siberia, the Arctic, and eventually the Americas and elsewhere. The framing is observational and comparative, not doctrinal: shamanism is defined by technique (ecstasy, trance, spirit‑journey) and social function (healer, diviner, ritual leader) rather than by a shared theology.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Vocation and the Shamanic Call

How one becomes a shaman — hereditary transmission, spontaneous calling, or affliction. The Siberian material emphasizes a period of crisis (often described as “Arctic hysteria”) that is mastered and channeled into the shaman’s power.

### Sub-question: Types and Specialization

Family shamans vs. professional shamans; ecstatic shamans, prophet‑shamans, incantation shamans; “white” vs. “black” shamans. The degree of specialization varies by tribe and by the shaman’s personal gifts.

### Sub-question: Techniques and Performances

Drumming, ventriloquism, binding and release, spirit voices, feats of endurance (swallowing sticks, eating coals). The performance is both a demonstration of power and a communicative act with the spirit world.

### Sub-question: Social Role and Esteem

The shaman as healer, diviner, keeper of tradition, and sometimes as a political figure. Esteem is not simply a function of technical skill; an inspired but untricked shaman may be more revered than a virtuoso performer.

## Western canon

### Czaplicka — *Aboriginal Siberia* (1914)

> “Whether his calling be hereditary or not, a shaman must be a capable— nay, an inspired person. Of course, this is practically the same thing as saying that he is nervous and excitable, often to the verge of insanity. So long as he practises his vocation, however, the shaman never passes this verge. It often happens that before entering the calling persons have had serious nervous affections. … I was told that people about to become shamans have fits of wild paroxysms alternating with a condition of complete exhaustion. They will lie motionless for two or three days without partaking of food or drink. Finally they retire to the wilderness, where they spend their time enduring hunger and cold in order to prepare themselves for their calling. To be called to become a shaman is generally equivalent to being afflicted with hysteria; then the accepting of the call means recovery.”
>
> *Gloss: The shamanic vocation is framed as a pathological condition that, once accepted and disciplined, becomes the source of the shaman’s power — a mastered crisis rather than a permanent illness.*

> “Although hysteria … lies at the bottom of the shaman’s vocation, yet at the same time the shaman differs from an ordinary patient suffering from this illness in possessing an extremely great power of mastering himself in the periods between the actual fits, which occur during the ceremonies. ‘A good shaman ought to possess many unusual qualities, but the chief is the power, acquired by tact and knowledge, to influence the people round him.’”
>
> *Gloss: The shaman’s self‑mastery and social intelligence distinguish him from the mere hysteric; the performance is controlled, not uncontrolled.*

> “Among the Chukchee … we find that there exist three categories of professional shamans: (A) Ecstatic shamans, (B) Shaman‑prophets, (C) Incantation shamans. … The ecstatic shaman communicates with ‘spirits’ and is called *kalatkourgin*. … The shaman‑prophet … foretells the future, finds lost objects, and discovers thieves. … The incantation shaman … uses only set formulas and does not fall into ecstasy.”
>
> *Gloss: A functional typology based on the mode of interaction with spirits — ecstatic communication, divination, or formulaic ritual — showing that shamanism is not a single technique.*

> “Among the Yakut and Buryat there are shamans of both sexes. … Among the Palaeo‑Siberians, women receive the gift of shamanizing more often than men. ‘The woman is by nature a shaman,’ declared a Chukchee shaman to Bogoras. She does not need to be specially prepared for the calling, and so her novitiate is much shorter and less trying. Ventriloquism, however, is not practised among female shamans.”
>
> *Gloss: Gender is a variable in shamanic practice; female shamans are seen as more naturally gifted but are excluded from certain techniques (ventriloquism).*

> “After the shaman has learnt all he wishes from the spirits, the latter communicate the will of the god to the people. If he is to foretell the future, he employs his staff. He throws it on the ground, and if it falls with the side inscribed with mystical signs turned upward, this is a good omen; if the blank side shows, ill‑fortune may be looked for. To prove his trustworthiness to those present, the shaman uses the following means. He sits on a reindeer skin, and his hands and feet are bound. The room is completely darkened. Then, as if in answer to his call to the spirits, various noises are heard both within and without the yurta: the beating of a drum, the grunting of a bear, the hissing of a serpent, the squeak of a squirrel, and mysterious scratchings on the reindeer‑skin where he sits. Then the shaman’s bonds are untied, he is set free, and every one is convinced that what they heard was the work of the spirits.”
>
> *Gloss: A classic description of the shamanic seance — binding, darkness, ventriloquized spirit‑voices, and the dramatic release that confirms the shaman’s power.*

### Bogoras — *The Chukchee – Religion* (1904–1909)

> “To a cossack who wanted to know whether the yearly mail steamer would bring him a furlough, the shaman answered, ‘The big boat brings change and joy to all people in this country.’ I could cite other answers not less worthy of the oracle of Delphi. To my own questions of this kind, the shamans usually answered that my country was too far away, and the feet of their *ke’let* too small, to go there. … A shaman of the interior refused to give advice about the maritime pursuits of the people of the coast, explaining that his ‘spirits’ were good only for walking upon the land, and that they were afraid of the sea.”
>
> *Gloss: Shamanic divination is pragmatic and locally bounded; spirits have territorial limits, and the shaman’s answers are often diplomatically vague — a parallel to Greek oracular practice.*

> “The Chukchee divide all acts of shamanism into three categories, more or less distinct, though merging into each other. Under the first category comes ‘communication with the spirits’ (*kalatko’urgin*). This includes all kinds of intercourse with the ‘spirits’ which becomes apparent to the listeners; that is, the voices of ‘spirits’ talking through the medium of the shaman.”
>
> *Gloss: The indigenous taxonomy confirms the threefold division reported by Czaplicka; the core act is audible spirit‑communication.*

## Theoretical framework

The shamanism documented by Czaplicka and Bogoras is1904 early-20th-century ethnography — a second-order observational framework. The theoretical architecture that organized this material into a comparative category came later, primarily through Mircea Eliade's *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy* (1951, in copyright). Eliade's contribution was to identify the shamanic complex as a coherent cross-cultural phenomenon defined not by culture area (Siberian) but by technique (ecstatic journey). Seven structural features organize the8001:

1. **The shamanic call** — spontaneous election through crisis, illness, or hereditary transmission. The call is involuntary; accepting it is the first act of shamanic agency.

2. **Initiation / death-and-rebirth** — the novice undergoes symbolic dismemberment by spirits, journey to the underworld, and reconstitution. This is not a ceremony administered by human officiants but a direct encounter the shaman survives.

3. **The cosmic axis** — the shaman travels along a world-axis (*axis mundi*) represented by the World Tree, a mountain, or a pole. This structures the cosmology: upper world, middle world, lower world.

4. **Spirit helpers and familiars** — the shaman does not work alone. Animal spirits, ancestor spirits, and nature spirits are allies. Control over spirits distinguishes the shaman from the possessed person.

5. **The ecstatic technique** — drumming, dancing, chanting, and sometimes psychoactive plants induce the altered state in which soul-journey is possible. The technique is teachable but activated by the shaman's personal relationship with spirits.

6. **Healing function** — soul-retrieval (the shaman recovers the patient's lost soul) and spirit-extraction (the shaman removes intruding spirits) are the primary healing modalities. The shaman is first and foremost a healer.

7. **Psychopomp role** — the shaman guides the souls of the dead to the afterlife. This makes shamanism a complete spiritual technology: birth, illness, death.

These features recur across Siberian, Arctic, Central Asian, and (with variations) Amerindian and circumpolar traditions, forming what Eliade called "archaic techniques of ecstasy." The term "shamanism" thus refers to a technology of altered-consciousness mediation, not to a particular culture's religion. No non‑Western tradition in the current corpus offers a native term that directly reframes “shamanism” as a philosophical Idea. The Siberian peoples studied by Czaplicka and Bogoras have their own categories (e.g., Chukchee *enen* “shamanistic spirit,” *kalatko’urgin* “communication with spirits”), but these are not developed into a systematic concept comparable to Dharma or Logos. The closest analogue in the existing Term index is **Ecstasy** (not yet a Term page) or **Spirit Possession** (not yet a Term page). A future Term page for **Ecstasy** could capture the Greek *ekstasis* (Plato’s *Phaedrus*, the Dionysian rites) and the Siberian shamanic trance as parallel but distinct phenomena. For now, shamanism remains a Western ethnographic category applied to practices that the practitioners themselves do not unify under a single term.
