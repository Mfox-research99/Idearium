# Idea: Definition

## Working definition

What does it mean to give a definition? Is a definition an arbitrary starting point that fixes the meaning of a term, or is it a conclusion that captures the essence of something already known? The Western tradition has wrestled with this distinction most sharply in the contrast between mathematical and philosophical method. The question touches the foundations of logic, science, and metaphysics: can everything be defined, or must some things be grasped without definition?

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Definition as starting point vs. conclusion

Whether a definition precedes inquiry (as in mathematics) or emerges as its result (as in philosophy).

### Sub-question: Nominal vs. real definition

The distinction between defining what a name means versus defining what a thing essentially is.

## Western canon

### Aristotle — *Posterior Analytics*

> "Since definition is said to be the statement of a thing's nature, obviously one kind of definition will be a statement of the meaning of the name, or of an equivalent nominal formula. A definition in this sense tells you, e.g. the meaning of the phrase 'triangular character'. … Another kind of definition is a formula exhibiting the cause of a thing's existence. Thus the former signifies without proving, but the latter will clearly be a quasi-demonstration of essential nature, differing from demonstration in the arrangement of its terms."
>
> — *Posterior Analytics* II.10, trans. G.R.G. Mure, 1928

*Gloss: Aristotle establishes the foundational distinction between nominal definition (what a name means) and real definition (the causal formula that explains the essence of a thing). Real definition is not arbitrary; it is akin to demonstration.*

> "One might equally well call it the definition of mountain copper. For definitions do not carry a further guarantee that the thing defined can exist or that it is what they claim to define: one can always ask why."
>
> — *Posterior Analytics* II.7, trans. G.R.G. Mure, 1928

*Gloss: A nominal definition can be given even for non-existing things; real definition must presuppose that the thing exists. This introduces the ontological commitment behind real definition.*

### Immanuel Kant — *Critique of Pure Reason*

> "There are no other conceptions which can bear definition, except those which contain an arbitrary synthesis, which can be constructed à priori. Consequently, the science of mathematics alone possesses definitions. For the object here thought is presented à priori in intuition; and thus it can never contain more or less than the conception, because the conception of the object has been given by the definition—and primarily, that is, without deriving the definition from any other source. Philosophical definitions are, therefore, merely expositions of given conceptions, while mathematical definitions are constructions of conceptions originally formed by the mind itself; the former are produced by analysis, the completeness of which is never demonstratively certain, the latter by a synthesis. In a mathematical definition the conception is formed, in a philosophical definition it is only explained."
>
> — *Critique of Pure Reason* A730/B758, trans. J.M.D. Meiklejohn, 1855

*Gloss: Kant radicalizes Aristotle's distinction: only mathematics, which constructs its objects, can give genuine definitions. Philosophy cannot define; it can only explicate. A definition in philosophy is always an asymptotic ideal, never a secure starting point.*

> "In one word, a full and clear definition ought, in philosophy, rather to form the conclusion than the commencement of our labours. In mathematics, on the contrary, we cannot have a conception prior to the definition; it is the definition which gives us the conception, and it must for this reason form the commencement of every chain of mathematical reasoning."
>
> — *Critique of Pure Reason* A731/B759, trans. J.M.D. Meiklejohn, 1855

*Gloss: The temporal order of definition matches its logical status. Philosophy begins with a vague given concept and works toward clarity; mathematics begins with a stipulated definition and works deductively. This has profound implications for the limits of philosophical knowledge.*

### Thomas Aquinas — *Summa Theologica* (glossary passages, note on method)

The supplied corpus passages from Aquinas are glossary entries (e.g., "being," "principle," "property") drawn from a modern edition of the *Summa Theologica*; they do not represent Aquinas's own treatment of definition. Aquinas in fact follows Aristotle on real vs. nominal definition, adding the nuance that definitions of God can only be negative (via negativa). No usable passage was retrieved.

## Reframings

### Chinese Daoism — Wu 無 (non-being) and the critique of definition

The Western tradition assumes that definition is either possible or an ideal. The opening of the *Tao Te Ching* ("The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao") suggests that ultimate reality resists definition altogether. This is not a failure of method but a feature of the Way itself. The Daoist tradition therefore does not produce a theory of definition; it warns against the hubris of fixing what cannot be fixed.

### Indian philosophy — Neti neti (negative definition)

Vedantic traditions, especially in the Upanishads, define Brahman not by positive attributes but by negation: "not this, not that" (neti neti). This is a deliberate refusal to define in the Western sense, holding that ultimate reality is beyond predication. Positive definition is seen as limiting the unlimited.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Logic]] — definition is a central operation in syllogistic and demonstration; Aristotle's *Posterior Analytics* treats it as a principle of science.
- [[Mathematics]] — Kant's argument that only mathematics can define properly ties the status of definition to the possibility of a priori intuition.
- [[Metaphysics]] — the question of whether real definition is possible depends on whether essences are knowable.
- [[Language]] — nominal definition is a linguistic act; the adequacy of language to capture reality is at stake.
- [[Truth]] — definition can be true or false; nominal definition is a stipulation, real definition must correspond to the essence of the thing.
