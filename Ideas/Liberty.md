# Idea: Liberty

## Working definition

What does it mean to be free — free from constraint, free to act, free within a political order. The Western tradition frames liberty primarily as a question about the scope of individual action against external impediments, whether from government, law, or physical necessity, and about the relation between liberty and moral reason. Other traditions reframe it through spontaneous action in harmony with cosmic order or through the priority of social duty; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: The nature of liberty — absence of impediment vs. rational self-determination

Hobbes defines liberty in purely physical terms; Locke distinguishes liberty from license and ties it to rational pursuit of happiness.

### Sub-question: Liberty and political order — the citizen's freedom under law

Hobbes locates liberty in the silence of the laws; Burke and Tocqueville insist liberty requires virtue, tradition, and moral restraint.

### Sub-question: Liberty and authority — the historical struggle

Mill frames liberty as protection against the tyranny of rulers, extending to protection against the tyranny of the majority.

## Western canon

### Thomas Hobbes — *Leviathan* (1651)

> *Chapter XXI. Of the Liberty of Subjects* — "Liberty, or FREEDOME, signifieth (properly) the absence of Opposition; (by Opposition, I mean externall Impediments of motion;) ... And according to this proper, and generally received meaning of the word, A FREE-MAN, is 'he, that in those things, which by his strength and wit he is able to do, is not hindred to doe what he has a will to do.'"
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes gives a mechanical, physical definition of liberty — the absence of external obstacles — and insists that liberty is consistent with fear (e.g., throwing goods overboard to save a ship) because the agent is not externally prevented from choosing otherwise.*

> *Chapter XXI. Of the Liberty of Subjects* — "For seeing there is no Common-wealth in the world, for the regulating of all the actions, and words of men, (as being a thing impossible:) it followeth necessarily, that in all kinds of actions, by the laws praetermitted, men have the Liberty, of doing what their own reasons shall suggest, for the most profitable to themselves. ... The Liberty of a Subject, lyeth therefore only in those things, which in regulating their actions, the Soveraign hath praetermitted."
>
> *Gloss: Civil liberty is the silence of the laws — whatever the sovereign has not forbidden, the subject is free to do. This is not a right against the sovereign but a residual domain.*

### John Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1689)

> *Book II, Chapter XXI, §51* — "A constant Determination to a Pursuit of Happiness no Abridgment of Liberty. ... Would any one be a changeling, because he is less determined by wise considerations than a wise man? Is it worth the name of freedom to be at liberty to play the fool, and draw shame and misery upon a man's self? If to break loose from the conduct of reason, and to want that restraint of examination and judgment which keeps us from choosing or doing the worse, be liberty, true liberty, madmen and fools are the only freemen."
>
> *Gloss: Locke rejects the equation of liberty with license. True liberty is acting according to reason's guidance toward happiness; the deterministic pull of happiness is no abridgment of freedom.*

### Edmund Burke — *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790)

> — "The effects of the incapacity shown by the popular leaders in all the great members of the commonwealth are to be covered with the 'all-atoning name' of Liberty. In some people I see great liberty, indeed; in many, if not in the most, an oppressive, degrading servitude. But what is liberty without wisdom and without virtue? It is the greatest of all possible evils; for it is folly, vice, and madness, without tuition or restraint."
>
> *Gloss: Burke warns that liberty abstracted from inherited wisdom, virtue, and social order becomes destructive — the mere name masks a new servitude imposed by disorder.*

### Alexis de Tocqueville — *Democracy in America* (1835)

> *Trans. Henry Reeve, 1835* — "Nor would I have you to mistake in the point of your own liberty. There is a liberty of a corrupt nature which is effected both by men and beasts to do what they list, and this liberty is inconsistent with authority, impatient of all restraint; by this liberty 'sumus omnes deteriores': 'tis the grand enemy of truth and peace, and all the ordinances of God are bent against it. But there is a civil, a moral, a federal liberty which is the proper end and object of authority; it is a liberty for that only which is just and good: for this liberty you are to stand with the hazard of your very lives."
>
> *Gloss: Tocqueville reproduces John Winthrop's distinction between corrupt license (to do what one lists) and civil/moral liberty (to do only what is just and good) — the latter requires authority, not opposes it.*

### John Stuart Mill — *On Liberty* (1859)

> *Chapter I* — "The struggle between Liberty and Authority is the most conspicuous feature in the portions of history with which we are earliest familiar, particularly in that of Greece, Rome, and England. But in old times this contest was between subjects, or some classes of subjects, and the government. By liberty, was meant protection against the tyranny of the political rulers."
>
> *Gloss: Mill frames the historical meaning of liberty as a limit on government power — protection for the governed against the ruler's tyranny. This sets up his larger argument for extending that protection to social tyranny as well.*

## Reframings

### Confucianism — Yi (Rightness/Duty)

Confucian thought does not foreground an individual right to be free from external constraint. Instead, the proper action (Yi) is determined by one's roles in a hierarchical, relationally-constituted order. Liberty understood as personal autonomy would seem close to moral chaos; the Confucian question is not "how much freedom do I have?" but "what does my station require?" This is not the same as Burke's appeal to tradition — it is a distinct ontology of personhood.

→ See [[Terms/Yi]]

### Daoism — Wu Wei (Spontaneous Action)

The Daoist tradition reframes liberty not as the absence of external impediment but as unforced, spontaneous action in harmony with the Dao (the Way). One who acts through *wu wei* is not coerced by desire, convention, or government — yet is not "free" in the Western sense of exercising will against obstacles. The ideal is effortless flow, not autonomous choice.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Law]] — Liberty is bounded by law for Hobbes, Mill, and Burke; law defines the space of freedom by proscription and permission.
- [[Government]] — Liberty is the limit of government's legitimate authority and the aim of its restraint.
- [[State]] — The state as the agent whose power liberty limits; for Hobbes, liberty exists only where the state is silent.
- [[Virtue_and_Vice]] — Liberty without virtue is the greatest evil (Burke); true liberty requires rational self-direction (Locke).
- [[Happiness]] — For Locke, liberty is the condition for pursuing true happiness under reason.
