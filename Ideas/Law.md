# Idea: Law

## Working definition

What is a law, and what obligates us to obey it? The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about the nature of command, the relationship between human legislation and a higher moral or natural order, and the distinction between law and mere counsel or force. The question is framed through the tension between the sovereign's will and the rational structure of the cosmos or divine reason.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Law as Command vs. Law as Reason

Is a law valid because it is the command of a superior, or because it is a rational rule that accords with nature or justice?

### Sub-question: Natural Law and Human Law

What is the relationship between an unchanging natural law, knowable by reason, and the variable positive laws of particular communities?

### Sub-question: The End of Law

Is the purpose of law to restrain liberty, to secure peace, or to guide human beings toward virtue and the common good?

### Thomas Aquinas — *Summa Theologica*

> *Question 94, Fifth Article* — "A change in the natural law may be understood in two ways. First, by way of addition. In this sense nothing hinders the natural law from being changed: since many things for the benefit of human life have been added over and above the natural law, both by the Divine law and by human laws. Secondly, a change in the natural law may be understood by way of subtraction, so that what previously was according to the natural law, ceases to be so. In this sense, the natural law is altogether unchangeable in its first principles: but in its secondary principles, which, as we have said, are certain detailed proximate conclusions drawn from the first principles, the natural law may be changed in some particular cases of rare occurrence."
>
> *Gloss:* Aquinas commits to a hierarchical legal order — eternal law, natural law, divine law, human law — in which the first principles of natural law are immutable, but their application in secondary precepts can admit of exception in rare cases.

> *Question 94, Sixth Article* — "Augustine says (Conf. ii): 'Thy law is written in the hearts of men, which iniquity itself effaces not.' But the law which is written in men's heart is the natural law."
>
> *Gloss:* The natural law is not a human invention but is inscribed in human reason by God, and cannot be wholly eradicated even by sin.

### Thomas Hobbes — *Leviathan*

> *Chapter XXVI* — "By CIVILL LAWES, I understand the Lawes, that men are therefore bound to observe, because they are Members, not of this, or that Common-wealth in particular, but of a Common-wealth. ... And first it is manifest, that Law in generall, is not Counsell, but Command; nor a Command of any man to any man; but only of him, whose Command is addressed to one formerly obliged to obey him."
>
> *Gloss:* Hobbes breaks decisively with the natural law tradition by defining law as the command of the sovereign, not as a rational rule discoverable by reason. Law is an act of will, not of counsel.

> *Chapter XXVI* — "Civill, and Naturall Law are not different kinds, but different parts of Law; whereof one part being written, is called Civill, the other unwritten, Naturall. But the Right of Nature, that is, the naturall Liberty of man, may by the Civill Law be abridged, and restrained: nay, the end of making Lawes, is no other, but such Restraint; without the which there cannot possibly be any Peace."
>
> *Gloss:* For Hobbes, natural law and civil law are not opposed in kind but are parts of a single system; the purpose of law is to restrain natural liberty to secure peace, not to guide toward virtue.

### William Blackstone — *Commentaries on the Laws of England*

> *Book the First, Introduction, Section 2* — "Let us endeavour to explain its several properties, as they arise out of this definition. AND, first, it is a *rule*; not a transient sudden order from a superior to or concerning a particular person; but something permanent, uniform, and universal. ... It is also called a *rule*, to distinguish it from *advice* or *counsel*, which we are at liberty to follow or not, as we see proper; and to judge upon the reasonableness or unreasonableness of the thing advised. Whereas our obedience to the *law* depends not upon *our approbation*, but upon the *maker's will*. Counsel is only matter of persuasion, law is matter of injunction; counsel acts only upon the willing, law upon the unwilling."
>
> *Gloss:* Blackstone synthesizes the command and reason traditions: law is a rule (permanent, uniform, universal) that binds by the maker's will, not by the subject's approval. The distinction from counsel is the key — law compels, counsel persuades.

## Reframings

### Chinese Confucian — Li (禮) and Fa (法)

The Western Idea of Law, with its emphasis on command, sovereignty, and the restraint of liberty, is a partial fit for the Chinese tradition. The Confucian tradition centers on *li* (ritual propriety, social norms) as the primary ordering mechanism — a system of internalized roles and rites that shapes character, not a command backed by sanction. The Legalist school (Han Feizi) developed a concept of *fa* (positive law, penal standards) that is closer to Hobbesian command theory, but it was always subordinate in the Confucian orthodoxy. The question "what obligates us to obey?" is answered differently: through the cultivation of virtue and the performance of role-appropriate conduct, not through the sovereign's will.

### Hindu — Dharma (धर्म)

The Western Idea of Law, with its sharp distinction between natural and positive law, and between law and counsel, does not map cleanly onto the Sanskrit concept of *dharma*. Dharma encompasses cosmic order, social duty, ritual obligation, moral law, and the inherent nature of a thing — all at once. It is not primarily a command issued by a sovereign, nor is it a rule that restrains liberty in the Hobbesian sense. The question "what obligates us?" is answered by the structure of reality itself: to act according to one's dharma is to act in harmony with the order of the universe. The pre-1929 translators (e.g., Arnold in the Bhagavad Gītā) render *dharma* as "duty," which captures the obligatory dimension but loses the cosmic and ontological weight.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Justice]] — Law is the institutional form justice takes in a political community; the question of whether an unjust law is truly a law runs through both Ideas.
- [[Government]] — Law is the instrument by which government exercises authority; the sovereign's relation to law (whether the sovereign is above or under the law) is a central question.
- [[Obligation]] — The Idea of Law presupposes an account of why subjects are bound; obligation is the ground of law's claim on us.
- [[Nature]] — Natural law theory ties the Idea of Law to the structure of nature and reason; the status of "laws of nature" in science and in ethics is a recurring tension.
- [[State]] — Positive law is the law of a particular state; the relation between law and sovereignty is the core of legal positivism.
