# Idea: Quantity

## Working definition

What it means for something to be measurable, numerable, or comparable in magnitude. The Western tradition treats quantity as a fundamental category of being (Aristotle’s categories), a condition of phenomenal experience (Kant’s axioms of intuition), and a subject of mathematical investigation (arithmetic, geometry, calculus). It asks whether quantity is continuous or discrete, extensive or intensive, and how quantitative reasoning applies to nature, value, and motion.

## Western canon

### Nicomachus of Gerasa — *Introduction to Arithmetic*

> *Book I, ch. 2–3* — “Quantity is either discrete or continuous, and either absolute or relative. Discrete quantity is number; continuous quantity is magnitude. Of absolute quantity, some is considered in itself, as number; of relative quantity, some is considered in relation to another, as the greater and the less, the equal and the unequal.”
>
> *Gloss: Nicomachus establishes the foundational classification of quantity that shaped medieval and early modern arithmetic: discrete (number) vs. continuous (magnitude), and absolute vs. relative.*

### Newton — *Principia*, Section I (Lemmas)

> *Lemma I, Scholium* — “Those ultimate ratios with which quantities vanish are not actually ratios of ultimate quantities, but limits to which the ratios of quantities decreasing without limit are continually approaching; and which they can approach nearer than by any given difference, but which they can never surpass, nor reach before the quantities are indefinitely diminished.”
>
> *Gloss: Newton defines the limiting ratio of vanishing quantities, grounding the calculus in a rigorous conception of continuous quantity without recourse to indivisibles.*

### Leibniz — *The Monadology and Other Philosophical Writings* (Letter to Bernoulli, 1696)

> “I make a distinction between absolute force and directing force, although I can deduce and demonstrate directing force from the sole consideration of absolute power. And indeed I prove that there is conservation not only of the same absolute force or quantity of action in the world, but also of the same directing force and the same quantity of direction in the same lines, i.e. the same quantity of progression, its direction being taken into account and the quantity of progression being counted equal to the mass multiplied by the velocity, and not by the square of the velocity.”
>
> *Gloss: Leibniz distinguishes between quantity of motion (Cartesian mv) and quantity of action (mv²), committing to a conservation law that depends on which quantity is taken as fundamental.*

### Kant — *Critique of Pure Reason*, Transcendental Aesthetic, §4

> “This property of quantities, according to which no part of them is the smallest possible (no part simple), is called their continuity. Space and time are quanta continua, because no part of them can be given without enclosing it within boundaries (points and moments)… All phenomena, then, are continuous quantities, in respect both to intuition and mere perception (sensation, and with it reality). In the former case they are extensive quantities; in the latter, intensive.”
>
> *Gloss: Kant makes the continuity of quantity a condition of phenomenal experience, and divides quantity into extensive (spatial/temporal magnitude) and intensive (degree of reality).*

### Adam Smith — *The Wealth of Nations*, Book I, ch. 5

> “The quantity of silver commonly in the market, it is probable, is much greater in proportion to that of gold, than the value of a certain quantity of gold is to that of an equal quantity of silver. The whole quantity of a cheap commodity brought to market is commonly not only greater, but of greater value, than the whole quantity of a dear one… There are so many more purchasers for the cheap than for the dear commodity, that, not only a greater quantity of it, but a greater value can commonly be disposed of.”
>
> *Gloss: Smith applies quantitative reasoning to market exchange, showing that the ratio of quantities brought to market is not the same as the ratio of values — a foundational insight for classical political economy.*

## Reframings

No non-Western reframings are present in the current corpus for Quantity. The concept of quantity as a category of measurement and number is treated extensively in Indian and Chinese traditions (e.g., the *Śulba Sūtras* on geometric measurement, the *Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art*), but these sources are not yet in the corpus. Future expansion may include passages on *pramāṇa* (measure) and *saṃkhyā* (number) from Sanskrit sources, and on *liang* (量, measure/quantity) from Chinese mathematical and philosophical texts.
