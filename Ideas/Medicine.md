# Idea: Medicine

## Working definition

What is the art of healing, and what authority does it hold over the human body? The Western tradition asks whether medicine is a genuine *techne* with a determinate good, a set of empirical guesses about nature, or a form of mastery that risks displacing the wisdom of the body itself. The question runs from Plato's analogies of care to Descartes's project of rendering humans "lords and possessors of nature" through medical knowledge, to Montaigne's radical skepticism about the whole enterprise.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Medicine as techne

Whether medicine is a true art with a specific object (health) and a rational method, or a counterfeit art that flatters the body without understanding it.

### Sub-question: Medicine and nature

Whether the physician's art cooperates with nature or violates it — whether health is something the body achieves on its own or something the doctor imposes.

### Sub-question: Medicine and human mastery

Whether medicine is the foundational science for human flourishing, as Descartes argues, or a dangerous overreach of the mind's inventions against the body's own laws, as Montaigne suspects.

## Western canon

### Plato — *Republic*

> *Book I, 346a–d* — "And each art gives us a particular good and not merely a general one—medicine, for example, gives us health; navigation, safety at sea, and so on? … Then, if there be any good which all artists have in common, that is to be attributed to something of which they all have the common use? … And when the artist is benefited by receiving pay the advantage is gained by …"
>
> *Gloss:* Plato establishes medicine as a *techne* with a determinate and exclusive good — health — distinct from the art of payment. This is the foundational Western statement of medicine as a rational art with a specific object.

> *Gorgias, 464b–465c* — "I would rather not be tedious, and therefore I will only say, after the manner of the geometricians (for I think that by this time you will be able to follow) as tiring: gymnastic:: cookery: medicine; or rather, as tiring: gymnastic:: sophistry: legislation; and as cookery: medicine:: rhetoric: justice. … For if the body presided over itself, and were not under the guidance of the soul, and the soul did not discern and discriminate between cookery and medicine, but the body was made the judge of them, and the rule of judgment was the bodily delight which was given by them, then … cookery, health, and medicine would mingle in an indiscriminate mass."
>
> *Gloss:* Plato distinguishes true medicine (which aims at the body's real good) from cookery (which aims at pleasure). The physician, unlike the cook, must be guided by knowledge of what actually benefits the body, not by what pleases it. Trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1892.

### Plato — *Symposium*

> *186a–e* — "But my art further informs me that the double love is not merely an affection of the soul of man towards the fair, or towards anything, but is to be found in the bodies of all animals and in productions of the earth, and I may say in all that is; such is the conclusion which I seem to have gathered from my own art of medicine, whence I learn how great and wonderful and universal is the deity of love, whose empire extends over all things, divine as well as human. … There are in the human body these two kinds of love, which are confessedly different and unlike, and being unlike, they have loves and desires which are unlike; and the desire of the healthy is one, and the desire of the diseased is another … and this is what the physician has to do, and in this the art of medicine consists."
>
> *Gloss:* Eryximachus the physician extends the concept of medicine to a cosmic principle: the physician's task is to reconcile the healthy and diseased loves within the body, making medicine a microcosmic instance of a universal harmony. Trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1892.

### Montaigne — *Essays*

> *"Of the Resemblance of Children to Fathers," Book II, Chapter 37* — "All means that conduce to health can neither be too painful nor too dear to me. But I have some other appearances that make me strangely suspect all this merchandise. I do not deny but that there may be some art in it, that there are not amongst so many works of Nature, things proper for the conservation of health: that is most certain … but I mistrust the inventions of our mind, our knowledge and art, to countenance which, we have abandoned Nature and her rules, and wherein we keep no bounds nor moderation."
>
> *Gloss:* Montaigne accepts that nature provides remedies but rejects the systematic pretensions of medical art. The physician's knowledge, he argues, is a human invention that has abandoned nature's own rules — a skeptical counterpoint to the Platonic and Cartesian confidence in medicine as a rational techne. Trans. Charles Cotton, 1685–86.

### Descartes — *Discourse on Method*

> *Part VI* — "For by them I perceived it to be possible to arrive at knowledge highly useful in life; and in room of the speculative philosophy usually taught in the schools, to discover a practical, by means of which … we might also apply them in the same way to all the uses to which they are adapted, and thus render ourselves the lords and possessors of nature. And this is a result to be desired, not only in order to the invention of an infinity of arts … but also and especially for the preservation of health, which is without doubt, of all the blessings of this life, the first and fundamental one; for the mind is so intimately dependent upon the condition and relation of the organs of the body, that if any means can ever be found to render men wiser and more ingenious than hitherto, I believe that it is in medicine they must be sought for."
>
> *Gloss:* Descartes makes medicine the crown of the new practical philosophy — the science on which all other human goods depend, because the mind depends on the body. This is the most ambitious statement in the early modern canon of medicine's foundational role in human flourishing. Trans. John Veitch, 1901.

## Reframings

No non-Western passages were retrieved from the corpus for this refresh. The corpus does not currently contain primary-source material from the Chinese medical tradition (Huangdi Neijing, Shanghan Lun), the Ayurvedic tradition (Charaka Samhita, Sushruta Samhita), or the Islamic medical tradition (Ibn Sina's Canon of Medicine). These represent significant gaps. When such material is added, Reframings should address:

- **Chinese tradition** — Medicine as a branch of cosmic harmony (qi, yin-yang, the five phases) rather than an interventionist techne; the physician as restorer of balance rather than conqueror of disease.
- **Ayurvedic tradition** — Medicine as a science of individual constitution (dosha) integrated with spiritual practice and diet; health as alignment with one's inherent nature (prakriti).
- **Islamic tradition** — Ibn Sina's synthesis of Galenic and Aristotelian medicine within a monotheistic framework; the physician as both scientist and moral agent.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Body]] — Medicine is the art that takes the body as its direct object; the Western canon's shifting valuation of the body (Platonic suspicion, Cartesian mechanism, Montaignean trust in nature) directly shapes what medicine is thought to be.
- [[Nature]] — The central tension in medicine is whether the art cooperates with nature (Hippocratic) or masters it (Cartesian); Montaigne's skepticism is a protest against the latter.
- [[Science]] — Descartes makes medicine the practical end of all science; Plato makes it the paradigm of a genuine techne with a determinate good.
- [[Soul]] — In Plato's *Gorgias*, medicine (care of the body) is structurally parallel to justice (care of the soul); the physician's authority is a model for the statesman's.
- [[Health]] — Medicine's specific good; the concept of health is presupposed by every medical claim but rarely examined in the Western canon with the same rigor as the art itself.
