# Idea: Slavery

## Working definition

The condition of being owned as property, deprived of personal freedom and legal personhood. The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about law, morality, economics, and social consequence — whether slavery can be justified, how it corrupts both master and slave, and what happens after abolition. Other traditions frame servitude through different categories (debt, conquest, caste, household management); see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Legal and moral foundations

What rights, if any, can justify one person owning another? The natural-law tradition denies it; positive law sometimes permits it.

### Sub-question: Economic and social consequences

How does slavery affect the prosperity and character of societies that tolerate it? Tocqueville and Gibbon both examine this empirically.

### Sub-question: Abolition and its aftermath

Why is slavery harder to abolish in modern democracies than in ancient republics? What happens to freed slaves after emancipation?

## Western canon

### Blackstone — *Commentaries on the Laws of England*

> *Book the First, ch. 14 (1765)* — "If neither captivity, nor the sale of oneself, can by the law of nature and reason, reduce the parent to slavery, much less can it reduce the offspring. … Upon these principles the law of England abhors, and will not endure the existence of, slavery within this nation."
>
> *Gloss: Blackstone rejects the Roman-law categories of slavery (captivity, self-sale, hereditary status) as violations of natural law, establishing the common-law presumption against slavery.*

### Gibbon — *The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire*

> *Vol. 1, ch. 2 (1776)* — "In the free states of antiquity, the domestic slaves were exposed to the wanton rigor of despotism. … The slaves consisted, for the most part, of barbarian captives, taken in thousands by the chance of war, purchased at a vile price, accustomed to a life of independence, and impatient to break and to revenge their fetters. Against such internal enemies … the most severe regulations, and the most cruel treatment, seemed almost justified by the great law of self-preservation."
>
> *Gloss: Gibbon describes Roman slavery as a harsh necessity born of conquest, where the master's cruelty is rationalized as self-defense against a captive population.*

### Tocqueville — *Democracy in America*

> *Vol. 1, ch. 18 (1835)* — "The most formidable of all the ills which threaten the future existence of the Union arises from the presence of a black population upon its territory; … there is one calamity which penetrated furtively into the world … it was wafted like some accursed germ upon a portion of the soil, but it afterwards nurtured itself, grew without effort, and spreads naturally with the society to which it belongs. I need scarcely add that this calamity is slavery."
>
> *Gloss: Tocqueville identifies slavery as a self-perpetuating calamity that the American republic cannot easily expel, and whose racial legacy outlasts the institution itself.*

> *Vol. 1, ch. 18 (1835)* — "Thus the abolition of slavery does not set the slave free, but it merely transfers him from one master to another, and from the North to the South. … The emancipated negroes … remain half civilized, and deprived of their rights in the midst of a population which is far superior to them in wealth and in knowledge; where they are exposed to the tyranny of the laws and the intolerance of the people."
>
> *Gloss: Tocqueville argues that formal emancipation without social equality merely shifts the burden; the freedman remains a subject of prejudice and poverty, worse off than the Indian.*

## Reframings

No non-Western passages were retrieved from the current corpus for this Idea. The Islamic, African, and Asian traditions contain extensive discussions of servitude (e.g., *mamluk* systems, debt bondage, the Confucian household hierarchy) that do not map neatly onto the Western legal category of chattel slavery. Future corpus expansion should include sources such as the Qur'an and hadith on slavery, the *Arthashastra*, and African customary law to enable proper reframing.
