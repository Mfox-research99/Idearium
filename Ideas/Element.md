# Idea: Element

## Working definition

What are the irreducible constituents from which all things—whether physical bodies, logical proofs, or mathematical structures—are composed? The Western tradition asks this question along two main axes: the physical elements (earth, water, air, fire) as the building blocks of the cosmos, and the logical or mathematical elements (axioms, definitions, prime numbers) as the foundational principles of a science. The term carries a double sense: that which is simplest and underlies the composite, and that which is first in order of demonstration.

## Sub-questions

### Physical elements

The four (or five) material substrates that compose all bodies, whose mixture determines qualities, health, and even the character of the soul.

### Logical / mathematical elements

The axioms, postulates, definitions, or simplest numbers from which a whole system of knowledge is derived—as in Euclid's *Elements* or Nicomachus's *Introduction to Arithmetic*.

## Western canon

### Euclid (via Proclus) — *Commentary on Euclid's Elements*

> "Again, the term element is used in two senses, as Menaechmus says. For that which is the means of obtaining is an element of that which is obtained, as the first proposition in Euclid is of the second, and the fourth of the fifth. … But the term element is otherwise used of that into which, being more simple, the composite is divided; and in this sense we can no longer say that everything is an element of everything, but only that things which are more of the nature of principles are elements of those which stand to them in the relation of results, as postulates are elements of theorems."
>
> *Gloss: Euclid's *Elements* is the paradigmatic case of logical elements: the simplest propositions and postulates from which all geometry is derived, and into which it can be resolved.*

### Nicomachus of Gerasa — *Introduction to Arithmetic*

> "Στοιχεῖον, element, of the universe, ibid.; used of equality, the element of relative number, II. 1.2; also of the triangle."
>
> *Gloss: For Nicomachus, "element" applies both to the physical cosmos and to the simplest mathematical entities (equality, the triangle) that underlie all number and figure.*

### Hermes Trismegistus — *Hermetica* (trans. Walter Scott, 1924)

> "The body is a mixture of the elements, that is, of earth, water, air, and fire; and so, since the body of the female has in its composition an excess of the fluid element and the cold element, and a deficiency of the dry element and the hot element, the result is that the soul which is enclosed in a bodily frame of this nature is melting and voluptuous, just as in males one finds the reverse."
>
> *Gloss: In the Hermetic tradition, the four physical elements are the constituents of the body, and their proportional mixture directly affects the qualities of the soul—tying element theory to psychology and gender.*

## Reframings

### Chinese — Five Phases (Wu Xing)

The Chinese tradition does not conceive of elements as static, irreducible substances but as dynamic phases of a cyclical process: Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, Water. Each phase generates and overcomes another in a continuous cosmic metabolism. The English "element" is a poor fit because it implies fixity; the Chinese model is one of transformation and mutual influence. There is no single term page for Wu Xing yet; see the related concept of [[Terms/Yin-Yang]] for the underlying dynamic.

### Indian — Mahābhūta (Five Great Elements)

Hindu and Buddhist cosmology posits five great elements: earth, water, fire, air, and ether (ākāśa). Unlike the Western four, ether is the subtlest and most pervasive, serving as the medium of sound and the substrate of space. The elements are not merely material but are linked to the senses and to the constituents of the subtle body. No dedicated Term page exists yet; see [[Terms/Brahman]] for the metaphysical context.
