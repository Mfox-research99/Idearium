# Idea: Life

## Working definition

What is the nature of life, and how should it be lived? The Western tradition asks this primarily through the ethical question of the good life (eudaimonia), the metaphysical question of life as a cosmic principle, and the biological question of life's origin and essence. Other traditions reframe it through cycles of transformation and the unity of all existence; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### The Good Life

The ethical dimension: what kind of life is worth living, and how do pleasure, virtue, and activity define it.

### The Cosmic Life

The metaphysical dimension: life as a universal principle pervading the cosmos, often identified with divinity or eternal being.

### The Communal Life

The social dimension: human life as fundamentally embedded in community, antedating the individual.

## Western canon

### Plato — *Laws*

> *Laws* (Jowett, 1871) — "He who knows the temperate life will describe it as in all things gentle, having gentle pains and gentle pleasures, and placid desires and loves not insane; whereas the intemperate life is impetuous in all things, and has violent pains and pleasures, and vehement and stinging desires, and loves utterly insane; and in the temperate life the pleasures exceed the pains, but in the intemperate life the pains exceed the pleasures in greatness and number and frequency. Hence one of the two lives is naturally and necessarily more pleasant and the other more painful, and he who would live pleasantly cannot possibly choose to live intemperately. And if this is true, the inference clearly is that no man is voluntarily intemperate; but that the whole multitude of men lack temperance in their lives, either from ignorance, or from want of self-control, or both."
>
> *Gloss: Plato argues that the temperate life is objectively more pleasant and that ignorance or lack of self-control, not will, leads to intemperance; the choice of life is determined by knowledge.*

### Hermes Trismegistus — *Hermetica*

> *Hermetica Vol 1* (Scott, 1924) — "For you must deem the Kosmos a second god, Asclepius, a god who governs all living things, both those which have souls and those which are soulless. For if the Kosmos has been and is and will be a living and ever-living being, nothing in the Kosmos is mortal. It is the everlasting life of each of its several parts that makes the Kosmos what it is; and seeing that the Kosmos is ever one, and is a living and ever-living being, mortality can have no place in it. It must therefore be filled with life, and with eternal life, if it needs must live for ever."
>
> *Gloss: The Hermetic tradition affirms a living cosmos, filled with eternal life, with God as the dispenser of life; mortality is an illusion within the ever-living whole.*

### Blavatsky — *The Secret Doctrine*

> *The Secret Doctrine Vol 1* (Blavatsky, 1888) — "Each particle—whether you call it organic or inorganic—is a Life. Every atom and molecule in the Universe is both life-giving and death-giving to such forms, inasmuch as it builds by aggregation universes, and the ephemeral vehicles ready to receive the transmigrating soul, and as eternally destroys and changes the forms, and expels the souls from their temporary abodes. It creates and kills; it is self-generating and self-destroying; it brings into being, and annihilates, that mystery of mysteries, the living body of man, animal, or plant, every second in time and space; and it generates equally life and death, beauty and ugliness, good and bad, and even the agreeable and disagreeable, the beneficent and maleficent sensations. It is that mysterious LIFE, represented collectively by countless myriads of Lives."
>
> *Gloss: Blavatsky posits a universal life principle: all matter is alive, and life and death are alternating phases of a single cosmic process, with every atom participating in both creation and destruction.*

### Adler — *Understanding Human Nature*

> *Understanding Human Nature* (Wolfe, 1927) — "The communal life of man antedates the individual life of man. In the history of human civilization no form of life whose foundations were not laid communally can be found. No human being ever appeared except in a community of human beings. This is very easily explained. The whole animal kingdom demonstrates the fundamental law that species whose members are incapable of facing the battle for self-preservation, gather new strength through herd life."
>
> *Gloss: Adler insists that human life is fundamentally social; the individual is always embedded in a community that precedes and shapes it, and communal life is a condition of survival.*

## Reframings

### Chinese Daoist — Tao / Wu Wei

The Daoist tradition, as represented by Zhuangzi, reframes life not as a fixed state but as a continuous transformation. Life and death are relative; what we call death may be life, and vice versa. The "eternal round" of transformations connects all beings—from germs to plants to animals to humans and back into the great Scheme. This challenges the Western focus on the individual life and its ethical quality, emphasizing instead the unity and fluidity of all existence.

> *Chuang Tzu: Mystic, Moralist, and Social Reformer* (Giles, 1889) — "Who can say whether what we call death may not after all be life, and life death? ... Then man goes back into the great Scheme, from which all things come and to which all things return."

→ See [[Terms/Tao]], [[Terms/Wu_Wei]]

### Hindu / Buddhist — Samsara / Nirvana

The Indian traditions see life as a cycle of birth, death, and rebirth (samsara), driven by karma. The goal is liberation (moksha, nirvana) from this cycle. Life is not an end in itself but a stage in a longer journey; the quality of a life determines the next, and ultimate release is the cessation of individual life altogether.

→ See [[Terms/Samsara]], [[Terms/Nirvana]], [[Terms/Moksha]]

## Cross-cuts

- [[Death]] — Life and death are paired concepts; the meaning of life is often defined against mortality.
- [[Soul]] — The nature of life is tied to the soul's relation to the body and its immortality.
- [[Happiness]] — The good life is a central concern of ethics, especially in Aristotle and Plato.
- [[Nature]] — Life as a natural phenomenon, biological and cosmic, intersects with physics and metaphysics.
