# Idea: Tyranny

## Working definition

Rule without law, for the ruler's private benefit rather than the common good. The Western tradition asks this as a question about the corruption of legitimate authority — when does a ruler become a tyrant, and what distinguishes tyranny from other forms of government? The concept is primarily political and legal, framed against the background of constitutional rule, and the tradition consistently treats tyranny as a degradation of monarchy rather than a distinct form in its own right.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Defining Tyranny — Rule Beyond Right

What makes a ruler a tyrant? The Western tradition converges on a definition: the tyrant governs by private will rather than law, for private appetite rather than the public good.

### Sub-question: Tyranny as a Corrupted Form of Government

How does tyranny relate to other regimes? Aristotle classifies it as the perversion of monarchy, but also notes that the extreme of democracy collapses into tyranny — a structural instability within the typology of constitutions.

### Sub-question: The Dissolution of the State Through Usurpation

How does tyranny begin? Both Locke and Rousseau treat it as a breach of the fundamental compact — the ruler steps outside the law and seizes a power no one could rightfully grant.

### Sub-question: The Destruction of Tyrannies

What brings tyranny down? Aristotle catalogues internal and external causes — faction among the tyrant's allies, contempt from the ruled, and opposition from states with contrary principles of government.

## Western canon

### Aristotle — *Politics*

> "A tyranny also is exposed to the same destruction as all other states are, from too powerful neighbours: for it is evident, that an opposition of principles will make them desirous of subverting it; and what they desire, all who can, do: and there is a principle of opposition in one state to another, as a democracy against a tyranny, as says Hesiod, 'a potter against a potter;' for the extreme of a democracy is a tyranny; a kingly power against an aristocracy, from their different forms of government — for which reason the Lacedaemonians destroyed many tyrannies; as did the Syracusians during the prosperity of their state. Nor are they only destroyed from without, but also from within, when those who have no share in the power bring about a revolution, as happened to Gelon, and lately to Dionysius…"
>
> *Gloss: Tyranny is structurally unstable — it faces opposition from democracies (which are its opposite in principle) and from within, when excluded factions or the tyrant's own allies turn against him.*

> "In short, whatever causes may be assigned as the destruction of a pure oligarchy unmixed with any other government and an extreme democracy, the same may be applied to a tyranny; for these are divided tyrannies."
>
> *Gloss: Tyranny is not a sui generis form but the common endpoint of all extreme regimes — oligarchy and democracy, when pushed to their limits, become indistinguishable from tyranny.*

*Trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1885*

### John Locke — *Two Treatises of Government*

> "AS usurpation is the exercise of power, which another hath a right to; so tyranny is the exercise of power beyond right, which no body can have a right to. And this is making use of the power any one has in his hands, not for the good of those who are under it, but for his own private separate advantage. — When the governor, however intitled, makes not the law, but his will, the rule; and his commands and actions are not directed to the preservation of the properties of his people, but the satisfaction of his own ambition, revenge, covetousness, or any other irregular passion."
>
> *Gloss: Locke gives the canonical definition — tyranny is not usurpation of another's right but exercise of power beyond any possible right, measured by the shift from public good to private appetite.*

> "…a king governing in a settled kingdom, leaves to be a king, and degenerates into a tyrant, as soon as he leaves off to rule according to his laws. …the difference betwixt a king and a tyrant to consist only in this, that one makes the laws the bounds of his power, and the good of the public, the end of his government; the other makes all give way to his own will and appetite. …It is a mistake, to think this fault is proper only to monarchies; other forms of government are liable to it, as well as that: for wherever the power, that is put in any hands for the government of the people, and the preservation of their properties, is applied to other ends… there it presently becomes tyranny."
>
> *Gloss: Tyranny is not a form of government but a corruption of any form — the test is whether power is exercised within law for the public good, regardless of how many rule.*

*1690*

### Jean-Jacques Rousseau — *The Social Contract*

> "…when the prince ceases to administer the State in accordance with the laws, and usurps the Sovereign power. A remarkable change then occurs: not the government, but the State, undergoes contraction; I mean that the great State is dissolved, and another is formed within it, composed solely of the members of the government, which becomes for the rest of the people merely master and tyrant. So that the moment the government usurps the Sovereignty, the social compact is broken and all private citizens recover by right their natural liberty, and are forced, but not bound, to obey."
>
> *Gloss: Tyranny is not merely bad government — it is the dissolution of the political community itself. When the executive seizes sovereign power, the social contract is void and citizens are under force, not obligation.*

*Trans. G. D. H. Cole, 1923*

## Reframings

### Chinese — No direct equivalent in the pre-1929 corpus

The Chinese tradition does not isolate "tyranny" as a distinct political category in the Greek sense. The closest structural analogue is the concept of a ruler who has lost the Mandate of Heaven (Tianming) — a cosmic rather than constitutional standard. A tyrant-like ruler is one who has forfeited Heaven's mandate through cruelty or misrule, but the remedy is not legal reform or resistance by right but dynastic overthrow as a cosmic event. The question is asked differently: not "when does a ruler become a tyrant?" but "when has a ruler lost Heaven's mandate?" The English term "tyranny" does not appear in the corpus translations of the Chinese classics in a way that maps to a native concept.

### Islamic tradition — *Zulm* as a moral category

The concept of *zulm* (injustice, oppression, tyranny) is primarily moral and theological in the Islamic tradition — a violation of the divine order rather than a constitutional category. A ruler who commits *zulm* sins against God and the community, but the tradition does not develop a systematic typology of regimes in the Aristotelian manner. The question is framed as "when does authority become oppression before God?" rather than "when does a ruler cease to be a lawful magistrate?" No direct corpus passages on tyranny as a political form were retrieved from the Islamic canon in this search.
