# Idea: War and Peace

## Working definition

The paired states of organized conflict and its cessation, and the moral, legal, and political questions that arise at their boundary. The Western tradition asks: under what conditions is war justifiable, what laws govern its conduct, and what kind of peace is worth pursuing? Augustine establishes peace as the universal end — even the aggressor seeks a peace that suits him. Hobbes defines war as the natural condition of mankind without sovereignty. Grotius builds the framework of international law in which war is lawful only as a means to peace. Tolstoy strips war of its romantic and causal pretensions, revealing its moral horror and the inadequacy of all historical explanation. Kant provides the philosophical architecture for perpetual peace — republican constitutions, a federation of free states, and the cosmopolitan right of universal hospitality. Other traditions reframe the question through cosmic duty and sacred struggle; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: The Nature of War

What war is — a legal condition between states (Grotius), a state of nature among individuals (Hobbes), or "the most horrible thing in life" conducted for reasons that no single cause can explain (Tolstoy).

### Sub-question: Just War

The moral and legal criteria for beginning and conducting war. Augustine and Grotius as architects of the just-war tradition.

### Sub-question: Peace as the End of War

Augustine's foundational claim: "every man seeks peace by waging war, but no man seeks war by making peace." Kant's extension: genuine peace requires the annihilation of the conditions that make war thinkable.

### Sub-question: The Architecture of Perpetual Peace

Kant's three Definitive Articles: republican constitutions (citizens who bear the costs of war will not vote for it), a federation of free states (the foedus pacificum), and cosmopolitan right (universal hospitality grounded in the finite spherical earth).

### Sub-question: The Horror and the Explanation

Tolstoy's dual contribution: war cannot be justified, and it cannot be adequately explained. The "great man" theory and monocausal history collapse before the sheer scale of collective violence.

## Western canon

### Augustine — *City of God*

> *Book XIX, Chapter 12* — "Whoever gives even moderate attention to human affairs and to our common nature, will recognise that if there is no man who does not wish to be joyful, neither is there any one who does not wish to have peace. For even they who make war desire nothing but victory — desire, that is to say, to attain to peace with glory. For what else is victory than the conquest of those who resist us? and when this is done there is peace. It is therefore with the desire for peace that wars are waged, even by those who take pleasure in exercising their warlike nature in command and battle. And hence it is obvious that peace is the end sought for by war. For every man seeks peace by waging war, but no man seeks war by making peace."
>
> *Gloss: Augustine establishes peace as the universal desire and the final cause of war — even the aggressor seeks a peace that suits him better. Trans. Marcus Dods, 1872.*

### Hobbes — *Leviathan*

> *Chapter XIII* — "To this warre of every man against every man, this also is consequent; that nothing can be Unjust. The notions of Right and Wrong, Justice and Injustice have there no place. Where there is no common Power, there is no Law: where no Law, no Injustice. Force, and Fraud, are in warre the two Cardinall vertues."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes defines the state of war as the absence of common power, where justice has no meaning — war is the natural condition of mankind without sovereignty.*

> *Chapter XIII* — "The Passions that encline men to Peace, are Feare of Death; Desire of such things as are necessary to commodious living; and a Hope by their Industry to obtain them."
>
> *Gloss: Peace is not a moral choice but a rational one, driven by fear and the desire for comfortable self-preservation.*

### Grotius — *The Rights of War and Peace*

> *Book III, Chapter XXV* — "In the very heat of war the greatest security and expectation of divine support must be in the unabated desire, and invariable prospect of peace, as the only end for which hostilities can be lawfully begun. So that in the prosecution of war we must never carry the rage of it so far, as to unlearn the nature and dispositions of men."
>
> *Gloss: Peace is the only lawful end of war, and the conduct of war must never abandon the humanity that makes peace possible. Trans. A. C. Campbell, 1901.*

> *Book III, Chapter XX* — "The observance of this [the bond of faith] is a matter of conscience with all lawful kings and sovereign princes, and is the basis of that reputation by which the honour and dignity of their crowns are maintained with foreign nations."
>
> *Gloss: Treaties and public faith are the foundation of peace among nations, binding sovereigns even in the absence of a common judge.*

### Leo Tolstoy — *War and Peace* (trans. Aylmer and Louise Maude)

Tolstoy's novel is the most sustained literary treatment of war in the Western canon. His dual philosophical contribution has no parallel: a moral indictment of war's horror, and a devastating critique of the very possibility of explaining it.

> *Book Ten, Chapter XXV* — "War is not courtesy but the most horrible thing in life; and we ought to understand that and not play at war. We ought to accept this terrible necessity sternly and seriously. It all lies in that: get rid of falsehood and let war be war and not a game. … The aim of war is murder; the methods of war are spying, treachery, and their encouragement, the ruin of a country's inhabitants, robbing them or stealing to provision the army, and fraud and falsehood termed military craft. The habits of the military class are the absence of freedom, that is, discipline, idleness, ignorance, cruelty, debauchery, and drunkenness."
>
> *Gloss: Prince Andrew's speech to Pierre on the eve of Borodino. Tolstoy's most concentrated philosophical attack on the romanticization of war. By anatomizing war as murder, treachery, and systematic vice, he strips away the chivalric and patriotic justifications that make war possible.*

> *Book Nine, Chapter I* — "On the twelfth of June, 1812, the forces of Western Europe crossed the Russian frontier and war began, that is, an event took place opposed to human reason and to human nature. Millions of men perpetrated against one another such innumerable crimes, frauds, treacheries, thefts, forgeries, issues of false money, burglaries, incendiarisms, and murders as in whole centuries are not recorded in the annals of all the law courts of the world, but which those who committed them did not at the time regard as being crimes."
>
> *Gloss: The famous opening of Book Nine. War is introduced as an event "opposed to human reason and to human nature." The catalog of crimes is Tolstoy's rhetorical device to force confrontation with war's moral reality before any causal analysis begins.*

> *Book Nine, Chapters I–II* — "It naturally seemed to Napoleon that the war was caused by England's intrigues … to members of the English Parliament that the cause of the war was Napoleon's ambition; to the Duke of Oldenburg, that the cause of the war was the violence done to him; to businessmen that the cause of the war was the Continental System which was ruining Europe; to the generals and old soldiers that the chief reason for the war was the necessity of giving them employment. … To us, to posterity who view the thing that happened in all its magnitude and perceive its plain and terrible meaning, these causes seem insufficient."
>
> *Gloss: Tolstoy deploys a perspectival argument against monocausal explanation: each party locates the cause in its own interests. To posterity, none of these explanations is adequate to the "plain and terrible meaning" of the slaughter. The search for a single cause is itself a failure to grasp the nature of historical events.*

> *Book Nine, Chapter II* — "Man lives consciously for himself, but is an unconscious instrument in the attainment of the historic, universal, aims of humanity. A deed done is irrevocable, and its result coinciding in time with the actions of millions of other men assumes an historic significance. The higher a man stands on the social ladder, the more people he is connected with and the more power he has over others, the more evident is the predestination and inevitability of his every action."
>
> *Gloss: Tolstoy's fatalism: individual agency is real in the private sphere but illusory in the historical. The "great man" — Napoleon, Alexander — is not the author of events but their most constrained instrument, precisely because he stands at the intersection of the most causes.*

> *Book Fourteen, Chapters I–II* — "The cudgel of the people's war was lifted with all its menacing and majestic strength, and without consulting anyone's tastes or rules … it rose and fell with stupid simplicity, but with certainty, and belabored the French till the whole invasion had perished. … Guerilla warfare, unknown to the military strategists, was the one form of warfare that could succeed against Napoleon's army."
>
> *Gloss: Tolstoy's theory of guerrilla warfare as organic collective resistance. The "cudgel of the people's war" (dubina narodnoy voyny) operates outside the rules of military science — clumsy, simple, but effective precisely because it draws on the diffuse power of an entire people rather than the directed will of a commander.*

### Immanuel Kant — *Perpetual Peace: A Philosophical Sketch* (trans. Mary Campbell Smith, 1903)

Kant's essay is the most influential philosophical treatment of peace in the Western tradition. It proposes a three-part architecture for ending war as an institution.

> *Preliminary Article 1* — "No treaty of peace shall be regarded as valid, if made with the secret reservation of material for a future war. For then it would be a mere truce, a mere suspension of hostilities, not peace. A peace signifies the end of all hostilities and to attach to it the epithet 'eternal' is not only a verbal pleonasm, but matter of suspicion."
>
> *Gloss: Kant redefines peace itself — not the absence of hostilities but the annihilation of the conditions that make hostilities thinkable. A treaty with secret reservations is not peace but armed truce.*

> *First Definitive Article* — "The republican constitution … has the prospect of attaining the desired result, namely, perpetual peace. And the reason is this. If, as must be so under this constitution, the consent of the subjects is required to determine whether there shall be war or not, nothing is more natural than that they should weigh the matter well, before undertaking such a bad business. … On the other hand, in a government where the subject is not a citizen holding a vote … the plunging into war is the least serious thing in the world."
>
> *Gloss: Kant's most consequential argument: republican constitutions produce peace structurally — citizens who must fight, pay, and rebuild will be reluctant to vote for war. In non-republican states, the ruler loses nothing. Peace is built into the architecture of self-government.*

> *Second Definitive Article* — "There must be an alliance of a particular kind which we may call a covenant of peace (foedus pacificum), which would differ from a treaty of peace (pactum pacis) in this respect, that the latter merely puts an end to one war, while the former would seek to put an end to war for ever. … The problem of the formation of a state … is not insoluble, even for a race of devils, granted that they have intelligence."
>
> *Gloss: Kant's federation is not a world-state but a voluntary association of free republics. It requires no moral transformation — even "a race of devils" with sufficient intelligence could construct it through the mutual checking of self-interests.*

> *Third Definitive Article* — "Cosmopolitan right shall be limited to conditions of universal hospitality. … It is the right of a stranger, on his arrival on foreign soil, not to be treated with hostility. … Since the earth's surface is limited and spherical, men cannot scatter to infinite distance but must finally tolerate living alongside one another."
>
> *Gloss: Kant grounds the right of universal hospitality in the material fact of a finite spherical earth. No one has more original right to any place on it than anyone else. Cosmopolitan right is not benevolence but a legal principle derived from shared inhabitation of a bounded world.*

## Reframings

### Islamic — Jihad

The Arabic term *jihād* (struggle) encompasses both the inner spiritual struggle and armed conflict in defense of the community. Classical Islamic jurisprudence developed a detailed framework for just war that shares concerns with the Western tradition but grounds them in revelation. Peace (*salām*) is the normative state, and war is a limited exception.

→ See [[Terms/Jihad]]

### Hindu — Dharma-yuddha

In the Sanskrit epics, war is framed as *dharma-yuddha* (righteous war) when fought according to cosmic duty. The *Bhagavad Gītā* presents Arjuna's dilemma: is it ever right to kill one's own kin? Krishna's answer grounds the duty to fight in the eternal nature of the self and the preservation of social order. Peace is not the absence of conflict but the restoration of *dharma*.
