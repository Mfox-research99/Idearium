# Idea: War_and_Peace

## Working definition

The paired states of organized conflict and its cessation, and the moral, legal, and political questions that arise at their boundary. The Western tradition asks: under what conditions is war justifiable, what laws govern its conduct, and what is the nature of the peace that war seeks? Other traditions reframe the question through cosmic duty, sacred struggle, or the harmony of social orders; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: The Nature of War

What war is — a condition of organized violence between political communities, or a state of nature among individuals.

### Sub-question: Just War

The moral and legal criteria for beginning and conducting war.

### Sub-question: Peace as the End of War

Whether peace is the natural telos of war, and what kind of peace is worth pursuing.

### Sub-question: The Laws of War and Peace

The role of faith, treaties, and the law of nations in limiting conflict and securing peace.

## Western canon

### Augustine — *City of God*

> *Book XIX, Chapter 12* — "Whoever gives even moderate attention to human affairs and to our common nature, will recognise that if there is no man who does not wish to be joyful, neither is there any one who does not wish to have peace. For even they who make war desire nothing but victory,—desire, that is to say, to attain to peace with glory. For what else is victory than the conquest of those who resist us? and when this is done there is peace. It is therefore with the desire for peace that wars are waged, even by those who take pleasure in exercising their warlike nature in command and battle. And hence it is obvious that peace is the end sought for by war. For every man seeks peace by waging war, but no man seeks war by making peace."
>
> *Gloss: Augustine establishes peace as the universal desire and the final cause of war — even the aggressor seeks a peace that suits him better.*

> *Translator: Marcus Dods, 1872.*

### Hobbes — *Leviathan*

> *Chapter XIII* — "To this warre of every man against every man, this also is consequent; that nothing can be Unjust. The notions of Right and Wrong, Justice and Injustice have there no place. Where there is no common Power, there is no Law: where no Law, no Injustice. Force, and Fraud, are in warre the two Cardinall vertues. Justice, and Injustice are none of the Faculties neither of the Body, nor Mind. If they were, they might be in a man that were alone in the world, as well as his Senses, and Passions. They are Qualities, that relate to men in Society, not in Solitude."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes defines the state of war as the absence of common power, where justice has no meaning — war is the natural condition of mankind without sovereignty.*

> *Chapter XIII* — "The Passions that encline men to Peace, are Feare of Death; Desire of such things as are necessary to commodious living; and a Hope by their Industry to obtain them."
>
> *Gloss: Peace is not a moral choice but a rational one, driven by fear and the desire for comfortable self-preservation.*

> *No translator; original English edition, 1651.*

### Grotius — *The Rights of War and Peace*

> *Book III, Chapter XXV* — "In the very heat of war the greatest security and expectation of divine support must be in the unabated desire, and invariable prospect of peace, as the only end for which hostilities can be lawfully begun. So that in the prosecution of war we must never carry the rage of it so far, as to unlearn the nature and dispositions of men."
>
> *Gloss: Grotius insists that peace is the only lawful end of war, and that the conduct of war must never abandon the humanity that makes peace possible.*

> *Book III, Chapter XX* — "The observance of this [the bond of faith] is a matter of conscience with all lawful kings and sovereign princes, and is the basis of that reputation by which the honour and dignity of their crowns are maintained with foreign nations."
>
> *Gloss: Treaties and public faith are the foundation of peace among nations, binding sovereigns even in the absence of a common judge.*

> *Translator: A. C. Campbell, 1901.*

## Reframings

### Islamic — Jihad

The Arabic term *jihād* (struggle) encompasses both the inner spiritual struggle and armed conflict in defense of the community. Classical Islamic jurisprudence developed a detailed framework for just war (jus ad bellum and jus in bello) that shares concerns with the Western just-war tradition but grounds them in revelation and the duty to establish justice. Peace (*salām*) is the normative state, and war is a limited exception. The term resists reduction to "holy war" as commonly misused in modern discourse.

→ See [[Terms/Jihad]]

### Hindu — Dharma-yuddha

In the Sanskrit epics, war is framed as *dharma-yuddha* (righteous war) when fought according to cosmic duty and the laws of the warrior caste (*kṣatriya-dharma*). The *Bhagavad Gītā* presents Arjuna's dilemma: is it ever right to kill one's own kin? Krishna's answer grounds the duty to fight in the eternal nature of the self and the preservation of social order. Peace is not the absence of conflict but the restoration of *dharma*.

## Status notes

- Western canon section is minimal (three authors) but each passage is dense and commits the author to a clear position. Tolstoy's *War and Peace* is a major work on the theme but the corpus returned only the title page; a future expansion should include substantive passages from the novel (e.g., Pierre's reflections on history and war, Prince Andrei's thoughts on glory and death).
- Non-Western reframings are drawn from existing Term pages (Jihad, Dharma) rather than direct corpus passages, because the query returned no non-Western primary-source material on war and peace. The reframings are brief orienting paragraphs; fuller treatment belongs on the Term pages.
- Grotius passages are from the Campbell translation (1901), which is the only complete English translation in the public domain. A more modern translation (e.g., the 2005 edition by Richard Tuck) would be preferable for scholarly use but is not yet in the corpus.
- Sub-questions could be expanded to include "Total War" and "Pacifism" if the corpus yields relevant passages (e.g., from Kant's *Perpetual Peace*, Clausewitz, or Tolstoy's later writings).
