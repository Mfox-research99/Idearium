# Idea: Labor

## Working definition

Human activity that transforms nature to produce use-values and sustain life. The Western tradition asks whether labor is a curse (Genesis), a source of value (classical economics), a commodity (Marx), or a path to dignity (craft traditions). The question is entangled with property, exploitation, and the division of society into those who work and those who do not.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Labor as value-creating activity

What makes labor productive, and how is its product measured? The classical political economy tradition distinguishes between labor that creates exchange-value and labor that merely reproduces the worker.

### Sub-question: Labor-power versus labor

The distinction between the capacity to work (labor-power) and the actual expenditure of effort (labor) is central to Marx's critique of capitalism. The wage contract purchases the former; the latter is extracted during the working day.

### Sub-question: Necessary labor and surplus labor

The portion of the working day required to reproduce the worker's subsistence versus the portion appropriated by the capitalist. The ratio defines the rate of exploitation.

## Western canon

### Karl Marx — *Capital, Volume I*

> "All those things which labour merely separates from immediate connexion with their environment, are subjects of labour spontaneously provided by Nature. Such are fish which we catch and take from their element, water, timber which we fell in the virgin forest, and ores which we extract from their veins. If, on the other hand, the subject of labour has, so to say, been filtered through previous labour, we call it raw material; such is ore already extracted and ready for washing. All raw material is the subject of labour, but not every subject of labour is raw material: it can only become so, after it has undergone some alteration by means of labour."
>
> *Gloss: Marx defines labor as a purposeful activity that mediates between humanity and nature, distinguishing between natural objects of labor and raw material that has already been shaped by prior work.*

> "The capitalist pays the value, so far as price coincides with value, of the labour-power, and receives in exchange the disposal of the living labour-power itself. His usufruct is spread over two periods. During one the labourer produces a value that is only equal to the value of his labour-power; he produces its equivalent. … During the other period, the period of surplus-labour, the usufruct of the labour-power creates a value for the capitalist without costing him any value."
>
> *Gloss: The distinction between necessary labor (reproducing the worker) and surplus labor (appropriated by capital) is the foundation of Marx's theory of exploitation.*

> "The law of supply and demand of labor falls to pieces. … Not only does the degree of exploitation of the wage-laborer remain indecently low. The wage-laborer loses into the bargain, along with the relation of dependence, also the sentiment of dependence on the abstemious capitalist."
>
> *Gloss: In colonial contexts, the absence of a dependent wage-labor class disrupts capitalist accumulation, revealing that the "free" labor market is historically constructed, not natural.*

## Reframings

### Hindu — Karma (action as labor without attachment)

The Bhagavad Gītā reframes labor as *karma* — action performed without attachment to its fruits. This is not a theory of economic value but a spiritual discipline: work done as duty (*dharma*) for its own sake, offered to the divine, liberates the actor from the cycle of rebirth. The Western category of "labor" as a commodity or a curse is partially reframed as a path of selfless action. No direct passage from the corpus is available; the reframing is drawn from the Term page for [[Terms/Karma]].

### Confucian — Yi (righteousness in work)

In the Confucian tradition, labor is not primarily an economic category but a moral one. The gentleman (*junzi*) engages in work that accords with *yi* (righteousness) and *li* (ritual propriety). Manual labor is not despised, but the highest labor is the cultivation of virtue through study and governance. The Western distinction between productive and unproductive labor does not map cleanly onto this framework.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Wealth]] — Labor is the source of wealth in classical political economy; the distribution of wealth determines who labors and who does not.
- [[Slavery]] — The extreme case of labor-power as property; Marx's analysis of wage-labor as "wage slavery" draws the parallel explicitly.
- [[Property]] — Locke grounds property rights in the mixing of labor with nature; Marx inverts this to argue that capital is accumulated surplus labor.
- [[Virtue_and_Vice]] — The Protestant work ethic treats labor as a moral duty; idleness as vice.
