# Idea: Memory

## Working definition

What is the power by which the past is retained, retrieved, and made present to the mind? The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about the nature of the faculty itself — whether it is a storehouse of images, a function of the body, a power of the soul, or something that can be trained and strengthened. Memory sits at the boundary between sense and intellect, between time and eternity, and between the self that was and the self that is.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Memory as storehouse or faculty

Is memory a container of images and ideas, or an active power of the mind? The tradition divides between those who treat memory as a passive receptacle and those who treat it as a constitutive faculty of the self.

### Sub-question: Memory and the body

How far does memory depend on the physical constitution of the brain or the temper of the body? Disease, fever, and age can strip the mind of its ideas — what does this tell us about the relation of memory to the material substrate?

### Sub-question: Artificial memory and training

Can memory be strengthened by technique — order, commonplaces, verse, narration — and what does this reveal about its nature?

### Sub-question: Memory and the self

Is memory identical with the mind itself? If I can remember past sorrow without feeling sorrow, what does that say about the relation between memory and the affections? And if I can pass beyond memory to approach God, what lies beyond it?

## Western canon

### Plato — *Theaetetus*

> "To think of the place in which we have last seen a thing is often the best way of recalling it to the mind. Hence memory is dependent on association. The act of recollection may be compared to the sight of an object at a great distance which we have previously seen near and seek to bring near to us in thought. Memory is to sense as dreaming is to waking; and like dreaming has a wayward and uncertain power of recalling impressions from the past."
>
> *Gloss: Plato grounds memory in association and compares it to dreaming, treating it as a power that depends on the intensity of perception and emotion — a natural memory allied to sense, shared with children, barbarians, and animals.*

### Augustine — *Confessions*

> "Behold, in the plains, and caves, and caverns of my memory, innumerable and innumerably full of innumerable kinds of things, either through images, as all bodies; or by actual presence, as the arts; or by certain notions or impressions, as the affections of the mind, which, even when the mind doth not feel, the memory retaineth... So great is the force of memory, so great the force of life, even in the mortal life of man. What shall I do then, O Thou my true life, my God? I will pass even beyond this power of mine which is called memory: yea, I will pass beyond it, that I may approach unto Thee, O sweet Light."
>
> *Gloss: Augustine treats memory as a vast inner landscape containing images, arts, and affections — and then insists that God lies beyond memory, not within it. Memory is the self, but the self is not the ultimate.*

### Augustine — *Confessions*

> "The memory then is, as it were, the belly of the mind, and joy and sadness, like sweet and bitter food; which, when committed to the memory, are as it were passed into the belly, where they may be stowed, but cannot taste."
>
> *Gloss: Memory stores affections without experiencing them — a paradox Augustine uses to argue that memory is not identical with the mind's present state, yet is somehow the mind itself.*

### Bacon — *Novum Organum*

> "All these commonplaces materially assist memory, and raise it far above its natural strength. Verse, too, is recollected and learned more easily than prose. From this group of three instances — order, the commonplaces of artificial memory, and verses — is constituted one species of aid for the memory, which may be well termed a separation from infinity."
>
> *Gloss: Bacon treats artificial memory as a technique for "separating from infinity" — imposing order and preconceived structure on the chaos of recall, thereby strengthening a natural faculty.*

### Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*

> "The pictures drawn in our minds are laid in fading colours; and if not sometimes refreshed, vanish and disappear. How much the constitution of our bodies are concerned in this; and whether the temper of the brain makes this difference, that in some it retains the characters drawn on it like marble, in others like freestone, and in others little better than sand, I shall here inquire; though it may seem probable that the constitution of the body does sometimes influence the memory, since we oftentimes find a disease quite strip the mind of all its ideas, and the flames of a fever in a few days calcine all those images to dust and confusion, which seemed to be as lasting as if graved in marble."
>
> *Gloss: Locke treats memory as a physical trace — images drawn in fading colours — and openly speculates that the brain's constitution determines retention, with disease capable of destroying even the most durable ideas.*

### Spinoza — *On the Improvement of Understanding*

> "What, then, is memory? It is nothing else than the actual sensation of impressions on the brain, together with the thought of the definite duration of the sensation... The memory is also strengthened without the aid of the understanding by means of the power wherewith the imagination or the sense called common, is affected by some particular physical object... As, then, the memory is strengthened both with and without the aid of the understanding, we may conclude that it is different from the understanding, and that in the latter considered in itself there is neither memory nor forgetfulness."
>
> *Gloss: Spinoza defines memory as sensation of brain-impressions plus the thought of duration, and argues that memory is distinct from the understanding — the understanding in itself has no memory.*

### Freud — *Three Essays on the Theory of Sexuality*

> "You probably all know from your own experiences the peculiar amnesia, that is, loss of memory, concerning childhood. I mean the fact that the first years, to the fifth, sixth or eighth, have not left the same traces in our memory as have later experiences... The child is able to speak well at the age of two, it soon shows that it can become adjusted to the most complicated psychic situations, and makes remarks which years later are retold to it, but which it has itself entirely forgotten."
>
> *Gloss: Freud identifies childhood amnesia as an archaic characteristic of memory — the earliest years leave no conscious traces despite the child's evident psychic complexity, a phenomenon he will later link to repression.*

## Reframings

### Buddhist tradition — *Smriti* / *Sati*

The Western tradition treats memory primarily as retention and retrieval of the past. The Buddhist term *smriti* (Pali: *sati*) is routinely translated as "mindfulness" or "memory," but it means something closer to present-moment awareness — the capacity to hold an object in attention without forgetfulness. This is not a storehouse of the past but a quality of presence. The Buddha's teaching on the four foundations of mindfulness (*satipaṭṭhāna*) treats memory as a discipline of continuous attention to body, feelings, mind, and phenomena, not as a faculty for recalling what is gone.

→ See [[Terms/Anatman]] (the no-self doctrine that reframes who or what is doing the remembering)

### Hindu tradition — *Smriti*

In the Hindu tradition, *smriti* ("that which is remembered") designates an entire class of authoritative texts — the epics, the Purāṇas, the Dharmaśāstras — as distinct from *śruti* ("that which is heard," the Vedas). Memory here is not a personal faculty but a category of revealed knowledge, transmitted through human recollection rather than direct audition. The term also appears in the Yoga-sūtras where *smriti* is one of the five *vṛttis* (mental modifications) that must be stilled for liberation. The personal memory of past lives is a power acquired through yogic practice, not a natural endowment.

→ See [[Terms/Karma]] (the law that links memory across lives through latent impressions or *saṃskāras*)

## Cross-cuts

- [[Mind]] — Memory is one of the faculties of mind; the question of whether memory is identical with mind or distinct from it runs through Augustine, Spinoza, and Locke.
- [[Soul]] — Memory's relation to the body (Locke's "temper of the brain," Spinoza's brain-impressions) touches the question of whether the soul can retain memory after death.
- [[Time]] — Memory is the faculty that holds the past present; Augustine's meditation on memory in the *Confessions* is inseparable from his meditation on time.
- [[Knowledge]] — For Locke, memory is the repository of ideas that constitutes knowledge; for Plato, recollection (*anamnesis*) is the mechanism by which knowledge is recovered.
- [[Experience]] — Memory stores the impressions of experience; Freud's childhood amnesia raises the question of which experiences are retained and which are lost.
- [[Body]] — The physical substrate of memory (brain, fever, disease) is a persistent theme from Locke through Freud.
