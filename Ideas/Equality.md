# Idea: Equality

## Working definition

Whether all persons are due equal treatment and equal shares of goods, and on what basis — proportion to merit, universal count, or something else. The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about justice and distribution. Other traditions reframe it through cosmic order or relational propriety; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Equality as proportional justice

What it means to give each person their due — equal treatment for equals, unequal treatment for unequals, in proportion to some relevant measure.

### Sub-question: Equality as universal humanitarian consideration

Whether every person counts equally in the moral and legal calculus regardless of merit or station.

## Western canon

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics*

> Book V, chapter 3 (1131a). "If then the Unjust is unequal the Just is equal, which all must allow without further proof: and as the equal is a mean the Just must be also a mean. Now the equal implies two terms at least: it follows then that the Just is both a mean and equal, and these to certain persons; and, in so far as it is a mean, between certain things (that is, the greater and the less), and, so far as it is equal, between two, and in so far as it is just it is so to certain persons. The Just then must imply four terms at least, for those to which it is just are two, and the terms representing the things are two. And there will be the same equality between the terms representing the persons, as between those representing the things: because as the latter are to one another so are the former: for if the persons are not equal they must not have equal shares; in fact this is the very source of all the quarrelling and wrangling in the world, when either they who are equal have and get awarded to them things not equal, or being not equal those things which are equal."
>
> *Gloss: Aristotle defines justice as proportional equality — equals should receive equal shares, unequals unequal shares in proportion to their inequality. This is a geometric proportion (not arithmetic identity), and deviation from it is the root of civil conflict.*

— Trans. W. D. Ross, 1925.

### John Stuart Mill — *Utilitarianism*

> Chapter 5. "Bentham's dictum, 'everybody to count for one, nobody for more than one,' might be written under the principle of utility as an explanatory commentary. The equal claim of everybody to happiness in the estimation of the moralist and the legislator, involves an equal claim to all the means of happiness, except in so far as the inevitable conditions of human life, and the general interest, in which that of every individual is included, set limits to the maxim; and those limits ought to be strictly construed. … All persons are deemed to have a right to equality of treatment, except when some recognised social expediency requires the reverse. And hence all social inequalities which have ceased to be considered expedient, assume the character not of simple inexpediency, but of injustice, and appear so tyrannical, that people are apt to wonder how they could ever have been tolerated."
>
> *Gloss: Mill anchors equality in utility — each person's happiness counts equally, and unequal treatment must be justified by social expediency. Unequal treatment that loses its expedient rationale becomes not merely inefficient but unjust.*

— 1863 (English original).

## Reframings

No non-Western passages were returned from the corpus for this Idea. The Western framing of equality as a distributive or universal-rights question does not have a direct parallel in several traditions. In Confucianism, the value of *Yi* (appropriateness/righteousness) prescribes *differential* treatment according to relationship and context, not uniform consideration. In the Hindu *Dharma* tradition, duties and entitlements vary with *varna* (social class) and *āśrama* (stage of life), making proportional treatment role-specific rather than universal. These reframings resist the very terms of the Western question.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Justice]] — Equality is the structural content of justice for Aristotle; Mill treats unequal treatment as presumptively unjust.
- [[Liberty]] — Equal liberty is a sub-claim within equality; Mill's liberty principle presupposes equal persons.
- [[Democracy]] — Political equality (one person, one vote) is the institutional form of Bentham's dictum.
- [[State]] — The state is the agent that enforces or violates equal treatment.
- [[Good_and_Evil]] — Equality of consideration in the moral calculus is a utilitarian extension of the greatest-happiness principle.
- [[Law]] — Equal protection under law is the juridical expression of the idea.
