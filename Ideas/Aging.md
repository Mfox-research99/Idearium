# Idea: Aging

## Working definition

Aging is the biological and existential process of growing old, raising questions about the duties appropriate to each stage of life, the loss of physical and social powers, the psychology of decline, and the cultural valuation of the elderly. The Western tradition has addressed aging primarily through moral exhortation (Cicero), poetic carpe diem (Ovid), and psychological analysis (Adler). Other traditions reframe aging through filial piety, natural cycles, or the telos of the soul; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Duties of youth and age

How do the moral obligations of the young differ from those of the old? Cicero argues that youth must defer to elders and train for active service, while age should reduce physical labor and increase mental activity.

### Sub-question: The psychology of aging

Adler examines the psychological crisis of aging, especially the loss of social significance and the compensatory envy or avarice that can arise.

### Sub-question: The poetic lament of lost youth

Ovid urges the young to enjoy their prime before it passes, framing aging as an irreversible loss that the wise anticipate.

## Western canon

### Cicero — *Duties*

> *122* XXXIV. Since, too, the duties that properly belong to different times of life are not the same, but some belong to the young, others to those more advanced in years, a word must be said on this distinction also. … It is, then, the duty of a young man to show deference to his elders and to attach himself to the best and most approved of them, so as to receive the benefit of their counsel and influence. For the inexperience of youth requires the practical wisdom of age to strengthen and direct it. … *123* The old, on the other hand, should, it seems, have their physical labours reduced; their mental activities, however, should be actually increased.
>
> *Gloss: Cicero commits to a moral division of labor across the lifespan: youth owes deference and training, age owes counsel and mental exertion.*

Trans. Walter Miller, 1913.

### Ovid — *Art of Love*

> Even now, be mindful of old age, that one day will come; then will no time be passed by you in idleness. Disport yourselves, while yet you may, and while even now you confess to your true years; after the manner of the flowing stream, do the years pass by. Neither shall the water which has past by, be ever recalled; nor can the hour which has past, ever return. You must employ your youthful age; with swift step age is gliding on; and that which follows, is not so pleasing as that which having passed was charming.
>
> *Gloss: Ovid frames aging as a relentless current; the only ethical response is to seize the present before it is lost.*

Trans. Henry T. Riley, 1885.

### Ovid — *Heroides*

> "Robuster now the year, to spring succeeds / "The summer, and a sturdy youth becomes: / "No age is stronger, none more fertile yields / "Its stores, and none with heat more fervid glows. / "Next autumn follows, all the fire of youth / "Allay'd, mature in mildness, just between / "Old age and youth a medium temper holds; / "Some silvery tresses o'er his temples strew'd. / "Then aged winter, frightful object! comes / "With tottering step, and bald appears his head; / "Or snowy white the few remaining hairs. / "Our bodies too themselves submit to change / "Without remission. Nor what we have been, / "Nor what we are, to-morrow shall we be."
>
> *Gloss: Ovid uses the four seasons as an analogy for the stages of human life, ending with winter as a frightful, tottering old age.*

Trans. Henry T. Riley, 1869.

### Alfred Adler — *Understanding Human Nature*

> Let us take this occasion to discuss another relationship which is often used in order to degrade woman: the so-called “dangerous age,” that period which occurs about the fiftieth year, accompanied by the accentuation of certain character traits. Physical changes serve to indicate to woman in the menopause that the bitter time in which she must lose forever that little semblance of significance which she has so laboriously built up during the course of her life, has come. … Our civilization is dominated by a principle in which present performance alone is a source of value; every aging individual, but especially a woman who is growing old, experiences difficulties at this time.
>
> *Gloss: Adler diagnoses the social devaluation of aging women as a crisis of significance, rooted in a culture that prizes only current performance.*

Trans. Walter Béran Wolfe, 1927.

### Alfred Adler — *The Neurotic Constitution*

> Age often intensely stimulates envy and avarice. … No matter how beautifully poets and philosophers endeavor to picture age, it is nevertheless only given to the select souls, to maintain their equilibrium, when they see looming up in the distance the gate which leads to death. Then again the denials and restrictions, which the senium naturally carries with it, and the perceptible dominancy of the younger folks … will almost always lead to a depression of the ego-consciousness.
>
> *Gloss: Adler argues that aging typically provokes envy and avarice as the ego-consciousness is depressed by the loss of status and the approach of death.*

Trans. Paul L. Glueck and John E. Lind, 1917.

## Reframings

### Confucian — Xiao (Filial Piety)

The Confucian tradition does not frame aging primarily as a problem of loss or duty to self, but as a stage that commands reverence from the young. The elder is owed care, deference, and ritual honor (li) as a matter of cosmic and social order. Aging is not a decline to be managed but a status to be respected. The Western emphasis on individual performance (Adler) or carpe diem (Ovid) is largely absent.

→ See [[Terms/Xiao]]

### Daoist — Wu Wei and Natural Cycles

Daoist thought sees aging as part of the natural unfolding of the Dao, to be met with non-action (wu wei) rather than resistance or lament. The body's decay is not a tragedy but a phase of the great transformation. This reframes the Western anxiety about loss of power as a failure to align with the Way.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Death]] — aging is the approach of death; the two Ideas share the question of how to face finitude.
- [[Time]] — aging is the experience of time's passage in the body; Ovid's stream metaphor ties directly to the nature of time.
- [[Body]] — aging is the body's gradual change; the physical dimension is central to both Cicero's reduction of labor and Adler's menopause crisis.
- [[Memory]] — the old are custodians of memory; Cicero assigns them counsel based on experience.
- [[Wisdom]] — age is traditionally associated with wisdom; Cicero's old are to increase mental activities.
- [[Generations]] — the relation between young and old is a generational structure; Cicero's duties of youth to elders and Adler's "dominancy of the younger folks" both engage this.
