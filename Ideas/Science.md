# Idea: Science

## Working definition

What it means to know something systematically — to move from isolated observation to demonstrable, communicable knowledge. The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about method, demonstration, and the boundary between what can be known with certainty and what remains opinion. Other traditions reframe it through the integration of knowledge with practice, or through the subordination of systematic inquiry to spiritual ends; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: The nature of scientific knowledge

What distinguishes science from mere opinion or experience? Is it the certainty of its demonstrations, the method of its inquiry, or the systematic character of its propositions?

### Sub-question: The division of the sciences

How are the sciences ordered and distinguished from one another? What principles govern their boundaries, and which science is most fundamental?

### Sub-question: Science and experience

Is science a departure from ordinary experience or its rational refinement? Does it begin in doubt or in the systematic organization of what we already know?

## Western canon

### Aristotle — *Posterior Analytics*

> *Part 27* — "The science which is knowledge at once of the fact and of the reasoned fact, not of the fact by itself without the reasoned fact, is the more exact and the prior science. A science such as arithmetic, which is not a science of properties qua inhering in a substratum, is more exact than and prior to a science like harmonics, which is a science of properties inhering in a substratum."
>
> *Gloss: Aristotle defines science as knowledge of causes, not merely of facts, and establishes a hierarchy of sciences based on abstraction — the more abstract the subject, the more exact the science.*

### Aristotle — *Posterior Analytics*

> *Part 27* — "Hence it is clear that not every question will be relevant to geometry, nor to medicine, nor to any other science: only those questions will be geometrical which form premisses for the proof of the theorems of geometry or of any other science, such as optics, which uses the same basic truths as geometry. ... There is a limit, then, to the questions which we may put to each man of science; nor is each man of science bound to answer all inquiries on each several subject, but only such as fall within the defined field of his own science."
>
> *Gloss: Each science has its own domain and first principles; the scientist qua scientist is not required to answer questions outside that domain. This is the foundational Western statement of disciplinary boundaries.*

### Kant — *Critique of Pure Reason*

> *Introduction* — "In the solution of the above problem is at the same time comprehended the possibility of the use of pure reason in the foundation and construction of all sciences which contain theoretical knowledge à priori of objects, that is to say, the answer to the following questions: How is pure mathematical science possible? How is pure natural science possible? ... But as to metaphysics, the miserable progress it has hitherto made, and the fact that of no one system yet brought forward, far as regards its true aim, can it be said that this science really exists, leaves an..."
>
> *Gloss: Kant reframes the question of science as a question about the possibility of synthetic a priori judgments — mathematics and natural science are actual, so their possibility must be demonstrable; metaphysics is not yet actual, so its possibility is the problem.*

### Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*

> *Chapter XXI: Of the Division of the Sciences* — "All that can fall within the compass of human understanding, being either, FIRST, the nature of things, as they are in themselves, their relations, and their manner of operation: or, SECONDLY, that which man himself ought to do, as a rational and voluntary agent, for the attainment of any end, especially happiness: or, THIRDLY, the ways and means whereby the knowledge of both the one and the other is attained and communicated."
>
> *Gloss: Locke divides science into three domains — physics (knowledge of nature), ethics (knowledge of right action), and semiotics (knowledge of signs and the means of knowledge itself). This is the empiricist map of the sciences.*

### Dewey — *Democracy and Education*

> *Chapter 17* — "Science is experience becoming rational. The effect of science is thus to change men's idea of the nature and inherent possibilities of experience. By the same token, it changes the idea and the operation of reason. Instead of being something beyond experience, remote, aloof, concerned with a sublime region that has nothing to do with the experienced facts of life, it is found indigenous in experience:—the factor by which past experiences are purified and rendered into tools for discovery and advance."
>
> *Gloss: Dewey rejects the Kantian separation of reason and experience; science is not a transcendent faculty but experience itself rendered methodical and self-correcting.*

### Dewey — *Democracy and Education*

> *Chapter 17* — "A Socrates is thus led to declare that consciousness of ignorance is the beginning of effective love of wisdom, and a Descartes to say that science is born of doubting. ... Our predilection for premature acceptance and assertion, our aversion to suspended judgment, are signs that we tend naturally to cut short the process of testing."
>
> *Gloss: Science begins in the willingness to doubt and to suspend judgment — a habit of mind that runs against our natural inclination toward premature certainty.*

### Kropotkin — *Fields, Factories and Workshops*

> *Page 408* — "The name of 'applied science' is quite misleading, because, in the great majority of cases, invention, far from being an application of science, on the contrary creates a new branch of science. The American bridges were no application of the theory of elasticity; they came before the theory, and all we can say in favour of science is, that in this special branch, theory and practice developed in a parallel way, helping one another."
>
> *Gloss: Kropotkin inverts the common hierarchy — practice often precedes theory, and invention generates new sciences rather than merely applying existing ones.*

## Reframings

### Indian — The sciences as subordinate to the ends of life

The *Kama Sutra* treats the arts and sciences not as autonomous domains of inquiry but as instruments subordinate to the four human aims (dharma, artha, kama, moksha). The "science of Kama" (Kama Shastra) is studied alongside the sciences of Dharma and Artha, and even women are encouraged to study it — against the objection that females should not study any science. The question is not "what can be known systematically" but "what systematic knowledge serves human flourishing in its full scope."

→ See [[Terms/Dharma]], [[Terms/Karma]]

### Chinese — No equivalent term; systematic knowledge as practical wisdom

Classical Chinese thought has no single term corresponding to the Western "science" as an autonomous domain of theoretical inquiry. Systematic knowledge is embedded in the cultivation of virtue (ren), ritual propriety (li), and the attunement of human action to the Dao. The question "how is pure natural science possible?" would be unintelligible in this framework — knowledge is always knowledge-for-action.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Knowledge]] — Science is the systematic form of knowledge; the boundary between them is the central question of epistemology.
- [[Philosophy]] — The Western tradition long treated philosophy as the queen of the sciences; Kant's question about metaphysics as a science is the hinge.
- [[Mathematics]] — For Aristotle and Kant alike, mathematics is the paradigm of exact science; the relation between mathematical and empirical science is a persistent sub-question.
- [[Experience]] — Dewey's reframing of science as "experience becoming rational" collapses the distinction that earlier thinkers maintained.
- [[Logic]] — Aristotle's *Posterior Analytics* treats scientific demonstration as a species of syllogism; logic is the instrument of science.
