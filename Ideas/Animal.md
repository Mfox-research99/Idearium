# Idea: Animal

## Working definition

What distinguishes animals from humans, plants, and gods? The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question of classification (biological, psychological, moral) and of symbolic function (sacrifice, totem, kinship). The boundary between human and animal has been drawn by reason, soul, language, and moral standing; each drawing carries consequences for how we treat animals and how we understand ourselves.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Classification and biological difference

How are animals ordered by their bodily parts, modes of reproduction, and habitats? Aristotle’s zoology provides the foundational Western taxonomy.

### Sub-question: Soul and the human-animal boundary

What faculties do animals share with humans (sensation, memory, emotion) and what is unique to humans (reason, recollection)? This sub-question touches the definition of the soul.

### Sub-question: The animal as sacred kin

In totemic societies, the animal is not a resource but a relative—sacrificed, mourned, and eaten only under ritual law. Freud reads this as a key to the origins of religion and social organization.

## Western canon

### Aristotle — *On the Soul* (History of Animals)

> “Animals also exhibit many differences of disposition. Some are gentle, peaceful, and not violent, as the ox. Some are violent, passionate, and intractable, as the wild boar. Some are prudent and fearful, as the stag and the hare. Serpents are illiberal and crafty. Others, as the lion, are liberal, noble, and generous. … But man is the only animal capable of reasoning, though many others possess the faculty of memory and instruction in common with him. No other animal but man has the power of recollection.”
>
> *Gloss: Aristotle establishes a hierarchy of psychic faculties shared across animals, but reserves reason and recollection for humans alone.*

> “All animals with four feet and no wings have blood. Some of these are viviparous, others oviparous. … The class of serpents, sanguineous land animals, is naturally without feet.”
>
> *Gloss: Aristotle’s classificatory scheme uses blood, feet, and reproduction as primary differentiae, laying the groundwork for biological taxonomy.*

> “Some animals hunt for their food, and some make a store, which others do not. There are also animals which make habitations for themselves, and others which do not. … There are tame animals and wild animals. Man and the mule are always tame, the leopard and the wolf are invariably wild, and others, as the elephant, are easily tamed.”
>
> *Gloss: Behavior and domestication are further axes of animal difference; man is uniquely and permanently tame.*

### Sigmund Freud — *Totem and Taboo*

> “A life which no individual might take and which could be sacrificed only through the consent and participation of all the members of the clan was on the same plane as the life of a member of the kin. … In other words: the sacrificial animal was treated like one of kin; *the sacrificing community, its god, and the sacrificial animal were of the same blood*, and the members of a clan.”
>
> *Gloss: Freud, following Robertson Smith, argues that the totem animal is not a mere symbol but a blood-relative; the sacrificial meal re‑enacts the primal crime and reinforces social bonds.*

> “Certain animals must not be killed or eaten, but men bring up individual animals of these species and take care of them. … An animal that dies accidentally is mourned and buried with the same honours as a member of the tribe. … If the animal is sacrificed by ritual, it is solemnly mourned.”
>
> *Gloss: The totemic code treats the animal as a sacred kinsman, subject to taboo, mourning, and ritual killing—a structure Freud reads as the origin of religion and law.*
