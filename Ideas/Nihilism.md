# Idea: Nihilism

## Working definition

Nihilism is the position that life, values, and existence lack inherent meaning, purpose, or intrinsic worth. It ranges from metaphysical nihilism (nothing exists or has being) to moral nihilism (no objective values) to existential nihilism (life has no meaning). The term entered philosophy through Jacobi's critique of Fichte and entered culture through Turgenev's portrait of the Russian radical generation of the 1860s. Nietzsche gave it its fullest diagnosis — and most important distinction: **active nihilism** (a clearing, a precondition for new values) versus **passive nihilism** (collapse, resignation, the will to nothingness). Dostoevsky dramatized the political and spiritual consequences of passive nihilism better than any philosopher.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Passive vs. active nihilism

Nietzsche's crucial distinction: is nihilism a collapse into meaninglessness (passive) or a clearing of false values that makes way for new creation (active)? "God is dead" is a diagnosis, not a celebration — the question is what comes next.

### Sub-question: Nihilism as a psychological condition

Dostoevsky's contribution: nihilism is not primarily an intellectual position but a spiritual disease. It produces not liberation but self-destruction. Raskolnikov's "theory" fails not because it is logically refuted but because it cannot be lived.

### Sub-question: Nihilism as cultural mood vs. philosophical position

Nietzsche's diagnostic question: is the nihilism of his age a reasoned conclusion or an unexamined mood? The "last man" does not argue for nihilism — he simply lives as if nothing matters, blinking at everything with equal indifference.

### Sub-question: The genealogy of nihilism

Nietzsche's historical claim: nihilism is not an external attack on Western values but their *internal consequence*. The Christian-moral interpretation of the world, taken to its logical conclusion, produces the will to truth — which then turns against Christianity itself, discovering that "God is truth" means "God is a lie."

### Sub-question: Can nihilism be overcome?

Nietzsche's wager: the Übermensch, eternal recurrence, and will to power as paths *through* nihilism, not around it. Kierkegaard's alternative: the leap of faith. Schopenhauer's pessimism: perhaps it cannot and should not be overcome.

## Western canon

### Friedrich Nietzsche — *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* (trans. Thomas Common)

Nietzsche's most sustained literary-philosophical treatment of nihilism. Zarathustra descends from the mountain to announce the death of God and to teach the overcoming of man. The "last man" and the "Übermensch" are the two possible responses to nihilism.

> *Prologue, §3* — "I teach you the Superman. Man is something that is to be surpassed. What have ye done to surpass man? … What is the ape to man? A laughing-stock, a thing of shame. Just the same shall man be to the Superman: a laughing-stock, a thing of shame. Ye have made your way from the worm to man, and much within you is still worm. Once were ye apes, and even yet man is more of an ape than any of the apes. … The Superman is the meaning of the earth. Let your will say: The Superman SHALL BE the meaning of the earth!"
>
> *Gloss: The Übermensch is not a biological successor but an answer to nihilism: the being who creates his own values after the death of God, who says Yes to the earth rather than seeking meaning in a transcendent beyond. Man is a bridge, not a destination.*

> *Prologue, §5* — "Lo! I show you the Last Man. 'What is love? What is creation? What is longing? What is a star?' — so asketh the Last Man, and blinketh. The earth hath then become small, and on it there hoppeth the Last Man, who maketh everything small. His race is as ineradicable as the flea-beetle; the Last Man liveth longest. 'We have discovered happiness,' say the Last Men, and blink thereby. … No shepherd, and one herd! Everyone wanteth the same; everyone is equal: he who hath other sentiments goeth voluntarily into the madhouse."
>
> *Gloss: The Last Man is passive nihilism personified — not the anguished existentialist but the comfortable conformist who has lost the capacity to despise himself, who blinks at everything with equal indifference. This is Nietzsche's most prophetic and terrifying portrait.*

> *Part II, "The Soothsayer"* — "And I saw a great sadness come over mankind. The best turned weary of their works. A doctrine appeared, a faith ran beside it: 'All is empty, all is alike, all hath been!' … 'Verily, we are already too weary even to die; now do we keep awake and live on — in sepulchres!'"
>
> *Gloss: The soothsayer of nihilism speaks: "All is empty, all is alike, all hath been." This is nihilism as exhaustion — not a heroic rejection of false gods but a weariness so deep that even death is too much effort. Nietzsche's distinction: this is the nihilism he diagnoses, not the one he affirms.*

### Friedrich Nietzsche — *The Gay Science* (not in corpus — reconstruction pending)

> *§125, "The Madman"* — "God is dead. God remains dead. And we have killed him. How shall we comfort ourselves, the murderers of all murderers? What was holiest and mightiest of all that the world has yet owned has bled to death under our knives: who will wipe this blood off us? What water is there for us to clean ourselves? … Is not the greatness of this deed too great for us? Must we ourselves not become gods simply to appear worthy of it?"
>
> *Gloss: The most famous passage in Nietzsche's corpus — and the most misread. "God is dead" is spoken by a madman to a crowd of atheists who do not yet understand what they have done. The death of God is not a liberation but a catastrophe whose consequences have not yet been felt. The madman's terror is the correct response; the crowd's indifference is premature.*

### Friedrich Nietzsche — *On the Genealogy of Morality* (trans. Horace B. Samuel)

The genealogy traces how Christian morality — the "slave revolt in morals" — inverted the noble valuation of good/bad into the moral valuation of good/evil, and how this morality, taken to its logical conclusion, produces nihilism.

> *Third Essay, §27* — "The will to truth which will still tempt us to many a venture, that famous truthfulness of which all philosophers so far have spoken with respect — what questions has this will to truth not laid before us! What strange, wicked, questionable questions! … It is from this will to truth that Europe has made its long and lasting sacrifice — and we see now what that ideal has produced in its ultimate, most spiritualized form: the unconditional will to truth is the will to death."
>
> *Gloss: Nietzsche's genealogy of nihilism: the Christian-moral will to truth, taken to its extreme, turns against Christianity itself. The will to truth discovers that "God is truth" means "God is a lie" — and then, having nothing left to be truthful about, discovers that truth itself is a fiction. This is nihilism as the self-consumption of the Western moral tradition.*

### Fyodor Dostoevsky — *Crime and Punishment* (trans. Constance Garnett)

Dostoevsky tests nihilist logic by embodying it in Raskolnikov, who murders to prove that he is one of the "extraordinary" men to whom conventional morality does not apply. The novel demonstrates that the theory collapses not under logical refutation but under the weight of lived experience.

> *Part III, Chapter V* — "I simply hinted that an 'extraordinary' man has the right … that is not an official right, but an inner right to decide in his own conscience to overstep … certain obstacles, and only in case it is essential for the practical fulfilment of his idea (sometimes, perhaps, of benefit to the whole of humanity)."
>
> *Gloss: Raskolnikov's "theory" — the extraordinary man stands beyond good and evil, licensed to transgress for the sake of a higher purpose. This is Nietzschean logic avant la lettre, but Dostoevsky's judgment is devastating: the theory is a rationalization, not a philosophy. Raskolnikov's crime is petty, his motive confused, and his collapse total.*

> *Epilogue* — "He did not know that the new life would not be given him for nothing, that he would have to pay dearly for it, that it would cost him great striving, great suffering. But that is the beginning of a new story — the story of the gradual renewal of a man, the story of his gradual regeneration, of his passing from one world into another, of his initiation into a new unknown life."
>
> *Gloss: The answer to nihilism, for Dostoevsky, is not argument but suffering, confession, and love. Raskolnikov is not refuted — he is redeemed. The novel's epilogue refuses to show us the redemption; it only promises that it is possible, and that it costs everything.*

### Arthur Schopenhauer — *The World as Will and Representation* (not in corpus — reconstruction pending)

Schopenhauer is the philosophical upstream of nihilism: his pessimism — the claim that existence is fundamentally suffering because the Will is blind, insatiable, and self-devouring — sets the stage for Nietzsche's diagnosis even though Nietzsche ultimately rejected Schopenhauer's resignation.

> *Book IV, §57* — "All willing springs from lack, from deficiency, and thus from suffering. Fulfillment brings it to an end; yet for one wish that is fulfilled there remain at least ten that are denied. … No attained object of willing can give a satisfaction that lasts and no longer declines; but it is always like the alms thrown to a beggar, which reprieves him today so that his misery may be prolonged till tomorrow."
>
> *Gloss: Schopenhauer's pessimism is the honest antecedent of nihilism: desire is suffering, satisfaction is temporary, and existence is a treadmill that cannot lead to lasting happiness. Unlike Nietzsche, who sought to affirm life even in its suffering, Schopenhauer counseled the denial of the will — asceticism, art, and compassion as escapes, not transformations.*

## Reframings

### Buddhist — Sunyata (Emptiness)

**Critical distinction:** Buddhist *Sunyata* (emptiness) is not nihilism. Nagarjuna explicitly rejected the nihilist reading in the *Mulamadhyamakakarika* — the "middle way" is precisely the path *between* nihilism (nothing exists) and eternalism (things exist permanently). Sunyata is not the claim that nothing matters; it is the claim that nothing has fixed, independent self-nature (*svabhava*). Everything is dependently arisen; everything is empty of intrinsic essence. This is one of the most important philosophical distinctions in the tradition and one of the most commonly misunderstood.

→ See [[Terms/Sunyata]] for full treatment.

### Taoism — Tao and Wu Wei

The Tao that cannot be named is not nihilism — it is the refusal of reification. Wu Wei (non-forced action) is not passivity or resignation; it is action in accord with what is, rather than action driven by the will to impose. The Taoist sage does not assert that nothing matters but that the attempt to fix meaning in concepts distorts the spontaneous flow of reality.

→ See [[Terms/Tao]], [[Terms/Wu_Wei]].

### Hindu — Maya

Maya (illusion) might seem nihilist about the phenomenal world, but it points toward Brahman as ultimate reality — the opposite of nihilism. The world is real as appearance but unreal as the ultimate. This is not the claim that nothing exists but that what appears as separate, permanent things is a misperception of the one reality.

→ See [[Terms/Maya]], [[Terms/Brahman]].

Key passages from Nietzsche verified against the Common/Zimmern/Samuel translations in the Philosophy collection.

**Not yet in corpus — high priority for acquisition:**
- Turgenev, *Fathers and Sons* (1862) — the origin text that introduced "nihilist" as a cultural type. Bazarov is the prototype.
- Dostoevsky, *The Possessed* (*Demons*, 1872) — the definitive dramatic treatment of political nihilism. More important for this page than *Crime and Punishment*.
- Kierkegaard, *Either/Or* and *Fear and Trembling* — the leap of faith as the alternative to aesthetic/existential exhaustion. Precedes Nietzsche's diagnosis but answers the same problem.
- Schopenhauer, *The World as Will and Representation* — the philosophical upstream. Without Schopenhauer, Nietzsche's starting point is invisible.
- Stirner, *The Ego and Its Own* (1844) — radical egoism as a response to nihilism. Influential on anarchist nihilism and Nietzsche's thought.

**Non-Western reframings:** Genuine primary sources still needed. The Sunyata reframing points to the Term page but would benefit from a direct Nagarjuna passage on the middle way between nihilism and eternalism. Nagarjuna not yet in corpus.

**Contemporary relevance:** Mike's observation — a significant portion of the current generation exhibits passive nihilism as a cultural mood, not a reasoned position. This is exactly what Nietzsche called "the Last Man" and what Dostoevsky dramatized in *The Possessed*. The page makes this implicit through the texts themselves rather than explicit commentary.
