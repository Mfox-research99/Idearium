# Idea: Punishment

## Working definition

What harm may be inflicted on a person by authority, and for what ends, in response to wrongdoing. The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about the state's right to coerce, the proportionality of sanctions, and the moral purposes — retribution, deterrence, rehabilitation, or incapacitation — that justify or limit that coercion. Other traditions reframe it through cosmic consequence or ritual purification; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Definition and limits of punishment

What distinguishes punishment from mere hostility, revenge, or natural consequence? Hobbes insists on public authority, prior law, and proportionality as necessary conditions.

### Sub-question: Purposes of punishment

Is punishment primarily backward-looking (retribution for a wrong) or forward-looking (deterrence, reform, protection)? Nietzsche catalogues the bewildering multiplicity of ends that have been attached to the same procedure.

### Sub-question: Divine and human punishment

How does the justice of God relate to the justice of the state? Grotius treats the Gospel as limiting the scope of human punishment, while Hobbes distinguishes natural evil consequences (divine punishment) from civil punishment.

## Western canon

### Thomas Hobbes — *Leviathan*

> "Harme inflicted for a Fact done before there was a Law that forbad it, is not Punishment, but an act of Hostility: For before the Law, there is no transgression of the Law; But Punishment supposeth a fact judged, to have been a transgression of the Law; Therefore Harme inflicted before the Law made, is not Punishment, but an act of Hostility."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes establishes the principle of legality — no punishment without a pre-existing law — as a necessary condition distinguishing punishment from war.*

> "If the harm inflicted be lesse than the benefit, or contentment that naturally followeth the crime committed, that harm is not within the definition; and is rather the Price, or Redemption, than the Punishment of a Crime: Because it is of the nature of Punishment, to have for end, the disposing of men to obey the Law; which end (if it be lesse that the benefit of the transgression) it attaineth not, but worketh a contrary effect."
>
> *Gloss: Punishment must outweigh the expected gain from crime; otherwise it fails its deterrent purpose and becomes merely a price.*

> "Exile, (Banishment) is when a man is for a crime, condemned to depart out of the dominion of the Common-wealth … and seemeth not in its own nature, without other circumstances, to be a Punishment; but rather an escape, or a publique commandement to avoid Punishment by flight."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes argues that banishment without confiscation of goods is no punishment, because it does not deter and may harm the commonwealth by creating an enemy.*

*Hobbes, Leviathan, Part II, Chapter 28. Trans. author, 1651.*

### Hugo Grotius — *The Rights of War and Peace*

> "General utility of punishments … What is determined by the law of the Gospel, in this respect … Capital punishments objected to as cutting off all possibility of repentance … Internal acts not punishable by man … Open acts, when inevitable through human infirmity not punishable … Actions, neither directly nor indirectly injurious to society, not punishable by human laws … The opinion, that pardon can never be granted, refuted … Pardon shewn to be allowable before the establishment of penal law … Allowable also subsequently to the establishment of penalties."
>
> *Gloss: Grotius frames punishment within natural law and Gospel mercy, limiting human jurisdiction to acts that harm society and allowing pardon as a legitimate exercise of authority.*

*Hugo Grotius, The Rights of War and Peace, Book II, Chapter 20. Trans. unknown (early English edition), 1625.*

### Friedrich Nietzsche — *On the Genealogy of Morality*

> "Punishment, as the payment of a fee stipulated for by the power which protects the evil-doer from the excesses of revenge.—Punishment, as a compromise with the natural phenomenon of revenge … Punishment as a declaration and measure of war against an enemy of peace, of law, of order, of authority …"
>
> *Gloss: Nietzsche catalogues the shifting, contingent meanings of punishment across history, denying that any single essence or purpose defines it.*

> "It is nowadays impossible to say definitely *the precise reason* for punishment: all ideas, in which a whole process is promiscuously comprehended, elude definition; it is only that which has no history, which can be defined."
>
> *Gloss: The historical sedimentation of punishment's functions makes it indefinable in principle — a claim that challenges every systematic theory of punishment.*

*Friedrich Nietzsche, On the Genealogy of Morality, Second Essay, §13–14. Trans. Horace B. Samuel, 1913.*

## Reframings

### Indian — Karma

The concept of *karma* reframes punishment as an impersonal cosmic law of moral cause and effect, not an act of authority. Suffering is not inflicted by a judge but arises from the agent's own past actions across lifetimes. The question shifts from "what may the state do?" to "what have I done to deserve this?" — and the answer is not retributive but karmically corrective.

→ See [[Terms/Karma]]

### Chinese — Li and punishment

The Confucian tradition subordinates punishment to ritual propriety (*li*) and moral education. Punishment is a last resort for those who cannot be reformed by example and shame; its use signals a failure of governance. The Legalist tradition (Han Fei, Shang Yang) reverses this, making punishment the primary instrument of order. The corpus lacks direct passages for this reframing; it is noted here as a known contrast.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Justice]] — punishment is the enforcement side of retributive and distributive justice.
- [[Law]] — punishment presupposes a legal order; Hobbes makes this definitional.
- [[State]] — the sovereign's monopoly on punishment is a defining feature of the commonwealth.
- [[Good_and_Evil]] — punishment is justified by the evil of crime and the good of deterrence or reform.
- [[Will]] — Nietzsche ties punishment to the will to power and the creditor-debtor relationship.
