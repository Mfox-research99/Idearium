# Idea: Space

## Working definition

What is space — a real container independent of its contents, a relational order among coexisting things, or a form of human sensibility that structures all outer experience? The Western tradition has asked this question primarily through physics, metaphysics, and epistemology, with the Newtonian absolute space, Leibnizian relational space, and Kantian transcendental space as the three great competing positions. Other traditions approach the question through emptiness, void, and the ground of appearance; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Absolute vs. Relational space

Is space a substance or entity in its own right (Newton, Clarke), or merely the order of coexistence among bodies (Leibniz)?

### Sub-question: A priori vs. Empirical space

Is the concept of space derived from experience, or is it a necessary precondition for any experience of outer objects?

### Sub-question: Space and measurement

How do we measure space, and what is the relation between spatial extension and temporal duration?

## Western canon

### Augustine — *Confessions*

> *Book XI, Chapter 26* — "Do I then measure, O my God, and know not what I measure? I measure the motion of a body in time; and the time itself do I not measure? Or could I indeed measure the motion of a body how long it were, and in how long space it could come from this place to that, without measuring the time in which it is moved? … Thus measure we the spaces of stanzas, by the spaces of the verses, and the spaces of the verses, by the spaces of the feet, and the spaces of the feet, by the spaces of the syllables, and the spaces of long, by the space of short syllables; not measuring by pages (for then we measure spaces, not times)…"
>
> *Gloss: Augustine treats spatial and temporal measurement as parallel problems, showing that we measure motion through space by time, and that the measurement of spatial extension (verse-length) is always entangled with temporal duration (utterance).*

### Leibniz — *The Monadology and Other Philosophical Writings*

> "‘Real’ space is the order of co-existing things" and is inseparable from the things themselves. God alone can have a perfectly adequate notion of it and can thus actually perceive its continuity. But we can form an abstract or ‘imaginary’ notion of space, by thinking it as distinct from (or indifferent to) the co-existing things of which it is an ‘order’; and this imaginary space is, of course, homogeneous and continuous."
>
> *Gloss: Leibniz commits to the relational view: space is not a substance but the order of coexistence among things. The "imaginary" homogeneous space we conceive is an abstraction, not a reality.*

### Kant — *Critique of Pure Reason*

> *Transcendental Aesthetic, §2* — "Space then is a necessary representation à priori, which serves for the foundation of all external intuitions. We never can imagine or make a representation to ourselves of the non-existence of space, though we may easily enough think that no objects are found in it. It must, therefore, be considered as the condition of the possibility of phenomena, and by no means as a determination dependent on them, and is a representation à priori, which necessarily supplies the basis for external phenomena. … Space is no discursive, or as we say, general conception of the relations of things, but a pure intuition."
>
> *Gloss: Kant rejects both the Newtonian absolute (space as real container) and the Leibnizian relational (space as concept of relations) in favor of a transcendental idealist position: space is the a priori form of outer sense, a pure intuition that makes experience possible but does not apply to things in themselves.*

## Reframings

### Buddhist — Emptiness (Śūnyatā)

The Western debate about space as container, relation, or form of intuition presupposes a subject-object duality that Buddhist thought questions at a deeper level. In the Mādhyamaka tradition, space (ākāśa) is often used as an example of a non-substantial entity — it has no own-being, it is merely the absence of obstruction. This is not a theory of space but a demonstration of emptiness itself. The question "what is space?" becomes a diagnostic for the reification of concepts.

→ See [[Terms/Sunyata]]

### Daoist — Void (Xu)

The Daoist tradition does not treat space as a problem for epistemology or physics but as the productive emptiness that makes function possible. The *Tao Te Ching* speaks of the hollow of a wheel, the empty space of a room, the void of a vessel — not as absence but as the condition of usefulness. This reframes space as active potential rather than passive container or subjective form.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Time]] — Augustine and Kant treat space and time as parallel forms; the measurement of one is entangled with the other.
- [[Motion]] — Space is the field in which motion occurs; Aristotle and Newton define motion as change of place.
- [[Infinity]] — The infinite divisibility of space raises questions about continuity and the nature of the continuum.
- [[Matter]] — Space is either the container of matter (Newton) or the order of material coexistence (Leibniz).
- [[Geometry]] — Kant argues that geometry is synthetic a priori because its axioms are grounded in the pure intuition of space.
