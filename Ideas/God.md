# Idea: God

## Working definition

What is the ultimate divine reality, and how is it known or related to? The Western tradition asks this primarily through questions of existence, nature, attributes, and relation to creation and humanity. Other traditions reframe it through impersonal cosmic order, non-dual awareness, or radical immanence; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Nature of God

What attributes define the divine—goodness, love, self-sufficiency, transcendence? How does God relate to other beings called "gods"?

### Sub-question: Knowledge of God

How can humans know God—through revelation, reason, mystical union, or not at all? What are the limits of such knowledge?

### Sub-question: Critique and Transformation of the Concept of God

How have modern thinkers questioned or reimagined the inherited conception of God, and what new figures (e.g., the "tempter-god") emerge?

## Western canon

### 1 John — The Holy Bible (King James Version)

> *1 John 4:8, 16* — "He that loveth not knoweth not God; for God is love. … God is love; and he that dwelleth in love dwelleth in God, and God in him."
>
> *Gloss: The Johannine epistles commit to the identification of God with love itself, making love the essential attribute and the mode of divine indwelling.*

### Hermes Trismegistus — Hermetica

> *Corpus Hermeticum, §14 (Scott 1924)* — "God alone is the Good, and nothing but God is the Good. … For the Good is the most incomprehensible of all things, and it is also the most unlike God, even as God Himself is."
>
> *Gloss: The Hermetic tradition insists on the absolute uniqueness of God's goodness, distinguishing it from all derivative goods and from any human or daemonic claim to goodness.*

### Augustine — City of God

> *Book X, ch. 1 (trans. Marcus Dods, 1887, attribution uncertain)* — "And where it is said, 'He is to be feared above all gods,' the reason is forthwith added, for it follows, 'for all the gods of the nations are idols, but the Lord made the heavens.' He said, 'above all gods,' but added, 'of the nations;' that is to say, above all those whom the nations count gods, in other words, demons. … But the same Scripture also calls men who belong to God's people 'gods': 'I have said, Ye are gods, and all of you children of the Most High.' Accordingly, when God is styled God of gods, this may be understood of these gods."
>
> *Gloss: Augustine distinguishes the one Creator God from both pagan deities (demons) and deified humans, while preserving the biblical language of 'gods' for the faithful—a key move in Christian monotheistic polemic.*

### Meister Eckhart — Sermons

> *Sermon on the Eternal Generation (Pfeiffer/Evans 1924)* — "God loves for his own sake, works for his own sake alone, that is to say, he loves for love's sake and works for the sake of the work; for God would never, surely, have begotten in eternity his only Son if born meant never being born. Accordingly holy men declare the Son is born eternally in the sense that he is ever being born. And God would never have made the world if creation meant not being created, — but God has in suchwise created the world that he is still creating it incessantly."
>
> *Gloss: Eckhart radicalizes divine self-sufficiency into an eternal, ongoing act of generation and creation—God's love and work have no external motive, and the Son's birth is a perpetual present.*

### Meister Eckhart — Sermons (Knowledge of God)

> *Sermon on Moses (Pfeiffer/Evans 1924)* — "Moses besought the Lord his God saying, 'Lord, why is thy wrath enkindled against thy people?' God answered and said, 'Moses, let me alone, grudge not, permit, consent, that my wrath be kindled and that I reckon with the people.' … What does he mean by saying, 'Moses besought the Lord his God'? Verily, if God is thy Lord then thou art his servant, but if thou dost work for the good of thyself or for thy own pleasure then in truth thou art not his servant, for thou art seeking not only God's glory but thine own profit."
>
> *Gloss: Eckhart uses the Moses narrative to probe the conditions of true knowledge of God: one must serve God solely for God's own sake, not for personal gain, or else God is not truly one's Lord.*

### Friedrich Nietzsche — Beyond Good and Evil

> *Aphorism 295 (trans. Helen Zimmern, 1907)* — "The genius of the heart, as that great mysterious one possesses it, the tempter-god and born rat-catcher of consciences, whose voice can descend into the nether-world of every soul, who neither speaks a word nor casts a glance in which there may not be some motive or touch of allurement, to whose perfection it pertains that he knows how to appear,—not as he is, but in a guise which acts as an ADDITIONAL constraint on his followers to press ever closer to him, to follow him more cordially and thoroughly."
>
> *Gloss: Nietzsche replaces the traditional God with a 'tempter-god' who seduces souls through concealment and allure—a deliberate inversion of the Christian God of revelation and a figure for the philosopher's own art of psychological depth.*

## Reframings

### Hindu — Brahman

The Western idea of a personal, creator God is only a partial fit for the Hindu concept of Brahman, which is the impersonal, non-dual ground of all existence, beyond attributes and often described as *neti neti* ("not this, not that"). The personal gods (Ishvara, Vishnu, Shiva) are manifestations within Maya, not the ultimate reality itself.

→ See [[Terms/Brahman]]

### Daoist — Tao

The Dao is not a creator or ruler but the unnameable, spontaneous way of nature. The Western question "Does God exist?" is replaced by "How does the Tao flow?" The Daoist tradition has no analogue for divine will, judgment, or providence.

→ See [[Terms/Tao]]

### Buddhist — Sunyata / Nirvana

Buddhism systematically rejects a creator God (theism is seen as a form of clinging) and instead points to emptiness (Sunyata) and the unconditioned (Nirvana) as the ultimate. The Western God-concept is reframed as a useful but ultimately empty designation.

→ See [[Terms/Sunyata]] and [[Terms/Nirvana]]
