# Idea: Man

## Working definition

What is a human being? The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about nature, reason, soul, and the relation between the animal and the divine. The answer oscillates between defining man by his rational faculty, his fallen condition, his capacity for grace, or his social and political existence. Other traditions reframe the question through cosmic identity (Purusha) or relational virtue (Ren); see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

- **Nature of man:** What distinguishes man from other beings? Reason, soul, language, or something else?
- **The two men:** The inner vs. outer man, the old vs. new man, the animal vs. spiritual man.
- **Man and God:** How does man relate to the divine? As image, as servant, as participant?
- **Man and society:** Is man naturally social, or is society a remedy for his defects?

## Western canon

### Augustine — *City of God*

> *Book XIV, ch. 2* — "For Paul says very plainly to the Corinthians, 'For whereas there is among you envying and strife, are ye not carnal, and walk according to man?' So that to walk according to man and to be carnal are the same; for by *flesh*, that is, by a part of man, man is meant. … It is to men of this kind, then, that is, to animal men, he shortly after says, 'And I, brethren, could not speak unto you as unto spiritual, but as unto carnal.' And this is to be interpreted by the same usage, a part being taken for the whole."
>
> *Gloss:* Augustine uses the Pauline distinction between the "animal man" (who cannot perceive the things of God) and the "spiritual man" to argue that "walking according to man" is equivalent to being carnal — a fallen state that defines human nature apart from grace.

### Meister Eckhart — *Sermons* (Pfeiffer/Evans, 1924)

> *The Book of Benedictus* — "Our Lord says in the Gospel: 'A certain nobleman went out into a far country to receive for himself a kingdom, and returned.' In these words our Lord teaches us how noble man is by nature and how divine is that to which he may attain by grace. … To start with you must know and it is manifest that man has in him two natures: body and spirit. … The other man, within us, is the inner man. The scriptures call him the new man, the heavenly man, the young man, the friend, the nobleman."
>
> *Gloss:* Eckhart posits a dual anthropology: an outward man entangled with the flesh and an inward man who is noble and capable of union with God. The nobility of man lies in his capacity for grace, not in his fallen state.

### Spinoza — *Ethics*

> *Part IV, Proposition 35, Corollaries* — "There is no individual thing in nature, which is more useful to man, than a man who lives in obedience to reason. For that thing is to man most useful, which is most in harmony with his nature … that is, obviously, man. But man acts absolutely according to the laws of his nature, when he lives in obedience to reason … wherefore among individual things nothing is more useful to man, than a man who lives in obedience to reason."
>
> *Gloss:* Spinoza defines man's essence as rational activity; the man who lives by reason is the most useful to other men because he acts in harmony with human nature. This is a naturalistic, non-theological anthropology.

## Reframings

### Hindu — Purusha

The Vedic tradition identifies the cosmic Person (Purusha) as both the primordial sacrifice and the inner self of all beings. In the commentary on the Rig Veda (Śaṅkara on the Vedānta-sūtras), Agni Vaishvanara is spoken of as "man" (purusha) and the highest Lord is the Self of everything, "man-like" in that he has heaven for his head and earth for his feet. This reframes the question of man not as a species definition but as a cosmic identity: the individual self (ātman) is ultimately identical with the universal Self (Brahman). The English word "man" here translates *purusha*, which carries a far broader metaphysical meaning than the Western concept of a human being.

> *Vedānta-sūtras with Śaṅkara's commentary* (Thibaut, 1896) — "The Vājasaneyins speak of him—in their sacred text—as man, 'This Agni Vaishvanara is man; he who knows this Agni Vaishvanara as man-like, as abiding within man,' &c. … The highest Lord, on the other hand, who is the Self of everything, may be spoken of as well as man, as abiding within man."
>
> *Gloss:* The term "man" (purusha) is applied to both the cosmic fire and the highest Lord, indicating that human identity is a microcosm of the divine.

### Chinese — Ren

The Confucian tradition asks not "what is man?" but "what does it mean to be fully human?" The answer is *ren* (仁), often translated as "humaneness" or "benevolence." It is not a descriptive category but an ethical achievement: one becomes human through relational virtue. The Western Idea of "Man" as a fixed nature is thus reframed as a dynamic process of cultivation.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Soul]] — The inner/outer man distinction maps onto the faculties of the soul.
- [[Nature]] — Man's nature is the central question; Spinoza and Augustine disagree on whether it is rational or fallen.
- [[Grace]] — Eckhart and Augustine both tie the nobility of man to grace, not nature.
- [[Reason]] — Spinoza and Augustine both use reason as the defining mark of man, but with different conclusions.
- [[Body]] — The "outward man" is the body and its senses; the "inward man" is the spirit.

## Status notes

**v1 draft.** The Western canon section is thin — only three authors, and none from the classical Greek or medieval scholastic traditions. Ideally this page would include Plato (*Republic* on the tripartite soul), Aristotle (*Nicomachean Ethics* on man as a political animal), Aquinas (*Summa Theologica* on the definition of the human person), and early modern figures like Descartes (mind-body dualism) and Kant (man as an end in himself). The corpus search for "man" returned many passages, but most were either too diffuse (Jung's word-association study, Krafft-Ebing's case histories, Lilly's astrology) or misattributed (the "Raggedy Man" poem). The three passages selected are dense and commit their authors to clear positions. The Vedic reframing is based on a single passage from the Vedānta-sūtras commentary; a fuller treatment would require direct passages from the Purusha-sūkta (Rig Veda 10.90). The Confucian reframing is noted but lacks a primary-source passage in the corpus; it is included as a pointer to the Ren Term page. Future work should add at least Aristotle and Aquinas to the Western canon, and a direct passage from the Rig Veda for the Purusha reframing.
