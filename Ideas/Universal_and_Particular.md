# Idea: Universal_and_Particular

## Working definition

What is the relationship between the general and the individual? The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about knowledge (how do we move from particular experiences to universal truths?), about being (do universals exist independently of particulars?), and about action (how do universal principles apply to particular cases?). The framing originates in Plato’s theory of Forms and Aristotle’s logic, and it remains a central axis of metaphysics, epistemology, and ethics.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Logical and Epistemic — Demonstration and Definition

How does the universal function in scientific proof and in defining the essence of things? Aristotle argues that demonstration from commensurately universal premises yields the cause, and that definition must be universal and commensurate.

### Sub-question: Moral — Universal Knowledge and Particular Action

How does a person who knows the universal good nevertheless act against it? Aristotle’s account of akrasia turns on the distinction between having a universal proposition and applying it to the particular case.

### Sub-question: Pragmatic — Generic Unity and the Utility of Kinds

What is the pragmatic meaning of the claim that things exist in kinds? William James argues that generic unity is the most momentous pragmatic specification of the world’s oneness, because without it logic and reasoning from past to future would be impossible.

## Western canon

### Plato — *Republic* (Introduction by Benjamin Jowett)

> "Such ideals are framed partly by the omission of particulars, partly by imagination perfecting the form which experience supplies… Plato represents these ideals in a figure as belonging to another world."
>
> *Gloss: Jowett’s commentary summarizes Plato’s view that universals (Forms) are arrived at by abstracting from particulars and are ontologically separate.*

### Aristotle — *Posterior Analytics*

> "Consequently commensurately universal demonstration is superior as more especially proving the cause, that is the reasoned fact."
>
> *Gloss: Universal demonstration is superior because it reveals the cause, not merely the fact.*

> "Every definition is always universal and commensurate: the physician does not prescribe what is healthy for a single eye, but for all eyes or for a determinate species of eye."
>
> *Gloss: Definitions must be universal; the procedure should move from species to genera to avoid equivocation.*

> "Some occurrences are universal (for they are, or come-to-be what they are, always and in every case); others again are not always what they are but only as a general rule… So connexions which embody a general rule… will also derive from immediate basic premisses."
>
> *Gloss: The universal holds always and in every instance; the general rule holds only for the most part, and its middle term must also be a general rule.*

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics*

> "Again, as there are two kinds of propositions affecting action, universal and particular, there is no reason why a man may not act against his Knowledge, having both propositions in his mind, using the universal but not the particular, for the particulars are the objects of moral action."
>
> *Gloss: Akrasia is possible when the agent grasps the universal premise but fails to apply the particular minor premise to the concrete situation.*

### William James — *Pragmatism*

> "Things exist in kinds, there are many specimens in each kind, and what the 'kind' implies for one specimen, it implies also for every other specimen of that kind. We can easily conceive that every fact in the world might be singular, that is, unlike any other fact and sole of its kind. In such a world of singulars our logic would be useless, for logic works by predicating of the single instance what is true of all its kind."
>
> *Gloss: Generic unity is the pragmatic condition for reasoning; without kinds, logic and induction would be impossible.*

## Reframings

### Chinese Philosophy — Li (Principle) and Qi (Material Force)

The Western universal/particular distinction maps imperfectly onto the Chinese pair *li* (principle, pattern) and *qi* (material force, ether). In Neo-Confucianism, *li* is the universal principle inherent in all things, but it is not a separate realm of Forms; it is immanent in the particular *qi* configurations. The question is not whether universals exist apart from particulars, but how principle is realized in concrete material circumstances. ### Indian Philosophy — Sāmānya and Viśeṣa

In Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika metaphysics, universals (*sāmānya*) and particulars (*viśeṣa*) are both real categories. A universal inheres in many particulars, while a particular (*viśeṣa*) is the ultimate differentiator that makes each atom unique. This is a realist ontology that avoids both Platonic transcendence and nominalist reduction. No representative quotation from the corpus is available.
