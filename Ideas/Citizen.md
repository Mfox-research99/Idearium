# Idea: Citizen

## Working definition

Who belongs to a political community, and what rights and duties does that membership entail. The Western tradition frames this primarily through the Greek *polites* — one who shares in ruling and being ruled — and later through the Roman *civis* and the modern citizen as bearer of rights against the state. Other traditions reframe membership through religious community, filial obligation, or cosmic duty; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions (optional)

### Sub-question: Definition and criteria

What formally distinguishes a citizen from a subject, a resident, a slave, or a foreigner? Aristotle's answer — participation in judgment and office — sets the Western baseline.

### Sub-question: Citizen and class conflict

How does the citizen's role play out when the community is divided by wealth, birth, or faction? Shakespeare's Roman plays dramatize the plebeian citizen as both a political actor and a threat to order.

### Sub-question: The modern citizen

Rousseau redefines the citizen as a member of the sovereign, distinguishing the title from mere subjecthood and critiquing its misuse.

## Western canon

### Aristotle — *Politics*

> *Book III, Chapter I* — "he who has a right to a share in the judicial and executive part of government in any city, him we call a citizen of that place; and a city, in one word, is a collective body of such persons sufficient in themselves to all the purposes of life."
>
> *Gloss: Aristotle defines the citizen by participation in the two functions that constitute self-rule: judging and holding office. This excludes residents, slaves, children, and the aged from full citizenship.*

> *Book III, Chapter II* — "Now it is not residence which constitutes a man a citizen; for in this sojourners and slaves are upon an equality with him; nor will it be sufficient for this purpose, that you have the privilege of the laws, and may plead or be impleaded…"
>
> *Gloss: Citizenship is not mere residence or legal protection; it is an active share in the political life of the city.*

### Rousseau — *The Social Contract*

> *Book I, Chapter VI* — "I have never read of the title of citizens being given to the subjects of any prince, not even the ancient Macedonians or the English of to-day, though they are nearer liberty than any one else. The French alone everywhere familiarly adopt the name of citizens, because, as can be seen from their dictionaries, they have no idea of its meaning; otherwise they would be guilty in usurping it, of the crime of *lèse-majesté*: among them, the name expresses a virtue, and not a right."
>
> *Gloss: Rousseau insists that "citizen" is a political status tied to sovereignty, not a courtesy title. To call a subject of a monarch a citizen is to misunderstand the term entirely.*

### Shakespeare — *Coriolanus*

> *Act I, Scene I* — "FIRST CITIZEN: We are accounted poor citizens, the patricians good. What authority surfeits on would relieve us. If they would yield us but the superfluity while it were wholesome, we might guess they relieved us humanely. But they think we are too dear. The leanness that afflicts us, the object of our misery, is as an inventory to particularize their abundance; our sufferance is a gain to them. Let us revenge this with our pikes ere we become rakes; for the gods know I speak this in hunger for bread, not in thirst for revenge."
>
> *Gloss: Shakespeare's plebeian citizen speaks from the gap between formal membership and material deprivation. The citizen's voice is a demand for subsistence, not for office — a challenge to Aristotle's participatory ideal.*

## Reframings

### Islamic tradition — Umma

The Islamic concept of *umma* designates the community of believers, transcending tribal and territorial boundaries. Membership is defined by shared faith and submission to God's law, not by participation in a polis or state. The citizen of a secular republic and the member of the *umma* have overlapping but distinct obligations; the latter's primary loyalty is to the divine covenant, not to a human constitution.

→ See [[Terms/Umma]]

### Confucian tradition — Ren

Confucian thought does not posit an abstract citizen with rights against the state. Instead, the person is constituted by relational roles — ruler/subject, parent/child, elder/younger — each carrying specific duties (*yi*). The question "who belongs" is answered not by legal status but by the performance of these roles within a hierarchical, harmonious order. The Western citizen is a poor fit for this framework.

→ See [[Terms/Ren]], [[Terms/Yi]]

### Hindu tradition — Dharma

The *dharma* of a person depends on *varna* (social class) and *ashrama* (stage of life). One's duties to the community are determined by birth and station, not by a universal political status. The Western citizen, in principle equal before the law, has no direct analogue in the *dharma*-based social order.

→ See [[Terms/Dharma]]

## Cross-cuts

- [[State]] — the citizen is the member of the state; the state is the association of citizens.
- [[Government]] — citizenship entails a share in government, whether direct or representative.
- [[Law]] — the citizen is both subject to law and, in the Greek view, its author through participation.
- [[Liberty]] — Rousseau ties citizenship to freedom from personal dependence.
- [[Justice]] — distributive justice determines who counts as a citizen and what they are owed.
- [[Slavery]] — Aristotle's citizen is defined partly by contrast with the slave, who has no share in government.
- [[Democracy]] — the citizen is the basic unit of democratic theory.
- [[Aristocracy]] — Aristotle's "best" citizens are those with leisure for virtue.
- [[Revolution]] — Shakespeare's citizens riot when their needs are unmet.
