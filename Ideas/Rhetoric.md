# Idea: Rhetoric

## Working definition

What is the art of persuasion, and what is its relationship to truth, justice, and knowledge? The Western tradition asks this primarily through a dispute about whether rhetorical skill is a genuine art (*techne*) that serves the good, or a mere knack for flattery that manipulates belief without regard for truth. Plato’s *Gorgias* frames the question as a life-or-death contest between philosophy and sophistry; later writers (Aristotle, Cicero, Quintilian) attempt to reclaim rhetoric as a rational art, while Enlightenment thinkers like Locke renew the suspicion that rhetoric is a cheat. Other traditions reframe the question through dialectical debate, the rectification of names, or the inseparability of speech and character; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Art or Knack?

Is rhetoric a systematic art (*techne*) with a definable subject matter, or is it a mere experience (*empeiria*) — a habituation in producing gratification without genuine knowledge?

### Sub-question: Persuasion and Knowledge

Does rhetoric produce belief (*pistis*) without knowledge, or can it also produce genuine understanding? The distinction between persuasion that teaches and persuasion that flatters is central to the Platonic critique.

### Sub-question: Moral Responsibility of the Rhetorician

Can the rhetorician be held accountable for how a listener uses the persuasive skill? Or is rhetoric a neutral instrument, like a weapon, whose misuse belongs to the user alone?

### Sub-question: Rhetoric and Truth

Is rhetoric inherently deceptive, as Locke charges, or can it be placed in the service of truth, as the later tradition (Aristotle, Cicero, Augustine) maintains?

## Western canon

### Plato — *Gorgias*

> *Gorgias:* "A marvel, indeed, Socrates, if you only knew how rhetoric comprehends and holds under her sway all the inferior arts. Let me offer you a striking example of this. On several occasions I have been with my brother Herodicus or some other physician to see one of his patients, who would not allow the physician to give him medicine, or apply the knife or hot iron to him; and I have persuaded him to do for me what he would not do for the physician just by the use of rhetoric. And I say that if a rhetorician and a physician were to go to any city, and had there to argue in the Ecclesia or any other assembly as to which of them should be elected state-physician, the physician would have no chance; but he who could speak would be chosen if he wished; and in a contest with a man of any other profession the rhetorician more than any one would have the power of getting himself chosen, for he can speak more persuasively to the multitude than any of them, and on any subject."
>
> *Gloss: Gorgias stakes the strongest possible claim for rhetoric: it is the master art, capable of overruling any expert's authority in the public arena, because persuasion matters more than knowledge in assemblies and courts.*

> *Socrates:* "Then rhetoric, as would appear, is the artificer of a persuasion which creates belief about the just and unjust, but gives no instruction about them? … And the rhetorician does not instruct the courts of law or other assemblies about things just and unjust, but he creates belief about them; for no one can be supposed to instruct such a vast multitude about such high matters in a short time?"
>
> *Gorgias:* "Certainly not."
>
> *Gloss: Socrates extracts the admission that rhetoric works on belief, not knowledge — its domain is persuasion without instruction. This is the hinge of the Platonic indictment.*

> *Socrates:* "In my opinion then, Gorgias, the whole of which rhetoric is a part is not an art at all, but the habit of a bold and ready wit, which knows how to manage mankind: this habit I sum up under the word 'flattery'; and it appears to me to have many other parts, one of which is cookery, which may seem to be an art, but, as I maintain, is only an experience or routine and not an art: — another part is rhetoric, and the art of attiring and sophistry are two others."
>
> *Gloss: Socrates completes his redefinition: rhetoric is not a techne but a species of flattery (kolakeia), a knack for pleasing audiences, and is structurally analogous to cookery — both aim at gratification, not the good.*

### John Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*

> *Book III, Chapter X, §34* — "Since wit and fancy find easier entertainment in the world than dry truth and real knowledge, figurative speeches and allusion in language will hardly be admitted as an imperfection or abuse of it. I confess, in discourses where we seek rather pleasure and delight than information and improvement, such ornaments as are borrowed from them can scarce pass for faults. But yet if we would speak of things as they are, we must allow that all the art of rhetoric, besides order and clearness; all the artificial and figurative application of words eloquence hath invented, are for nothing else but to insinuate wrong ideas, move the passions, and thereby mislead the judgment; and so indeed are perfect cheats: and therefore, however laudable or allowable oratory may render them in harangues and popular addresses, they are certainly, in all discourses that pretend to inform or instruct, wholly to be avoided; and where truth and knowledge are concerned, cannot but be thought a great fault, either of the language or person that makes use of them."
>
> *Gloss: Locke is the great Enlightenment heir of Plato's suspicion: rhetoric is a "perfect cheat," admissible only in discourse that aims at pleasure; in any discourse that aims at truth, it must be expelled entirely.*

## Reframings

### Greek — Logos

The Greek concept of *logos* spans speech, reason, argument, and the rational structure of reality itself. Rhetoric, in the Platonic framing, is a counterfeit *logos* — it mimics reasoned discourse but serves persuasion rather than truth. The Stoics and later the Christian tradition (the Johannine *Logos*) recover a positive sense of *logos* that rhetoric can serve, but the tension between persuasion and truth remains structurally unresolved within the Greek frame.

### Indian — Nyaya / Tarka (not yet a Term page)

The Indian tradition of *Nyaya* (logic) and *Tarka* (debate) develops a sophisticated analysis of argumentation, inference, and fallacious reasoning. Unlike the Greek tradition's focus on public persuasion in the assembly or law-court, Nyaya is primarily concerned with valid means of knowledge (*pramana*) and the defeat of false views in formal debate. The emphasis falls on the logical structure of argument rather than the psychology of the audience. No Term page has yet been built for Nyaya; see Status notes.

### Chinese — Zheng Ming (not yet a Term page)

The Confucian concern with "rectifying names" (*zheng ming*) approaches the problem of persuasive speech through the ethical responsibility of the speaker to use language correctly. For Confucius (Analects 13.3), if names are not correct, speech is not in accord, and if speech is not in accord, affairs cannot be carried to completion. This reframes rhetoric as an ethical practice of naming the world accurately, rather than a technique for swaying an audience. No Term page has yet been built for Zheng Ming; see Status notes.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Dialectic]] — The method of question-and-answer that Plato opposes to rhetoric as the genuine art of discourse.
- [[Logic]] — The formal structure of reasoning that rhetoric can either serve or corrupt.
- [[Language]] — The medium through which rhetoric operates; Locke's critique of rhetoric is part of his broader inquiry into the abuses of language.
- [[Propaganda]] — The modern descendant of the ancient indictment of rhetoric as manipulation at scale.
- [[Truth]] — The epistemic target against which rhetoric is measured: persuasion-without-knowledge versus instruction.
- [[Sophistry]] — The practice most commonly paired with rhetoric in Plato's critique; the sophist and the rhetorician are near-identical in the *Gorgias*.
- [[Sign_and_Symbol]] — Rhetoric's use of figures and tropes as signs designed to move the passions rather than inform the intellect.
