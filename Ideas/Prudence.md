# Idea: Prudence

## Working definition

The capacity to deliberate well about what is good and advantageous for oneself, and to choose effective means toward that end. In the Western tradition, prudence is treated both as a practical virtue (the right rule of action, especially in moral matters) and as a merely instrumental skill (the efficient pursuit of happiness or self-interest). The question is whether prudence is a genuine moral virtue or a value-neutral technique, and how it relates to wisdom, temperance, and the good life.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Prudence as practical wisdom vs. instrumental calculation

Whether prudence is a virtue of character that discerns the right end, or merely a calculative faculty for achieving given ends.

### Sub-question: Prudence and the other cardinal virtues

How prudence relates to temperance, fortitude, and justice — especially whether it governs them or is subordinate to them.

### Sub-question: Prudence, experience, and science

The distinction between prudence as knowledge gained from experience and sapience or science as demonstrative knowledge.

## Western canon

### Cicero — *Duties* (De Officiis)

> *Book I, sections 142–143* — "A similar definition can be given for prudence, of which I have spoken in an early chapter. But in this part we are considering temperance and self-control and related virtues. Accordingly, the properties which, as we found, are peculiar to prudence, were discussed in their proper place, while those are to be discussed now which are peculiar to these virtues of which we have for some time been speaking and which relate to considerateness and to the approbation of our fellow-men."
>
> *Gloss: Cicero aligns prudence with the science of doing the right thing at the right time (opportunity), linking it to orderliness and the Stoic concept of decorum.*

### Thomas Hobbes — *Leviathan*

> *Part I, Chapter 3* — "Which kind of thoughts, is called Foresight, and Prudence, or Providence; and sometimes Wisdome; though such conjecture, through the difficulty of observing all circumstances, be very fallacious. But this is certain; by how much one man has more experience of things past, than another; by so much also he is more Prudent, and his expectations the seldomer faile him. The Present onely has a being in Nature; things Past have a being in the Memory onely, but things To Come have no being at all; the Future being but a fiction of the mind, applying the sequels of actions Past, to the actions that are Present; which with most certainty is done by him that has most Experience; but not with certainty enough. And though it be called Prudence, when the Event answereth our Expectation; yet in its own nature, it is but Presumption."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes reduces prudence to empirical conjecture based on past experience, denying it any rational certainty and reserving true foresight (Providence) to God alone.*

### Adam Smith — *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*

> *Part VII, Section II, Chapter 1* — "Prudence, for example, though according to this philosophy, the source and principle of all the virtues, was not desirable upon its own account. That careful and laborious and circumspect state of mind, ever watchful and ever attentive to the most distant consequences of every action, could not be a thing pleasant or agreeable for its own sake, but upon account of its tendency to procure the greatest goods and to keep off the greatest evils. … Temperance, in short, was nothing but prudence with regard to pleasure."
>
> *Gloss: Smith reports the Epicurean view that prudence is the master virtue, valued only for its utility in maximizing pleasure and minimizing pain, and that temperance is merely a species of prudence.*

### Immanuel Kant — *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals*

> *Section II* — "Now skill in the choice of means to his own greatest well-being may be called prudence, in the narrowest sense. And thus the imperative which refers to the choice of means to one's own happiness, i.e., the precept of prudence, is still always hypothetical; the action is not commanded absolutely, but only as means to another purpose."
>
> *Gloss: Kant classifies prudence as a hypothetical imperative — a counsel of skill in the service of happiness — and sharply distinguishes it from the categorical imperative of morality.*

## Cross-cuts

- [[Temperance]] — Smith and Cicero explicitly link prudence to temperance; temperance is prudence applied to pleasure.
- [[Wisdom]] — Hobbes distinguishes prudence (experience-based) from sapience (science-based); the relation between practical and theoretical wisdom is a perennial question.
- [[Happiness]] — Kant and Smith treat prudence as the means to happiness; the nature of that end determines whether prudence is moral or merely technical.
- [[Virtue_and_Vice]] — Prudence is one of the four cardinal virtues in classical and Christian thought; its status as a virtue depends on whether it serves the good or merely self-interest.
- [[Reasoning]] — Prudence involves deliberation about means; its logical structure is that of the practical syllogism.
