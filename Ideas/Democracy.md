# Idea: Democracy

## Working definition

A form of government in which political authority is vested in the people, exercised either directly or through elected representatives. The Western tradition has debated democracy's stability, its relationship to liberty and equality, its educational prerequisites, and its tendency to degenerate into tyranny or mob rule. Other traditions approach popular participation through different frameworks (consultation, consensus, cosmic hierarchy); see Reframings.

## Western canon

### Plato — *Republic*

> *Book VIII* — "And does not tyranny spring from democracy in the same manner as democracy from oligarchy—I mean, after a sort? … The good which oligarchy proposed to itself and the means by which it was maintained was excess of wealth—am I not right? … And the insatiable desire of wealth and the neglect of all other things …"
>
> *Gloss:* Plato presents democracy as the penultimate stage in the cycle of regimes, born from oligarchy's excesses and destined to produce tyranny through its own license.

> *Book VIII* — "His life has neither law nor order; and this distracted existence he terms joy and bliss and freedom; and so he goes on. … Yes, I said; his life is motley and manifold and an epitome of the lives of many;—he answers to the State which we described as fair and spangled."
>
> *Gloss:* The democratic man, like the democratic city, is characterized by variety and the absence of hierarchy — a condition Plato regards as unstable and ultimately self-destructive.

### Aristotle — *Politics*

> *Book IV* — "And first, we will speak of a democracy; and this will at the same time show clearly the nature of its opposite which some persons call an oligarchy; and in doing this we must examine into all the parts of a democracy, and everything that is connected therewith; for from the manner in which these are compounded together different species of democracies arise: and hence it is that they are more than one, and of various natures. Now, there are two causes which occasion there being so many democracies; one of which is that which we have already mentioned; namely, there being different sorts of people; for in one country the majority are husbandmen, in another mechanics, and hired servants; if the first of these is added to the second, and the third to both of them, the democracy will …"
>
> *Gloss:* Aristotle classifies democracies by the composition of the citizen body, arguing that the type of democracy depends on which social class predominates.

### Publius (Madison) — *Federalist Papers*

> *No. 10* — "Hence it is that such democracies have ever been spectacles of turbulence and contention; have ever been found incompatible with personal security or the rights of property; and have in general been as short in their lives as they have been violent in their deaths. … A republic, by which I mean a government in which the scheme of representation takes place, opens a different prospect, and promises the cure for which we are seeking. … The two great points of difference between a democracy and a republic are: first, the delegation of the government, in the latter, to a small number of citizens elected by the rest; secondly, the greater number of citizens, and greater sphere of country, over which the latter may be extended."
>
> *Gloss:* Madison distinguishes pure democracy (direct, unstable) from representative republic, arguing that representation and large territory mitigate faction and protect property.

### Alexis de Tocqueville — *Democracy in America*

> *Volume I, Chapter [on charges levied by the state]* — "Democracy, carried to its furthest limits, is therefore prejudicial to the art of government; and for this reason it is better adapted to a people already versed in the conduct of an administration than to a nation which is uninitiated in public affairs. … Although a democratic government is founded upon a very simple and natural principle, it always presupposes the existence of a high degree of culture and enlightenment in society."
>
> *Gloss:* Tocqueville warns that democracy demands prior civic education and administrative experience; it is not a primitive or easy form of government.

> *Volume I, Chapter [on advantages of democracy]* — "When the opponents of democracy assert that a single individual performs the duties which he undertakes much better than the government of the community, it appears to me that they are perfectly right. … Democratic liberty is far from accomplishing all the projects it undertakes, with the skill of an adroit despotism. … but in the end it produces more than any absolute government, and if it do fewer things well, it does a greater number of things."
>
> *Gloss:* Tocqueville concedes democracy's inefficiency but argues that its overall productivity and the energy it releases outweigh the superior precision of autocratic rule.

> *Volume I, Chapter [on true advantages]* — "If it be your intention to confer a certain elevation upon the human mind, and to teach it to regard the things of this world with generous feelings, to inspire men with a scorn of mere temporal advantage, to give birth to living convictions, and to keep alive the spirit of honorable devotedness … if you would constitute a people not unfitted to act with power upon all other nations, nor unprepared for those high enterprises …"
>
> *Gloss:* Tocqueville frames democracy's highest aim as the cultivation of human excellence and public spirit, not merely efficient administration.

### John Dewey — *Democracy and Education*

> *Chapter 7* — "The two elements in our criterion both point to democracy. The first signifies not only more numerous and more varied points of shared common interest, but greater reliance upon the recognition of mutual interests as a factor in social control. The second means not only freer interaction between social groups … but change in social habit—its continuous readjustment through meeting the new situations produced by varied intercourse. And these two traits are precisely what characterize the democratically constituted society."
>
> *Gloss:* Dewey defines democracy as a mode of associated living characterized by shared interests and continuous readjustment through interaction — making education essential to democratic life.

> *Chapter 7* — "The devotion of democracy to education is a familiar fact. The superficial explanation is that a government resting upon popular suffrage cannot be successful unless those who elect and who obey their governors are educated. Since a democratic society repudiates …"
>
> *Gloss:* Dewey argues that democracy's need for education is not merely instrumental (informed voters) but constitutive: democratic social life requires deliberate cultivation of shared interests.

## Reframings

### Islamic tradition — Shura (consultation)

The corpus contains no primary-source passages on Islamic political thought. The concept of *shura* (consultation) in the Qur'an and early Islamic governance provides a non-democratic framework for popular participation, rooted in communal deliberation under divine law rather than popular sovereignty. ### Confucian tradition — Minben (people as root)

The corpus contains no primary-source passages on Confucian political thought. The classical Confucian idea that the people are the foundation of the state (*minben*) emphasizes the ruler's responsibility to the people's welfare without granting them political agency. ## Cross-cuts

- [[Government]] — democracy is one species of government; the Western canon classifies it alongside monarchy, aristocracy, oligarchy, and tyranny.
- [[Liberty]] — democracy's promise of freedom is central to its defenders and a source of its instability for its critics.
- [[Equality]] — democracy presupposes political equality; debates turn on whether it tends toward social and economic leveling.
- [[Education]] — both Tocqueville and Dewey argue that democracy requires deliberate education of citizens.
- [[Constitution]] — the American Founders sought to temper democracy through constitutional structures (representation, separation of powers, federalism).
- [[Tyranny]] — Plato and Aristotle see democracy as the regime most likely to produce tyranny.
- [[State]] — democracy is a form of the state; its legitimacy and limits are contested.
- [[Revolution]] — democratic revolutions (American, French) are a recurring theme in the modern canon.
