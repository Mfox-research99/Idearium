# Idea: Opinion

## Working definition

What is the status of opinion — as a faculty, as a kind of belief, and as a social force — in relation to knowledge, choice, and truth? The Western tradition asks this primarily as an epistemological and political question: how opinion differs from knowledge (Plato), how it differs from moral choice (Aristotle), and how the expression of opinion ought to be protected or constrained in the pursuit of truth (Mill). Other traditions reframe the question through right view, consensus, or the rectification of names; see Reframings.

## Western canon

### Plato — *Republic*

> *Book V (477d–478b)* — "Would you say that knowledge is a faculty, or in what class would you place it? … Certainly knowledge is a faculty, and the mightiest of all faculties. And is opinion also a faculty? Certainly, he said; for opinion is that with which we are able to form an opinion. And yet you were acknowledging a little while ago that knowledge is not the same as opinion? Why, yes, he said: how can any reasonable being ever identify that which is infallible with that which errs? … Then knowledge and opinion having distinct powers have also distinct spheres or subject-matters? … Being is the sphere or subject-matter of knowledge, and knowledge is to know the nature of being … And opinion is to have an opinion? … And do we know what we opine? or is the subject-matter of opinion the same as the subject-matter of knowledge? Nay, he replied, that has been already disproven; if difference in faculty implies difference in the sphere …"
>
> *Gloss: Plato commits to a sharp ontological and epistemological divide: knowledge is infallible and grasps being; opinion is fallible and occupies a distinct sphere (the realm of becoming).*

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics*

> *Book III, ch. 2 (1111b30–1112a13)* — "Neither can it be Opinion; for Opinion is thought to be unlimited in its range of objects, and to be exercised as well upon things eternal and impossible as on those which are in our own power: again, Opinion is logically divided into true and false, not into good and bad as Moral Choice is. However, nobody perhaps maintains its identity with Opinion simply; but it is not the same with opinion of any kind, because by choosing good and bad things we are constituted of a certain character, but by having opinions on them we are not."
>
> *Gloss: Aristotle distinguishes moral choice (proairesis) from opinion: opinion ranges over any subject, is evaluated as true or false, and does not directly constitute character — choice does.*

### John Stuart Mill — *On Liberty*

> *Chapter II* — "If all mankind minus one, were of one opinion, and only one person were of the contrary opinion, mankind would be no more justified in silencing that one person, than he, if he had the power, would be justified in silencing mankind. … the peculiar evil of silencing the expression of an opinion is, that it is robbing the human race; posterity as well as the existing generation; those who dissent from the opinion, still more than those who hold it. If the opinion is right, they are deprived of the opportunity of exchanging error for truth: if wrong, they lose, what is almost as great a benefit, the clearer perception and livelier impression of truth, produced by its collision with error."
>
> *Gloss: Mill argues that no majority or government has the right to suppress an opinion, because the harm of suppression is the loss of truth or of the clarifying confrontation with error.*

> *Chapter II* — "But there is a commoner case than either of these; when the conflicting doctrines, instead of being one true and the other false, share the truth between them; and the nonconforming opinion is needed to supply the remainder of the truth, of which the received doctrine embodies only a part. Popular opinions, on subjects not palpable to sense, are often true, but seldom or never the whole truth. They are a part of the truth; sometimes a greater, sometimes a smaller part, but exaggerated, distorted, and disjoined from the truths by which they ought to be accompanied and limited."
>
> *Gloss: Mill introduces a third case: even a false or heretical opinion may contain a portion of truth that the dominant opinion lacks, making free discussion necessary for the whole truth to emerge.*

## Reframings

No non-Western passages were retrieved from the corpus for this Idea. The question of opinion is reframed in several traditions:

- **Buddhist tradition** — The concept of *sammā diṭṭhi* (right view) distinguishes correct understanding from wrong view (*micchā diṭṭhi*), but the emphasis is on the path to liberation rather than on opinion as a faculty or a political right. See [[Terms/Dukkha]] and [[Terms/Nirvana]] for adjacent concepts.
- **Confucian tradition** — The rectification of names (*zhèngmíng*) treats correct opinion as a matter of social and cosmic order, not individual belief. The Analects focus on the authority of the sage rather than on the liberty of opinion. See [[Terms/Ren]] and [[Terms/Li]].

→ No Term page currently exists for *doxa* or *right view*; these reframings are noted for future expansion.
