# Idea: Hypothesis

## Working definition

What is a hypothesis, and under what conditions is it admissible in inquiry? The Western tradition frames this as a question about the provisional positing of a principle or explanation, subject to verification or falsification, and distinguished from axioms, postulates, and dogmas. The term carries a tension between the necessary role of conjecture in extending knowledge and the discipline required to prevent speculation from overreaching experience.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Hypothesis in mathematics and science

The role of hypothesis in deductive systems and empirical investigation — from Euclid's postulates to the non-Euclidean geometry revolution.

### Sub-question: Hypothesis in philosophy

The epistemological status of hypotheses — Kant's critique of transcendental hypotheses, James's pragmatic defense of live hypotheses.

### Sub-question: Hypothesis vs. postulate vs. axiom

The Aristotelian distinction, preserved in Proclus, between what is assumed with the learner's assent (hypothesis) and what is assumed without it (postulate), and the later evolution of these terms.

## Western canon

### Aristotle (via Proclus) — *Euclid's Elements* (Heath's commentary)

> *1005a 11–b 11* — "…the pupil has not the notion of what is told him which carries conviction in itself, but nevertheless lays it down and assents to its being assumed, such an assumption is a hypothesis. … When again what is asserted is both unknown and assumed even without the assent of the learner, then, he says, we call this a postulate, e.g. that all right angles are equal."
>
> *Gloss:* Aristotle distinguishes hypothesis (assumed with the learner's assent) from postulate (assumed without it), grounding the logic of axiomatic systems in the psychology of teaching and demonstration.

### Euclid (via Saccheri and Lambert) — *Euclid's Elements* (Heath's historical notes)

> *Note on Postulate 5* — "…the hypothesis of the right angle being for Saccheri the first, that of the obtuse angle the second, and that of the acute angle the third hypothesis… The most remarkable is the discovery that the area of a triangle, under the second and third hypotheses, is proportional to the difference between the sum of the three angles and two right angles."
>
> *Gloss:* The three hypotheses about the fourth angle in a quadrilateral (right, obtuse, acute) become the foundation for non-Euclidean geometries, showing how a hypothesis can be entertained without immediate proof and lead to new mathematical worlds.

### Immanuel Kant — *Critique of Pure Reason* (trans. Meiklejohn, 1855)

> *Section III: The Discipline of Pure Reason in Hypothesis* — "Transcendental hypotheses are therefore inadmissible; and we cannot use the liberty of employing, in the absence of physical, hyperphysical grounds of explanation. … The next requisite for the admissibility of a hypothesis is its sufficiency. That is, it must determine à priori the consequences which are given in experience and which are supposed to follow from the hypothesis itself."
>
> *Gloss:* Kant restricts legitimate hypotheses to those grounded in possible experience and capable of explaining given phenomena; transcendental hypotheses that appeal to supersensible causes are forbidden because they stop inquiry rather than advancing it.

### William James — *Pragmatism* (1907)

> *Lecture IV* — "Meanwhile the opposite hypothesis, of a world imperfectly unified still, and perhaps always to remain so, must be sincerely entertained. This latter hypothesis is pluralism's doctrine. Since absolute monism forbids its being even considered seriously, branding it as irrational from the start, it is clear that pragmatism must turn its back on absolute monism, and follow pluralism's more empirical path."
>
> *Gloss:* James treats hypotheses as live options to be tested by their consequences; the pragmatic method requires that even metaphysical hypotheses be entertained sincerely if they make a difference in experience.

### Archimedes — *The Works of Archimedes* (trans. Heath, 1897)

> *Introduction* — "Where in a reductio ad absurdum the original hypothesis is referred to, and generally where an earlier step is quoted, the past tense of the verb is used … the plane is supposed to have been drawn through the centre (τὸ ἐπίπεδον ὑπόκειται διὰ τοῦ κέντρου ἄχθαι)."
>
> *Gloss:* Archimedes' usage shows the hypothesis as the initial supposition in a reductio argument, a standard logical tool in Greek mathematics.

## Reframings

No non-Western reframings are available from the current corpus. The concept of hypothesis as a formal, provisional positing within a deductive or empirical framework is predominantly Western in its explicit articulation. Other traditions (e.g., Nyāya inference, Buddhist logic) have analogous concepts of provisional assumption (*pramāṇa* procedures, *prasaṅga* in reductio), but these are not directly mapped to the English term "hypothesis" in pre-1929 translations.
