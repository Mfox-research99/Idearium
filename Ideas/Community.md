# Idea: Community

## Working definition

What is the human group that precedes and shapes the individual, and what are its proper boundaries, functions, and obligations? The Western tradition asks this question across several registers: the political community (polis, republic, commune), the economic community (village, guild, cooperative), the educational community (school as social environment), and the psychological community (the innate need for belonging). The term carries a tension between voluntary association and inherited membership, between local autonomy and central authority, and between the community as a source of mutual aid and as a constraint on individual liberty.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: The village community as economic and political unit

The historical and anthropological record of communal land tenure, common cultivation, and mutual support.

### Sub-question: The community as educational environment

How the school functions as a deliberately shaped community that balances the diverse social groups a child encounters.

### Sub-question: The community as psychological necessity

The claim that communal life is prior to individual life, and that the psyche is formed by and for community.

### Sub-question: The commune as revolutionary political form

The demand for local autonomy and direct democracy against centralized state power.

## Western canon

### Peter Kropotkin — *Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution*

> "The village community was not only a union for guaranteeing to each one his fair share in the common land, but also a union for common culture, for mutual support in all possible forms, for protection from violence, and for a further development of knowledge, national bonds, and moral conceptions; and every change in the judicial, military, educational, or economical manners had to be decided at the folkmotes of the village, the tribe, or the confederation. The community being a continuation of the gens, it inherited all its functions. It was the universitas, the mir—a world in itself."
>
> *Gloss: Kropotkin presents the village community as a total institution—economic, judicial, moral, educational—whose decisions are made collectively, and which is the natural successor to the clan.*

> "Communal cultivation does not, however, imply by necessity communal consumption. Already under the clan organization we often see that when the boats laden with fruits or fish return to the village, the food they bring in is divided among the huts and the 'long houses' inhabited by either several families or the youth, and is cooked separately at each separate hearth. The habit of taking meals in a narrower circle of relatives or associates thus prevails at an early period of clan life. It became the rule in the village community. Even the food grown in common was usually divided between the households after part of it had been laid in store for communal use. However, the tradition of communal meals was piously kept alive; every available opportunity, such as the commemoration of the ancestors, the religious festivals, the beginning and the end of field work, the births, the marriages, and the funerals, being seized upon..."
>
> *Gloss: Kropotkin distinguishes communal production from communal consumption, showing that the village community maintained a sphere of private domestic life even while practicing common agriculture.*

### John Dewey — *Democracy and Education*

> "Such words as 'society' and 'community' are likely to be misleading, for they have a tendency to make us think there is a single thing corresponding to the single word. As a matter of fact, a modern society is many societies more or less loosely connected. Each household with its immediate extension of friends makes a society; the village or street group of playmates is a community; each business group, each club, is another. Passing beyond these more intimate groups, there is in a country like our own a variety of races, religious affiliations, economic divisions. Inside the modern city, in spite of its nominal political unity, there are probably more communities, more differing customs, traditions, aspirations, and forms of government or control, than existed in an entire continent at an..."
>
> *Gloss: Dewey warns against reifying "community" as a single entity; modern life consists of overlapping, often conflicting communities, and the school's task is to mediate among them.*

### Alfred Adler — *Understanding Human Nature* (trans. Wolfe, 1927)

> "The compulsion toward the community and communal life exists in institutions whose forms we need not entirely understand, as in religion, where the sanctification of communal formulae serves as a bond between members of the community. If the conditions of our life are determined in the first place by cosmic influences, they are also further conditioned by the social and communal life of human beings, and by the laws and regulations which arise spontaneously from the communal life. The communal need regulates all relationships between men. The communal life of man antedates the individual life of man. In the history of human civilization no form of life whose foundations were not laid communally can be found. No human being ever appeared except in a community of human beings."
>
> *Gloss: Adler asserts the ontological priority of community over the individual: communal life is the precondition for human existence itself, not a later contract.*

> "'Gemeinschaftsgefühl' however con- ... and taboo, superstition, or education, must be governed by the concept of the community and be appropriate to it. We have already examined this idea in the case of religion, and we find adaptation to the community is the most important function of the psychic organ, in the case of the individual, as in the case of society. What we call justice and righteousness, and consider most valuable in the human character, is essentially nothing more than the fulfillment of the conditions which arise in the social needs of mankind. These conditions give shape to the soul and direct its activity; responsibility, loyalty, frankness, love of truth, and the like are virtues which have been set up and retained only by the universally valid principle of communal life."
>
> *Gloss: Adler derives the entire moral vocabulary—justice, loyalty, truthfulness—from the demands of communal life, making community the source of ethics.*

### Edward Gibbon (via the Paris Commune declaration) — *The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire*, Vol. 5 (the appended text is a 19th-century document, not Gibbon's own words; cited here as a primary source for the commune ideal)

> "What does Paris demand? ... The recognition of the Republic. Absolute autonomy of the Commune extended to all localities of France. The inherent rights of the Commune are: the vote of the communal budget; the settlement and repartition of taxes; the direction of the local services; the organisation of its magistracy, of its internal police, and of education; the administration of communal goods; the choice and permanent right of control over the communal magistrates and functionaries; the absolute guarantee of individual liberty, of the liberty of conscience and the liberty of labour; the organisation of urban defence and of the National Guard; the Commune alone charged with the surveillance and assurance of the free and just exercise of the right of meeting and of publicity.... Paris wants nothing more ... on condition of finding in the great central administration, the delegation of the federated communes, the realisation and practical application of the same principle."
>
> *Gloss: The Paris Commune declaration of 1871 articulates the revolutionary ideal of community: absolute local autonomy, direct democratic control over all public functions, and a federation of communes as the only legitimate central authority.*

## Reframings

### Islamic tradition — Umma

The Islamic concept of *umma* (community of believers) transcends ethnic, linguistic, and political boundaries, uniting all Muslims under divine law (*shari'a*). Unlike the Western commune, which is often territorial and political, the *umma* is defined by shared faith and submission to God. The individual's primary identity is as a member of the *umma*, not as a citizen of a state. This reframing challenges the Western assumption that community is necessarily local or secular.

→ See [[Terms/Umma]]

### Confucian tradition — Ren, Li, Xiao

Confucian thought does not posit an abstract "community" separate from the web of specific relationships: ruler-subject, parent-child, elder-younger, friend-friend. The virtue of *ren* (humaneness) is realized through the observance of *li* (ritual propriety) and *xiao* (filial piety). Community is not a contract or a commune but a moral order enacted through hierarchical yet reciprocal duties. The Western emphasis on equality and autonomy is replaced by an emphasis on role-based obligation and harmony.

→ See [[Terms/Ren]], [[Terms/Li]], [[Terms/Xiao]]

### Hebrew tradition — Covenant (Berit)

The biblical concept of *berit* (covenant) grounds community in a divine agreement rather than in natural association or political contract. Israel is constituted as a community through God's promise and the people's acceptance of the law. This covenantal community is both religious and political, and it carries obligations of justice and mutual care that are binding across generations.
