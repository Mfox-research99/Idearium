# Idea: Art

## Working definition

What distinguishes making from doing, and what makes a making worthy of the name “art”? The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about *techne* (skilled craft), the ends it serves, and its relation to truth, beauty, and the good. From Plato’s suspicion of mimetic art to Aristotle’s teleological framing of every art as aiming at some good, the canon treats art as a rational productive capacity. Later voices—Bacon, Kropotkin—question whether art can flourish under conditions of alienation or whether it requires a transformed society. Other traditions reframe the question through divine inspiration or skillful means; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Art as techne vs. fine art

Is art any skilled making, or is there a special class of “fine arts” that serve disinterested delight? The canon moves from the broad Greek sense (medicine, shipbuilding, rhetoric) toward a narrower modern sense (painting, poetry, music).

### Sub-question: Art and truth

Does art reveal or conceal reality? Plato’s critique of mimetic poetry as thrice-removed from the Forms stands against Aristotle’s claim that poetry is more philosophical than history.

### Sub-question: Art and society

Can art thrive under capitalism? Kropotkin argues that only a society of free labor can produce genuine art, while the Renaissance masters worked under conditions of popular inspiration now lost.

### Sub-question: Art and inspiration

The Hermetic tradition and some Buddhist commentaries treat art as a channel for divine or cosmic spirit, a view that resists reduction to craft or technique.

## Western canon

### Plato — *Republic*

> *Book I (341c–342e)* — “Now, I said, every art has an interest? … For which the art has to consider and provide? … And the interest of any art is the perfection of it—this and nothing else?”
>
> *Gloss:* Plato establishes that each art has its own proper good (the perfection of the art itself) and that the practitioner’s personal gain (pay) is external to the art’s function. This frames art as a rational, end-directed activity.

> *Book I (346a–d)* — “And each art gives us a particular good and not merely a general one—medicine, for example, gives us health; navigation, safety at sea, and so on? … Then, if there be any good which all artists have in common, that is to be attributed to something of which they all have the common use?”
>
> *Gloss:* The art’s specific benefit is distinct from the universal benefit of pay; the artist qua artist produces the art’s proper work, not wealth.

### Plato — *Gorgias*

> *450b–c* — “SOCRATES: And the same, Gorgias, is true of the other arts:—all of them treat of discourse concerning the subjects with which they severally have to do. … GORGIAS: Because, Socrates, the knowledge of the other arts has only to do with some sort of external action, as of the hand; but there is no such action of the hand in rhetoric which works and takes effect only through the medium of discourse.”
>
> *Gloss:* Rhetoric is distinguished from arts like painting and statuary by its exclusive reliance on discourse; the passage shows Plato’s concern with classifying arts by their medium and mode of action.

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics*

> *Book I, Chapter 1 (1094a)* — “Every art, and every science reduced to a teachable form, and in like manner every action and moral choice, aims, it is thought, at some good: for which reason a common and by no means a bad description of the Chief Good is, ‘that which all things aim at.’ … Now there plainly is a difference in the Ends proposed: for in some cases they are acts of working, and in others certain works or tangible results beyond and beside the acts of working.”
>
> *Gloss:* Aristotle universalizes the teleological structure of art: every art aims at an end, and some arts produce a product beyond the activity (e.g., health from medicine, a vessel from shipbuilding). This frames art as a rational productive capacity ordered to an external good.

### Francis Bacon — *Novum Organum*

> *Book II, Aphorism 31* — “On the contrary, that which is really wonderful, from some specific difference distinguishing it from other species, is carelessly observed, if it be but familiar. Yet the singular instances of art should be observed no less than those of nature … For example: paper, a very common substance, is a singular instance of art; for if you consider the subject attentively, you will find that artificial substances are either woven … or coagulated … paper is a tenacious substance, which can be cut and torn, so as to resemble and almost rival the skin of any animal, or the leaf of vegetables.”
>
> *Gloss:* Bacon treats art as a domain of “singular instances” worthy of scientific investigation, breaking down the boundary between natural and artificial by analyzing paper as a remarkable human invention.

### Peter Kropotkin — *The Conquest of Bread*

> *Chapter V* — “And what about art? From all sides we hear lamentations about the decadence of art. … The technicalities of art have recently made great progress; thousands of people gifted with a certain amount of talent cultivate every branch, but art seems to fly from civilization! … Only a grand idea can inspire art. *Art* is in our ideal synonymous with creation, it must look ahead; but save a few rare, very rare exceptions, the professional artist remains too philistine to perceive new horizons.”
>
> *Gloss:* Kropotkin diagnoses a crisis of inspiration under capitalism: technical skill advances while the social conditions for genuine creation decay. Art requires a society of free, equal labor to recover its vitality.

> *Chapter V* — “You must have been a peasant among peasants to keep the splendour of it in your eye. … You must have spent time in a factory, known the fatigues and the joys of creative work, forged metals by the vivid light of a blast furnace, have felt the life in a machine, to understand the power of man and to express it in a work of art.”
>
> *Gloss:* The artist must share the life of the people; art cannot be produced from outside the experience of labor. This is a radical democratization of artistic authority.

### Saadi — *Gulistan* (via Sidney Colvin, quoted in translator’s note)

> *Preface* — “There are some things which we do because we must; these are our necessities. There are other things which we do because we ought; these are our duties. There are other things which we do because we like; these are our play. Among the various kinds of things done by men only because they like, the fine arts are those of which the results afford to many permanent and disinterested delight, and of which the performance, calling for premeditated skill, is capable of regulation up to a certain point, but that point passed, has secrets beyond the reach and a freedom beyond the restraint of rules.”
>
> *Gloss:* A late-19th-century definition of fine arts as disinterested play, quoted in the translator’s apparatus of Saadi’s *Gulistan*. It marks the modern Western separation of fine art from craft and utility.

## Reframings

### Buddhist tradition — Skillful Means (Upāya)

The corpus contains no direct pre-1929 primary-source passage on art from a Buddhist text. The closest is a translator’s commentary in Kern’s *Lotus Sutra* (1884) that describes the Divine Spirit working through artists in Christian terms—this is not an authentic Buddhist framing. The concept of *upāya* (skillful means) might be the nearest analogue: the Buddha’s pedagogical art adapts the teaching to the audience’s capacity. But the term does not appear in the supplied corpus. A proper reframing would require a passage from a text like the *Lotus Sutra* itself or a commentary on the bodhisattva’s creative activity.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Beauty]] — Art aims at the beautiful, but the canon debates whether beauty is objective or subjective, and whether art’s primary end is beauty or truth.
- [[Poetry]] — The paradigmatic art in Plato and Aristotle; the *Republic*’s critique of poetry is the founding document of Western aesthetics.
- [[Rhetoric]] — Plato’s *Gorgias* distinguishes rhetoric from other arts by its medium; the art of persuasion stands in tension with truth-telling arts.
- [[Techne]] — The Greek concept of skilled making that underlies the Western idea of art; no separate Term page yet, but relevant.
