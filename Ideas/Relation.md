# Idea: Relation

## Working definition

What it means for one thing to stand in relation to another — comparison, connection, correlation, dependence, or the mere fact of being considered together. The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about the mind's capacity to compare objects and about the metaphysical status of the "between": whether relations are real features of the world or products of mental comparison. Other traditions reframe the question through cosmic interdependence or the patterned coherence of reality; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Philosophical vs. natural relation

Whether relation requires a "connecting principle" between objects (the common sense of the term) or merely any subject of comparison (the philosophical extension). Hume draws this line explicitly.

### Sub-question: The taxonomy of relations

What kinds of relation are there? The Western canon attempts exhaustive classification — resemblance, identity, space/time, quantity, quality, contrariety, cause/effect.

### Sub-question: Relation in language

How language encodes relation — through correlative terms (father/son, husband/wife), through prepositions (above, below, of), and through grammatical cases. The difficulty of apprehending relation when language lacks a correlative term.

## Western canon

### John Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*

> *Book II, Chapter XXV, §2* — "These and the like relations, expressed by relative terms that have others answering them, with a reciprocal intimation, as father and son, bigger and less, cause and effect, are very obvious to every one, and everybody at first sight perceives the relation. For father and son, husband and wife, and such other correlative terms, seem so nearly to belong one to another, and, through custom, do so readily chime and answer one another in people’s memories, that, upon the naming of either of them, the thoughts are presently carried beyond the thing so named; and nobody overlooks or doubts of a relation, where it is so plainly intimated. But where languages have failed to give correlative names, there the relation is not always so easily taken notice of."
>
> *Gloss: Locke grounds the perception of relation in linguistic pairing — a relation is most readily grasped when language supplies a reciprocal term; without it, the relational character of a concept may go unnoticed.*

### David Hume — *A Treatise of Human Nature*

> *Book I, Part I, Section V* — "In common language the former is always the sense, in which we use the word, relation; and it is only in philosophy, that we extend it to mean any particular subject of comparison, without a connecting principle. Thus distance will be allowed by philosophers to be a true relation, because we acquire an idea of it by the comparing of objects: But in a common way we say, THAT NOTHING CAN BE MORE DISTANT THAN SUCH OR SUCH THINGS FROM EACH OTHER, NOTHING CAN HAVE LESS RELATION: as if distance and relation were incompatible. … [W]ithout difficulty they may be comprised under seven general heads, which may be considered as the sources of all philosophical relation. (1) The first is RESEMBLANCE: And this is a relation, without which no philosophical relation can exist; since no objects will admit of comparison, but what have some degree of resemblance."
>
> *Gloss: Hume distinguishes the common notion of relation (requiring a connecting principle) from the philosophical notion (any subject of comparison), then provides a sevenfold taxonomy — resemblance, identity, space/time, quantity/number, degrees of quality, contrariety, and cause/effect — with resemblance as the condition for any relation at all.*

> *Book I, Part I, Section V* — "The relation of CONTRARIETY may at first sight be regarded as an exception to the rule, THAT NO RELATION OF ANY KIND CAN SUBSIST WITHOUT SOME DEGREE OF RESEMBLANCE. But let us consider, that no two ideas are in themselves contrary, except those of existence and non-existence, which are plainly resembling, as implying both of them an idea of the object; though the latter excludes the object from all times and places, in which it is supposed not to exist."
>
> *Gloss: Hume defends the universality of resemblance by arguing that even contrariety presupposes a shared conceptual domain — existence and non-existence are both ideas of the object.*

### Adam Smith — *Considerations Concerning the First Formation of Languages* (appended to *Theory of Moral Sentiments*)

> *§27* — "Every preposition, as I have already observed, denotes some relation considered in concrete with the co-relative object. The preposition *above*, for example, denotes the relation of superiority, not in abstract, as it is expressed by the word *superiority*, but in concrete with some co-relative object. … A preposition always requires, in order to complete the sense, some other word to come after it. … First of all, a relation is, in itself, a more metaphysical object than a quality. Nobody can be at a loss to explain what is meant by a quality; but few people will find themselves able to express, very distinctly, what is meant by a relation."
>
> *Gloss: Smith argues that prepositions express relations "in concrete" with a co-relative object, and that relation is inherently more abstract and harder to define than quality — a metaphysical rather than a sensible object.*

> *§28* — "The preposition *of*, denotes relation in general, considered in concrete with the co-relative object. It marks that the noun substantive which goes before it, is somehow or other related to that which comes after it, but without in any respect ascertaining, as is done by the preposition *above*, what is the peculiar nature of that relation. We often apply it, therefore, to express the most opposite relations; because, the most opposite relations agree so far that each of them comprehends in it the general idea or nature of a relation."
>
> *Gloss: The preposition "of" is the most abstract relational term — it signals that a relation exists without specifying its kind, making it a linguistic marker for relation-as-such.*

## Reframings

### Chinese — Yin-Yang

The Chinese tradition does not ask whether relations are "real" or "mental" in the Western sense. Instead, the cosmos is constituted by relation: the interdependence of opposites (dark/light, receptive/active, yielding/firm) is not a comparison the mind makes but the very texture of reality. Yin and Yang are not two substances that enter into relation; they are relation-as-substance — each term exists only through and as the other's complement. This reframes the question from "what is a relation?" to "what is not relation?"

→ See [[Terms/Yin-Yang]]

### Chinese — Li

The Confucian concept of *li* (principle/pattern) treats the intelligible order of the world as a network of relations — the patterned coherence of things, the proper articulation of roles and rites. A thing is known not by its substance but by its place in the relational web. This offers an alternative to the Western taxonomy of relations: the question is not "under what heads do we compare objects?" but "what pattern of relation makes this situation coherent?"

→ See [[Terms/Li]]

### Hebrew — Hesed

The Hebrew term *hesed* (covenantal loyalty/loving-kindness) names a relation that is neither a comparison nor a mere connection but a binding commitment that constitutes the identity of the parties. God's *hesed* toward Israel, and the human response, is not a quality of either party but the relation itself — the covenant is the reality. This reframes relation as fidelity rather than comparison.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Cause_and_Effect]] — Hume's seventh head of philosophical relation; the causal relation is the most debated species.
- [[Same_and_Other]] — Resemblance and contrariety are the two poles of relational comparison.
- [[One_and_Many]] — Identity (Hume's second head) is the relation of a thing to itself across time.
- [[Quality]] — Smith contrasts quality (easily grasped) with relation (metaphysical, hard to define).
- [[Quantity]] — Hume's fourth head of relation; comparison by number or measure.
- [[Space]] and [[Time]] — Hume's third head; the relational framework of all physical comparison.
- [[Language]] — Locke and Smith both treat relation as a problem of linguistic expression.
- [[Logic]] — The formal study of relations as the structure of propositions and inference.
