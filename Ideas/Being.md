# Idea: Being

## Working definition

What does it mean for something to *be*? The Western tradition distinguishes between being as existence (that something is), essence (what it is), and the act of existing (the "is" itself). It also asks whether Being is a property, a category, or the ground of all things — and whether it can be known, spoken, or separated from becoming. Other traditions reframe the question through non-duality, emptiness, or the unnameable; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Being and Becoming

Whether true reality is permanent being or perpetual flux; whether being is the object of knowledge and becoming the object of opinion.

### Sub-question: Being as Act

The Aristotelian-Scholastic analysis of being into potency and act, essence and existence, substance and accident.

### Sub-question: Being and God

Whether the eternal, necessary being is identical with God, and whether that being is material or immaterial, personal or impersonal.

### Sub-question: Being and Knowledge

How the mind grasps being — through perception, intellection, or participation — and whether being is the correlate of truth.

## Western canon

### Plato — *Republic*

> *Book V (478a–479d)* — "And not-being is not one thing but, properly speaking, nothing. […] Of not-being, ignorance was assumed to be the necessary correlative; of being, knowledge? […] Then opinion is not concerned either with being or with not-being? […] And also to be within and between them? […] But were we not saying before, that if anything appeared to be of a sort which is and is not at the same time, that sort of thing would appear also to lie in the interval between pure being and absolute not-being; and that the corresponding faculty is neither knowledge nor ignorance, but will be found in the interval between them?"
>
> *Gloss:* Plato establishes being as the exclusive object of knowledge, with opinion occupying a middle realm between being and not-being — the realm of becoming and sensible particulars.

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics*

> *Book IX, ch. 9 (1170a25–b10)* — "Now to perceive that one lives is a thing pleasant in itself, life being a thing naturally good, and the perceiving of the presence in ourselves of things naturally good is pleasant. But life is a thing desirable, and specially so for the good, because existence is good and pleasant to them; for they are pleased by the consciousness of that which is in itself good."
>
> *Gloss:* Aristotle identifies being with perceiving and knowing, making self-awareness the ground of the experience of existence — existence is defined as perceiving or intellectually knowing.

### Thomas Aquinas — *Summa Theologica* (selections)

> *Glossary entries* — "being: (1) that which is, whether actual or potential and whether in the mind (a 'being of reason') or in objective reality (a 'being in nature'); (1A) ens: entity, thing, substance, that which is; (1B) esse: the act of existing; (1C) essentia: essence, what a thing is; (2) the affirmative predicate 'is' stating that the subject is, or is something (the predicate)."
>
> *Gloss:* Aquinas formalizes the Scholastic distinction between *ens* (the entity), *esse* (the act of existing), and *essentia* (what a thing is) — a tripartite analysis that became the standard framework for Western ontology.

### John Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*, Vol. 2

> *Book IV, ch. 10, §§12–13* — "Though this discovery of the NECESSARY EXISTENCE OF AN ETERNAL MIND does sufficiently lead us into the knowledge of God; since it will hence follow, that all other knowing beings that have a beginning must depend on him […] Yet, to clear up this a little further, we will see what doubt can be raised against it. […] FIRST, Perhaps it will be said, that, though it be as clear as demonstration can make it, that there must be an eternal Being, and that Being must also be knowing: yet it does not follow but that thinking Being may also be MATERIAL. Let it be so, it equally still follows that there is a God."
>
> *Gloss:* Locke argues from the necessity of an eternal being to the existence of God, but leaves open the question of whether that being is material — a characteristically modern move that separates the ontological argument from Cartesian dualism.

### Hermes Trismegistus — *Hermetica* (Chambers 1882)

> *Excerpts by Stobaeus* — "Being departing [is also succeeding, the succeeding] is also departing. Of the Entities some indeed are in bodies, some in ideas, but some energies; but body is in ideas, but idea and energy in body. The immortal partakes not of the mortal, but the mortal does partake of the immortal. The mortal indeed comes not into immortal body, but the immortal becomes present in mortal."
>
> *Gloss:* The Hermetic tradition presents a fluid ontology where being and becoming interpenetrate, and the immortal is present within the mortal without mixture — a bridge between Platonic participation and later Neoplatonic emanation.

## Reframings

### Buddhist — Anatman / Sunyata

The Western question "what does it mean to be?" presupposes a stable subject that *is*. Buddhist thought, particularly the Mahāyāna tradition represented in the *Diamond Sūtra*, denies the reality of any permanent self or being. The passage retrieved from the corpus (SBE Vol. 49, *Buddhist Mahāyāna Texts*, trans. F. Max Müller, 1894) speaks of "beings who believe in self, in beings, in living beings, and in persons" as those of little faith — the very categories of being are the obstacles to awakening. For Buddhism, the question of Being is a question of clinging; the answer is not a higher being but the cessation of the reification of beings.

→ See [[Terms/Anatman]], [[Terms/Sunyata]]

### Hindu — Brahman / Atman

The Upaniṣads ask the question of Being as the identity of the self (*ātman*) with the ground of reality (*brahman*). Being is not a predicate or a category but the single non-dual reality from which all names and forms arise. The Western distinction between essence and existence collapses: *brahman* is both *sat* (being) and *cit* (consciousness). The corpus does not yet contain direct passages on this; a future expansion should include the *Chāndogya Upaniṣad*'s "tat tvam asi" and Śaṅkara's commentaries.

→ See [[Terms/Brahman]], [[Terms/Atman]]

### Daoist — Tao

The *Tao Te Ching* opens with the refusal to name the ultimate: "The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao." Being, in the Daoist frame, is not a substance or a ground but the spontaneous process of the Way. The Western ontology of fixed essences is replaced by a cosmology of dynamic transformation and non-action (*wu wei*). The corpus lacks direct passages; the *Tao Te Ching* (trans. James Legge, 1891) would be the primary source.

→ See [[Terms/Tao]], [[Terms/Wu_Wei]]

## Status notes

- The Western canon section is thin on pre-Socratic material (Parmenides, Heraclitus) and on the medieval Islamic tradition (Avicenna, Averroes). Both should be added when corpus passages become available. The Hermetic passage partially fills the Neoplatonic gap but is not a substitute for Plotinus.
- The Buddhist reframing uses an equivalent-match passage: the *Diamond Sūtra* speaks of "beings" (sattva) in translation, but the concept at stake is *anatman* — the denial of a permanent self. The passage is flagged as an equivalent match; the original term does not appear.
- The Hindu and Daoist reframings are placeholder notes; no direct or equivalent passages were retrieved from the current corpus. They are included to orient the reader toward the relevant Term pages.
- The Locke passage is from a single chapter; a fuller treatment would include his distinction between real and nominal essence (Book III) and his critique of substance (Book II, ch. 23).
