# Idea: Sense

## Working definition

What is the role of sensory perception in human knowledge, experience, and reality? The Western tradition has debated whether the senses are reliable sources of truth, how they relate to reason and intellect, whether there are multiple kinds of sense (the five external senses, internal common sense, moral sense, and the interpretive senses of Scripture), and how far the senses can be extended or corrected. This Idea traces the philosophical and theological uses of "sense" from Plato through the Enlightenment, including its application to moral judgment and biblical hermeneutics.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: The Five Senses and Common Sense

The classification of the senses, their passivity, and the notion of a "common sense" that integrates them.

### Sub-question: Sense as Origin of Knowledge

The empiricist claim that all conceptions derive from sense, and the limits of that claim.

### Sub-question: Moral Sense

The extension of "sense" to moral perception, as in the Scottish Enlightenment.

### Sub-question: Hermeneutic Senses

The fourfold sense of Scripture (literal, allegorical, moral, anagogical) in medieval exegesis.

### Sub-question: Limits and Extensions of Sense

What escapes the senses, and how instruments or inference can reduce the insensible to the sensible.

## Western canon

### Plato (as interpreted by Benjamin Jowett) — Introduction to the *Theaetetus*

> "The senses are not merely 'holes set in a wooden horse' (Theaet.), but instruments of the mind with which they are organically connected. There is no use of them without some use of words—some natural or latent logic—some previous experience or observation. Sensation, like all other mental processes, is complex and relative, though apparently simple. The senses mutually confirm and support one another; it is hard to say how much our impressions of hearing may be affected by those of sight, or how far our impressions of sight may be corrected by the touch, especially in infancy. The confirmation of them by one another cannot of course be given by any one of them. Many intuitions which are inseparable from the act of sense are really the result of complicated reasonings."
>
> *Gloss: Jowett, summarizing the Platonic position, argues that the senses are not passive holes but instruments integrated with mind and language; sensation involves complex reasoning and mutual confirmation among the senses.*

### Hermes Trismegistus — *Hermetica* (trans. Chambers, 1882)

> "For I call general Energies those really corporeal, generate through the senses and the motions; for apart from these the energies, it is not possible that the body subsist. But there are other special Energies in the souls of men, through Arts, Sciences, and studies and energizings. For the Senses follow along with the Energies, or rather the senses are effects of the energies. Understand then, O Child, the difference of Energy and Sense; for Energy is sent from above; but the Sense being in the body, and from this having the Essence, having received the Energy makes it manifest, just as if having made it corporeal. Wherefore I say the Senses are both corporeal and mortal, subsisting so long as the body."
>
> *Gloss: In the Hermetic tradition, sense is a corporeal, mortal faculty that receives and manifests a higher, immaterial Energy; the two are distinct but linked.*

### Thomas Aquinas — *Summa Theologica* (trans. Fathers of the English Dominican Province, 1911–1925)

> "Therefore, so far as the things of the Old Law signify the things of the New Law, there is the allegorical sense; so far as the things done in Christ, or so far as the things which signify Christ, are types of what we ought to do, there is the moral sense. But so far as they signify what relates to eternal glory, there is the anagogical sense. Since the literal sense is that which the author intends, and since the author of Holy Writ is God, Who by one act comprehends all things by His intellect, it is not unfitting, as Augustine says (Confess, xii), if, even according to the literal sense, one word in Holy Writ should have several senses."
>
> *Gloss: Aquinas extends "sense" to the interpretation of Scripture, distinguishing the literal sense from three spiritual senses (allegorical, moral, anagogical), all founded on the literal.*

### Michel de Montaigne — *Essays* (trans. Charles Cotton, 1685–1686)

> "We seize an apple with all our senses; we there find redness, smoothness, odour, and sweetness; but it may have other virtues besides these, as to heat or binding, which no sense of ours can have any reference unto. Is it not likely that there are sensitive faculties in nature that are fit to judge of and to discern those which we call the occult properties in several things, as for the loadstone to attract iron; and that the want of such faculties is the cause that we are ignorant of the true essence of such things? 'Tis perhaps some particular sense that gives cocks to understand what hour it is at midnight…"
>
> *Gloss: Montaigne speculates that animals may possess senses humans lack, and that our ignorance of occult properties may stem from the limited range of our sensory faculties.*

### Francis Bacon — *Novum Organum* (trans. Spedding, Ellis, Heath, 1863)

> "We are wont also to call them invoking instances, and their property is that of reducing to the sphere of the senses objects which do not immediately fall within it. Objects escape the senses either from their distance, or the intervention of other bodies, or because they are not calculated to make an impression upon the senses, or because they are not in sufficient quantity to strike the senses, or because there is not sufficient time for their acting upon the senses, or because the impression is too violent, or because the senses are previously filled and possessed by the object, so as to leave no room for any new motion."
>
> *Gloss: Bacon catalogues the ways objects escape the senses and proposes "invoking instances" — experimental devices that reduce insensible phenomena to sensible effects.*

### Thomas Hobbes — *Leviathan* (1651)

> "The Originall of them all, is that which we call Sense; (For there is no conception in a mans mind, which hath not at first, totally, or by parts, been begotten upon the organs of Sense.) The rest are derived from that originall. … The cause of Sense, is the Externall Body, or Object, which presseth the organ proper to each Sense, either immediatly, as in the Tast and Touch; or mediately, as in Seeing, Hearing, and Smelling: which pressure, by the mediation of Nerves, and other strings, and membranes of the body, continued inwards to the Brain, and Heart, causeth there a resistance, or counter-pressure, or endeavour of the heart, to deliver it self."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes grounds all mental content in sense, defining it as a mechanical reaction of the body to external pressure — a thoroughgoing empiricist and materialist account.*

### Adam Smith — *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759)

> "The various senses or powers of perception, from which the human mind derives all its simple ideas, were, according to this system, of two different kinds, of which the one were called the direct or antecedent, the other, the reflex or consequent senses. The direct senses were those faculties from which the mind derived the perception of such species of things as did not presuppose the antecedent perception of any other. Thus sounds and colours were objects of the direct senses. … The reflex or consequent senses, on the other hand, were those faculties from which the mind derived the perception of such species of things as presupposed the antecedent perception of some other. Thus harmony and beauty were objects of the reflex senses. … The moral sense was considered as a faculty of this kind."
>
> *Gloss: Smith reports and endorses the distinction between direct senses (e.g., colour) and reflex senses (e.g., harmony, beauty, morality), placing moral judgment on the same footing as aesthetic perception.*

## Reframings

No non-Western passages were retrieved from the corpus for this Idea. The Western concept of "sense" as a distinct faculty of perception, classified into five external senses plus internal or moral senses, does not have a direct equivalent in many other traditions. In Indian epistemology, perception (*pratyaksha*) is one of several valid means of knowledge (*pramana*), but it is not separated into a "sense" faculty in the same way; the sense organs (*indriyas*) are instruments of the mind, and the mind itself is considered a sixth sense. In classical Chinese thought, the five senses are recognized but subordinated to the heart-mind (*xin*), which governs perception and moral discernment. A fuller treatment would require Term pages for *Pratyaksha* and *Xin*.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Body]] — The senses are bodily organs; their mortality and passivity are central to debates about the soul.
- [[Mind]] — The relation of sense to intellect and the question of whether the mind actively interprets sensory data.
