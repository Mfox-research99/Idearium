# Idea: Beauty

## Working definition

What makes an object, person, or experience worthy of admiration and delight? The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about perception, pleasure, form, and the relation between the senses and the intellect. Beauty is treated as both a property of things and a response of the beholder, with persistent debates about whether it is objective or subjective, intrinsic or relational, and whether it belongs to the body, the mind, or the divine. Other traditions reframe the question through cosmic harmony, divine self-disclosure, or the dissolution of the subject-object distinction; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Beauty as objective order vs. subjective response

Is beauty a property of the object itself—proportion, harmony, fitness—or is it a feeling produced in the perceiver? The canon divides sharply on this question.

### Sub-question: The ascent to Beauty itself

Plato's Symposium establishes a ladder of beauty that moves from physical forms to souls, laws, sciences, and finally to Beauty itself—an eternal, unchanging Form that is the ultimate object of eros.

### Sub-question: Beauty and utility

Is beauty independent of function, or does the perception of fitness for an end constitute a major part of aesthetic pleasure? Hume and others argue that utility pleases by sympathy.

### Sub-question: Beauty and the divine

In Neoplatonic and Augustinian traditions, beauty is a trace of God—the proportion and harmony of creation reflects the Creator. In Persian Sufi poetry, beauty is the self-disclosure of the divine Beloved.

## Western canon

### Plato — *Symposium*

> "For he who would proceed aright in this matter should begin in youth to visit beautiful forms; and first, if he be guided by his instructor aright, to love one such form only—out of that he should create fair thoughts; and soon he will of himself perceive that the beauty of one form is akin to the beauty of another; and then if beauty of form in general is his pursuit, how foolish would he be not to recognize that the beauty in every form is one and the same! ... In the next stage he will consider that the beauty of the mind is more honourable than the beauty of the outward form."
>
> *Gloss: Plato establishes the ladder of love—from a single beautiful body to all beautiful bodies, then to souls, laws, sciences, and finally to Beauty itself, which is eternal, ungenerated, and imperishable.*

> *Location: 210a–211b* — Trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1892.

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics*

> *[The translator retains the Greek term *kalon* throughout, noting: "The mere translation of this term would convey no idea of its meaning, I have therefore retained the Greek term."]*
>
> *Gloss: Aristotle treats the beautiful (*kalon*) as the object of the highest actions—virtue is pursued for its own sake because it is noble, not merely useful. The beautiful is tied to the fine and the honorable, distinct from the merely pleasant or advantageous.*

> *Location: Book I* — Trans. F.H. Peters, 1881. (Equivalent match: the translator's note signals that "beautiful" is an inadequate rendering of *kalon*.)

### Augustine — *City of God*

> "There are some things, too, which have such a place in the body, that they obviously serve no useful purpose, but are solely for beauty, as *e.g.* the teats on a man's breast, or the beard on his face; for that this is for ornament, and not for protection, is proved by the bare faces of women, who ought rather, as the weaker sex, to enjoy such a defence."
>
> *Gloss: Augustine argues that some bodily features exist for beauty alone, not utility—a claim that separates aesthetic from functional purpose and grounds beauty in the Creator's design.*

> *Location: Book XXII, Chapter 24* — Trans. Marcus Dods, 1887.

### Hume — *A Treatise of Human Nature*

> "Wherever an object has a tendency to produce pleasure in the possessor, or in other words, is the proper cause of pleasure, it is sure to please the spectator, by a delicate sympathy with the possessor. Most of the works of art are esteemed beautiful, in proportion to their fitness for the use of man, and even many of the productions of nature derive their beauty from that source. Handsome and beautiful, on most occasions, is not an absolute but a relative quality, and pleases us by nothing but its tendency to produce an end that is agreeable."
>
> *Gloss: Hume reduces beauty to a subjective response grounded in sympathy and utility—an object is beautiful because it pleases us, and it pleases us in part because we sympathetically register its usefulness to its possessor.*

> *Location: Book II, Part I, Section VIII* — Trans. L.A. Selby-Bigge, 1888.

### Voltaire — *Philosophical Dictionary*

> "Ask a toad what beauty is, the *to kalon*? He will answer you that it is his toad wife with two great round eyes issuing from her little head, a wide, flat mouth, a yellow belly, a brown back. Interrogate a Guinea negro, for him beauty is a black oily skin, deep-set eyes, a flat nose. Interrogate the devil; he will tell you that beauty is a pair of horns, four claws and a tail. Consult, lastly, the philosophers, they will answer you with gibberish: they have to have something conforming to the arch-type of beauty in essence, to the *to kalon*."
>
> *Gloss: Voltaire satirizes the notion of objective beauty by multiplying perspectives—each creature judges beauty by its own nature, and the philosophical appeal to an "arch-type" is empty gibberish.*

> *Location: "Beauty"* — Trans. William F. Fleming, 1901.

### Nietzsche — *On the Genealogy of Morality*

> "Kant thought that he showed honour to art when he favoured and placed in the foreground those of the predicates of the beautiful, which constitute the honour of knowledge: impersonality and universality. This is not the place to discuss whether this was not a complete mistake; all that I wish to emphasise is that Kant, just like other philosophers, instead of envisaging the æsthetic problem from the standpoint of the experiences of the artist (the creator), has only considered art and beauty from the standpoint of the spectator, and has thereby imperceptibly imported the spectator himself into the idea of the 'beautiful'!"
>
> *Gloss: Nietzsche attacks the Kantian tradition for defining beauty from the spectator's standpoint rather than the creator's—the "impersonality" and "universality" of aesthetic judgment are, for Nietzsche, symptoms of a failure to understand the artist's lived experience.*

> *Location: Third Essay, Section 6* — Trans. Horace B. Samuel, 1913.

### Cervantes — *Don Quixote*

> "By that natural understanding which God has given me I know that everything beautiful attracts love, but I cannot see how, by reason of being loved, that which is loved for its beauty is bound to love that which loves it; besides, it may happen that the lover of that which is beautiful may be ugly, and ugliness being detestable, it is very absurd to say, 'I love thee because thou art beautiful, thou must love me though I be ugly.' ... True love, I have heard it said, is indivisible, and must be voluntary and not compelled."
>
> *Gloss: Through the voice of Marcela, Cervantes challenges the assumption that beauty creates an obligation in the beloved—beauty may attract love, but it does not compel reciprocity, and the lover's claim on the beautiful object is not justified by the beloved's beauty alone.*

> *Location: Part I, Chapter 14* — Trans. John Ormsby, 1885.

## Reframings

### Persian Sufi — *Jamal* (جمال)

The Persian Sufi tradition, especially in the poetry of Jami, reframes beauty not as a property of objects or a subjective response, but as the primary mode of divine self-disclosure. Beauty is what the Beloved (God) sees in Himself before creation; the world is brought into being so that beauty might be seen and loved by another. This is not an ascent *toward* beauty (as in Plato) but an emanation *from* beauty. The human experience of beauty—in faces, in nature, in art—is a trace of that primordial self-disclosure. Jami writes: "Then Beauty shone, from stranger glances free, / Seen of herself, with naught beside to see" — and then, "So beauty ne'er will quit the urgent claim / Whose motive first from heavenly beauty came."

→ See [[Terms/Jamal]]

### Hindu — *Sundara* (सुन्दर)

The Sanskrit term *sundara* (beautiful) appears in the corpus primarily in Buddhist Mahayana texts (the *Saddharma-Puṇḍarīka* or *Lotus Sutra*) where it describes the radiant, serene beauty of the Buddha's appearance and the exquisite beauty of pure lands and celestial beings. This beauty is not merely aesthetic—it is a sign of spiritual attainment, a visible manifestation of inner purity and enlightenment. The Buddha's serene organs of sense, his clear and yellowish complexion, are compared to an autumn cloud: pale, clear, bright. Beauty here is the radiance of wisdom, not a separate quality.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Eros]] — Plato's Symposium treats beauty as the ultimate object of erotic desire; the ascent to Beauty itself is the culmination of the lover's education.
- [[Pleasure_and_Pain]] — Hume and others ground the perception of beauty in pleasure; the beautiful is what pleases, and the debate is whether that pleasure is disinterested or tied to utility.
- [[Form]] — The Platonic Form of Beauty is the archetype of all beautiful things; Augustine's account of bodily beauty as a trace of divine order echoes this.
- [[God]] — In Augustinian and Sufi framings, beauty is a property of the divine—either as a reflection of the Creator's order or as the Beloved's self-disclosure.
- [[Truth]] — Keats's famous equation ("Beauty is truth, truth beauty") has antecedents in the Platonic tradition; the Idea page for Truth may touch this.

## Status notes

**Complete-v1.** The Western canon section covers the major positions: Plato (objective Form, ascent), Aristotle (*kalon* as the noble), Augustine (beauty as divine ornament), Hume (subjective, utility-based), Voltaire (relativist satire), Nietzsche (creator vs. spectator), and Cervantes (beauty and the ethics of love). The Persian Sufi reframing via *jamal* is strong—Jami's poem is a dense primary-source commitment to the emanationist view. The *sundara* reframing is thinner; the corpus matches are mostly descriptive (the Buddha's complexion, the beauty of pure lands) rather than philosophical. A dedicated search for *sundara* in the Upanishads or Vedantic texts would strengthen this, but the current corpus returned only Buddhist Mahayana material. The *kalon* term page is noted as a cross-reference; Aristotle's translator explicitly flags the inadequacy of "beauty" as a rendering, which is itself instructive. The Voltaire passage on *to kalon* is a useful satirical counterweight to the Platonic tradition.
