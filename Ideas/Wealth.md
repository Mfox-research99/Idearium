# Idea: Wealth

## Working definition

What are the material resources that sustain human life, and what is their proper place in a good life? The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about the nature of value (labor, money, exchange), the distinction between natural and artificial wealth, and whether wealth can be an ultimate end or is always a means. Other traditions reframe the question through detachment, generosity, and the spiritual dangers of accumulation; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Natural vs. artificial wealth

The distinction between goods that directly satisfy natural needs (food, shelter) and those invented for exchange (money). The latter is doubly instrumental.

### Wealth as end vs. means

Whether wealth can be the *summum bonum* or is always ordered to something beyond itself.

### Wealth and virtue

The moral psychology of acquisition, hoarding, and generosity.

## Western canon

### Aristotle (via Aquinas) — *Politics* / *Summa Theologica*

> *Summa Theologica, First Part of the Second Part, Question 2, Article 1* — "I answer that, It is impossible for man’s happiness to consist in wealth. For wealth is twofold, as the Philosopher says (Polit. i. 3), viz., natural and artificial. Natural wealth is that which serves man as a remedy for his natural wants: such as food, drink, clothing, dwellings, and such like, while artificial wealth is that which is not a direct help to nature, as money, but is invented by the art of man, for the convenience of exchange, and as a measure of things salable."
>
> *Gloss: Aquinas, following Aristotle, denies that wealth can be the final end of man because both natural and artificial wealth are sought for the sake of something else — the support of nature or the procurement of necessities.*

> *Summa Theologica, First Part of the Second Part, Question 2, Article 1, Objection 2* — "Further, according to Boethius (De Consol. iii), happiness is a state of life made perfect by the aggregate of all good things. Now money seems to be the means of possessing all things: for, as the Philosopher says (Ethic, v. 5), money was invented, that it might be a sort of guarantee for the acquisition of whatever man desires."
>
> *Gloss: The objection that money commands all things is answered by Aquinas's distinction: money is a conventional guarantee, not a real possession of the goods that constitute happiness.*

*Citation: Thomas Aquinas, *Summa Theologica*, trans. Fathers of the English Dominican Province, 1911–1925.*

### Montaigne — *Essays*

> *Book I, Chapter 14* — "Plato places corporal or human goods in this order: health, beauty, strength, riches; and riches, says he, are not blind, but very clear-sighted, when illuminated by prudence."
>
> *Gloss: Montaigne reports Plato's ranking of riches as the lowest of human goods, valuable only when guided by prudence — a classical subordination of wealth to wisdom.*

> *Book I, Chapter 14* — "I continued some years in this hoarding humour, when I know not what good demon fortunately put me out of it, as he did the Syracusan, and made me throw abroad all my reserve at random, the pleasure of a certain journey I took at very great expense having made me spurn this fond love of money."
>
> *Gloss: Montaigne's autobiographical turn: the cure for avarice is not moral philosophy but the experience of generous expenditure.*

*Citation: Michel de Montaigne, *Essays*, trans. Charles Cotton, 1685.*

### Adam Smith — *An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations*

> *Book I, Chapter 5* — "The real price of every thing, what every thing really costs to the man who wants to acquire it, is the toil and trouble of acquiring it. What every thing is really worth to the man who has acquired it and who wants to dispose of it, or exchange it for something else, is the toil and trouble which it can save to himself, and which it can impose upon other people. … Labour was the first price, the original purchase money that was paid for all things. It was not by gold or by silver, but by labour, that all the wealth of the world was originally purchased."
>
> *Gloss: Smith grounds the value of wealth in labor — the toil required to produce it — making wealth a measure of command over others' labor, not an intrinsic property of gold or silver.*

*Citation: Adam Smith, *An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations*, ed. Edwin Cannan, 1904.*

## Reframings

### Persian ethical tradition — Generosity and the danger of Korah

The Persian poet Saadi, in the *Bustan*, treats wealth as a moral test: the miser who hoards is like Korah (Qārūn), whose treasure became his ruin. True wealth is generosity, not accumulation. The "running water" of liberality is life-giving; the "stagnant water" of miserliness stinks. This reframing shifts the question from *what wealth is* to *what wealth does to the soul* — a concern that the Western canon treats through virtue ethics but that Saadi makes the central axis.

> *Bustan, Chapter VII* — "Did not the wealth-worshipping Korah know that the treasure of safety lies in the corner of retirement? … Exert not thyself in the amassing of wealth, for evil is the smell of stagnant water; strive, rather, to be generous, for running water becomes a flood."
>
> *Gloss: Wealth is not an end; the generous man attains perfection without gold. The miser who falls from wealth seldom rises again.*

*Citation: Saadi, *The Bustan*, trans. H. Wilberforce Clarke, 1879.*

## Cross-cuts

- [[Happiness]] — Aquinas's central argument: wealth cannot constitute happiness because it is always a means, not an end.
- [[Labor]] — Smith's labor theory of value makes wealth a function of human effort, not a natural property.
- [[Virtue_and_Vice]] — Montaigne and Saadi both treat the disposition toward wealth (generosity vs. avarice) as a moral index.
- [[Justice]] — Distributive justice concerns the proper allocation of wealth; the Western tradition debates property rights and the just price.
- [[Desire]] — Wealth is the object of a specific desire (acquisitiveness) that can become infinite; the tradition asks whether that desire can be moderated.

## Status notes

- **v1 draft.** The Western canon section is thin: only Aquinas, Montaigne, and Smith. Missing essential voices: Aristotle's *Politics* and *Nicomachean Ethics* (the natural/artificial distinction originates there), the Bible (Ecclesiastes, Proverbs, the rich young ruler), the Church Fathers (Clement of Alexandria, *Who Is the Rich Man That Shall Be Saved?*), Marx (*Capital*), and the classical economists (Ricardo, Mill). These should be added when corpus passages are available.
- **Reframings.** Only one non-Western voice (Saadi) was supplied. A Buddhist reframing (detachment from wealth as a cause of dukkha) and a Confucian reframing (wealth as subordinate to ren and yi) would strengthen the page. The Saadi passage is treated as an equivalent match: Clarke translates the Persian term for wealth (māl) directly as "wealth," so no transliteration issue arises.
- **Duplicates.** The corpus returned duplicate passages for Smith, Montaigne, and Aquinas (different editions). Only one edition per author is cited.
- **Translator provenance.** Aquinas: the standard Dominican translation is used; no alternative was supplied. Smith: the Cannan edition is standard. Montaigne: Cotton's 1685 translation is the classic English version. Saadi: Clarke's 1879 translation is the only pre-1929 English version in the corpus.
