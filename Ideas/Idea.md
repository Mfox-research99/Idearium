# Idea: Idea

## Working definition

What is an idea — a mental representation, a concept, a regulative principle, or a transcendent Form? The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about the origin, composition, and function of the contents of the mind. The debate runs between empiricists (Locke, Hume), who derive all ideas from sensation and reflection, and rationalists (Kant), who treat certain ideas as a priori regulative principles of reason. The term itself carries a history: from Plato's *eidos* (the Form that is the true object of knowledge) through the British empiricists' reduction of ideas to mental copies, to Kant's transcendental reclamation of the Idea as a necessary but non-constitutive unity. Other traditions reframe the question through different ontologies of thought; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Origin — innate or empirical?

Are ideas present in the mind from birth, or are they derived entirely from experience? Locke and Hume argue the latter; Kant argues that some ideas (the Ideas of Reason) are a priori, though they have no empirical object.

### Sub-question: Composition — simple or complex?

Ideas may be simple (irreducible units of sensation or reflection) or complex (compounded by the mind's own operations). Locke's taxonomy of simple and complex ideas is foundational.

### Sub-question: Function — representation or regulation?

Do ideas merely copy reality, or do they serve as regulative principles that organize experience without claiming to represent a transcendent object? Hume holds the former view; Kant the latter.

## Western canon

### John Locke — *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1689)

> *Book II, Chapter XXIII, §14* — "These ideas of substances, though they are commonly simple apprehensions, and the names of them simple terms, yet in effect are complex and compounded. Thus the idea which an Englishman signifies by the name swan, is white colour, long neck, red beak, black legs, and whole feet, and all these of a certain size, with a power of swimming in the water, and making a certain kind of noise, and perhaps, to a man who has long observed this kind of birds, some other properties: which all terminate in sensible simple ideas, all united in one common subject."
>
> *Gloss: Locke commits to the empiricist thesis that even the idea of a substance is a complex built entirely from simple ideas of sensation, with no innate content.*

> *Book II, Chapter XXIV, §1* — "Besides these complex ideas of several single substances, as of man, horse, gold, violet, apple, &c., the mind hath also complex collective ideas of substances; which I so call, because such ideas are made up of many particular substances considered together, as united into one idea, and which so joined are looked on as one; v. g. the idea of such a collection of men as make an army, though consisting of a great number of distinct substances, is as much one idea as the idea of a man."
>
> *Gloss: Locke extends his compositional account to collective ideas, showing that the mind's power of composition can unify many particulars into a single idea — a precursor to Hume's and Kant's treatments of unity.*

### David Hume — *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739)

> *Book I, Part I, Section I* — "Now since all ideas are derived from impressions, and are nothing but copies and representations of them, whatever is true of the one must be acknowledged concerning the other. Impressions and ideas differ only in their strength and vivacity. … An idea is a weaker impression; and as a strong impression must necessarily have a determinate quantity and quality, the case must be the same with its copy or representative."
>
> *Gloss: Hume radicalizes Locke's empiricism: ideas are not merely derived from experience but are literally copies of impressions, differing only in force. This commits him to a copy-theory of representation with no room for innate or a priori ideas.*

### Immanuel Kant — *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781, trans. J.M.D. Meiklejohn, 1855)

> *Transcendental Dialectic, Appendix* — "We mistake the true meaning of this idea when we regard it as an enouncement, or even as a hypothetical declaration of the existence of a real thing, which we are to regard as the origin or ground of a systematic constitution of the universe. On the contrary, it is left completely undetermined what the nature or properties of this so-called ground may be. The idea is merely to be adopted as a point of view, from which this unity, so essential to reason and so beneficial to the understanding, may be regarded as radiating. In one word, this transcendental thing is merely the schema of a regulative principle, by means of which Reason, so far as it can, extends the unity of systematic experience over the whole field of the understanding's employment."
>
> *Gloss: Kant redefines the Idea as a regulative principle of reason, not a claim about a transcendent object. This is a decisive break from both the empiricist copy-theory and the Platonic realist theory of Ideas.*

> *Transcendental Dialectic, Introduction* — "Our present duty is to make ourselves perfectly acquainted with the transcendental use made of pure reason, its principles and ideas, that we may be able properly to determine and value its influence and real worth. … I beg those who really have philosophy at heart … to exert themselves to preserve to the expression idea its original signification, and to take care that it be not lost among those other expressions by which all sorts of representations are loosely designated."
>
> *Gloss: Kant insists on a terminological discipline: "idea" must not be conflated with "concept" or "representation" — it names a distinct product of pure reason, not a mere mental image.*

## Reframings

### Greek — *Eidos* / *Idea* (Plato)

The Western tradition's own origin for the term is absent from the corpus passages retrieved. Plato's *eidos* (usually translated "Form" or "Idea") is not a mental representation but a transcendent, eternal reality that makes knowledge possible. The empiricist and Kantian debates are, in part, reactions against or transformations of this Platonic inheritance. For the Platonic concept, see the Term page for [[Terms/Logos]], which covers the intelligible structure of reality that includes the Forms.

### Buddhist — *Vikalpa* (conceptual construction)

The Sanskrit tradition treats the ordinary "idea" as a *vikalpa* — a mental construction that superimposes conceptual distinctions onto a non-dual reality. This is not a copy of an impression nor a regulative principle of reason, but a distortion to be seen through. No corpus passages were retrieved for this page; the concept is noted here for orientation. See also [[Terms/Sunyata]] (emptiness of all conceptual constructs).

## Cross-cuts

- [[Knowledge]] — the debate over the origin of ideas is the debate over the foundation of knowledge.
- [[Mind]] — ideas are the primary contents of mind; theories of mind determine theories of ideas.
- [[Perception]] — Hume's impressions are perceptions; Locke's simple ideas of sensation are perceptual atoms.
- [[Truth]] — if ideas are copies, truth is correspondence; if ideas are regulative, truth is systematic coherence.
- [[Logic]] — Kant's Ideas of Reason are the subject of transcendental logic, not general logic.
- [[Metaphysics]] — the status of Ideas (realist vs. nominalist vs. transcendental) is a metaphysical question.
