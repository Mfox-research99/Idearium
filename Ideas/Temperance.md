# Idea: Temperance

## Working definition

The virtue of self-rule — the ordering and controlling of pleasures and desires so that the whole person (or polity) is not at war with itself. The Western tradition frames temperance primarily as an internal harmony: reason governing appetite, the better part of the soul mastering the worse. This is distinct from ascetic renunciation (which rejects desire) and from mere moderation (which lacks the element of internal struggle). The question temperance poses is: what does it mean to be one's own master, when the master and the servant are the same person?

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Temperance as harmony of the soul

The Platonic and Stoic tradition treats temperance as the concord among the parts of the soul — the rational, irascible, and concupiscible faculties acting in agreement under reason's direction.

### Sub-question: Temperance as tested by pleasure

The tradition also tests temperance through exposure to pleasure and desire, particularly wine and bodily appetites. The temperate person is not the one who flees temptation but the one who can engage with pleasure without being mastered by it.

### Sub-question: Temperance and the cardinal virtues

Temperance is one of the four cardinal virtues (alongside prudence, justice, and courage). Its relation to justice is particularly close — both are forms of harmony and order — and the tradition sometimes struggles to distinguish them.

## Western canon

### Plato — *Republic*

> "Temperance, I replied, is the ordering or controlling of certain pleasures and desires; this is curiously enough implied in the saying of 'a man being his own master;' and other traces of the same notion may be found in language. [...] There is something ridiculous in the expression 'master of himself;' for the master is also the servant and the servant the master; and in all these modes of speaking the same person is denoted."
>
> *Gloss: Plato identifies the paradox at the heart of temperance — the self that rules and the self that is ruled are the same self — and locates the virtue in the harmony between the better and worse parts of the soul.*

*Republic*, Book IV. Trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1892.

### Plato — *Gorgias*

> "And will not the temperate man do what is proper, both in relation to the gods and to men;—for he would not be temperate if he did not? Certainly he will do what is proper. In his relation to other men he will do what is just; and in his relation to the gods he will do what is holy; and he who does what is just and holy must be just and holy? Very true. And must he not be courageous? for the duty of a temperate man is not to follow or to avoid what he ought not, but what he ought, whether things or men or pleasures or pains, and patiently to endure when he ought; and therefore, Callicles, the temperate man, being, as we have described, also just and courageous and holy, cannot be other than a perfectly good man."
>
> *Gloss: Temperance is the root virtue from which justice, holiness, and courage all follow — the temperate person necessarily acts rightly toward gods, men, pleasures, and pains alike.*

*Gorgias*. Trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1892.

### Plato — *Laws*

> "Let us not forget that there are two qualities which should be cultivated in the soul—first, the greatest fearlessness, and, secondly, the greatest fear, which are both parts of reverence. Courage and fearlessness are trained amid dangers; but the greatest fear is trained amid the greatest pleasures and desires. [...] If a man have sufficient confidence in himself to drink the potion amid his boon companions, leaving off in time and not taking too much,—would be an equally good test of temperance."
>
> *Gloss: The Athenian Stranger proposes wine as a test of temperance — not to be avoided, but to be engaged with under controlled conditions, so that the soul learns to master pleasure rather than flee it.*

*Laws*, Book I. Trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1892.

### Augustine — *City of God*

> "Temperance she commands to take only a certain quantity even of the most favourite food, lest, through immoderate use, anything prove hurtful by disturbing the health of the body, and thus Pleasure, which the Epicureans make to consist chiefly in the health of the body, be grievously offended. Thus the virtues, with the whole dignity of their glory, will be the slaves of Pleasure, as of some imperious and disreputable woman."
>
> *Gloss: Augustine rejects the Epicurean subordination of temperance to pleasure — if temperance serves only bodily health, it is not a virtue but a slave. True temperance serves God, not the body.*

*City of God*, Vol. I, Book V, Ch. 20. Trans. Marcus Dods, 1887.

### Augustine — *City of God*

> "For we must not fancy that there is no vice in us, when, as the apostle says, 'The flesh lusteth against the spirit;' for to this vice there is a contrary virtue, when, as the same writer says, 'The spirit lusteth against the flesh.' [...] And as we cannot attain to this in the present life, however ardently we desire it, let us by God's help accomplish at least this, to preserve the soul from succumbing and yielding to the flesh that lusts against it, and to refuse our consent to the perpetration of sin."
>
> *Gloss: For Augustine, temperance is not the achieved harmony of the soul but the ongoing war between flesh and spirit — a struggle that defines the Christian life and cannot be fully won on earth.*

*City of God*, Vol. II, Book XIX, Ch. 4. Trans. Marcus Dods, 1887.

### Adam Smith — *Theory of Moral Sentiments*

> "When all those three different parts of our nature were in perfect concord with one another, when neither the irascible nor concupiscible passions ever aimed at any gratification which reason did not approve of, and when reason never commanded any thing, but what these of their own accord were willing to perform; this happy composure, this perfect and complete harmony of soul, constituted that virtue which in their language is expressed by a word which we commonly translate temperance, but which might more properly be translated good temper, or sobriety and moderation of mind."
>
> *Gloss: Smith recovers the Platonic/Stoic definition of temperance as the concord of the soul's three parts, but notes that the English word 'temperance' is too narrow — 'good temper' or 'sobriety of mind' better captures the original sense.*

*The Theory of Moral Sentiments*, Part VII, Section II, Ch. 1. 1759.

## Reframings

### Buddhist tradition — *Sīla* (virtue/ethical conduct)

The Buddhist tradition does not frame self-restraint as a harmony of the soul's parts (since it denies a substantial self to harmonize) but as the cultivation of *sīla* — ethical conduct that reduces the fuel for craving and aversion. Temperance in the Western sense is closest to the practice of restraint (*saṃvara*) of the sense faculties, but the goal is not the mastery of appetite by reason but the uprooting of attachment itself. The Buddha's Middle Way between asceticism and indulgence is a structural parallel to Aristotelian moderation, but the metaphysical ground is entirely different.

### Chinese Confucian tradition — *Li* (ritual propriety)

Confucian self-cultivation does not separate temperance as a distinct cardinal virtue. Instead, the regulation of desire is embedded in *li* — ritual propriety — which shapes the person through external form into internal harmony. The temperate person is the one whose desires have been so thoroughly shaped by ritual that no internal conflict remains; the question of "mastering oneself" is answered not by reason ruling appetite but by the transformative power of practiced ceremony.

## Cross-cuts

- [[Courage]] — the two virtues of the non-rational parts of the soul in Plato's scheme; courage governs fear, temperance governs pleasure.
- [[Justice]] — temperance is the harmony of the individual soul; justice is the same harmony extended to the city and to the whole person.
- [[Pleasure_and_Pain]] — temperance is the virtue that governs our relation to pleasure and pain, neither fleeing nor pursuing them indiscriminately.
- [[Prudence]] — prudence discerns what is to be done; temperance ensures the appetites do not corrupt that discernment.
- [[Virtue_and_Vice]] — temperance is one of the four cardinal virtues in the Western tradition, alongside prudence, justice, and courage.

## Status notes

- The corpus returned no direct passages from Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* on temperance (*sōphrosynē*), which is a significant gap. Aristotle's treatment of temperance as the mean with respect to bodily pleasures is arguably the canonical Western formulation. This page should be revisited when Aristotelian passages are available.
- The Ptolemy *Tetrabiblos* passages (temperaments of zodiac signs) were considered and dropped — they use "temperate" in the meteorological/astrological sense, not the ethical sense, and do not commit Ptolemy to a position on the virtue.
- The Adam Smith passage is the best single statement of the Platonic/Stoic position in the corpus; it was retained despite being a secondary commentary rather than a primary source.
- Reframings are thin — the Buddhist and Confucian traditions have substantial material on self-restraint, but the corpus returned no direct or equivalent matches for *sīla* or *li* in the context of temperance. The reframings are based on general knowledge and should be expanded when corpus material becomes available.
