# Idea: Government

## Working definition

By what authority and through what institutional structures does a political community exercise power over its members? The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about sovereignty, legitimacy, and the distribution of powers between rulers and ruled. The term itself has generated centuries of debate over whether government is a natural outgrowth of human association, a contractual creation, or a delegated commission — and whether its primary task is to execute law, maintain liberty, or cultivate virtue.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: The Nature and Definition of Government

What government *is* — an intermediate body, an executive force, a commission from the sovereign people, or a natural expression of hierarchy — shapes everything else.

### Sub-question: Forms of Government

The classification problem — monarchy, aristocracy, democracy, and their mixtures — and the question of whether form is determined by who rules or by how sovereignty is located.

### Sub-question: Virtue and Government

What qualities rulers and subjects must possess, and whether the virtue required for governing differs in kind or only in degree from the virtue required for being governed.

## Western canon

### Aristotle — *Politics*

> *Book I, 1260a* — "…for to govern, and to be governed, are things different in species, but more or less are not. And yet it is wonderful that one party ought to have them, and the other not; for if he who is to govern should not be temperate and just, how can he govern well? or if he is to be governed, how can he be governed well? … it is evident then that both parties ought to be virtuous; but there is a difference between them, as there is between those who by nature command and who by nature obey, and this originates in the soul; for in this nature has planted the governing and submitting principle… a freeman is governed in a different manner from a slave, a male from a female, and a man from a child."
>
> *Gloss:* Aristotle commits to the view that governing and being governed are distinct in kind, not degree — a natural hierarchy rooted in the soul itself. Both ruler and ruled require virtue, but different virtues, because the relationship of command and obedience is not a matter of more and less but of species-difference.

> **Note on attribution:** This passage is tagged in the corpus under *Physics* but is in fact from the *Politics* (Book I, 1260a). Cited here as *Politics*, trans. Benjamin Jowett, 1885.

### Hobbes — *Leviathan*

> *Chapter 19* — "The Soveraignty therefore was alwaies in that Assembly which had the Right to Limit him; and by consequence the government not Monarchy, but either Democracy, or Aristocracy; as of old time in Sparta; where the Kings had a priviledge to lead their Armies; but the Soveraignty was in the Ephori. … For though where the people are governed by an Assembly, chosen by themselves out of their own number, the government is called a Democracy, or Aristocracy; yet when they are governed by an Assembly, not of their own choosing, ’tis a Monarchy; not of One man, over another man; but of one people, over another people."
>
> *Gloss:* Hobbes commits to the view that the form of government is defined solely by the location of sovereignty — who holds the ultimate right to limit and command — not by the number of participants in administration. A people governed by an assembly not of their choosing is, for Hobbes, living under a monarchy of one people over another.

### Rousseau — *The Social Contract*

> *Book III, Chapter I* — "What then is government? An intermediate body set up between the subjects and the Sovereign, to secure their mutual correspondence, charged with the execution of the laws and the maintenance of liberty, both civil and political. … [I]t is simply and solely a commission, an employment, in which the rulers, mere officials of the Sovereign, exercise in their own name the power of which it makes them depositaries. This power it can limit, modify or recover at pleasure; for the alienation of such a right is incompatible with the nature of the social body, and contrary to the end of association."
>
> *Gloss:* Rousseau commits to the sharpest definition of government in the canon — it is not a party to the social contract but a commissioned agent of the Sovereign (the people), holding power on revocable loan. Government is executive, nothing more; the legislative will remains with the people.

### Rousseau — *The Social Contract*

> *Book III, Chapter VII* — "Strictly speaking, there is no such thing as a simple government. An isolated ruler must have subordinate magistrates; a popular government must have a head. There is therefore, in the distribution of the executive power, always a gradation from the greater to the lesser number, with the difference that sometimes the greater number is dependent on the smaller, and sometimes the smaller on the greater. … Simple government is better in itself, just because it is simple. But when the executive power is not sufficiently dependent upon the legislative power, … the remedy for this want of dependence must be sought in mixed government."
>
> *Gloss:* Rousseau complicates his own taxonomy by arguing that no government is purely simple — all actual regimes mix elements, and the choice between simple and mixed turns on whether the executive needs to be checked by the legislative.

## Reframings

### Confucian tradition — De (Virtue / Potency)

The Western question about government focuses on institutions, sovereignty, and legitimate coercion. The Confucian tradition, by contrast, asks about government primarily through the moral quality of the ruler. A ruler governs not by law or contract but by *de* — the attractive moral power that radiates from a cultivated person and orders society without command. The question is not "what structure" but "who is the ruler becoming."

### Indian tradition — Dharma

Where the Western canon debates the legitimacy of governmental authority, the Indian tradition frames government (especially kingship) as the enforcement of *dharma* — the cosmic order that precedes and encompasses the state. The ruler's primary duty is not to execute the general will but to protect the hierarchy of duties that sustains the world. Government failure is a cosmic disorder, not merely a political one.

## Cross-cuts

- [[State]] — Government is the executive arm of the State in much of the Western tradition; the two are not synonymous but are often confused.
- [[Law]] — Government executes law, but the relationship between legislative and executive power is the central tension Rousseau identifies.
- [[Citizen]] — The citizen is the subject of government in one relation and the source of sovereignty in another; the distinction animates the social contract tradition.
- [[Constitution]] — A constitution establishes the form of government; the classification of regimes (monarchy, aristocracy, democracy) is a subset of constitutional theory.
- [[Sovereignty]] — Hobbes and Rousseau both locate sovereignty outside government proper, making government the servant rather than the master.
- [[Justice]] — Aristotle ties the virtue of the ruler directly to justice; a government without just rulers cannot govern well.
- [[Liberty]] — Rousseau defines government as charged with the maintenance of liberty; the tension between governmental authority and individual freedom is the modern form of the question.
- [[Democracy]], [[Monarchy]], [[Aristocracy]], [[Oligarchy]], [[Tyranny]] — The taxonomy of governmental forms runs through Aristotle, Hobbes, and Rousseau; each form answers the question "who rules?" differently.
- [[Revolution]] — If government oversteps its commission, the people may recover their power; Rousseau's theory of government as revocable commission is a hinge point.
