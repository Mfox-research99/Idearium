# Idea: Physics

## Working definition

What is the nature of the physical world, and how can it be known? The Western tradition asks this as a question about matter, motion, causes, and the principles governing change in the sublunary realm. Physics is distinguished from metaphysics (the study of being as such) and mathematics (the study of quantity and form), yet its boundaries have been contested since Aristotle. Other traditions do not always separate a discipline of "physics" from broader natural philosophy or cosmology; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

- **Scope and method** — How is physics demarcated from theology, mathematics, and other theoretical sciences?
- **Matter and form** — What are the ultimate constituents of physical bodies, and how do they change?
- **Laws of motion** — Can physical phenomena be reduced to universal laws, or do they require qualitative explanations?
- **Physics and metaphysics** — Where does physics end and first philosophy begin?

## Western canon

### Ptolemy — *Almagest*

> *Book I, ch. 1* — "The division [of theoretical philosophy] which investigates material and ever-moving nature, and which concerns itself with ‘white’, ‘hot’, ‘sweet’, ‘soft’ and suchlike qualities one may call ‘physics’; such an order of being is situated (for the most part) amongst corruptible bodies and below the lunar sphere."
>
> *Gloss: Ptolemy defines physics as the study of mutable, material nature, subordinate to mathematics in certainty and inferior to theology in dignity.*

> *Book I, ch. 1* — "From all this we concluded: that the first two divisions of theoretical philosophy should rather be called guesswork than knowledge, theology because of its completely invisible and ungraspable nature, physics because of the unstable and unclear nature of matter; hence there is no hope that philosophers will ever be agreed about them; and that only mathematics can provide sure and unshakeable knowledge to its devotees, provided one approaches it rigorously."
>
> *Gloss: Ptolemy denies physics the status of certain knowledge, reserving that for mathematics, which mediates between the sensible and the intelligible.*

### Leibniz — *Discourse on Metaphysics*

> *Section VII* — "The theory of substantial or accidental forms did more than to lead to nonsense like the above; it introduced errors which stood in the way of any clear investigation of real causes. For example, since some bodies fell toward the earth while others rose in the air, it was said that gravity was the substantial form of the former and lightness of the latter. Thus heavy and light bodies were distinguished as two classes of bodies having properties essentially different, and they were kept from the inquiry whether these apparently different phenomena did not have an identical cause and could not be explained by the same law."
>
> *Gloss: Leibniz rejects Aristotelian substantial forms as obstacles to a unified physics, advocating instead for a search for universal laws governing all motion.*

### Leibniz — *The Monadology and Other Philosophical Writings*

> *Section 18* — "These considerations, however metaphysical they may appear, are also of remarkable service in physics for establishing the laws of motion, as our Dynamics will be able to show. For it may be said that in the impact of bodies each suffers only through its own elasticity, caused by the motion which is already in it."
>
> *Gloss: Leibniz insists that metaphysical principles (e.g., the principle of sufficient reason, the nature of substance) are not separate from physics but provide the foundation for its laws.*

## Reframings

### Chinese Daoist tradition — No separate discipline of "physics"

The Chinese tradition does not isolate a study of matter and motion from the broader understanding of the Dao as the natural way of all things. The physical body is acknowledged as necessary for life, but the sage does not mistake its nourishment for the ultimate goal. The Zhuangzi treats the physical frame as something to be cared for but not clung to, and the concept of Qi (vital energy) underlies all natural processes without being reduced to a separate science.

> *Zhuangzi, Chuang Tzu: Mystic, Moralist, and Social Reformer, ch. 19* — "Life comes, and cannot be declined. It goes, and cannot be stopped. But alas! the world thinks that to nourish the frame is enough to keep life. And if indeed it is not enough, what then is the world to do? Although not enough, it must still be done. It cannot be neglected. For if one is to neglect the physical frame, better far to retire at once from the world. By renouncing the world, one gets rid of the cares of the world. The result is a natural level, which is equivalent to a re-birth. And he who is re-born is near to Tao."

## Cross-cuts

- [[Metaphysics]] — The boundary between physics and first philosophy is a persistent theme; Leibniz argues they are continuous, Ptolemy subordinates physics to mathematics.
- [[Matter]] — The object of physics is material nature; the concept of matter is central to both Aristotelian and modern physics.
- [[Cause]] — Physics investigates efficient and material causes; final and formal causes are often relegated to metaphysics.
- [[Nature]] — Physics is the study of nature (physis); the term itself derives from the Greek *physis*.
