# Idea: Virtue_and_Vice

## Working definition

What makes a character trait or action good (virtue) or bad (vice), and by what standard — reason, nature, social approval, or divine law. The Western tradition frames this primarily as a question about stable dispositions of the soul, the mean between extremes, and the relation of moral character to happiness. Other traditions reframe it through cosmic order, ritual propriety, or the cultivation of innate humaneness; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Virtue as the mean between extremes — Aristotle

> *Nicomachean Ethics* (Book II, ch. 6) — "Virtue then is 'a state apt to exercise deliberate choice, being in the relative mean, determined by reason, and as the man of practical wisdom would determine.' It is a middle state between too faulty ones, in the way of excess on one side and of defect on the other."
>
> *Gloss: Aristotle commits to virtue as a disposition of choice that hits the mean relative to us, determined by practical wisdom — vice is either excess or defect.*

> *Nicomachean Ethics* (Book II, ch. 3) — "every habit of the Soul by its very nature has relation to, and exerts itself upon, things of the same kind as those by which it is naturally deteriorated or improved: now such habits do come to be vicious by reason of pleasures and pains, that is, by men pursuing or avoiding respectively, either such as they ought not, or at wrong times, or in wrong manner."
>
> *Gloss: Virtue and vice are habits formed through pleasure and pain; the moral quality of an action depends on the circumstances of its pursuit or avoidance.*

### Virtue as social convention — Locke

> *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (Book II, ch. 28, §11) — "Virtue is everywhere, that which is thought praiseworthy; and nothing else but that which has the allowance of public esteem is called virtue. Virtue and praise are so united, that they are called often by the same name. ... And though perhaps, by the different temper, education, fashion, maxims, or interest of different sorts of men, it fell out, that what was thought praiseworthy in one place, escaped not censure in another; and so in different societies, virtues and vices were changed; yet, as to the main, the measure of what is everywhere called and esteemed virtue and vice is this approbation or dislike, praise or blame."
>
> *Gloss: Locke reduces the names "virtue" and "vice" to the law of opinion or reputation — they track public esteem, not any intrinsic moral nature.*

### Virtue as empty phantom — Voltaire

> *Philosophical Dictionary* ("Virtue," Section I) — "It is said of Marcus Brutus that, before killing himself, he uttered these words: 'O virtue! I thought you were something; but you are only an empty phantom!' You were right, Brutus, if you considered virtue as being head of a faction, and assassin of your benefactor; but if you had considered virtue as consisting only of doing good to those dependent on you, you would not have complained."
>
> *Gloss: Voltaire dramatizes the gap between virtue as a noble ideal and its betrayal by political violence, while still affirming a minimal virtue of doing good to those dependent on you.*

## Reframings

### Greek — Arete

The Greek concept *arete* (ἀρετή) is broader than "virtue" in the moral sense: it means excellence or fitness for purpose, whether of a horse, a knife, or a human being. The moral narrowing happens with Plato and especially Aristotle, who ties *arete* to the rational function of the soul. The English "virtue" captures only the ethical dimension, losing the sense of craft or functional excellence.

→ See [[Terms/Arete]]

### Greek — Eudaimonia

For Aristotle, virtue is not an end in itself but the activity that constitutes *eudaimonia* (flourishing). The Western Idea page treats virtue and vice as character traits; the Greek framing asks what kind of life makes virtue worth having. The two are inseparable in the *Nicomachean Ethics* but separable in modern moral philosophy.

→ See [[Terms/Eudaimonia]]

### Hindu — Dharma

The Sanskrit *dharma* (धर्म) encompasses duty, cosmic order, law, and righteousness — a single term that the Western tradition splits across Virtue, Justice, Duty, and Religion. Pre-1929 translators (e.g., Arnold in the Bhagavad Gītā) render it as "duty," but the concept carries a weight of cosmic maintenance that "virtue" misses. A virtuous act in the Western sense may or may not align with *dharma*; the two are not coextensive.

→ See [[Terms/Dharma]]

### Confucian — Ren and Yi

Confucian ethics centers on *ren* (仁, humaneness or benevolence) and *yi* (義, righteousness or appropriateness). These are not habits of choice in a mean but cultivated relational dispositions. *Ren* is the inward virtue of loving others; *yi* is the outward fittingness of action. The Western virtue/vice binary maps poorly onto a system where moral failure is more often a failure of attunement than of excess or defect.

→ See [[Terms/Ren]], [[Terms/Yi]]
