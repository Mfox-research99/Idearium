# Idea: Astrology

## Working definition

The art or science of interpreting the positions and motions of celestial bodies as signs or causes of terrestrial events, especially human character and destiny. The Western tradition frames this as a question about the legitimacy of divination, the relation between cosmic order and human freedom, and the boundary between astronomy and superstition. Other traditions embed astrological practice within broader cosmological and religious systems; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Astrology vs. Astronomy

The distinction between the mathematical study of celestial motions (astronomy) and the interpretive art of reading their significance (astrology), and whether the two are separable or interdependent.

### Sub-question: Determinism and Free Will

Whether astrological causation is absolute or leaves room for human agency, and how this tension is negotiated by practitioners and critics.

### Sub-question: Empirical Basis and Skepticism

The grounds on which astrology has been defended (observed correlations, tradition) and attacked (failed predictions, precession of equinoxes, lack of mechanism).

## Western canon

### Ptolemy — *Tetrabiblos*

> *Book I, Ch. 1 (translator's preface)* — "Not so slightly, however, does the astronomer differ from the astrologer, but *toto cœlo*: the astrologer considered the heavenly bodies and their motions merely as the mechanism wherewith he was to weave the tissue of his predictions; and astronomy is no more an integral part of astrology, than the loom is of the web which has been woven by it."
>
> *Gloss: Ptolemy (via his translator) insists on a sharp distinction between the two disciplines, positioning astrology as a practical art that uses astronomical data without being reducible to it.*

> *Book I, Ch. 25* — "Ptolemy seems to have been aware of this motion of the signs, and has fully provided for it in the 25th Chapter of the 1st Book of the Tetrabiblos. From that chapter it is clear that the respective influences he ascribes to the twelve signs (or divisions of the zodiac) were considered by him as appurtenant to the *places* they occupied, and not to the constellations themselves."
>
> *Gloss: Ptolemy addresses the objection of precession by grounding astrological influence in the tropical zodiac (positions relative to equinoxes), not the fixed stars — a move that preserves the system's coherence against astronomical change.*

> *Book I, Ch. 1 (translator's preface)* — "It is therefore clear that Ptolemy’s astrology is just as applicable to modern and improved astronomy as it was to his own."
>
> *Gloss: The translator argues that Ptolemy's system is independent of specific astronomical models, so it survives advances in astronomy — a claim that later skeptics would contest.*

### Voltaire — *Philosophical Dictionary*

> *"Astrology"* — "The great misfortune of the astrologers is that the sky has changed since the rules of the art were established. The sun, which at the equinox was in Aries in the time of the Argonauts, is now in Taurus; and the astrologers, unfortunately for them, follow the old calendar. However, that is not a demonstrative reason against astrology. The masters of the art deceive themselves; but it is not demonstrated that the art cannot exist."
>
> *Gloss: Voltaire grants that precession undermines traditional astrological rules, but he refuses to dismiss the art on purely logical grounds — a skeptical but not dogmatic position.*

> *"Astrology"* — "There is no absurdity in saying: Such and such a child is born in the waxing of the moon, during stormy weather, at the rising of such and such star; his constitution has been feeble, and his life unhappy and short; which is the ordinary lot of poor constitutions: this child, on the contrary, was born when the moon was full, the sun strong, the weather calm, at the rising of such and such star; his constitution has been good, his life long and happy. If these observations had been repeated, if they had been found accurate, experience would have been able after some thousands of years to form an art which it would have been difficult to doubt."
>
> *Gloss: Voltaire sketches a hypothetical empirical foundation for astrology — repeated correlation — but implies that such correlations have not in fact been reliably established.*

### Blavatsky — *The Secret Doctrine*, Vol. 1

> *"The Secret Doctrine and Modern Science"* — "Modern wisdom is satisfied with astronomical computations and prophecies, based on unerring mathematical laws. Ancient Wisdom added to the cold shell of Astronomy the vivifying elements of its soul and spirit—Astrology. And, as the sidereal motions *do* regulate and determine other events on Earth besides potatoes and the periodical diseases of that useful vegetable—a statement which, not being amenable to scientific explanation, is merely derided, while none the less accepted—these events have to submit to predetermination, by simple astronomical computations."
>
> *Gloss: Blavatsky reverses the standard hierarchy: astrology is the soul of astronomy, not its disreputable cousin, and she asserts a deterministic cosmic order that science refuses to acknowledge.*

## Reframings

The corpus contains no primary-source passages from non-Western traditions that directly address astrology as a concept. However, both Indian (Jyotisha) and Chinese (astrology based on calendrical cycles and five phases) traditions have developed elaborate astrological systems embedded in their cosmologies. These are not simply parallel arts but are integrated with religious practice, medicine, and statecraft in ways that the Western debate over legitimacy vs. superstition does not capture. A future expansion of the corpus may supply passages for a dedicated Term page (e.g., "Jyotisha" or "Chinese Astrology").

## Cross-cuts

- [[Astronomy]] — the mathematical study of celestial motions, historically intertwined with astrology and often distinguished from it.
- [[Fate]] — astrological prediction presupposes a fated or at least strongly conditioned order of events; the free-will debate is central.
- [[Magic]] — astrology shares with magic the claim that hidden correspondences between celestial and terrestrial realms can be manipulated or read.
- [[Prophecy]] — astrological prediction is a form of divination, akin to prophecy but based on systematic observation rather than inspiration.
- [[Science]] — astrology's claim to be a science (or its rejection as pseudoscience) is a recurring theme in the Western canon.

## Status notes

- The corpus returned 10 passages, but several were unusable: Firmicus Maternus (*Mathesis*) was a URL only; Nicomachus (*Introduction to Arithmetic*) was a scholarly introduction about astrology's origins, not a primary-source passage; Lilly (*Christian Astrology*) was a list of authors. These were excluded.
- The Ptolemy passages are from the 1822 Ashmand translation, which includes extensive translator commentary. The distinction between Ptolemy's own words and the translator's gloss is sometimes blurred in the corpus excerpts; I have cited the translator's preface where appropriate and flagged it.
- No passages from ancient critics of astrology (Cicero, *De Divinatione*; Augustine, *City of God*) were present in the corpus. These would strengthen the skeptical side.
- No non-Western astrological material was found. The Reframings section is therefore minimal; a note on the absence has been included.
- The Blavatsky passage represents the 19th-century occult revival of astrology, which is a distinct tradition from the classical and medieval practice. It is included to show the range of Western positions.
- Future additions: passages from Firmicus Maternus (if a usable text is obtained), from Islamic astrologers (Albumasar), and from Chinese or Indian sources would round out the page.
