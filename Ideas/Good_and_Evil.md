# Idea: Good_and_Evil

## Working definition

What is good, what is evil, and how are they distinguished? The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about the nature of moral value — whether good and evil are objective properties of actions and persons, or human projections, or divine commands, or necessary opposites. The framing assumes a binary moral ontology that other traditions do not always share; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: The objectivity of good — Aristotle

The good man is the measure of what is truly good; the many are deceived by pleasure into mistaking it for good.

### Sub-question: The origin of moral judgment — Hobbes

The knowledge of good and evil belongs properly to God; human beings who claim it usurp divine authority and cannot distinguish aright.

### Sub-question: The critique of moral categories — Nietzsche

Good and evil are not stable foundations but an old illusion, now dissolving under the pressure of revaluation.

## Western canon

### Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics*

> *Book III, Chapter IV* — "And so to the good man that is an object of Wish which is really and truly so, but to the bad man anything may be; just as physically those things are wholesome to the healthy which are really so, but other things to the sick. … For the good man judges in every instance correctly, and in every instance the notion conveyed to his mind is the true one. … The multitude of men seem to be deceived by reason of pleasure, because though it is not really a good it impresses their minds with the notion of goodness, so they choose what is pleasant as good and avoid pain as an evil."
>
> *Gloss: Aristotle commits to the objectivity of good — the virtuous person is the standard, not because good is relative, but because the good person's desires are aligned with what is truly good. Pleasure is a systematic deceiver.*

### Thomas Hobbes — *Leviathan*

> *Chapter 20* — "For the Cognisance of Judicature of Good and Evill, being forbidden by the name of the fruit of the tree of Knowledge, as a triall of Adams obedience; The Divell to enflame the Ambition of the woman, to whom that fruit already seemed beautifull, told her that by tasting it, they should be as Gods, knowing Good and Evill. Whereupon having both eaten, they did indeed take upon them Gods office, which is Judicature of Good and Evill; but acquired no new ability to distinguish between them aright."
>
> *Gloss: Hobbes reads the Fall as a political act — humans usurp the sovereign authority of God over moral judgment. The distinction between good and evil is not something humans can know independently; it is a function of command and obedience.*

### Friedrich Nietzsche — *Thus Spake Zarathustra*

> *"The thawing wind" (from "Old and New Tables")* — "O my brethren, is not everything AT PRESENT IN FLUX? Have not all railings and gangways fallen into the water? Who would still HOLD ON to 'good' and 'evil'? … There is an old illusion — it is called good and evil. Around soot and fire it hath circled hitherto."
>
> *Gloss: Nietzsche denies the stability of moral categories. Good and evil are not eternal truths but historical constructions, now dissolving. The "thawing wind" is the revaluation of all values that Zarathustra announces.*

### Hermes Trismegistus — *Hermetica* (Chambers, 1882)

> *Fragments* — "The God good, the Man evil. The Good voluntary, the evil involuntary. … Evil is aliment of World, Time corruption of Man. … Nothing in Heaven servile, nothing upon earth free."
>
> *Gloss: A gnostic-hermetic framing: good belongs to the divine realm, evil to the material. The opposition is ontological, not merely moral.*

## Reframings

### Buddhist tradition — Dukkha and skillful action

The Buddhist tradition does not posit a binary of good and evil substances. Actions are classified as *kusala* (skillful) or *akusala* (unskillful) based on whether they lead to the cessation of suffering (*dukkha*). The question is not "what is good?" but "what reduces suffering and uproots ignorance?" This reframes the entire moral project away from ontology and toward therapy.

→ See [[Terms/Dukkha]]

### Daoist tradition — Yin-Yang

In Daoist thought, good and evil are not independent opposites but interdependent phases of a single process. Light implies shadow; the sage does not eliminate evil but harmonizes the two. The *Tao Te Ching* (Legge, 1891) says "When all the world recognizes good as good, there arises the recognition of evil." The binary itself is a product of discrimination, not a feature of the Way.

→ See [[Terms/Yin-Yang]]

### Hindu tradition — Dharma and Karma

The Sanskrit terms *dharma* (cosmic order, duty) and *karma* (action and its consequence) do not map neatly onto good and evil. Actions are *dharmic* or *adharmic* — aligned with or against cosmic order — rather than intrinsically good or evil. The Bhagavad Gītā (Arnold, 1885) frames the problem as acting without attachment to the fruits of action, not as choosing the good over the evil.

→ See [[Terms/Dharma]], [[Terms/Karma]]
