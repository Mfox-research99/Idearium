# Idea: Judgment

## Working definition

The act of deciding — whether by a mind applying a rule to a case, a court pronouncing a sentence, or a perceiver distinguishing subjective impression from objective truth. The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about the conditions under which a judgment can claim validity: logical (analytic vs. synthetic), epistemic (perception vs. experience), and legal (interlocutory vs. final). Other traditions reframe it through discernment of the real from the illusory, or through the cultivation of practical wisdom in context; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: Judgment of perception vs. judgment of experience

How does a merely subjective connection of perceptions become an objectively valid judgment? Kant's critical distinction.

### Sub-question: Analytic vs. synthetic judgment

Whether the predicate is contained in the concept or adds something new — and what this distinction reveals about the work of judging.

### Sub-question: Legal judgment

The formal conclusion of a suit: the sentence of the law pronounced by the court upon the matter contained in the record.

## Western canon

### Immanuel Kant — *Prolegomena to Any Future Metaphysics*

> "Empirical judgments, so far as they have objective validity, are judgments of experience; but those which are only subjectively valid, I name mere judgments of perception. The latter require no pure concept of the understanding, but only the logical connexion of perception in a thinking subject. But the former always require, besides the representation of the sensuous intuition, particular concepts originally begotten in the understanding, which produce the objective validity of the judgment of experience."
>
> *Gloss: Kant commits to a two-tier structure of empirical judgment — perception-judgments are merely subjective records of mental states; experience-judgments require subsumption under an a priori concept of the understanding (e.g., cause) to become universally and necessarily valid.*

> "The given intuition must be subsumed under a concept, which determines the form of judging in general relatively to the intuition, connects its empirical consciousness in consciousness generally, and thereby procures universal validity for empirical judgments. A concept of this nature is a pure a priori concept of the Understanding, which does nothing but determine for an intuition the general way in which it can be used for judgments."
>
> *Gloss: The move from perception to experience is not a matter of adding more data but of subsuming intuition under a concept that legislates the form of judgment itself — the understanding does not passively receive but actively determines.*

### C. G. Jung — *Studies in Word Association*

> "In the analytic judgment a part of a concept, i.e. a predicate, is produced which was already necessarily represented in the concept. It, therefore, gives only that which was implicitly already present. In the synthetic judgment something is added to the concept which was not already necessarily included in the concept. In relation to associative work, therefore, synthetic judgment, if such an expression be allowed, stands above analytic judgment. Approaching the question practically we find (in so far as this mode of classification can be used at all in practice) that in simple judgment reactions the analytic judgment consists chiefly in the pointing out of a co-existent attribute perceptually evident, whilst the synthetic judgment is generally a judgment as to value, with more or less marked reference to the ego."
>
> *Gloss: Jung applies the analytic/synthetic distinction to the empirical data of word-association experiments, finding that synthetic judgments — which add something new and involve the ego's valuation — are psychologically richer and more diagnostic than analytic ones, which merely unpack what is already given.*

### William Blackstone — *Commentaries on the Laws of England, Book the Third: Private Wrongs*

> "The judgment, in short, is the remedy prescribed by law for the redress of injuries; and the suit or action is the vehicle or means of administering it. What that remedy may be, is indeed the result of deliberation and study to point out, and therefore the style of the judgment is, not that it is decreed or resolved by the court, for then the judgment might appear to be their own; but, 'it is considered,' consideratum est per curiam, that the plaintiff do recover his damages, his debt, his possession, and the like."
>
> *Gloss: Blackstone insists that legal judgment is not the court's arbitrary decree but the necessary conclusion following from the premises of law and fact — the court does not resolve but considers what the law itself already requires.*

> "Judgments are the sentence of the law, pronounced by the court upon the matter contained in the record; and are of four sorts. First, where the facts are confessed by the parties, and the law determined by the court; as in case of judgment upon demurrer: secondly, where the law is admitted by the parties, and the facts disputed; as in case of judgment on a verdict: thirdly, where both the fact and the law arising thereon are admitted by the defendant: which is the case of judgments by confession or default: or, lastly, where the plaintiff is convinced that either fact, or law, or both, are insufficient to support his action, and, therefore abandons or withdraws his prosecution; which is the case in judgments upon a nonsuit or retraxit."
>
> *Gloss: Blackstone classifies judgments by the posture of the parties toward law and fact — a procedural taxonomy that reveals judgment as the formal resolution of a dispute, not a free-standing act of will.*

### William Blackstone — *Commentaries on the Laws of England, Book the Fourth: Public Wrongs*

> "A judgment may be falsified, reversed, or avoided, in the first place, without a writ of error, for matters foreign to or dehors the record, that is, not apparent upon the face of it; so that they cannot be assigned for error in the superior court, which can only judge from what appears in the record itself."
>
> *Gloss: The appellate court's judgment is bounded by the record — it cannot consider matters outside the written proceedings, establishing a procedural limit on what can be judged and on what basis.*

## Reframings

### Hindu — Dharma

The Western tradition treats judgment primarily as an act of a mind or a court applying a rule to a case. The Hindu tradition reframes the question through *dharma* — the cosmic and social order that one discerns and performs, not a rule one applies from outside. Judgment here is less a cognitive operation than a recognition of one's place within a layered order (caste, life-stage, cosmic duty). The Bhagavad Gītā's central crisis is Arjuna's inability to judge rightly what he must do, and Krishna's teaching is not a rule but a reorientation of the judge's identity.

→ See [[Terms/Dharma]]

### Buddhist — Dukkha and Nirvana

Buddhist thought reframes judgment as a source of suffering (*dukkha*) rather than a path to truth. The discriminating mind that judges "this is good, this is bad" perpetuates attachment and the cycle of rebirth. The goal is not correct judgment but the cessation of judging — seeing things as they are (*yathābhūta*) without the overlay of conceptual construction. *Nirvana* is not a judgment one arrives at but the extinguishing of the conditions that make judgment necessary.

→ See [[Terms/Dukkha]], [[Terms/Nirvana]]

### Chinese — Wu Wei

The Daoist tradition reframes judgment as a kind of interference. The sage does not judge the world and then act; the sage acts without forcing (*wu wei*), responding spontaneously from the cultivated disposition (*de*) rather than deliberating and deciding. Judgment, in the Western sense of a conscious weighing of alternatives, is a symptom of being out of alignment with the Way (*Tao*).

→ See [[Terms/Wu_Wei]], [[Terms/Tao]]

## Cross-cuts

- [[Truth]] — Judgment is the act by which a proposition is affirmed or denied as true; Kant's "judgment of experience" is the vehicle of objective truth.
- [[Logic]] — The analytic/synthetic distinction is a logical classification of judgments before it is an epistemological one.
- [[Law]] — Legal judgment is the paradigmatic institutional form of judgment; Blackstone's taxonomy shows how procedure constrains the act of deciding.
- [[Reasoning]] — Judgment is the conclusion of a syllogism (Blackstone's "major and minor premises") and the terminus of reasoning.
- [[Ethics]] — Practical judgment (phronesis) applies general moral principles to particular cases; the Western tradition's sub-question of "judgment in action."
- [[Knowledge]] — Kant's distinction between judgments of perception and judgments of experience is a distinction between mere subjective awareness and genuine knowledge.
