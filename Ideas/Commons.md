# Idea: Commons

## Working definition

What is held in common, and by what right may it be used or appropriated? The Western tradition asks this primarily as a question about land, resources, and the boundary between collective ownership and private property. The concept emerges in English common law as a bundle of use-rights (pasture, fishing, turf, wood) and in political philosophy as the original state of nature from which property is carved by labor. Other traditions reframe the question through religious endowment, customary stewardship, or cosmic trusteeship; see Reframings.

## Sub-questions

### Sub-question: The State of Nature and the Commons

How does the original common condition of the earth justify or limit private appropriation? Locke’s labor-mixing argument is the canonical Western answer.

### Sub-question: Legal Taxonomy of Commons

What specific rights does “common” entail? English law distinguishes common of pasture, piscary, turbary, and estovers, each with its own rules of appendant, appurtenant, vicinage, and in gross.

### Sub-question: Enclosure and Improvement

When may the lord of a manor enclose common land, and what constraints protect the commoners? The statutes of Merton and Westminster II, and later General Inclosure Acts, mark the legal transformation of commons into private holdings.

## Western canon

### John Locke — *Two Treatises of Government*

> *Second Treatise, §28–29* — “Was it a robbery thus to assume to himself what belonged to all in common? If such a consent as that was necessary, man had starved, notwithstanding the plenty God had given him. We see in commons, which remain so by compact, that it is the taking any part of what is common, and removing it out of the state nature leaves it in, which begins the property; without which the common is of no use. And the taking of this or that part, does not depend on the express consent of all the commoners. Thus the grass my horse has bit; the turfs my servant has cut; and the ore I have digged in any place, where I have a right to them in common with others, become my property, without the assignation or consent of any body. The labour that was mine, removing them out of that common state they were in, hath fixed my property in them.”
>
> *Gloss: Locke grounds private property in the act of mixing labor with what is held in common, arguing that universal consent to appropriation is neither possible nor necessary for survival.*

### William Blackstone — *Commentaries on the Laws of England*

> *Book the Second: Rights of Things, ch. 3* — “And hence common is chiefly of four sorts: common of pasture, of piscary, of turbary, and of estovers. 1. Common of pasture is a right of feeding one's beasts on another's land: for in those waste grounds, which are usually called commons, the property of the soil is generally in the lord of the manor; as in common fields it is in the particular tenants. This kind of common is either appendant, appurtenant, because of vicinage, or in gross.”
>
> *Gloss: Blackstone codifies the English legal taxonomy of commons, distinguishing the types of use-right and the status of the commoner relative to the lord of the manor.*

> *Book the Second: Rights of Things, ch. 3* — “Common appendant is a right, belonging to the owners or occupiers of arable land, to put commonable beasts upon the lord's waste, and upon the lands of other persons within the same manor. Commonable beasts are either beasts of the plough, or such as manure the ground. This is a matter of most universal right: and it was originally permitted, not only for the encouragement of agriculture, but for the necessity of the thing.”
>
> *Gloss: Common appendant is the most ancient form, tied to the needs of arable farming and treated as a right of necessity rather than a privilege.*

> *Book the Second: Rights of Things, ch. 3* — “Common because of vicinage, or neighbourhood, is where the inhabitants of two townships, which lie contiguous to each other, have usually intercommoned with one another; the beasts of the one straying mutually into the other's fields, without any molestation from either. This is indeed only a permissive right, intended to excuse what in strictness is a trespass in both, and to prevent a multiplicity of suits: and therefore either township may inclose and bar out the other, though they have intercommoned time out of mind.”
>
> *Gloss: Vicinage common is a pragmatic accommodation of straying livestock, not a true right — it can be extinguished by enclosure.*

> *Book the Second: Rights of Things, ch. 3* — “This inclosure, when justifiable, is called in law, 'approving': an ancient expression signifying the same as 'improving.' But for a great many years past commons have only been inclosed under the provision of an act of parliament; these operations having been systematized by the General Inclosure Acts; under which commissioners are appointed for the purpose of superintending inclosures, and the process thus rendered less difficult and expensive.”
>
> *Gloss: Blackstone notes the shift from lordly enclosure by common-law right to parliamentary enclosure, marking the formalization of the process that would eventually extinguish most English commons.*

> *Book the Third: Private Wrongs, ch. 16* — “II. THE disturbance of common comes next to be considered; where any act is done, by which the right of another to his common is incommoded or diminished. This may happen, in the first place, where one who hath no right of common, puts his cattle into the land; and thereby robs the cattle of the commoners of their respective shares of the pasture. Or if one, who hath a right of common, puts in cattle which are not commonable, as hogs and goats; which amounts to the same inconvenience.”
>
> *Gloss: The law protects commoners against both outsiders and misuse by other commoners, defining the limits of commonable animals.*

> *Book the Third: Private Wrongs, ch. 16* — “The statute Westm. 2. 13 Edw. I. c. 46. extends this liberty of approving, in like manner, against all others that have common appurtenant, or in gross, as well as against the tenants of the lord, who have their common appendant; and further enacts that no assise of novel disseisin, for common, shall lie against a lord for erecting on the common any windmill, sheephouse, or other necessary buildings therein specified: which, sir Edward Coke says, are only put as examples; and that any other necessary improvements may be made by the lord, though in reality they abridge the common, and make it less sufficient for the commoners.”
>
> *Gloss: The lord’s right to improve (enclose) is balanced against the commoners’ right to sufficient pasture; the statute gives examples but Coke expands the principle.*

## Reframings

### Islamic tradition — Waqf

The Islamic institution of *waqf* (charitable endowment) creates a form of perpetual common use for religious or social purposes, but it is not a commons in the English legal sense — it is a dedicated trust, not a residual waste. The founder designates beneficiaries (the poor, a mosque, a school), and the corpus is inalienable. This reframes the question from “who may use what is common” to “who may dedicate property to God and for whom.” No Term page yet exists for *waqf*; see [[Terms/Covenant]] for related ideas of binding trust.

### Indigenous traditions — Usufruct and stewardship

Many Indigenous land systems recognize collective use-rights without individual ownership, often framed as stewardship or trusteeship for future generations. These are not “commons” in the Lockean sense of a state of nature awaiting appropriation, but rather managed landscapes with complex rules of access. No Term page currently covers this; the Idea page for [[Interdependence]] touches on related themes.
